Kathleen Thorpe Die Flügel altäre des Mittelalters erzählten eine vorgegebene Geschichte: die Heilsgeschichte. Derlei haben wir jetzt natürlich nicht. In manchen Badezimmern gibt es Klappspiegel, die ähnlich wie ein Flügelaltar konstruiert sind: die Konstruktion verfolgt den Zweck, den Anblick der 'Wahrheit' von ver schiedenen Seiten zu ermöglichen: Wie sieht's denn aus? — Jeder Befund entspringt einer Absicht, und die in die Zukunft verlängerte Absicht nennt man Politik.1 In choosing to structure his cycle of six books, the so-called "15 000 Seelen-Projekt" (1984-1988) as a triptych, Peter Rosei seizes on the promise of salvation and wholeness contained in this medieval religious art form in order to exploit and subvert its original intention by portraying the "Unheilsgeschichte" of everyday life. As indicated in the above quotation from the publisher's brochure, each individual work of the triptych contributes a scene related to the central panel. The banal content of the five "inner" works, i.e. Komödie (1984), Mann und Frau (1984), 15 000 Seelen (1985), Die Wolken (1985), and Der Aufstand (1987), covered by the sixth at first glance seemingly idyllic "outer" story Unser Landschaftsbericht (1988),2 perpetuates and refines the narrative formula achieved in the seven interrelated stories comprising Die Milchstrasse (1981),3 namely "der gewöhnliche Fall,"4 the ordinary case elevated to that of an example of the human condition in a late twentieth century urban environment. In this devotion to everyday life, which is the tenuous veil of subliminal tragedy, Peter Rosei follows in the footsteps of Adalbert Stifter, as noted by Richard Exner3 with reference to Rosei's earlier works.6 The critic Albert von Schirnding accurately describes in his remarks on Die Wolken Rosei's technique in the four peripheral works Modern Austrian Literature, Volume 26, No. 1, 1993</page><page sequence="2">88 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE of the cycle: "Roseis Fähigkeit, eine friedliche Alltagswelt zu zeichnen, in der unter sorgfältig geglätteter Oberfläche die Zerstörungswut lauert und die lautlosen Katastrophen sich abspielen, erreicht. . . die Meister schaft der äußersten Reduktion."7 The central panel of the triptych's feastday side, i.e. 15 000 Seelen, is by contrast a satirically cacophanous "Unheilsgeschichte." Thus the exalted outer intertextual frame of the "Flügelaltar" finds its secular expression in the winged bathroom mirror or, as Ditta Rudle observes ". . . die Spiegel des Toilettentischchens in den Schlafzimmern ordentlicher Bürger."8 reflecting the most private sphere of the characters populating Rosei's books. However, in establishing the structural principle underpinning the "15 000 Seelen-Projekt" and finding it to be the frame of a multi-faceted portrayal of human woe, the question as to the intention behind Peter Rosei's constant preoccupation with this theme arises, i.e. his "Politik" as remarked in the quotation at the outset. In the narrow sense of the word Rosei as a writer reportedly avows resistance to capitalist con sumerism without, however, showing "Ekel vor der kapitalistischen Gesellschaftsordnung," an attitude he is also known to dismiss as "pure Nostalgie", preferring rather to adapt to prevailing conditions in the art market and remarking with some bitterness that "die wahre Ideologie heißt heute cash."9 In the wider sense of "Politik" as applied to the intention and strategy of his literary output Rosei's views shed some light on these matters, providing at least a partial answer to the sort of criti cism directed at his predilection for banal themes as in a review of Die Wolken with its "kitchen sink" ending: "Peter Rosei hat es fertiggebracht, seinen über weite Strecken hochqualifizierten Roman in Blumenkohl Mystik enden zu lassen. Unverständlich."10 Rosei's credo as an artist, therefore, reads as follows: "Literatur hat sich zur sozialen Hilfswissen schaft entwickelt — dafür bin ich nicht zuständig. Ich gebe keine Ratschläge, verfasse keine Lebensregeln und leicht faßliche Aphorismen. Ich bin kein Politiker, der die Welt deuten und ordnen muß, damit er gewählt wird. Ich brauche keine Moral."11 While distancing his writing from overtly utilitarian considerations, Rosei does, however, not seek l'art pour l'art status for his work. In his consistent resistance to any predefined teleology in his works, Rosei nevertheless does aim at entering into a dialogue with the reader. This communicative process is made possible through the reader's recognition of the literary text as a work of</page><page sequence="3">Peter Rosei's Triptych 89 fiction, and as Jürgen Landwehr observes, the "poetologische(s) Prinzip formuliert ja die Erwartung, daß in Literatur, der als 'real' vor ausgesetzten Wirklichkeit andere, erfundene Wirklichkeiten gegenübergestellt werden."12 Thus by this contrastive process the reader becomes the site of synthesis in the dialectic of reality and the literary text Firmly rooted in the banality of a workaday urban existence, Rosei uses characters and situations to show and not to interpret it: "Ich muß nichts behaupten, sondern kann zeigen,"13 thus appearing to favor a naturalistic "camera eye" view over a tendency toward "magische(n) Realismus"14 coloring such of his earlier works as Bei schwebendem Verfahren and Wer war Edgar Allant1* Although elements of this approach are still evident in the novel 15 000 Seelen, Rosei sees his art as serving the purpose through the contrastive process already mentioned of promoting self-reflexion on the part of the reader and so fulfilling a facilitating role as an intermediary or catalyst between the individual and his experience of the world. For Rosei the great possibility in art is that "der Mensch dazu Stellung nehmen kann, sich abmessen, an dem, was er erfährt."16 It is clear therefore that Rosei aims at an active reader, one who is prepared to participate in the dialogue envisaged and to relate his findings to himself: "Der Leser muß sich schon selbst einbringen."17 In seeking to facilitate communication and self-reflexion Rosei would seem to deflect Exner's line of questioning back onto the reader and away from the author. Exner's questioning of the reticence of Rosei's characters: "Was verhindert es ihnen, müßte sich Rosei fragen, die wahre Intimität, nach der sie sich immerhin zu sehnen scheinen, zu verwirklichen?,"18 is countered also by Rosei's definition of his creative role as that of "Verdichten des Gesehenen," whereby "die Einbildungskraft des Künstlers . .. grosses Material (bündelt)."19 Of note also with regard to Rosei's intention is the coincidence of this stance with that of modern lyric poetry, which in the words of Hilde Domin invites self-reflexion: "Lyrik lädt uns zu der einfachsten und schwierigsten aller Begegnungen, der Begegnung mit uns selbst."20 In addition it may be observed that in spite of his protestations of a disinterested approach to "Moral," Rosei does not seek in his portrayal of human failure, to suppress all hope of a positive outcome in the reader. Although hope survives only in a residual form, Peter Prochnik's remarks on Die Milchstrasse retain their</page><page sequence="4">90 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE validity for the works of the 15 000 Seelen-Projekt: "Individuals may slither through life, be deluded losers, but the urge for some kind of self assertion is never wholly absent"21 In approaching the "15 000 Seelen-Projekt" as a whole the under lying structure of the triptych determines the chronology according to which the works are intended to be read to a large extent although, given the time over which the works were published, this chronology is artificial. The triptych is initially approached from the workaday cover Unser Landschaftsbericht which, when opened out to reveal the feastday side, shows the central panel, the novel 15 000 Seelen, flanked on the left by Komödie and beneath it Mann und Frau. On the right Die Wolken appears above Der Aufstand. Beginning with the workaday cover, the slim and modestly presented paperback volume Unser Landschaftsbericht, published at the conclusion of the cycle, contrasts markedly with the colorful dust-jackets of the remaining five works. This exterior side of the triptych divided into two sections, when read in the order of publication, contains readily recog nizable elements of the previous works, as Irène Bourquin observes par ticularly with regard to Die Wolken, 15 000 Seelen and the symbol of the ship in Der Aufstand,22 thus underscoring the interrelationships between the individual works and the unity of the triptych structure. Unser Landschaftsbericht confronts the reader with an archaic mythological landscape, described initially with "fast biedermeierlicher Beschaulich keit," which, gradually escalates into "groteske Visionen"23 in the second half of the work. Enhancing this mythological atmosphere is an invitation to participate creatively in this idyll: "Nimm ein Schilfrohr, schneide ein handliches Ende ab, stich Löcher hinein — da hast du eine Flöte: Jetzt musiziere!"(5). Having established the connection between artistic creativity and human participation, nature is personified: "die Wurzel umschlingt einen Stein, legt einen Arm um ihn, drückt ihn, da sie selber die Tiefe sucht, nach unten"(7). The flute, however, imitates the voice of nature, reminding man of his alienated state in a romantic "Sehnsuchtsmotiv" reminiscent of Eichendorff: "Und die Rohrflöten des blauen Himmels, von denen zu Anfang die Rede war, spielen dir im Anblick dieser Bäume ein Lied in der Brust: wie dieses Lied klingt? — Wenn ein Gefäß zerbricht, das dir aus irgendeinem Grund kostbar war, dann klingt das so" (7). Of note is the fact that man and not nature is the</page><page sequence="5">Peter Rosei's Triptych 91 source of the melancholia just described in his attempt to be at one with nature. In Unser Landschaftsbericht man is primarily an observer of the natural life cycle, "der leergefressene Brustkorb eines Tieres" (11), which he tries to understand. Nature appears, however, on the one hand to prof fer meaning in a manner recalling the lines of Eichendorff's poem Abschied, namely: "Da steht im Wald geschrieben/ Ein stilles, ernstes Wort . . ."24 but with an ironic twist that immediately turns the chiffre like "writing," which is man's attempt at fixing meaning, into a cruel joke. Nature's meaning quite literally not only eludes him, but also adds insult to injury by putting up a mocking barrier: "Und ehe du dich versiehst, werden die Baumstämme vor dir zur Höhlenschrift, zu einem einzigen, vibrierenden Schriftzug, der von dir mit seinem hingepatzten Lettern weghinkt, der gleich darauf gegen dich vorrückt wie ein Rechen, wie ein schwarzer Zaun: die kleinen Vogelstimmen sind daraufgespiesst und tirilieren (11). Trees not only provide the backdrop for a landscape description but supply the material for the link between man, nature, and language. This is in essence, as Irène Borquin observes, "ein verwun schener Wald, ein Märchenwald . . .n2S that is occasionally commented on with irony. As in an enchanted forest, barriers between reality and the imagined become transparent: "In der für Rosei typische Wechsel beziehung und Durchdringung von Innenwelt und Aussenwelt verändert der Wald ständig das Gesicht"26 This mutable Quid quality is most obvious in the figures that emerge out of the natural shape of the trees, almost as if Rosei were copying a painting. A buxom figure resembling the mythological huntress Diana appears with her hunting dogs, introduc ing an incongruous element of the threatening mechanical age into the mythical scene: "Die Köpfe der Hunde sind aus eloxiertem Blech herausgestanzt, mit kleinen Löchern an den Schnauzen, wo die Haare abstehen. Im Innern enthalten sie Maschinen, Wunderwerke der Präzision: In den aufgeregten Augen drückt sich der Wunsch dieser Technik unzweideutig aus" (14). In this blend of natural "killer instinct" and clockwork precision the destructive forces of 15 000 Seelen are prefigured. However, ordinary people are also placed in this anthropomorphic landscape; whereas the images of mythological figures such as Diana proceeded out of natural forms, now an inversion takes place. A group of people appears first as a foreign body in the landscape: "Ihre Körper</page><page sequence="6">92 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE werfen lange Schatten in die Landschaft hinaus. Solche Schatten werfen Steinsäulen, die einmal harmonisch geordnet, nun aber wie betrunken aus dem Boden zeigen" (16). These people, male and female, not only replace the trees standing there "wie ein Wald," but also appear as figures in an artificial structure, within which they can be manoeuvred and manipu lated, thus prefiguring the relationships forming the material of the internal wings in Rosei's triptych: "So reich kommt einem das Programm der Beziehungen vor, das sie miteinander verbindet" (16). The couples described are estranged from one another; each person seeks contact with someone other than his/her partner. Their emotional lives are reflected in their facial expressions. They are travelers without any predefined destination beyond their memories and expectations: "Ihre Schuhe sind von rotem Staub bedeckt, und an Kleinigkeiten in ihrem Aufzug glaubt man zu erkennen, dass sie schon lange unterwegs sind. Ob sie aber wirk lich irgendwohin wollen? Weder rasten sie, noch verrät ihre Haltung Unruhe; alles, was sie in den Abstufungen der Gemüter beschäftigt, sind ihre Erinnerungen und vielleicht ihre Erwartungen" (17). As the first section of Unser Landschaftsbericht draws to a close, the almost static atmosphere evoked by the people is churned up by the movement of fecund nature. Again nature gives birth to myth: "Aus der Seerose wird der Krake" and vice versa "aus dem lauen Wasser wird Urin (21). Visions of horror, death, and human greed appear, a crucifixion scene blends with that of an abbatoir, a Roman soldier desecrates corpses while searching for gold coins on a battlefield strewn with the bodies of the slain. Nature, however, in the image of a flowing river remains unmoved by human activity as it greedily feeds and flows on: "Drüben strömt, es ist, als fielen Schneeflocken in seine gierige, trinkbereite Oberfläche, der graue Fluß" (26). The short second section of Unser Landschaftsbericht portrays a sat urated swamp: The forest appears here only in the token form of "ein wenig zeremonieller" (27) forest. As empty as the landscape in the first section was full, the trees that do appear are cypresses like "Ketten von Wächtern" (28), emphasizing human forms, which now predominate, but in fragmented segments, i.e. cheeks and lips, all part of a group of primitive dancers covered with tufts of moss and grass, finally identified as blocks of stone: "Alles ist aus porösen Steinquadern zusammengefügt, aus Kalkstein . . ." (31). These monumental forms, brought into</page><page sequence="7">Peter Rosei's Triptych 93 movement by the imagination of the observer, give way to a nocturnal landscape, in which indistinct shapes in the starlight exude a raw sexual energy terminating at daybreak: "die Glocke läutet" (38). In the half-light the landscape is seen to be at an end. The chain of hills forming this barrier releases one last image, that of a powerful man playing a lyre. This identifies him as Apollo, who sings "Fangen wir an!" (39). He not only Teturns to the musical introduction of Unser Landschaftsbericht but also simultaneously concludes the "overture" of the triptych, signalling the opening of the altar to reveal the feastday scenes. Immediately confronting the reader therefore is the central panel of the triptych, 15 000 Seelen. This satiric novel reveals chaotic conditions more in keeping with an untidy bathroom than with its sacred analogy as the central panel of a story of salvation unless scenes from Hieromymus Bosch's "Last Judgment" are called to mind. 15 000 Seelen was met with either great enthusiasm or dismissal by critics upon its publication. The novel is a satire of a consumer society gone mad and prefigured by the carnage depicted in Unser Landschaftsbericht (22-26), and the unchar acteristically accelerated tempo of the narrative recalls similarly hectic passages in Rosei's earlier satire Bei schwebendem Verfahren.27 Whereas critics such as J.M. Adam are unreserved in their praise: "Alles geht, hinab den Bestienschlund, den Schlund der sich endlich totlachenden Bestie. Das ist genialisch, infernalisch, dionysisch, unglaublich, un glaublich gut Das müssen Sie lesen!".28 Others are less enthusiastic, finding the work to be "nur mässig witzig . . ." and also criticizing the exaggeration characterizing the work in its "Ausrufungsstil eines Jahr marktschreiers, der gegen Ende der Vorstellung wegen Heiserkeit aufgibt."29 15 000 Seelen is a novel about world records; while not without merit, especially when viewed as a vision of Hell at the center of Rosei's "Unheilsgeschichte," the work does exhaust the reader through the sheer accumulation of horrible fantasies. As Albert von Schirading notes: "Das Prinzip einer sich selbst überschlagenden Progression hat freilich auch zur Folge, daß ein Höhepunkt den anderen totschlägt und der Leser auf der Rekordstrecke bleibt . . . ."30 Others such as Marion Leske are alienated by the deliberately nauseating descriptions with which the novel abounds: "Roseis perfider Schwanengesang auf unsere Kultur erreicht die Grenze des Erträglichen."31 But, as even Werner Fuld somewhat grudg</page><page sequence="8">94 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE ingly concedes: "Roseis Grundidee (ist) gar nicht schlecht."32 By placing the view of a world determined by excess and revulsion at the center of his triptych the quieter waters of the surrounding four works, which are essentially "lautlose Katastrophen,"33 contrast Rosei's more character istic technique of reduction to better effect. Therefore, the basic idea of the entire triptych structure, shows a "Weltanschauung" in which the idea of a center as an orientation point is held up to scorn. Rosei's characters and their stories are peripheral and have as their only center their essen tially unstable emotional lives. This notion of life without a center is not unique to the "15 000 Seelen-Projekt" but has already been succinctly formulated in Die Milchstrasse with reference to the myriad characters and their stories making up this work: "Schliesslich war diese Milch strasse bloß eine von vielen. Nun in der äußersten Abgetrenntheit nähern sich die Menschen wieder der Mitte: Wo alles Mitte ist, verliert der Ort seine Bedeutung."34 From the outset 15 000 Seelen focuses on the "hero" of the work, an emasculated man whose name reveals his initial insignificance as the garrulously intruding narrator divulges: "Ganz tatsächlich hieß er . . . Klockmann, mit CK und doppeltem N. Doch hatte er das C eines Tages durch eine schnelle, fast schmerzlose Operation entfernt, um die Aussprache abzurunden. Das noch hinderliche N am Schluß war dann wie von selber irgendwo verlorengegangen" (7). Klok man's job as a representative for the verification of world records exposes him to the same tyranny of target figures determining the lives of commercial sales representatives in any capitalist economic system. Not only does Klokman have to heed the admonitions of his boss — "Die Zahlen müssen klettern!" (35) — he is also obliged, given his lowly station at the foot of the ladder to success, to treat his boss with deference: he has to listen to his boss's boring family anecdotes. After a brief introduction Klokman, notebook in hand, is sent on his way, accompanied by his supervising boss and the ironically commenting, in trusive narrator. Klokman's first assignment reminds the reader of the analogy be tween the triptych and the bathroom mirror as he witnesses a competition in continuous shaving, presumably to advertise disposable razors. True to the banality of his life, he succumbs to the charms of his boss's secretary, Frau Katz, during a pause between competitions. When the competition is resumed, not only is Klokman under pressure to ensure an</page><page sequence="9">Peter Rosei's Triptych 95 escalation of the target figures, but the competitors as well are subjected to the same pressure to "achieve" and get caught up in the frenzy gener ated by the competition's organizers. A record is broken amid hysteria and scenes of horrible self-mutilation. The state of the competition's winner is described in horrifying terms and with deliberate bad taste. Associating carnage with food, continually throughout the novel causes revulsion on the part of the reader: ". . . der Held selber! Kein Gesicht mehr, sondern bloß noch Fleischklumpen; ein Kübel, über den Gulasch rann. Und kleine Springfontänen von Blut . . . Blutspritzer tanzten auf den Schaumbergen, die den Helden hüfthoch umgaben. Erdbeeren mit Schlag" (38). Gripped by mass hysteria, the spectators urge the punch drunk "hero" to break yet another record while his wife, accompanied by his two children, "heulte herzzerreissend" (38). The narrator's final comment addressed to the "hero" goes unnoticed in the general din: "Wie konntest du nur so weit von dir weggehen — dich so sehr verlassen!" (39). Each of Klokman's subsequent assignments involves some kind of record in human degradation. His spirits rise and fall as he goes on his way, taking notes and verifying records. Following the pattern set by the first assignment, a visit to an ice-skating facility describes it as a murderous branch of the leisure industry. So many people are crowded into a confined space that they turn in on one another. Two circles of skaters are the "Servierplatten" on which a scene of carnage and pande monium is served: "gewaltig, brausend und krachend und aufjaulend . . . — denn die Urlauber purzelten wie Wanzen, als braune Knödel über die rasend nach oben schnappende, . . . still leuchtend dastehende Fläche!" (72). Klokman further witnesses a sado-masochistic scene at a circus in which thousands of naked and chained people are crammed into a huge drum, noting with grim satisfaction that the crazed participants are: "solide Leute,... die leibhaftige Mittelklasse" (95). Now well pleased with his progress, Klokman inspects his eyes in a bathroom mirror, and the "Aura von Glanz" he finds reflected there points to the deceptiveness of his apparent rise in the world, for he sees "Zacken einer Krone," to which the narrator adds "Oder Flaschenkorken" (105). His name also assumes larger proportions in keeping with his growing self-esteem; it now sports two Ns (109). After a few more record-breaking feats of re vulsion equal to those already mentioned, Klockmann can finally count</page><page sequence="10">96 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE himself successful, having also returned the C to his name. He is pro moted to "Direktor" (166) but correctly cannot quite believe his good fortune. His unresolved approval-seeking relationship with his parents suddenly rises up to haunt him, and he is finally destroyed in a riot around a heap of money, a sort of violent dance around a golden calf, recalling the role of gold coins in Unser Landschaftsbericht. The novel finally comes to a close in a sea of lava-like black cream. 15 000 Seelen is an overwhelming work, and the abiding impression created is one of excess and exhaustion: "der Korb der Ereignisse ist einfach zu voll" (94). The "message" of this cornucopia of degradation, horror, and ultimate failure is summed up as follows: "Nirgends Heil! ... die Welt ging entzwei. Alles im Eimer" (168), a view that sets the tone for the sur rounding works. Komödie, a type of (Turkish) fairytale once again introduces the sort of story for which Rosei is well-known but nevertheless echoes the cen tral message of 15 000 Seelen. The first-person narrator is a humble tour ist guide leading an unremarkable life until a waif, a little girl, follows him home, awakens new feelings in him, and brings new meaning and a sense of purpose to his life. Although he is able to perceive the world in a new light through the childs's eyes, even this cannot assuage his exis tential loneliness. The child in the narrative provides a source of joy but also a constant reminder of his own alienation from life, "denn es schaute alles so an, daß es jetzt durch dieses Anschauen, auch für mich wirklich war, fand ich mich doch allein" (27). The child's growing independence only serves to make him aware of his own dispensability, because "Das Leben geht weiter, wenn du fehlst" (31). This fairy tale, like the Tales from the Arabian Nights (39), assures the continuation of life for the duration of the narrative. Writing is the search for self-knowledge and identity, as the narrator muses: "Wer ich bin. Vielleicht habe ich die Arbeit des Berichtens nur aus dem Grund auf mich genommen" (85). In articulating his life with his adopted daughter the narrator is able to imagine a wholeness of being: "In meinen Träumen bin ich selbstgenügsam wie Gott" (87). When the child finally leaves him, his life returns to what it was, evoking also the idea that the exist ence of the child could have been a figment of his imagination: "Ich fahre zur Arbeit. Ich arbeite. Ich fahre heim" (101).</page><page sequence="11">Peter Rosei 's Triptych 97 This melancholy tale is not without charm, as the critic Hans Chris tian Kosler admits, in spite of his criticism of the story's banality, which is intentional and in keeping with Rosei's "Politik" as a writer. Kosler notes: "Beinahe fibersieht man, daß in diesem Schwall von abgegriffenen Bildern und unbeholfen formulierten Impressionen noch eine zarte Hand lung eingebunden ist, an der sich in ihrer tiefen Nachdenklichkeit und edlen Einfalt nur Handkes 'Kindergeschichte' messen kann".33 Indeed, the "märchenhafte Unbestimmtheit"36 of this story — the setting is not directly identified — demonstrates once again Rosei's narrative formula of turning the banal into the remarkable. It presents an attempt to cope with life through dreaming and writing. Loneliness and a life held together by the fabric of a monotonous humdrum existence that determines the conclusion of Komödie also forms the basic theme of Mann und Frau, the story of a failed relationship. The precarious emotional stability achieved by the two main characters, Murad und Susanne, with their child Almut in a peripheral role is re flected in the carefully balanced construction (Ausgewogenheit)11 of the composition which does not allow either of the protagonists to dominate the narrative. Despite the occasional rather labored attempts at departing from conventional expressions38 in order to lend the banal tale an aura of the remarkable, Mann und Frau provides a further exploration of hu man inability to communicate emotional needs and thus perhaps create the conditions within which a mutually satisfying relationship between a man and a woman could flourish. This failure results because the narra tive cannot provide any basis to establish a relationship. Rosei feels that his aim is not to interpret but merely to show. This view is supported by György Sebestyén's insight into the function of the character Murad: Der Name des Mannes kann als Anagram verstanden werden. Er heisst Murad = Darum. Mit diesem Darum beantwortet Rosei die pedantischen Fragen: Warum und wieso? eine Erklärung gibt es nicht. Murad schwebt durch das Leben, Susanna durchschreitet die Wirklichkeit, Almut erlebt dies und jenes — so ist es.39 Plagued by guilt because of the failed relationship, Susanne keeps a residual contact with Murad because she is unable to achieve emotional</page><page sequence="12">98 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE independence. Like Murad she too is cannot enter into any lasting rela tionship with a member of the opposite sex. Susanne's casual relation ships with men do, however, lead her to the admission: "Ich habe mich nicht befreit, wie sollte ich auch? Aber gleichgültig bin ich geworden" (93). Her indifference is a carefully guarded precarious state of mind: "Ein Sturm von außen, von innen, hätte Susanne vernichtet .... Äuß erlich ging das Leben gleichmäßig hin" (94). In defining Rosei's painstaking "pointillistische Methodik" Sebestyén accurately remarks: "Er stellt Zustände dar, die sich miteinander verbunden oder scheinbar isoliert bleiben, sich allmählich zum Schicksal summieren . ..." 40 These ob servations are based on Rosei's reference to the fragments (Tropfen) making up the whole. In exploring the choice open to the individual while selecting his view of life Rosei points to the role tradition and culture play in imposing images of the world onto Man. Only through the creative act could these images be altered. Although the elements making up life (described by analogy to the ocean) would not change, one's atti tude to life could alter: "Wir stellen klar: Daß es die Frage ist, ob alles Unglück nicht daher kommt, daß man nicht mitfühlen kann. Daß der Feh ler vielleicht schon im Bild sitzt, das da gesetzt wird: Ich als einzelner. — Das leuchtet ein: Statt des Bildes mit dem See, den Felsen, könnte man ein anderes wählen, entwerfen, etwa ein Meer, in dem lauter Tropfen sind, die dann doch die Woge formen" (94). Die Wolken, occupying the top right-hand wing of the triptych, pre sents a variation on the theme of Mann und Frau with the introduction of a disruptive third party into the relationship between Reinhard and Eva. This seemingly orderly relationship is disturbed by the presence of Gobbo, Reinhard's friend from his student days, who engages in an affair with Eva. Although the marriage remains intact, Eva's lack of fulfillment remains. She is unable to love Reinhard, to give him "die Antwort, die er sich wünscht" (141). Devoting himself to the cultivation of his vegeta ble garden, Reinhard tries to maintain a semblance of normality. The tragedy underlying this essentially unremarkable tale of the everyday life of a couple held together by their children illustrates Rosei's technique of merely showing what is. Thus, because the partici pation of the reader reconstructs and connects events and links in the narrative, "eröffnet sich die ganze Abgründigkeit einer Lebensform, die weder Glück noch Unglück zuläßt. Wer da nicht steigen kann, muß stürzen" (114). The final work completing the triptych and placed immediately after Die Wolken is Der Aufstand, which contains elements of all the preceding works: loneliness, lack of purpose, an inability to communicate, and an everyday monotonous existence. Recalling 15 000 Seelen directly, how ever, is the element of collective activity that has been lacking in the three "scenes" of the triptych's side wings. This final segment of Rosei's "Unheilsgeschichte" seems to break the pattern by suggesting, at least in the title, some kind of activity, an active expression of dissatisfaction with the prevailing order which heralds a possible change of direction. However, this expectation is disappointed, when Der Aufstand is revealed to be ultimately a reformulation of the message contained in Rosei's earlier essay "Zur Frage der Revolution,"42 where he juxtaposes a desire for action in a continuous revolution, "ein Fest ohne Ende," with a denial of this type of action, constantly thwarted by the process of thinking, which, as long as it continues, will preclude physical action: "In der Stille, in der absoluten Bewegungslosigkeit, in der wir uns befinden, denken wir immer wieder an den Aufruhr. Solange wir uns mit unserer Lage nicht abgefunden haben und das wird nie der Fall sein, werden wir immer an die Revolution denken. Sie wird nicht Wirklichkeit werden. Ge danken, nichts als Gedanken."43 Der Aufstand, although ending in revolutionary action of a kind, is in essence a story about "die Unfähigkeit zum Aufruhr, der die Ver hältnisse umstürzt,"44 analogous to the character Perkins in Von hier nach dort, who states: "ich versuche zu leben, aber es gelingt mir nicht."45 Set in a small university town, Der Aufstand, as Albert von Schirnding remarks, "liefert eine ironisch-satirische Variation des hochwürdigen Stirb-und-werde-Themas . . . .n46 While recuperating from an accident in which he had been knocked off his bicycle, a university professor has the opportunity of reviewing his life and deciding to change direction. As Klaus Antes observes, the story is related from two perspec</page><page sequence="14">100 MODERN AUSTRIAN LITERATURE tives, of the disillusioned "68er Generation" and that in Von hier nach dort, the "Suche des Menschen nach Heimat, ausserhalb seiner Haut, also von der Sehnsucht nach Wiedergewinnung des Lebens, jetzt und hier . . . ."47 Reminiscing about his student days, the professor identifies his pre sent isolation as the cause of his discomfort because in recalling his participation in a student demonstration, he finds that it was not the cause itself that attracted him but the elation resulting from being part of a crowd: "Ich war so gut aufgelegt! Ich war so fröhlich, so frisch, so bieder!" (45). He seeks solace subsequently, by helping himself at the "Gabentisch der Aphrodite" (51) in the form of a homosexual affair with a student. Through contact with this student's radical views, he once again wishes to have a cause to champion, especially when his lover cools toward him, generating again "den grellen Schmerz des Alleinseins" (80). The professor finally joins students in a demonstration, the cause of which is not clear beyond a "vage Mißstimmung."48 This lack of a clear ly defined aim also points to the spuriousness of this revolutionary ges ture, when the words of a student: "Im wahrhaft revolutionären Moment brauchst du nicht gegen die Polizei zu kämpfen — sie steht dann auf unserer Seite" (118) — are negated by the police attacking the demon strators. However, the final paragraph of the story is related mostly in the first person plural, calling to mind the professor's being at one with the crowd regardless of the cause or consequences. This review of the six individual works comprising the "15 000 Seelen-Projekt" presents a multi-faceted "Unheilsgeschichte" of late twentieth-century urban existence. Fulfillment slips through the fingers of Rosei's characters like a piece of wet soap. With the exception of the catastrophic riotousness of the overpopulated central satire, 15 000 Seelen, the spare stories of the peripheral works narrated in Rosei's carefully honed reductive style reflect everyday tragedies, i.e. scenes more in keeping with the analogy of a bathroom or dressing table mir ror49 than the "Heilsgeschichte" suggested by the triptych-like outer structure. Indeed, a review of the "15 000 Seelen-Projekt" as a whole reveals a more differentiated affinity to Adalbert Stifter than was possible to ascertain from the vantage point of Exner.50 While the validity of Rosei's own evaluation of Stifter stands, it is of interest to note that Rosei's new project adheres even more closely to Stifter than his previ</page><page sequence="15">Peter Rosei's Triptych 101 ous works do. Rosei's characterization of Stifter's style, especially with reference to his later works, reads increasingly like a self-evaluation in the light of the "IS 000 Seelen-Projekt." Particularly noteworthy in this connection are Stifter's efforts as he grew older to mask his anxiety about the discrepancy between the ordered artistic form and his disastrous per sonal life by resorting to the method of reduction, "die Sprache bis auf ihr Skelett abzumagern, die Phantasie auf ein Minimum zurückzunehmen und nichts dem Zufall zu überlassen."31 Rosei's everyday tragedies contained within the wholeness of a reli gious art form maintain a balance between order and chaos. Indeed, Stifter's personal life could have served as the inspiration for Rosei's "lautlose Katastrophen"32 with regard to his unhappy childless marriage to the prosaic Amalia Mohnhaupt, which is reflected in many of his sto ries.33 Just as Stifter held his life in a precarious balance, which is reflected in the extreme order expressed both in the form and the surface contents of his works, so Rosei also adopts a similar stance in his triptych. Stifter's cautious style masking subliminal tragedy leads to the much-researched combination of boredom and fascination emanating from his works.34 Following Stifter's examples Rosei harvests a similar re sponse from many readers. However, Rosei allows the "Brüchigkeit" of existence to show through his carefully constructed stories unlike Stifter, who took pains to conceal his own problematic life from the reader. In so doing Rosei permits his twentieth-century characters to struggle on in the knowledge that all is not well. Life is lived, no matter how shabbily.
