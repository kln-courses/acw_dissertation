Within an essentially Aristotelian tradition, Stegmann's treatise expounds material- ist theories of the mind and God which are entirely opposed to Leibniz's own system, and since these theories are clearly designed to serve as the philosophical basis of a theodicy, they provided Leibniz with a stimulus to show that true theology rests on quite different grounds. In the course of elaborating his own rival metaphysics, however, Leibniz makes statements which are to my knowledge unparalleled in his writings so far published. Indeed the views which Leibniz advances in this critique are a challenge to some standard interpretations of the fundamental principles of his philosophy. This introduction will attempt to assess the contribution made by the new manuscript to an understand- ing of Leibniz's metaphysics, and to consider how far the surprising statements which it contains can be reconciled with his professed aims in the critique. Part of the interest of the new manuscript derives from parallels with the Clarke correspondence which it antedates by only a few years. Leibniz gives his own explanation of how the paper originated. On the first page of the manuscript he tells us that he came across some notes on Stegmann during a recent inspection of old papers : he decided that, when suitably revised, these notes were worth publishing as an introduction to Stegmann 's treatise. Significantly Leibniz thought that the treatise was too poisonous to be published on its own without some sort of antidote. In general terms this account sounds plausible, for as a young man Leibniz was commissioned by his patron von Boineburg to reply to the Socinians. That Leibniz was acquainted with Stegmann's treatise during this period is confirmed by a letter he wrote to von Hessen-Rheinfels in 1691, in which he defended himself against the charge of harbouring Socinian sympathies10. Ac- cording to Leibniz then, the first version of the manuscript dates from a very early period in his life (circa 1669), and indeed though the paper bears the unmistakable impress of Leibniz's later thought, it also contains statements which link it with such early works as De Stylo Nizolii11. The persistence of such traces in this paper is a reminder that Leibniz never abandoned his youthful subscription to the nominalist doctrine that entities are not to be multiplied beyond necessity. Indeed we need to appeal to this very principle to explain some of the mature statements in this manuscript. How far Leibniz succeeds in his own counter-attempt at theodicy may be doubted : some of his metaphysical ideas appear to create tensions with the announced aims of the critique. Leibniz is surely right to think that the metaphysical doctrines of Christoph Stegmann are in part designed to provide a philosophical basis for a theodicy. As Leibniz himself recognised on other occasions, the Socinians felt constrained to deny God's providence of future contin- gent events because they saw divine foreknowledge as a threat to human freedom, and hence to divine justice13. Stegmann's solution to this problem is to argue that God exists in time, and his whole conception of God tends to minimise the distance which separates the Creator from His creation. The crude anthropomorphism of the Socinian deity, inspired by a literalist reading of scripture, is indeed one of Leibniz's most insistent complaints throughout the critique. The materialist doctrine of the soul which Stegmann maintains is no less rooted in the demands of a theodicy. For the Socinians held that only the souls of the blessed are immortal; in place of the doctrine of hell they claim that the souls of those who do not achieve salvation are simply annihilated. It is clearly for this reason that Stegmann argues that the soul is not an immortal, immaterial substance, but is rather an accident of the body which naturally perishes with it. In the face of these doctrines Leibniz can of course reply with fairly plausible ad hominem arguments for insisting on the omniscience of God. (We shall see later that Leibniz also has ad hominem answers to the Socinian case for a materialist theory of mind.) To deprive God of one of His perfections, namely His omniscience, is to jeopardise His possession of the very attribute which the Socinians are most anxious to vindicate, namely His justice. In other words, if God is admitted to be less than a perfect being, then what reason is there for supposing that His imperfection is limited to His lack of omniscience and does not extend to other perfections? One of the difficulties of discussing the relationship of the two great principles is that neither is ever stated unambiguously by Leibniz. Many commentators have noticed that when Leibniz speaks of the principle of contradiction he seems to have a number of logical axioms in mind: in the present manuscript we find that the principle of bivalence and what we would call the law of non-contradiction both get brought under the heading of principles of an identical kind20. The principle of sufficient reason is even more radically ambiguous. Some of his statements suggest that Leibniz means by it the causal principle, though at other times he states that this vulgar axiom is merely a corollary of a deeper principle. There are even grounds for doubting whether Leibniz is strictly thinking of a proposition at all: he sometimes seems to envisage a rule of research - an interpretation which is encouraged by such phrases as the principium reddendae rationis. Margaret Wilson has plausibly suggested that the principle of contradiction allows of a similar analysis21. There is perhaps a hint of such an idea in the way in which Leibniz formulates both principles in the present manuscript. Despite these ambiguities, however, commentators tend to agree that in its most basic form the principle of sufficient reason is Leibniz's theory of truth, or at least a consequence of that theory. In the Ad Christophori Stegmanni Leibniz states the two great princi- ples of his philosophy in a highly unusual way. Hardly less striking is the account of the nature of metaphysics which succeeds this passage, for what Leibniz says here has a bearing on the controversial issue of whether there is a third fundamental principle in his philosophy: 'I rather think therefore that metaphysics is the science which treats of the causes of things using the principle that nothing happens without reason, and that thus the reason of existence is to be taken from the superior force ('praevalentia') of essences, whose reality is founded in some primitive substance existing per se. In comparison with his more popular accounts, the explanation which Leibniz gives here of why anything exists is an extremely economical one. The most remarkable feature of the passage perhaps is the use of the word 'praevalentia', for it brings to mind the postulate of Archimedes which Leibniz often invokes to illustrate the principle of sufficient reason. Leibniz claims that Archimedes applied the principle of sufficient reason to the case of the balance when he argued that unless there were a greater weight in one of the scales the whole would be at rest27. The term 'praevalentia' suggests, I think, that for Leibniz the existence of the world is exactly parallel to the case of the balance. The world exists for no other reason than the superior 'weight' of the essences which constitute it sub ratione possibilitatis: unless there were a collection of essences whose 'weight' was superior to all other collections of compossib- les, no world would exist. The account of existence which Leibniz gives appears to exclude any orthodox interpretation of providence. According to Leibniz, existence is the result of the superior force of essences, and thus God becomes a passive spectator of a process which does not involve His choice: God's only active role is to found the reality of the possibles. On such a view there is no place for the ideas of care and direction customarily associated with the theological notion of providence, and a fortiori there is no room for divine justice. (This has at best a precarious foothold even in Leibniz's more popular philosophy.) To say that Leibniz's metaphysics excludes providence, however, is not necessarily to say that it excludes omniscience, and here the situation seems more complex. Even the God of his esoteric writings may be said to conceive the essences, for essences are mind-dependent entities. In this sense God knows everything about the possibles, even if He cannot strictly be said to will them into existence. Leibniz's theory of God's eternity has different roots; it is closely tied in with the idea that the universe is dependent on God for the reality it has. An insistence on the perpetual dependence of the creature on the Creator runs through much of the present manuscript. For Leibniz, God is not affected by space and time, because space and time presuppose the existence of material bodies, and the phenomenal world is itself dependent on God. In one brief paragraph Leibniz sketches his relational theory of space and time which a few years later he defended at length in the Clarke correspondence : 'The author thinks that space is an accident. Certainly being in a given space is an accident, but it cannot well be said that space itself is an accident. Time and space are certain universal orders of existing things according to which one thing is either prior or subsequent, or nearer to or remoter from another. Thus they are not substances or accidents, but something ideal, founded however in the reality of things' 36. In a passage like this we can already sense the hostility which Leibniz would show to the Newtonian theory that space is, as it were, God's sensorium, for this seemed to make God causally dependent on space for His perception of the world. Such a connexion is not surprising; in the Clarke correspondence Leibniz explicitly compares the Newtonian deity to the God of the Socinians, to the disadvantage of the latter37. The Socinian argument that God exists in space and time brings us to another element in Leibniz's critique. We have seen how Leibniz thinks that to subject God to the flux of time is to undermine divine providence. To hold that God exists in space connects up with a quite different idea, namely, a materialist conception of the deity. Despite the undoubted parallels with the Clarke correspondence, Stegmann is not arguing, as Clarke argues, that infinite space is a property of God. On the contrary, Leibniz is faced here by the more extreme thesis that God occupies a certain limited portion of space : God's spatial dimensions are finite. Stegmann is putting forward a general materialist theory, in which the materialist conception of God is only a special, though the most important case. Leibniz thinks that the materiahst thesis runs into difficulties over personal identity which are fatal to its pretensions as a viable theodicy. Stegmann's argument for materialism is very much in the Aris- totelian tradition ; it depends on the concepts of form and matter which Aristotle made familiar. However, Stegmann's use of these concepts is unlike Aristotle's, for whereas Aristotle's God is pure form, Stegmann claims that matter and form are the principles of every being whatever. On this basis he further inverts the Aristotelian doctrine that matter is the principle of individuation : he holds instead that form gives a distinct nature, matter a common nature to things. According to Aristotle, of course, Plato and Socrates are distinct individuals because, although they have a common form (manhood), the 'stun' of the former is differ- ent from the 'stuff' of the latter. Even on Aristotelian terms it is possible to make some sense of Stegmann's argument: when water turns to steam, we can say that the same matter, or 'stuff', remains though it has taken on a different form as a result of substantial change. From a Leibnizian standpoint the argument is even more interesting; for Leibniz identifies the essence of an individual thing with the totality of its attributes: he is not an essentialist in the sense of believing in, as it were, partial essences38. If the form of a thing is taken to be its essence, then any two substances will be distinguished for Leibniz by virtue of having different essences, or complete individual concepts. Thus there is a sense in which Leibniz must be expected to endorse the thesis that form gives a distinct nature to things, since it is not really true for him that Socrates and Plato share the same form. In fact Leibniz does not criticise this thesis, but he does feel constrained by his metaphysics to criticise the second part of Stegmann's argument - the claim that matter gives a common nature to being: ' . . . But this again had to be proved. For two things can have this in common, that they are both beings or things, although they have no common matter. Indeed at a later stage in this manuscript Leibniz himself furnishes the materials for this very objection: he tells us that 'beings of reason' ('Entia rationis') are only 'terms' and that 'relations, which are nothing other than respects, are not things but truths'41. Thus on the strength of Leibniz's own metaphysics, the examples of space and time are not well chosen, and do not establish that things may have no common matter, since space and time are at most things in a very weak sense. Either Leibniz is being inconsistent with his deeper philosophy, or he is simply deploying an ad hominem argument without reference to his own distinctive metaphysical views. Stegmann's argument for God's materiality depends on the idea that matter and form are components of being as such : he thus departs from the Aristotelian doctrine that God is pure form. His argument for denying that souls are immaterial substances is really an epiphenomenalist one : Leibniz recognises that it has a respectable ancestry in the history of philosophy: 'He says that the soul is an accident, in nearly the same way in which some characters in Plato's Phaedo said that the soul is harmony. But he nowhere proves this opinion of his'42. An epiphenomenalist view of this kind is probably closer to the Aristo- telian-Thomist tradition than is the Leibnizian-Cartesian view which makes the soul a spiritual substance distinct from the body. Aquinas for instance holds that sense-perception is causally dependent on having a body, though he claims that this is not the case with intellectual operations. Some such distinction is made by Stegmann, for he argues that although intellectual operations do not depend on the body as an instrument, they nonetheless depend on it as a 'subject of inhesion'43. The relevance of this discussion to the underlying issue of theodicy becomes clear in the references to personal identity. Stegmann wishes to maintain that personal immortality is possible even though, as an accident of the body, the soul stands and falls with it: when the body dies, the soul dies. However, Stegmann believes that such an admission is compatible with faith in the resurrection, and hence with the system of rewards in an afterlife which his theodicy requires46. A theodicy based on divine rewards and punishments requires an adjustment of a person's experiences after death to his actions in this life. Yet how is Leibniz's God equipped to play such a part? In his esoteric philosophy, God's role is limited to conceiving the possibles. In the present manuscript Leibniz expresses this idea by saying that God is a primitive substance existing per se who founds the reality of the essences; and it is only in terms of this theory that we can interpret Leibniz's talk of the perfections in things coming from God. Thus, given God's essentially passive role in Leibniz's deeper metaphysics, there is no place for any kind of supernatural adjustment of a soul's experiences after death to its deserts in this life. However, there are no principles internal to the monads themselves which would ensure that their histories in an after-life would accord with the demands of justice. The criteria governing the actualisation of the possibles are totally non-moral and non-evaluative; perfection, when Leibniz's equivocations are removed, is simply quantity of essence. As a vindication of divine justice Ad Christophori Stegmanni Metaphysicam Unitariorum must be accounted less than successful, but for an understanding of Leibniz's metaphysics it is a fascinating and important, if sometimes puzzling, document.
