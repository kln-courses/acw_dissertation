If a properly excremental poetic exists in nineteenth-century literature, it could arguably be located in naturalist writing. Les Soeurs Vatard provides a classic example with the infamous "pisse de chat" that garnered Huysmans his reputation for literary impropriety. Drawing on this excremental example, Zola published a statement in the Figaro in defense of Huysmans, countering the notion that coarse language and imagery were tantamount to inferior writing: "On vous a dit, par exemple, que le romancier Huysmans était un écrivain de la dernière grossièreté. ... In Zola's view, the scatological was commensurate with literary refinement, delicacy, and good style. Although examples of excremental symbolism in Zola's own novels abound, suffice it to mention only one: the unforgettable coalmine of Germinal, whose beast-like, crouching position or accroupissement, as Jacques Noiray has aptly noted, implies defecation.7 Developing his theory of an anthropomorphic machine monster of industry in Le romancier et la machine, Noiray demonstrates how Zola's machines participate in a tripartite process of engloutissement, digestion, and évacuation, vocabulary Noiray takes directly from Zola (364). Stylistic or symbolic uses of scatology in such salient examples of naturalist writing allow us to view Flaubert's Crépitus as both part of and distinct from a broader tradition in nineteenth-century narrative. What Zola repeatedly indicated in Mes Haines as the modern writer's responsibility, namely "connaître le cadavre du cœur humain," forms part of Flaubert's theory of literature as well (83). In the letter to Louise Colet already quoted above, Flaubert queries: "Qui sait à quels sucs d'excréments nous devons le parfum des roses et la saveur des melons? Judging by the Plans et Scénarios, this finalized version of the Crépitus speech appears to have materialized out of nothing. When measured against Zola's breakdown of elements characterizing the naturalist novel, does the Tentation qualify as a naturalist work? First, stipulates Zola, the naturalist novel strives for "la reproduction exacte de la vie, l'absence de tout élément romanesque" (Les romanciers naturalistes 126). Zola might just as well have uttered alongside Flaubert, "et surtout ne pas oublier les latrines". Crépitus surely qualifies as anti- romanesque as he reproduces precisely those blatant life functions that romanticism would have us forget. Second, writes Zola, "le romancier tue le héros" (127). Whereas the romantic hero is bigger than life to the point of becoming illogical and absurd, the protagonist of naturalism is logical, proportionate to other things, a small player in a vast life fresco, whom readers tend to forget. If the scatological farce in the Tentation appears enormous to the few critics who have noted it, Crépitus has nonetheless fallen between the cracks of critical oubli. The third and final feature in Zola's portrait of naturalism is the author's perfect self-effacement behind the novel's action: "L'auteur n'est pas un moraliste, mais un anatomiste qui se contente de dire ce qu'il trouve dans le cadavre humain" (129). Not only does Crépitus describe himself as a subject of anatomical scrutiny - the bloating he causes, followed by both a resonating noise and the diner's overall sense of satisfaction; but also, his self-promotion stands independently of Flaubert, as evinced by the reflexive verb in the textual note, "Crépitus se fait entendre." Anti-romanesque, anti-heroic, and dramatically autonomous, one could argue Crépitus fulfills the three basic functions of the naturalist character. Zola intuitively grasped Flaubert's unique variety of naturalism when he wrote: "II y a un philosophe dans Flaubert. C'est le négateur le plus large que nous ayons eu dans notre littérature. Il professe le véritable nihilisme, - un mot en isme qui l'aurait mis hors de lui, - il n'a pas écrit une page où il n'ait creusé notre néant" (Les romanciers naturalistes 194). Indeed* as we have seen, Crépitus embodies a paradox. On the one hand, he participates in what Michel Foucault called the "bibliothèque fantastique," a comparative repertory and encyclopedic catalogue of human mythologies. In this sense, he forms part of Antoine's totalizing and concrete inventory of knowledge. On the other hand, just like the other deities appearing in the saint's visions, who take shape in a never-ending accumulation and multiplication of figures that fade out and disintegrate just as quickly as they appeared, Crépitus' materiality becomes increasingly dubious. He reiterates the "néant" that Zola detected in Flaubert's writing. Pierre Macherey has shown how deeply naturalism is rooted, not in defining or "fixing the contours" of the "real," but in making the invisible, visible, and in disclosing the impossibility of properly seizing reality through the organs of human perception - eye, mind, and text. L'objectif d'une telle démarche, c'était, pour le dire en un mot, de substituer à une littérature de la présence une littérature de l'absence et du vide: feignant de cerner les choses et les êtres dans les contours qui en fixent apparemment l'identité, elle visait en fait à révéler, en la désintégrant, la facticité de leur contenu. (162) Basing his analysis partially on the Schopenhauerian critique of representation, Macherey calls La Tentation a Bildungsroman, the story of a naïve worldview that assumes reality is what one sees and then becomes aware of itself as naïve (159-60). The crepitating of Flaubert's scatological god illustrates this point aptly. Crépitus, a legible material form, enters on stage, resonates briefly in a speech act and in a body performance, and then fades into traceless, immaterial silence. His momentary presence dissolves into absence; the borders of his fictionally represented identity blur and we discover, alongside Flaubert's saint, that he was never really there in the first place. If Flaubert's Tentation is an example of "virtual theater," as Evlyn Gould has argued, "a play that cannot be staged," it is precisely because of the illusory nature of its protagonists (7). Their invisibility becomes only fleetingly visible between the interstices of black and white prose - first, to saint Antoine, who imagines them as he turns the pages of "un gros livre;" to us, finally, as we succumb to a parallel illusion while turning the pages of La Tentation de saint Antoine. n If, as Zola suggested, Flaubert "n'a pas écrit une page où il n'ait creusé notre néant," was it not to comment on the urgent necessity of the literary act, in spite of its limited scope as a tool of representation? Flaubert recognized the artist for his ability to transfigure matter through prose, to make of excrement an esthetic, a poetic. "L'artiste est comme une pompe," he wrote to Louise Colet on June 25, 1853. "Il a en lui un grand tuyau qui descend aux entrailles des choses, dans les couches profondes. Il aspire et fait jaillir au soleil en gerbes géantes ce qui était plat sous terre et qu'on ne voyait pas" (qtd. in Macherey 169). To reread Crépitus, in the end, is to revive and transform him. Like Bouvard and Pécuchet, who see the profoundest value in manure and compost, to draw on the scatological metaphor is to recognize its literary and philosophical potential.
