Part I* IN comparison with that of his more celebrated brother, marchese Vincenzo, the art collecting of Cardinal Benedetto Giustiniani and the choices that informed it have been neglected in the art-historical literature. The aims of the pre- sent study are to establish which of the works of art in the vast collection that came together after Benedetto's death had in fact belonged to him, to distinguish between the two broth- ers' preferences, and in so doing to make an attempt at gaug- ing differences between religious patronage and secular taste in this period. As will become apparent, Vincenzo was per- haps closer to us in sensibility, while his brother embodied an all-encompassing religious viewpoint which is very far from modern concerns. Nonetheless, the two men co-existed sym- biotically and, with hindsight, we can see them as represent- ing two sides of a particular moment in Seicento culture, one made exceptional by the presence of Caravaggio. Luigi Salerno laid the foundations for this study with his important articles of 1960 in this Magazine, based on the 1638 inventories of the heirs of Vincenzo Giustiniani. Other publications since then have enriched the picture.' My own research has uncovered two earlier inventories relating specifically to Benedetto's collection, together with his will and other documents, which make it possible to distinguish with some certainty between the two brothers' holdings. An important new factor is the greater ease of access to collec- tions in the former East Germany resulting from German re- unification, which has made it possible for me to trace further paintings (in addition to those identified by Salerno) which passed to the King of Prussia after the sale of the Giustiniani collection in Paris in 1812,2 and had been thought lost. Par- ticularly useful for this purpose has been the list drawn up in 1829 of former Giustiniani pictures deemed unsuitable for museum purposes in Berlin and put at the disposition of the royal palaces; one item on this list was Caravaggio's Increduli- ty ofSt Thomas which was relegated to Charlottenburg instead of the Kaiser Friedrich Museum.3 But before entering into such details a brief sketch of Benedetto's career needs to be outlined. At the turn of the Seicento, that crucial period for Caravaggio and the whole Roman artistic scene, he was thus a man in his active maturity. Benedetto had studied in Perugia and Padua, and had graduated in law at Genoa in 1577. He was elected cardinal by Sixtus V in 1586.' Amayden's account outlines the close alliances in the Roman curia determining the vari- ous phases of his influence, which extended beyond the pure- ly ecclesiastical sphere. Known for his impetuous character, Benedetto had ties of friendship to the Cardinals del Monte, Mattei and Montalto. Benedetto's moment of greatest power was during the papacy of Clement VIII (1592-1605), in whose election he had, together with Cardinals Mattei and Montalto, played the r81e of 'grande elettore'." Giustiniani supported the pope's pro-Medicean and pro-French policies as well as his huge campaign of empowering the pauperist and preaching reli- gious orders. Amayden, who belonged to the pro-Spanish party, was a great admirer of marchese Vincenzo and, read- ing between the lines of his biography, one senses some antipathy towards Benedetto, whose alignment with the party included favouring the absolution of the converted Huguenot, Henry of Navarre, and with it his ascent to the French throne and marriage to Maria de' Medici. This deli- cate operation of international politics can be documented in Benedetto's unpublished correspondence, from which around six hundred letters survive, among them missives from the main figures in the public life of the early Seicento, including Henry IV,' his widow Maria and their son Louis XIII. Not all the paintings in the three lists have artists' names but, by collating them with one another and with the 1638 inventory published by Salerno,'" it has been possible to dis- tinguish the authorship of many of them and to reconstruct Benedetto's collection, as well as making a few corrections and additions to Salerno's identifications. Benedetto Giustiniani's 'Entrata della Guardarobba' The 1600 Guardarobba list (Appendix, Document I), though the least full, is nevertheless the most interesting of the new documents because of its early date, which coincides with a crucial moment for Roman culture, marked by the Carracci's encounter with classical and renaissance art and by Caravag- gio's switch from secular to sacred subjects, and from a light to a chiaroscuro manner. The term 'Guardaroba', which became almost synony- mous with collecting itself,'" originally referred to the storage area which contained silver, furnishings and linen, as well as precious objects in ivory or metals. It then came to encom- pass paintings and sculpture in store which were not access- ible to everyday visitors. The pages transcribed here from Benedetto's Guardarobba, headed 'Crucejissi, quadri spirituali e profani', are, of course, only a small part of the whole docu- ment, which is 186 pages long. Unlike the other documents under discussion it is not strictly speaking an inventory: it is ordered not topographically but chronologically, and 1600 is only its starting date. It first records the status quo in that year, and thereafter purchases of items such as silver, porcelain, tapestries and furniture (including a great quantity of'lettiere' for the vast household and its guests) are entered as they occur. In the Appendix below, Document I, only the 108 works of art are transcribed. Here as in other inventories of the period we find that custom of protecting the most pre- cious paintings with 'bandinelle' of various colours or with cur- tains which could be open and shut with tasselled cords. Sandrart records this of Caravaggio's Amor vincit omnia, but it was of course a frequent practice." Among the four hands who recorded the entries, only two are identifiable. Items 1-83 are due to the guardarobbiere Silvio Silva (in office from Ist April 1600 to 12th September 1601), who presumably started with what he found in the cardinal's possession when he took up service, including items from the estate of Benedetto's father Giuseppe (d. 1600). Nos.88-101 were entered by Alfonso Amarotti, Silva's immediate succes- sor, who seems to record new pieces as they come in, which makes the record even more interesting. Benedetto must have taken the book with him to Bologna in 1606-11, as an entry of 26thJuly 1610 in the silver section makes clear; this and the surrounding items (nos. 102-08) are not in Amarotti's hand." Among them (no. 105) is Lanfranco's 'S. At no.81, among Silva's list and therefore before September 1601, we find 'Un quadro grande in tela d'una Madalena nel deserto nuda e scapigliata nel deserto con un Cristo in mano con cornice negre', which reappears in the 1621 inventory (no.41), hanging in the Galleria Grande. We can now establish that this lost work was commissioned by Benedetto before September 1601, and that the penitent Magdalen was represented nude, holding a crucifix. In an entry by Amarotti, and therefore after 12th Septem- ber 1601, we find at no.98 the brief but evocative entry: 'Un quadro del Cardinale Giustiniani naturale a sedere di mano del Carava- gio'. The existence of this lost portrait was known, but this new reference allows us to give it a very significant date in Caravaggio's career as a portraitist, and the term 'naturale' is of some interest, although it may merely indicate that it was life size."2 The painting remained in Benedetto's ownership and is described as being in the Guardarobba, without a frame, in 1621 (no.122). In 1638 we find it transferred to a place of honour in the 'stanza grande de quadri antichi' as 'Un quadro di mezzafigura e pii, col ritratto della bona memoria del Signor Cardinale Benedetto Giustiniani, dipinto in tela d'Imperatore di mano di Michelangelo da Caravagio', in company with twelve other works by Caravaggio.27 This seated, three-quarter-length figure must have been quite close to the portrait in a private collec- tion in Florence, whose sitter Roberto Longhi identified as Maffeo Barberini.2" The Florentine painting too, is dateable to around this time, and the precise description of Benedet- to's lost portrait provides further evidence of a tendency in Caravaggio's portraiture around this time to cut the figure just below the knee. In order to assess the contents of the Guardarobba list as a whole, it is useful to isolate certain groups or themes. We find first of all a good nucleus of sixteenth-century works, all of sacred subjects, which demonstrate a refined and cultivated taste: works given to Raphael (no.10), Garofalo (no.12), Bra- mantino (no.24), Peruzzi (no.32), Lotto (no.42), Salviati (no.47), Bronzino (no.78), Veronese (no.80) and Giorgione (no.83). Another group is formed by the portraits, which are useful for reconstructing Benedetto's curial loyalties and aspi- rations. Evidence from the backs of the paintings One further observation may be made, the outcome of a fruitful discussion with Erich Schleier. The Giustiniani paint- ings now in German museums constitute a particularly inter- esting group, since many of them have never been relined and retain their original stretchers. In many cases painted numbers are visible on the back, sometimes corresponding to those of the Delaroche and Landon catalogues or of the Kaiser Friedrich Museum. There is also often a painted cipher (Fig. 79), which is hard to read, but could be a G and a B for Benedetto Giustiniani Bandini who was responsible for the Paris sale. Furthermore, there are often seals on the stretchers. Among those clarifying the Italian provenance, we can find three different types. The first has a rampant lion carrying a scimitar (Fig.80), a device which we often find on decrees published by Cardinal Benedetto during his Bolo- gnese legation; after his nomination as cardinal on 16th November 1586, Benedetto had added to his own coat of arms (a castle with three towers surmounted by an eagle) a rampant lion, in homage to Sixtus V who had elevated him to the purple. Under the rampant lion is a cipher which is hard to read, but could possibly be a G and aJ, standing for the Italian and Latin initial letters of the family name. This particular seal was used only by Cardinal Benedetto and is found on paintings which belonged specifically to him. On the back of a few paintings we find a GG seal, perhaps of Benedetto's father Giuseppe (for example on the Titian/ Palma il Vecchio Holy Family (1621, 216); much more com- mon is the seal VG, which obviously stands for Benedetto's heir, Vincenzo. Neither of these two latter seals has a crown or a coat of arms.66 In Part II of this article I will discuss the particular question of the Albani cycle and the arrangement of the paintings in the Palazzo Giustiniani, and will draw some conclusions about the differences between the collecting of Benedetto and Vincenzo Giustiniani.
