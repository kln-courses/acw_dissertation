<plain_text><page sequence="1">REPRESENTATION OF GOD IN JEWISH ART CECIL ROTH JERUSALEM The discovery of Jewish art — that is, of 'figurative' art — has been a long and painful process. It was not so long since it was stated categorically that the Jews down to modern times had no conception of 'art' in the accepted sense, and that 'Jewish art' was a contradic tion in terms, the responsibility for this state of affairs being normally ascribed to the categorical prohibitions contained in the Ten Commandments and elsewhere in the Bible. But logical consideration, and the testimony both of literary texts and of surviving evidences, made it clear before long that this sweeping generalization was ludicrous. The Jews worshipped God 'in the beauty of Holiness' ; they considered the 'beautification of the Mitsvah' to be a commendable deed. They interpreted a well-known verse in the Song of Moses as implying that God should be 'adorned' with loveliness in ritual appurtenances. And there were vast numbers of specimens of synagogal and private religious objects of every type which were produced in as lovely a form as could be achieved. Indeed, a whole series of biblical manuscripts existed which comprised superb decorative illuminations. In consequence, the traditional generalization had to be revised and qualified. Yes, the Jews indulged of course in art ; but in decorative art only, and they would on no account embody in this any representation of 'any living thing, male or female, in the heavens above or in the earth beneath or in the waters that are beneath the earth', in accordance with the Deuteronomic prohibition. However, in due course, representations of animals were verified: for example in the figured masora at the foot of German biblical manuscripts, as the Danish hebraist Tychsen demonstrated in 1778, to general amazement. As the result of this and other finds, the traditional generalization had to be modified even further. And indeed it was a matter of common observation that lions, eagles, and other symbolic beasts and animals figured sometimes even in the curtains that hung before the Ark and in other objects intimately connected with religious usage. However, Jews would surely never permit themselves to delineate the human form and face, it was now maintained; and it has been our personal experience to find persons who have denied the possibility of the Jewish provenance of some document or object simply because it bore the human likeness. This attitude, however, became impossible to maintain after the publication some seventy years ago of the original work on the Sarajevo Haggadah, with David Kaufmann's learned and it may be said almost epoch-making additional study, which made it clear that there were Hebrew illuminated manuscripts galore of the Middle Ages which showed scenes from life with as little inhibition as the contemporary Christian codices. Further vistas have, of course, been opened out in this study by the discoveries of the synagogue frescoes at Dura Europos, which have made it appear at least possible that fully illuminated codices of the Hebrew Bible were known in the period of classical antiquity, and that there may even have been a con tinuity of tradition into the Middle Ages. Now, our art historians were driven back into the last ditch. Yes, the Jews could not refrain from artistic expression of their religious enthusiasms; yes, they admitted even the likeness of animals to their art; yes, at least in certain areas and periods they even had no inhibitions regarding the delineation of human beings. They interpreted the biblical prohibitions in consequenoe quite liberally. But there remained one area of reservation, and herein lay the point in which there was no possibility of rapprochement between Jewish and Christian religious art: the Jew would not in any case tolerate — and quite properly not — the delineation and representation of the Deity. This was in the fullest sense of the term 'un-Jewishי: herein there could be no chance of com promise. True, in the Sarajevo Haggadah the Sabbath was represented by the figure of a man resting, and the original editors were inclined to think that it was intended to represent the Deity. But this could not be, for no Jews would be guilty of this. The figure must therefore represent Adam, or man in general, enjoying the Sabbatical repose. In the Ouderkerk cemetery in Amsterdam, the tomb of Samuel Senior Texeira is adorned by a bas-relief (and how this could have been tolerated in this orthodox community is an insoluble mystery) showing a male figure with a halo, appearing to Samuel (Fig. 38). But, pace the explicit words of the Bible, this must be intended to represent an angel, and not the Almighty, for (one said) no Jew would in any circumstances delineate the Almighty. But this presented no difficulty, for the hand of God is after all not God himself, and biblical an thropomorphism (not indeed as it happens at this specific point) justifies pictorial anthropomorphism. Some years ago, I called attention to the figure of the Almighty discreetly merging into or emerging from the clouds in the vignette depicting the vision of Ezekiel which is a detail of the thrice-repeated engraved title page of the Minhat Shai, published in the learned and God-fearing community of Mantua in 1742 (Fig. 39). This is most surprising, and not easily explicable, but the vignette is small, and the detail a little vague, and could conceivably have been overlooked, though it seems most extraordinary that it should have been. The same applies to the whole series of even tinier vignettes in the engraved border to a ketuba produced in Italy — again in all probability in Mantua — in the mid-seventeenth century, showing the story of Adam and Eve expelled from the Garden of Eden by the Almighty — who similarly appears in other of these little Creation scenes. Here again, with enormous difficulty, the presence of the detail so contrary to Jewish feelings and prescriptions and preconceptions can be explained away or mitigated or overlooked (Fig. 40). This does not apply, however, to a further représenta tion that has been brought to my notice through the kindness and generosity of Mr. M. Kaufman of Far Rockaway. In 1698, there was published at Frankfort on the Oder, a famous stronghold of Rabbinical orthodoxy, a highly Rabbinical work by Benjamin Zeeb Wolf Romaner, dealing with the aggadoth of the Talmud. The engraved title page of this volume shows the vision of Jacob at Beth-El, and at the head of the ladder, with the angels going up and down on it, we see the figure of the Deity, delineated in a fashion that admits of no equivocation and with a boldness that does not permit it to be overlooked. And this pious work was apparently admitted, apparently remained in circulation, and apparently aroused no violent protests, notwithstanding a feature which even some of the liberal among us (and I include myself) would not admit today (See Figs. 41, 42). With this, all previous conceptions crumble. The delineation of God was unquestionably admitted in 'Jewish art'. No a priori reason now remains for denying that the Deity appears in the Amsterdam tombstone referred to above and in the Flaggadah of Sarajevo. Jewish art, in fact, in the past knew no universal inhibitions. And the mystery remains, why it did in fact remain so restricted and why its progress was so halting. And another point remains to be discussed.
