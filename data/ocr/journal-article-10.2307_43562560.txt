<plain_text><page sequence="1">Gen'ichi Tsuge Bamboo, Silk, Dragon and Phoenix: Symbolism in the Musical Instruments of Asia The musical instrument is commonly understood as a contrivance which produces "musical" sound. As long as we confine ourselves to classical Western music of, perhaps, up to the nineteenth century, this definition may be generally accepted. However, once we look at other kinds of music in the world, we soon realize that the musical instrument is very often more than just a sound-producing tool. In fact, once we open our eyes to other aspects of the musical instrument, we are surprised by the diverse meanings attached to and associated with it, not to mention its physical diversity - its various "strange" decorations. A musical instrument may be possessed as a token of effective strength and spiritual hegemony (e.g. the Janissary band in the eighteenth century Europe; military bands in general). A musical instrument may constitute an important item of tribal or municipal wealth (examples abound in Asia and Africa, and, in fact, almost everywhere). Some instruments appear to have excessive 11</page><page sequence="3">design and ornaments which go far beyond musical function (e.g. a gilded harpsichord of bizarre form, supported by tritons and sea nymphs, at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, an overly decorated koto and shamisen of the Crosby Brown Collection at the same museum). Examples of this kind are inexhaustible. After all, the musical instrument symbolizes, by nature, something special of the culture which produced it. Modern technology can produce highly approximate substitutes for the old and conventional materials of the musical instrument, either the main body itself or its accessories. For example, plastic or laminated paper can be used in place of bamboo for various flutes such as the shakuhachi, ney, suling or bansuri, nylon or vinyl for the skin of the banjo and shamisen , and also for various kinds of drum heads; nylon in place of silk or gut strings. In fact, for "practical" purposes, those modern substitutes are in a way superior to the traditional materials. For instance, bamboo pipes are very sensitive to changes in humidity and temperature; silk and gut strings are also very elastic and easily broken under unfavourable climatic conditions. However, we often come across musicians who nevertheless adhere per- sistently to traditional materials. The argument that sound produced from substitute materials is acoustically unsatisfactory, seems not altogether per- suasive. Their attitude may be interpreted as a specific attachment to the material itself. For instance, koto and shamisen players of Japan still prefer to use silk strings in spite of persistent tuning problems during performance. Shakuhachi players, in particular, stubbornly insist that the flute must be made of bamboo despite frequent splitting. Even in the West, some fiddle players are still persistent in their use of gut strings. At the same time, we must recognize that musicians of certain cultures accept, without hesitation, new materials to substitute for traditional ones. For example, many banjo players from the United States today prefer plastic to traditional calfskin for their banjo heads. Also, skins of many Western drums, including the timpani, bass drum, snare drum and tom-tom, have been almost entirely replaced by plastic sheets, simply because plastic materials can hold practically the same level of consistency regardless of weather conditions. Today, having toured all over the world in varying climates, musicians may have found plastic more suitable. These two strikingly different attitudes should reflect the types of music and audience, or rather the culture which produced the respective instruments. Ancient Chinese classified their musical instruments into eight categories according to the primary material used for construction. This system is called pa yin ("the eight (sources) of sound"), and consists of the following 7 materials: metal, earth (or clay), skin, silk (threads), wood, gourd, and bamboo. This system is thought probably to be the oldest extant classification of musical instruments. This ancient system is actually too simple to be applied to later (and more elaborate) musical instruments, yet it reveals implicitly the profound symbolic relationship between material and instrument, and possibly the sound produced. First, let us inquire into the category of bamboo. Although a prototype of East Asian long zithers might possibly have been made of bamboo, various kinds of flutes are the primary instruments in the bamboo category (e.g. hisao "end-blown flute," p'ai-hsiao "panpipes," and ti-tzu "cross flute"). Bamboo is a unique plant from the botanical point of view, and forms an independent family by itself (though it is usually classified into the grass family). Its most striking characteristic is its immense vitality. Bamboo is said to overcome almost any kind of hardship. Its particular strength and flexibility is characteriz- ed by the fact that bamboo stalks will never break even under heavy snow. Bamboo is sometimes spoken of as a wise and patient man who knows how to bend before the winds of life. Peh Kü-yih (772-846), one of the most famous Chinese poets of the Tang dynasty, sung in praise of bamboo as follows: Bamboo's root is firm; With its firmness (a man of) virtue is established. Bamboo's character is straight; Straightforwardness enables one to rise to eminence. Bamboo's core is empty; Emptiness allows one to realize the way. Bamboo's node represents chastity; With a chaste mind one can set his aim in life. (from Yang Chu Chi) Thus, bamboo's attributes were considered as models in human ethics. A frank, open-hearted and straightforward man is often described as a neatly-split bamboo stalk. In Japan, bamboo is one of the three auspicious plants, the other two being the pine and the plum tree. These three plants as a set are indis- pensable for New Year decorations and are thought to bring happiness and good fortune. 14</page><page sequence="6">, The Japanese bamboo flute shakuhachi The gloss and luster of bamboo is sometimes associated with its high pro- pagation power. In fact, bamboo is generally considered to be a symbol of fecundity in the East, and closely connected with fertility rites. In a Japanese myth which describes the rite of alluring the disappeared sun-goddess out, Amenouzume, a young goddess, danced naked with bamboo leaves in her hands (Kojiki). The hollow cavity between the nodes of bamboo is a natural form which attracts and swallows a mind enchanted by mysteriousness. In an old Japanese legend, a bamboo-cutter discovers a shining princess (Kaguya- hime) inside a bamboo stalk, who, however, eventually returns to her heavenly home on the moon. Furthermore, a bamboo joint represents regularity in nature. A joint reminds us of scale, measure, links and regularity. Also the purity and 15</page><page sequence="7">cleanliness of bamboo together with its lightness make it an effective utensil, particularly in the tea ceremony of Japan. We may add to the above two more facts to remind us of the importance of bamboo: first, bamboo shoots are much esteemed as a food in the East and there are many remedies based on bamboo in Oriental medicine. Secondly, bamboo is frequently used for making furniture, tools, and, of course, various musical instruments (including resonators of Indonesian metallophones, and organ pipes). Even in the West, we should recall that gramophone needles were once made of bamboo for connoisseurs of SP records. Japanese shakuhachi players often call their instrument "bamboo" (take) rather than "flute." They concentrate their attention on how to please the heart of the bamboo, rather than on playing music as such. A similar attitude is found in a medieval Persian verse. Jalāl al-DIn Rūmi (1207-73), a mystic poet and the founder of the Mevlevi order of dervish, sang as follows: Hearken to this reed forlorn, Breathing, even since 'twas torn from its rushy bed, a strain of impressioned love and pain. (translated by R. A. Nicholson, quoted from Poet and Mystic , London, 1950, p. 31). Thus, in the East, the musician concentrates his attention on listening to the inner voice of bamboo (or reed) itself rather than on attempting to make artificial music on the flute. Let us turn to another category of pa yin. In ancient China, the word "silk and bamboo" (szü-chu) meant "music." To be more precise, it meant stringed and wind instruments which were made of silk and bamboo. In Chinese, szu ("string" or "thread") primarily means "silk". In fact, most Chinese stringed instruments (and East Asian stringed instruments of Chinese origin) use silk strings even today. To give but a few examples, ch'in (the seven-stringed long zither) and p7-p'a (the four-stringed short lute) of China; komun'go (the six-stringed long zither) and haegum (the two-stringed fiddle) of Korea; koto (the thirteen-stringed long zither) and shamisen (the three-stringed long lute) of Japan. Although recently wire and nylon have replaced silk strings of some instru- ments, we can still see a close connection between the string instruments of East Asia and silk. One may wonder why silk was preferred to gut, horse-tail, or wire for the strings of musical instruments. It is hardly necessary to mention the significance of silk in the ancient world, in both the East and West. The 16</page><page sequence="8">existence of the "silk road" will suffice to remind us of its immeasurable value at that time. In the ancient world, sounding the instrument was closely associated with sacrificial rites. Exciting strings stretched over an instrument (actually in many languages "beating" the strings) might possibly have been done in place of a sacrifice. The following lines from St. Augustine may be interpreted in this sense: On the timbrel leather is stretched, On the psaltery gut is stretched, On either instrument flesh is crucified. (cited from G. Reese, Music in the Middle Ages , New York, p. 64). Thus, we are tempted to speculate about the possible relationship between stringed instruments and rites, in particular, for the goddess of sericulture. An interesting connection between horse-cult and sericulture in ancient China was once suggested (R. van Gulik, Hayagriva, Leiden, 1935). A foreigner browsing in a Japanese Buddhist temple may soon become aware that some sound-producing tools there take strange forms; for example, a percussion board in the shape of fish (gyoban), a slit-drum in the shape of two arched fish (mokugyo), a temple-bell surmounted with a dragon head (bonshõ), etc. In fact, the dragon (and its fictive relatives such as reptiles, amphibia and fish) are the beasts which appear most frequently on Asian musical instruments, and are closely associated with music. The dragon is most commonly thought to be a kind of fabulous reptile with a ferocious head and four legs. It is gener- ally believed that the dragon of East Asia (lung in Chinese; ryū or tatsu in Japanese) is closely related to the Indian serpent-shaped nagā which is widely diffused throughout Southeast Asia. Javanese court gamelan instruments are often surmounted by a pair of beautifully carved nãgàs. As suggested by the technical terminology of its component parts, the Japa- nese koto (or sõ-no-koto) is historically viewed as a crouching dragon. The top surface is called a "dragon's back" (ryukö). The upper bridge is called a "dragon's horn (ryukaku) and the bottom is called a "dragon's belly" (ryufuku). The right end of the koto (from the player's point of view) is referred to as a "dragon's head" (rydtõ), and inside it there is a "dragon's tongue" (ryuzetsu). The left end is "dragon's tail" (rydbi). In addition the zither possesses "dragon's forelegs" (ryushu or maeashi) and "dragon's hind legs" (ryushi or atoashi). The soundholes underneath are sometimes called "dragon's mouths" (ryuku). Some names of other component parts also reveal symbolism of the dragon: the 17</page><page sequence="9">lower bridge is called the "cloud horn" (unkaku). And at the top of the dragon's head, there is a section known as a "sea" (umi) which is sometimes called the "dragon's forehead" (rydgaku). The long sides of the zither are called the "seashore" (¡so). The top of the left end is called "celestial seat" (Tenninza). However, the Japanese koto is by no means an isolated example of this type of zoomorphism. In fact, numerous musical instruments are traditionally viewed (or actually made) in the form of the dragon or related mythical creatures. The mi gyaun, the Burmese long zither, is an obvious example of this sort. It is usually made in the form of a crocodile, one of the earthly equivalents of the dragon. Appparently a similar long zither existed already in T'ang China. The name of "crocodile-head zither" (ťo-shou cheng) is recorded in Tang Shu. The viņā, a South Indian lute, is another instrument which is always surmounted by a mysterious "dragon" head. This creature, however, is identified as yāli which is a fabulous beast in Hindu mythology. The ch'in is also associated with the dragon. Among the technical names for component parts, we find "dragon's gum" (iung-yin), the lower bridge at the left end, and "dragon pond" (lung-ch'i), the larger soundhole on the bottom board. Interestingly enough, however, other technical terms suggest that this long zither of Chinese literati was also associated with the phoenix, another mystical creature. The ch'in possesses a "phoenix forehead" (fêng-ê), which is the narrow space beyond the bridge at the right end. Beneath it we find a "phoenix tongue" (fêng-shê), also called "phoenix eye" (fêng-yen). It also possesses "phoenix wings" (fêng-ch'ih) - the long sides of the zither, and a "scorched tail" (chiao-wei) - the left extremity of the ch'in. On the bottom board, there is a "phoenix pool" (fêng-chao), the smaller sound hole, which is the counterpart of the "dragon pond." We should recall here that various types of Chinese fiddles, represented by the hu-ch'in, are often decorated with a carved dragon head or, sometimes, with a phoenix or horse head. According to many myths and legends, the dragon is believed to possess di- vine power and to be an awesome creature. In ancient China, the appearance of a dragon was generally believed to be a lucky omen, though it may be a bad omen if it appears at the wrong time or in the wrong situation. The birth of great sages and emperors was said to be preceded by an appearance of the dragons and phoenixes. In India, the nāgā is thought to have knowledge of all secret languages and sounds. The dragon can raise clouds and cause thunder and rain. It is primarily associated with the spirit of waters. Thus, "from remote ages his images were used in times of drought in order to cause him to ascend by sympathetic magic" (M. de Visser, The Dragon in China and Japan, Amsterdam, 18</page><page sequence="10">1913, p. 113). In one of the war chronicles of medieval Japan, we read a story that, in offering prayers for rain, a secret tune was played on a biwa (short- necked lute), which caused a continuous and heavy rain to fall (genpei seisuiki). It is not uncommon practice to use some sound-producing instruments for such rites. Here we see a possible link between the musical instrument and the dragon image as a means to make rain magic more effective. Ancient people attempted to summon the dragon and evoke his song on the occasion of a rite. A twelfth-century Japanese source tells an interesting legend concerning this connection: "The dragon song came from the sea. To hear this voice again bamboo was cut and blown: in olden times five holes were cut, in later times seven. This is the reason why one calls the transverse flute 'the cry of the dragon.' Rydmei. Others say also Rydgin, 'dragon's song' ..." (M. Oga , Rydmeishõ. Quoted from E. Harich-Schneider, A History of Japanese Music. London, 1973, p. 258). We have so far enumerated examples which suggest a close symbolic relationship between musical instruments and the ethos of natural materials and mythical creatures. Why should so many musical instruments possess such peculiar symbolism? In conclusion, let us attempt to explicate this theme briefly. It is not easy to speculate about any symbolism and give a clear-cut explana- tion of its meaning, because a symbol always has multiple meanings. However, from the above examples, we can suggest one very important aspect of the musical instrument which is often overlooked in our technological society. The musical instrument bestows power upon its player for his magical (or musical) flight. Therefore, the musical instrument must be made from the right material and must have the right shape, touch and decoration. The bondage of the musician to his instrument is so significant that even the slightest alteration of a vital part will considerably affect its player, his spirit, and eventually the sound produced. No matter how rapidly and radically our technology changes, certain original aspects of the musical instrument and the musician's rapport to the instrument will be unchangeable. Whether it is consciously believed or not, the musical instrument bears some divine power which enables its players (mu- sician, magician or shaman) to transcend this world and to communicate with a spiritual world. Gen'ichi Tsuge Bambou, soie, dragon et phénix: le symbolisme des instruments de musique en Asie (résumé) On considère généralement l'instrument de musique comme un objet produisant un «son» musical. On peut accepter, en gros, cette définition si l'on se limite à la mu- sique classique occidentale, disons d'avant le 19ème siècle. Cependant, dès lors que l'on envisage d'autres formes de musique dans le monde, on comprend très vite que l'instrument de musique est bien souvent plus qu'un simple appareil à produire des sons. En fait, si l'on ouvre les yeux à d'autres aspects de l'instrument de mu- sique, on est étonné par les diverses significations qui s'y rapportent et qui y sont associées, sans parler de la diversité physique des instruments, avec, aussi, leurs décorations «curieuses». Dans une certaine mesure, l'instrument de musique peut révéler une existence respectée et un raffinement intellectuel. Le fait de le posséder peut être un signe de force effective et d'hégémonie spirituelle. Il peut aussi représenter un élément im- portant du patrimoine d'une tribu ou d'une commune. Il peut certainement être aussi 21</page><page sequence="13">considéré comme une œuvre d'art en soi. Presque partout, il est fabriqué avec le plus grand soin, et avec un raffinement extrême: les beaux instruments que possè- dent les musées des «beaux-arts» en sont la preuve. Après tout, l'instrument de mu- sique doit être un symbole d'un trait parti- culier de la culture qui l'a produit. Si, grâce à la technologie moderne, on peut utiliser pour les instruments de mu- sique des matériaux qui se rapprochent beaucoup des matériaux traditionnels, et qui sont parfois même mieux, on rencontre encore fréquemment des interprètes qui ne veulent pas abandonner ces derniers. C'est ainsi que les joueurs de koto et de shami - sen japonais continuent à préférer les cordes de soie, malgré les problèmes d'ac- cord des instruments qui ne cessent de se présenter durant les concerts. Les joueurs de shakuhachi utilisent encore des flûtes en bambou, bien que ce matériau se fende souvent. L'argument avancé, à savoir que le son produit avec d'autres matériaux n'est pas satisfaisant, ne semble pas tout à fait convaincant. Parfois, on peut inter- préter cette attitude des exécutants comme un attachement particulier au matériau lui- même. Mais alors, pourquoi ces musiciens sont-ils attachés à ces matériaux? Je suis enclin à soupçonner que l'une des raisons majeures de cette attitude pourrait être le symbolisme des matériaux naturels, sym- bolisme profondément ancré dans l'esprit des musiciens, consciemment ou incons- ciemment. Pa yin, le système utilisé dans la Chine ancienne pour la classification des instru- ments de musique, part de sept matières naturelles à partir desquelles étaient fa- çonnés les instruments de musique: le métal, la terre, la peau, la soie, le bois, la courge et le bambou. Bien que ce système soit trop simple pour pouvoir s'appliquer aux instruments plus récents, il révèle im- plicitement le rapport symbolique profond entre le matériau et l'instrument, et, peut- être, le son produit et apprécié par les anciens. Prenons comme exemple le bambou: dans ce cas, on peut faire ressortir une attitude du musicien, qui concentre son attention à écouter la voix intérieure du bambou lui-même plutôt qu'à essayer de produire artificiellement une musique avec le tube de bambou. Comme l'a dit un jour un interprète japonais de shakuhachi, la fin ultime de la musique de la flûte peut être de plaire au cœur du bambou. Cette atti- tude est étroitement en rapport avec l'éthos du bambou. En Asie orientale, le bambou a une grande importance comme aliment, comme remède et comme matériau dont on fabrique des meubles, des outils et des ustensiles divers. Il est également con- sidéré comme plante qui porte bonheur et comme symbole de la fertilité. On voit dans ses attributs des modèles pour l'éthique humaine. De ce fait, il n'est pas étonnant qu'il soit indispensable dans les divers rites et cérémonies, et qu'il serve à la fabrica- tion d'un certain nombre d'instruments de musique. Comme autre exemple, nous mention- nerons le dragon, motif zoomorphique qui orne souvent le corps de l'instrument de musique, ou que l'on rencontre souvent dans la nomenclature. D'après de nom- breux mythes et légendes, on croit que le dragon possède un pouvoir divin et qu'il est une créature terrifiante. Son apparition est généralement considérée comme un bon augure. Il peut soulever les nuages et provoquer la pluie et l'orage. On utilisait donc l'image du dragon comme moyen de rendre plus efficace le rite magique qui demande la pluie. D'après un récit japonais du moyen-âge, les hommes ont fabriqué une flûte de bambou afin d'écouter la voie du dragon. Dans des temps très reculés, le son de l'instrument était étroitement asso- cié aux rites de sacrifice. Il se peut que la 22</page><page sequence="14">vibration (ou le «battement») des cordes tendues sur une caisse de résonance (sur laquelle était souvent dessinée une créa- ture symbolique) ait accompagné (ou rem- placé) un sacrifice. Pour être un outil efficace pour l'inter- prète (musicien, magicien ou chaman), l'ins- trument doit être façonné à partir du ma- tériau approprié et symboliser un esprit «secourable». On croit, consciemment ou inconsciemment, que l'instrument de mu- sique est doté d'un pouvoir divin qui per- met à celui qui en joue de transcender ce monde et d'entrer en communication avec un univers spirituel.
