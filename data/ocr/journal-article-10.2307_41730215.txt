"Dreams of the Old Life on the Plains" (through Janu- ary 1986), illustrates the impact of the horse, firearms and the reservation sys- tem on the lives of the Plains Indians in the 19th century. The exhibit consists of 48 artifacts from ca. 1850-1900 and his- toric photographs of the Ghost Dance. American Museum of Natural History, New York, NY: "Maya: Treasures of an Ancient Civilization" (through July 28, 1985), features 275 Maya art objects in a variety of media including sculpture, ce- ramics and metalworks, ranging in age from 2000 B.C. to the 16th century. Some artifacts are made of gold, jade, wood, and shell. The Mayan achievements in astron- omy, calendrics, mathematics, hieroglyphic writing, and architecture are examined. The serious problem of looting is addressed in a section called "Looting and Illegal Collecting." Circulated by the Albuquer- que Museum, NM. (See ARCHAEOLOGY, March/ April 1985). "The Art of Cameroon" (July 15-October 15, 1985), reflects the historic and cultural context of one of the major West African art traditions- that of the Cameroon Grassland kings and their secret societies. More than 150 objects include ceremonial masks and thrones, carved beaded human figures and other objects of royal wealth and social status. Catalogue available. Circulated by SITES. The Asia Society, New York, NY: "Japa- nese Ghosts and Demons: Arts of the Su- pernatural" (through September 8, 1985), presents approximately 100 prints, scrolls, paintings, screens, books, netsuke and sculpture from the Edo and Meiji periods that depict subjects of the supernatural. The exhibit is arranged^hematically and includes Taoist Immortals, Shõki the De- mon Queller and the male ghosts of the kabuki. Catalogue available. Cooper-Hewitt Museum, New York, NY: "Timeless Sources: Rare Books in the Cooper-Hewitt Museum" (through Octo- ber 6, 1985), highlights the importance of illustrated books as tools in the design process. Dating from the 16th through the 20th centuries, the group includes techni- cal manuals and encyclopedias, catalogues of famous collections, and records of ar- chitectural and interior design. One of the earliest books is a study of perspective published in Venice in 1596. Catalogue available. "Wine: Celebration and Cere- mony" (through October 13, 1985), docu- ments the impact of wine on the history of design in cultures throughout the world, over a span of thousands of years. The Jewish Museum, New York, NY : "The Jewish Patrons of Venice" (through Sep- tember 29, 1985), is the second stage of the exhibition which explores the role of Jewish patronage in Venice from the 18th to the early 19th century. Ceremonial ob- jects, textiles, books, illuminated manu- scripts, prints, drawings, and photomurals of architectural monuments and tomb- stones are featured. The Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York, NY: "The Art of Precolumbian Gold: The Jan Mitchell Collection" (through Au- gust 11, 1985), is a selection of nearly 100 gold objects dating from the last centu- ries before Christ to A.D. 16th century, chiefly collected from Costa Rica, Panama, Columbia and Peru. Included are pen- dants, diadems, ear and nose ornaments, as well as funerary masks and cups. "The Bright Side of Battle" (through Septem- ber 1, 1985), chronicles the ceremonial and symbolic use of weaponry from the begin- ning of Islam, featuring examples of jew- eled swords and belts and richly decorated armor. "Man and the Horse" (through Sep- tember 1, 1985), celebrates the splendor of equestrian costumes with 150 examples of riding habits and accessories, coaches and carriages, and a large number of artworks representing horse and rider. "Taino Art of the Dominican Republic" (through October 13, 1985), exhibits some 90 objects of art of the Precolumbian Taino people of the Dominican Republic, and in- cludes major works made of stone, wood, ceramic, bone, shell, and textiles. Organ- ized by the University Gallery of the Uni- versity of Florida. Catalogue available. Museum of Our National Heritage, Lexington, MA: "Off the Walls: Historic Wallpapers in New England" (through October 13, 1985), presents over 150 examples of wallpaper, exploring the changing tastes from 1720 to 1920. The collection is presented in rooms with period furnishings. National Gallery of Art, Washington, DC: "Ancient Art of the American Woodland Indians" (through August 4, 1985), focuses on 120 stone, shell and copper artifacts of Native American art executed between the Late Archaic and Mississippian periods (3000 B.C. -A.D. 1600) in the woodland areas of the southeast and midwest of North America. "The Sculpture of India: 3000 B.C.-1300 A.D." (through September 2, 1985), presents a major survey of India's diverse sculptural heritage with 125 works of stone, ivory and bronze ranging in size from the miniature to the colossal. This exhibition inaugurates the "Festival of India" in the U.S., a series of artistic events in 1985 and 1986 which will illuminate the history and culture of India. Peabody Museum of Natural History, Yale University, New Haven, CT: "The Past Rediscovered: Everyday Life in Ancient Egypt" (through September 30, 1985), presents objects from the Museum's ex- tensive Egyptian collection which are not normally on view. The exhibit includes art and artifacts from virtually every Egyp- tian epoch, including a bronze cat statue and a model of a Nile boat. "Soft Gold: Captain Cook Discovers the Northwest Coast" (through December 31, 1985), doc- uments the changing fur trade relations between Northwest Coast Native Ameri- cans and Europeans in the 18th and 19th centuries. Especially important was the fur of the sea otter, called "soft gold" by the Russian traders. Also on view is a se- lection of 100 basketry items from the Mu- seum's collection. Schoharie Museum of the Iroquois Indian, Schoharie, NY: "Iroquois Arts Today: A Way of Understanding" (through Septem- ber 20, 1985), explores the values and themes of life most important to the Iro- quois people through contemporary Iro- quois painting, sculpture, pottery, bead- work, and cornhusk work. Semitic Museum, Harvard University, Cambridge, MA: "Mysteries, Ancient Curses, and Curiosities from the Curator's Cabinet" (through January 7, 1986), is a selection of artifacts from the Museum's collection. A wide variety of objects are featured such as figurines with monsters and curses from the Near East, Roman and Byzantine glass from the Holy Land, and objects which are no longer recogniz- able. Visitors are invited to guess the func- tion of these mystery objects. The Textile Museum, Washington, DC: "Collections and Recollections: The Tex- tile Museum at Sixty" (through Septem- ber 22, 1985), documents the founding, development and growth of the Museum with a history of its founder, George Hew- itt Myers, and a visual introduction to museum activities such as conservation and education. Over 100 textiles repre- senting major areas of the collection will be displayed including objects from Pre- Columbian South America, East and South- east Asia, and the Near and Middle East. The University Museum, University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, PA: "Tradi- tional Crafts of Saudi Arabia" (through January 6, 1986), is the first comprehen- sive exhibition in America of traditional Saudi Arabian crafts. Rugs, decorative camel trappings, jewelry, costumes, metal- ware, baskets, tent bands and curtain walls - over 150 objects - are shown as they would be used in daily life. Organ- ized by the Memorial Art Gallery, Univer- sity of Rochester, NY. Circulated by Sites. "The Silent Language of Guatemalan Tex- tiles" (through January 5, 1986), illus- trates new links between culture and clothing with an exhibit of colorful hand- woven textiles designed by the Maya In- dians of the Guatemalan highlands. Twenty-eight blouses or huípiles from the Lilly de Jongh Osborne collection are dis- played along with accompanying photo- graphs. (See pages 54-7.) MIDWEST Cincinnati Art Museum, Cincinnati, OH: "American Quilts" (through September 2, 1985), is a group of approximately thirty 19th and 20th-century American quilts from the Museum's collection and local private collections. The Cleveland Museum of Art, Cleveland, OH: "Venice: Silk and Lace" (through Au- gust 1985), presents aspects of Italian gen- ius in the textile arts through 25 textiles from the Museum collection. Included are exquisite pieces of 17th and 18th-century Venetian lace and Italian silks and velvets of the 14th through the 18th centuries. Fort Michilimackinac Visitor's Center, Mackinaw City, MI: "Woodland Indian Exhibit" (through October 1985), is a unique collection assembled mostly from Native Americans who have also docu- mented the religious meaning and oral history of the artifacts. Many date from the late 18th century, giving insight into the Great Lakes culture as it existed at the time of the first European contact in the late 17 th century. Indianapolis Museum of Art at Columbus, Columbus, IN: "African Art from the Harrison Eiteljorg Collection" (through July 21, 1985), showcases more than 150 examples of the material culture of the peoples of the Sudan, Guinea Coast, Equa- torial Forest, Southern Savanna, and East and South Africa. Included are masquer- ade costumes and masks, jewelry, sculp- ture and ceramics. Some of the major pieces are over 2,000 years old. Catalogue available. Kelsey Museum of Archaeology, Ann Ar- bor, MI: "Ancient Cyprus: Cultural Con- fluence of the Mediterranean" (through August 18, 1985), displays selected objects from the Museum's extensive collection of Cypriot antiquities. The exhibition in- cludes marble and limestone sculpture, terra-cottas and pottery, ranging in date from the Bronze Age to the Roman period. Brochure available. The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art, Kan- sas City, MO: "Tibetan Works from the Permanent Collection" (through August 18, 1985), is a selection of bronze sculp- tures, decorative objects and thankas, wall paintings representing famous monks and priest rulers as deities. The Oriental Institute, The University of Chicago, Chicago, IL: "The Quest for Pre- history: The Oriental Institute and the Origins of Civilization in the Near East" (through December 1985), presents the re- sults of the Prehistoric Project, concerned with the origins of agriculture and settled village life in the ancient Near East. The exhibit includes the reconstruction of a house excavated at Jarmo, a village in Iraq occupied 9,000 years ago. Stoval Museum, University of Oklahoma, Norman, OK: "Classical Art and Archae- ology Collection" (continuing), presents a new permanent exhibit of exceptional Clas- sical Period artifacts, including a fragment of an Apulian crater from ca. 350 B.C., the only surviving piece of pottery depicting Euripides' lost play "The Phoenix." Also included are coins, clay tablets, glass ves- sels, busts, and silverware.
