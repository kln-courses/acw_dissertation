<plain_text><page sequence="1">Mantle Hood Historical Reconstructions for Oral Traditions of Music Literate societies tend to produce histories of music. In fact, the greater the amount of literature the greater the likelihood that music is a conspi- cuous part of social concern. In ancient Greece, for example, more litera- ture was devoted to music than to all the other arts combined. The Greeks had a strong liking for putting things into logical relationships. They tried to understand the order of everything within their total world view. They were excellent observers, skilled at debate and prone to discussion of all manner of things. It is not surprising that the principal writers about music were statesmen, philosophers and scientists. They developed a theory of music in relation to the cosmos. Similar to the early Chinese, they were convinced that the very survival of the polis or city state depended on a proper understanding and usage of music. They defined the significance of music in masculine education, its symbology, its harmful and therapeutic effects on both man and society. Greek theo- rists recognized that vibrations are the cause of sound involving stationary waves and spheric waves, the former in an instrument or voice, the latter in the air which make stationary waves audible. About 1,000 years later than the Chinese, Pythagoras developed a theory for deriving a scale based on a succession of intervals of the fifth. Aristoxenos counterbalanced this mathematical approach by studying performance practice and became in- terested in problems of perception. He also considered music part of pe- dagogy and politics and showed interest in the psychological and aesthe- tic potentials of music. So far, we have been speaking of writings about music, its effect on the gods, man and society. We have not spoken of music per se. The few frag- ments of Hellenic notation are much later than these writings and really tell us nothing about the way music actually sounded. Without music lite- rature, notated manuscripts covering an extended period of time, do we really have a history of ancient Greek music? Before exploring that particular question further, we might note that the Chinese also wrote much about music and, more than the Greeks, left some early music manuscripts, which are subject to variable interpretation. So did the Koreans and the Japanese. Does the addition of a corpus of no- 5</page><page sequence="2">tated music give us sufficient information on which to base a history of music? Suppose we broaden the question a little more. Notwithstanding a co- pious music literature, how faithfully are we able to reconstruct the sounds of music heard in Medieval and Renaissance Europe? And, if there are rea- sonable doubts, how accurate is our history of Western music during these periods? Some scholars, like Charles Seeger and others, insist that we really know little about the sound of any musical practice prior to the invention of the phonograph, at the end of the 19th century. Permit me one last diversion before we return to the Greeks. Webster's Unabridged Dictionary offers several definitions of the word "history" that are germane to our discussion: 1. a narrative of events con- nected with a real or imaginary object, person or career; a tale, a story, etc.; 2. a systematic written account of events, particularly of those affect- ing a nation, institution, science or art, and usually connected with a philo- sophical explanation of their causes -disti net from annals and chronicles; 3. the branch of knowledge that records and explains past events etc.; 4. the events which form the subject matter of a history. Different writings about Greek music would seem to be accommodated under one or more of these: so, in the sense of dictionary definitions, we do have a history of Greek music, a record of Greek thought about music, even without reliance on music manuscripts. But I believe these definitions and especially roots of the word "history" justify an attempt to establish a history of music among societies with an oral tradition of music. I believe such histories can be at least as reliable as that of ancient Greece. Roots of the word "history", Webster tells us, are related to the Greek word "to know" and also "wit", with its lively connotations of intelligence and perspicacity. If we were to apply this broader understanding of history to definition number two, "the events which form the subject matter of history", we might be able to reconstruct a history of music without the usual music literature on which most histories depend so heavily. We might gain insight about abstract theories that could bring us closer to reconstructing the sound of ancient music. The approach I have in mind, in fact, might even shed light on some of the confusing theories described by the ancient Greeks themselves. 6</page><page sequence="3">By the 4th century B.C., the Greeks had simplified their maze of complex theories by adopting something known as the Perfect System. Earlier than that, Greek writers developed elaborate theories based on a great variety of microtonal intervals: six or eight different size thirds, seconds, several half-tones and smaller. They spoke of "shading" and of tense and relaxed modes, some pure, some mixed. Modern interpretation of these theoreti- cal writings is clouded, to say the least. We stated before, the Greeks were skilled observers. This habit of keen observation extended to the perception and apprehension of musical practice. What they so earnestly attempted to order in the form of compli- cated theories of microtonal deviants, I believe, was based on intelligent and acute observation of musical practice. For a helpful analogy alive today, suppose we look elsewhere. The first that comes to mind are individual singers and instrumentalists who have achieved a kind of immortality in the field of jazz and the blues. If the an- cient Greeks who created theories based on microtonal differences in in- tervals were to hear these stars of the jazz world, would they be able to dis- cern 7 or 8 thirds of different size, about the same number of seconds, 3 or 4 half-tones, as many microtones? I think so. I am suggesting a keenness of perception that amounts to 12 or 13 cents. A few years ago, practical experiments at UCLA indicated that a tru- ly fine musical ear may be capable of even greater discrimination. All the deviant intervals under dicussion can be heard in the styles of singers like Billie Holiday, Ella Fitzgerald, Al Jarvis; and instrumentalists like Charlie Parker, "Satchmo" Louis Armstrong, Hutchinrider and many others. I am confident that the logical bent and orderly mind of the ancient Greeks, if applied to jazz and the blues, would produce a body of theory not too dis- similar from that of their ancient writings. We might take a complementary example by following Alexander's con- quests to Indus civilization. From Mesopotamia and Egypt the Greeks in- herited the aulos, a double shawm. It became associated with the Cult of Dionysus and the Greek drama. The aulos was also an indispensable part of Alexander's military forces, playing a role something like that of the early Western army bugle, signaling und exciting the soldiers to battle. In the dry air of Egyptian deserts a specimen of double oboe was found dating from about 1500 B.C., probably before it was known in Greece. It 7</page><page sequence="4">still contained wax in the holes of all but one of one of its double tubes, which acted, therefore, as a kind of drone. This method of producing a drone accompaniment for the shanai is still known in India, where the two pipes of the aulos have been separated. We could speculate further about the impact of Alexander's arrival by noting the coincidence about the same time of the earliest written Indian source on music theory. We could also ponder the fact that Indian music is readily appealing to Western audiences, perhaps because the two in- herited the same Greek tradition. But let's do this some other time. For now, if you will accept my suggestion with an open mind, let us listen to an Indian shanai player for the sound of possible survivals of the same types of microtonal intervals that fascinated ancient Greek theorists. There are others present better qualified than I to speak of India, per- haps of ancient Greece. But do not miss my principal point. I am trying to illustrate an approach that may be applied to any musical culture, literate or non-literate, the finds of archeology, ethnology, theories of cultural ge- ography - these and others must be evaluated and reinterpreted by com- paring them with musical practices existing today. Some of these are sur- vivors from ancient times. Let me move now to my own more familiar area of speciality, Indonesia. The Javanese have had a literate society among the elite class for a dozen or more centuries. Yet prose and poetic literature relating to music is ex- tremely scant and most of it quite recent. Different forms of notated music literature are also very recent. Written lists in the 8th century mention a few musicians' titles and Indian instruments, but nothing else about music. The 8th-century Borobudur, the famous Buddhist stupa in central Java, tells the story of the Buddha in three miles of bas reliefs. Among these beautiful carvings many musical instruments are depicted in actual performance. How much do they tell us about early Javanese music? Considered apart from other evidence, not very much. With two or three exceptions, the host of instruments shown are from India. They were brought to Java by Indian overlords who, historians tell us, established a benevolent colonization. I wonder, has there ever been a benevolent colonization? It is true, the Indians brought with them many benefits, like the introduction of writing, 8</page><page sequence="5">which became the basis of Javanese script; principles of architecture; a calendar; laws of governance suitable for a social hierarchy; a refinement in manner and an etiquette that fostered the priyayi ideal, still the model of social behaviour among the central Javanese. Yes, there were many bene- fits from this colonization. But I question whether any Javanese of the time, prince or commoner, would have described it as benevolent. We do have additional musical evidence, evidence-by-omission connected with the bas reliefs of the Borobudur. The evidence supports the strong probability that the so-called Hindu-Javanese period was a time of political unrest, of up-risings, of a restive populace. Let me say again, whatever the ultimate benefits, I doubt there has ever been a benevolent colonization anywhere, if I understand the meaning of the word "benevo- lent". Sufficient documentation cannot be presented here. But let me mention the gist of the probes that led to such a thesis, a thesis no historian could apprehend, because he would lack a thorough knowledge of living Java- nese musical ensembles. On the Borobudur appear a saron, an instrument with bronze slabs over a trough resonator, a xylophone and possibly a ketuk, a small inverted bronze kettle with a central boss. These two or three individual instru- ments are the only ones on the Borobudur associated with the ruling class and not found in India. A simple indigenous ensemble is depicted on a single relief at the lowest level of the stupa, where the kledi, a bamboo mouth organ, and other folk instruments appear. It is the only Javanese ensemble found on the stupa. Both the saron and the xylophone, which may have had either wood or bronze keys, are relatively late additions to the gamelan. Extant ancient ensembles like gamelan munggang, gamelan Kodok Ngorêk and others do not include the saron, the xylophone or ketuk. The principal instru- ments are sets of very large bonang, shaped something like a ketuk. The saron does not appear as the 16th-century gamelan Sekati (my own studies indicate a somewhat earlier date). Why, we ask, are none of these ancient gamelan found among the bas reliefs of the Borobudur? Why, in fact, is the only Javanese ensemble a group of folk instruments on the lowest level of the stupa, which, mono- graphers tell us, is a depiction of "man's punishment in hell?" 9</page><page sequence="6">This is the 8th century. By the 10th century, among musical depictions on bas reliefs of the Hindu temple complex at Prambanan, even these few indigenous instruments are omitted. It is well established that bronze itself, the material of which most game- Ian instruments are made, has long been associated with royalty, the ru- ling class, with magic power and mana in Asia. Bronze, in by-gone centu- ries, was positively not the property of the common man. Ancient gamelan ensemble known in the 8th century, therefore, were extremely powerful symbols of Javanese royal authority, as they continued to be well into the 20th century. Their token representation on the Borobudur- the saron, not yet a regu- lar member of the gamelan-, the xylophone, used for melodic decoration; and perhaps the ketuk, not used in the large gamelan of the time and still today the smallest member of the gong family - even these least represen- tative instruments appear to be too powerful a symbolic reminder of Java- nese authority. As a consequence, I suspect, they were deliberately omit- ted from Candi Prambanan. From the 10th to the 16th centuries central Java is blacked out. There has been speculation that the sudden removal of civilization to East-Java was caused either by massive volcanic action or by a silting-up of the har- bour at Semarang. Discovery in the mid 60's of the apex of ancient temples would seem to support the former theory. I want to add the probability that concommitant with natural violence a period of social violence ended in the overthrow of the Indian overlords, who disappear with the beginning of the Eastern kingdoms. Let me stress that all sources familiar to the historian must be re-inter- preted by comparing them with implicit information found in ancient types of musical ensembles existing today. The first time I ever saw a bronze kettle drum from the Dongson period of about 300 B.C., I thought it was the most beautiful and impressive musical instrument I had ever seen. After much gentle persuasion in the Museum at Jakarta, two reluctant attendants agreed to hold the drum by the handles, while I struck it with my best big-gong technique. It may have been the first time that a bronze drum had sounded in 2,000 years. Its deep sonority set off speculations that have been reverberating ever since. I did not know then and not for some years that all published studies of bronze drums had been made from the viewpoint of the art historian, the iconographer and the archeolo- gist. No one seriously regarded these magnificent objects as musical in- struments! Since that first encounter, I have sounded almost every bronze drum I have been fortunate enough to see. At the time of my first encounter with bronze drums, I was in Java to con- duct research on the art of group improvisation practiced by the large gamelan orchestra. The evolution of a bronze orchestra had to begin with the impor- tation of bronze. With that decision came many questions. What do we know about the vague period from the 3rd century B.C. until the Hindu colonists arrived? How much is known about neolithic societies, living then and now? What kind of archeological evidence is there? After several years of digging out answers to these questions, came the big one: how to present what I had uncovered? One way was a survey of the archeological literature. But the art of group improvisation among Javanese musicians is very alive and warm. A survey seemed like a deadly dull way to begin. As a humanist, I could not bear to vivisect such a viable subject. I decided to attempt a reconstruction of neolithic society in Java. After an introduction, I take the reader back more than two millenia to that dra- matic moment in pre-history when bronze weaponry, armour, jewellery and kettle drums are abruptly introduced by invading forces. I use this little story as the target of a long critique documenting fact, probability, possi- bility and imagination. The study shows that sets of cast bronze drums were models for sets of hand forged bronze bonang, like those found in gamelan munggang and other ancient types of gamelan, where they comprise the principal instru- ments. Neolithic society in Java reached a high plane of civilization. Even with stone implements the people developed a considerable technology, were skillful wet- and dry-rice farmers, knew weaving, animal husbandry, fought local wars and gradually combined into larger social units as petty king- doms. They quickly mastered the art of bronze casting-by the lost-wax pro- cess and went on to develop great originality as metalsmiths, including hand forging. Apparently the method of hand forging used then is the same as that still used in making gamelan instruments today. 14</page><page sequence="11">Among the many sources consulted, Van Heekeren's The Bronze-Iron Age of Indonesia was one of the most valuable. From this summary study I learned that depicted on one of the ancient drums is a set of four bronze drums played by musicians located In a small raised pavilion. From this depiction came the idea that bronze drums may have been played in sets, notwithstanding evidence that single ones were also carried in battle. Then I learned that on the island of Sangeang can be found a set of six bronze drums. On the Kai Islands are several legends about the landing of a set of four bronze drums, although only two have survived. The greatest confirmation that bronze drums were played in sets came from an accidental browsing in a bookstore in Tokyo. I do not read Chi- nese, but I was flipping through photographs of a recent dig in China, when I saw a sacrificial ritual depicted in sculptured figures on top of a bronze money coffer. In this village scene two huge bronze drums sit near the rim of the coffer, and many persons are crowded before them and around an elevated pavilion, like the one shown by Van Heekeren. This pavilion houses a set of 16 bronze drums complete with musicians. Here was the clinching evidence I needed. Let me summarize the principal points of the paper: 1 . The writing of music history has relied primarily on other written sour- ces. Some of these sources, like those of the Greeks, tell much about music; some, like those of Asia and the West, include bodies of music notation. 2. Included in the writing of any history of music must be consideration of other kinds of sources, like the findings of archeology, ethnology, iconography, cultural geography and so forth. 3. All these sources have to be evaluated and re-interpreted in the light of actual performance practice still in usage today. All musical perfor- mance, from gamelan munggang to electronic music, contains a residue of past musical practices. Mantle Hood Reconstitution historique des traditions musicales orales (résumé) Les sociétés lettrées ont tendance à pro- duire des histoires de la musique. En fait, plus il y a de documents écrits, plus grande est la probabilité que la musique occupe une grande place dans les préoccupations de la société. Dans la Grèce antique, par exemple, on en a écrit beaucoup plus sur la musique que sur tous les autres arts réunis, et les au- teurs grecs en ont étudié d'innombrables aspects, comme par exemple sa fonction sociale, son symbolisme, l'acoustique, les échelles, l'exécution, la perception de la musique, son esthétique. Cependant, si l'on considère la musique en soi, les quelques 18</page><page sequence="15">fragments de notation hellénique dont on dispose sont beaucoup plus récents que ces écrits, et à vrai dire, ils ne nous renseignent pas sur la sonorité véritable de cette musi- que. Peut-on vraiment connaître l'histoire de la musique de la Grèce antique sans s'ap- puyer sur des documents écrits correspon- dant à une période prolongée, sans nota- tion? Avant de pousser plus loin l'examen de cette question, il serait peut-être bon de faire remarquer que les Chinois ont eux aussi beaucoup écrit sur la musique et qu'ils ont laissé, plus que les Grecs, des manuscrits très anciens, que l'on peut interpréter de différentes façons. Cette remarque s'appli- que également à la Corée et au Japon. Peut- on recueillir suffisamment d'informations pour reconstituer une histoire de la musique en additionnant toutes les notations? Supposons que nous élargissions la ques- tion encore un peu. Dans quelle mesure peut-on reconstituer fidèlement les sons de la musique tels qu'ils étaient perçus dans l'Europe du moyen-âge et de la Renaissan- ce? Et pourtant ici, on dispose d'une docu- mentation substantielle. Et, si l'on a des rai- sons valables de douter, quel est le degré d'exactitude de notre histoire de la musique occidentale dans ces périodes? Certains chercheurs, comme Charles Seeger et d'au- tres, soulignent que l'on ne sait vraiment pas grand chose sur les sonorités de la musique - quelle qu'elle soit- antérieure à l'invention du phonographe à la fin du 19ème siècle. J'aimerais donc illustrer une approche applicable à toute culture musicale, écrite ou non. Je suis convaincu qu'il importe d'évalu- er et de réinterpréter les preuves anciennes et contemporaines sous toutes leurs formes - la prose et la littérature poétique, la littéra- ture musicale, les découvertes de l'archéolo- gie, de l'ethnologie, et les théories sur la géographie culturelle - et d'autres encore, en les comparant aux pratiques musicales d'aujourd'hui, dont certaines remontent à des temps anciens. Prenons comme exemple les conquêtes d'Alexandre vers la vallée de l'Indus. De la Mésopotamie et de l'Egypte, les Grecs ont hérité I 'aulos, un chalumeau à deux tuyaux, qu'ils associèrent au culte de Dionysos et au drame grec. Cet instrument devint un élé- ment indispensable dans les armées d'Ale- xandre, où il jouait un rôle comparable à ce- lui du bugle militaire occidental d'autrefois, qui consistait à donner aux soldats le signal du combat et à les stimuler. Un spécimen de hautbois à deux tuyaux datant approximativement de 1500 avant notre ère, c'est-à-dire d'une période où il n'était probablement pas encore connu en Grèce, a été trouvé dans le désert aride d'Egypte. Tous les trous de ses deux tuyaux étaient encore bouchés par de la cire, à l'ex- ception d'un, qui servait donc à produire une sorte de bourdon. Ce système d'accompa- gnement par un bourdon est encore utilisé pour le shanal en Inde, où l'on a séparé les deux tuyaux de I 'aulos. Nous pourrions pousser plus loin nos spé- culations sur l'impact des conquêtes d'Ale- xandre en notant que les premières sources indiennes écrites sur la théorie de la musi- que coïncident avec cette période. Nous pourrions également considérer le fait que la musique de l'Inde est bien accessible pour les publics occidentaux, peut-être parce que, comme l'Occident, elle est héritière de la même tradition grecque. Si vous acceptez ma suggestion dans un esprit d'ouverture, essayez de déceler dans les sons du shanai les survivances d'intervalles microtonals du même type que ceux qui fascinèrent les théoriciens de la Grèce antique. Permettez-moi d'aborder maintenant une région qui m'est plus familière: l'Indonésie. Pendant quelque douze siècles, voire plus, l'élite de la société javanaise a compté dans ses rangs une classe de lettrés. Or, il y a très 19</page><page sequence="16">peu de documents - prose et littérature poé- tique - ayant trait à la musique, et la plupart d'entre eux sont assez récents. J'ai décidé de tenter de reconstituer la so- ciété néolithique de Java. Après l'introduc- tion, j'ai fait effectuer au lecteur un retour en arrière dans le temps de plus de deux millé- naires, jusqu'à cette période dramatique de la préhistoire, où les armes, l'armure, les bi- joux et les timbales en bronze furent brutale- ment introduites dans l'île par les envahis- seurs. Cette petite histoire fait l'objet d'un long essai partant des faits, des probabilités, des possibilités, et faisant intervenir l'imagi- nation. Cette étude montre que les jeux de tam- bours en bronze coulé ont servi de modèles aux bonang en bronze forgés à la main sem- blables à ceux que l'on trouve dans le game- lan munggang et dans d'autres types an- ciens de gamelan, dont ils forment les princi- paux instruments. La société néolithique de Java a atteint un haut degré de civilisation. Les habitants de l'île ont mis au point des techniques consi- dérables, même lorsqu'ils ne disposaient que d'instruments en pierre; ils cultivaient le riz en terrain sec ou par irrigation avec beau- coup d'habileté; ils savaient tisser; ils éle- vaient du bétail; ils se livraient des guerres intestines et s'unirent peu à peu pour former des entités sociales plus importantes, orga- nisées comme de minuscules royaumes. Ils maîtrisèrent rapidement l'art de la fonte du bronze à la cire et firent preuve d'une grande originalité dans le travail des métaux, et no- tamment dans le forgeage à la main. Appa- remment, le procédé de forgeage à la main était le même que celui qui est employé de nos jours pour la confection des instruments du gamelan. The Bronze-Iron Age of Indonesia, de Van Heekeren, compte parmi les plus précieuses des nombreuses sources consultées. J'ai appris de cette étude concise qu'un jeu de quatre tambours en bronze joués par des musiciens dans un petit pavillon surélevé sont représentés sur l'un des anciens tam- bours. Cette peinture m'a suggéré l'idée que les tambours en bronze étaient peut-être uti- lisés à plusieurs, bien que l'on sache de fa- çon certaine qu'ils l'étaient aussi seuls, dans les batailles. J'ai appris ensuite que l'on peut trouver dans l'île de Sangeang un jeu de six tambours en bronze. Dans les îles Kai, plu- sieurs légendes sont entretenues sur l'arri- vée d'un groupe de quatre tambours de bronze, dont deux seulement ont survécu. C'est en furetant par hasard dans une li- brairie de Tokyo que j'ai trouvé la confirma- tion la plus convaincante de mes présomp- tions sur l'emploi des tambours de bronze par groupes, grâce à une photo, car je ne sais pas lire le chinois; parmi les illustrations d'objets mis à jour récemment lors de fouil- les en Chine, il y en avait une qui montrait un sacrifice rituel représenté par des figures sculptées sur le couvercle d'un coffre à mon- naie en bronze. Dans cette scène de village, on peut voir deux énormes tambours de bronze placés près du rebord du coffre, et une foule massée devant eux et autour d'un pavillon surélevé, semblable à celui qui est évoqué par Van Heekeren. Ce pavillon abrite un jeu de seize tambours, avec les musi- ciens. J'avais donc sous les yeux la preuve parlante que je cherchais. Je résumerai pour terminer l'essentiel de cet exposé: 1 . Pour écrire une histoire de la musique, on s'est fondé en premier lieu sur d'autres sources écrites. Certaines, comme par exemple les sources grecques, en disent long sur la musique; d'autres, comme celles de l'Asie et de l'Occident, compor- tent un corpus de notation musicale. 2. Pour écrire toute histoire de la musique, il faut tenir compte de sources qui n'ont pas directement trait à la musique: dé- couvertes de l'archéologie, de l'ethnolo- 20</page><page sequence="17">gie, iconographie, géographie culturelle, etc. 3. Il importe d'évaluer et de réinterpréter toutes ces sources à la lumière de la prati- que musicale encore en usage de nos jours. Il y a dans toute exécution musica- le, du gamelan munggang à la musique électronique, des survivances des modes d'interprétation du passé. Si je suis dans le vrai, alors il faut réécrire une grande partie de la musique occidentale - de l'antiquité, avec son rayonnement, à au- jourd'hui. 21</page></plain_text>
