<plain_text><page sequence="1">Gérard Béhague South American Masked Dances: an Overview Numerous folk dances of South American traditional cultures frequently call for mask wearers who represent specific characters in the performance of dramas, plays or festivals. This form of disguise acts as a symbolic representa- tion of the essential attributes attached to the various dramatis personae in a particular performance. The fundamental uses and functions of masks asso- ciated with social and religious life do not differ markedly from those of other traditional cultures in Africa, Asia, Oceania or North America. What creates the uniqueness of many South American masked dances is the array of strongly divergent influences whose combination often epitomizes the very ethnohistori- cal development of a given community or group. This brief account only intends to provide some representative examples of the uses and functions of masks in some selected folk dances and festivals. The fairly complex relationship be- tween belief systems (often involving totemism) and the esthetic consideration in the making of masks, between mythical world views, social orders and the wide range of meanings of masked dances, is deliberately omitted in this overview. Archaeological findings related to tropical-forest Indian cultures and pre- Columbian highland or Pacific-coastal cultures have revealed the common use of masks. Sculptured wooden masks with different colored paintings, found in pre-Columbian Peruvian tombs, served as false heads of the mummies' fardos , or funeral bundles in which mummified figures were enclosed. Woven cloth masks with various hair styles representing several mythical figures were used for the same purpose among the ancient, pre-lncan, rather mysterious tribe of the Wallas, inhabitants of the Lima valley who were subjugated by the Incas. Whether these and the renowned people of the Mochica and Chimu in the North, the Nazca and Paracas in the South, developed masked dances similar to contemporary Indian dances is difficult to ascertain. The cult of the huacas or wakas, however, (pyramids, burial or any sacred places of a community or a single family), as described in sixteenth-century Spanish chronicles, involved various festivities and celebrations with dancing, singing and story telling. Since totems of huacas were frequently animals, costumes, masks, and dances were symbolic representations and activities of such animals. Moreover, as pre- lncan and Incan religions often had zoomorphic gods (such as jaguar, puma, condor) it seems plausible to assign a probable pre-Columbian origin to various 23</page><page sequence="2">zoomorphic masked dances of contemporary highland Indians in Bolivia, Peru and Ecuador, functioning in fertility rites or in Christian feasts coinciding with the Incan religious calendar. Among the Incas themselves, we have notices of a great variety of mimetic dances and pageantry celebrating their monthly festivals, and a few descriptions of dances in which masks were used in six- teenth« and seventeenth-century accounts, such as those of Pedro de Cieza de León, Huamán Poma de Ayala, El Inca Garcilaso de la Vega and Bernabé Cobo. Garcilaso de la Vega describes, for example, the totemic costumes used by provincial curacas (or chiefs) at Incan festivities in Cuzco and mentions spe- cifically the strange masks worn by the Yunka Indians of the coast. Cobo's de- scription of a dance which he calls guayayturilla, mentions the painting in red of the participants' faces and the fastening of a gold or silver band across their faces. Incan masks, made entirely of gold, have survived, but their use in dance performances has not been altogether elucidated. Numerous contemporary dances of the Andean highlands reenact ancestral Indian myths in which reinterpreted fantastic figures or characters of native and Hispanic origins appear side by side or combined. The most conspicuous of these characters in the Andean area is related to the beliefs in and representa- tions of the devil. The Catholic prince of the devils - Lucifer, Satanas - brought by the missionaries was soon syncretized with the equivalent devilish creature of the Indians of the Peruvian sierra and the Bolivian altiplano , Supay or Supaya. The Inca legend attributes the creation of mountain devils to Inti, the Sun deity, who had forbidden the first men to climb the mountains. Following their disobedience, Inti plunged them into the mountains and thus created hell and demons. Maybe Lucifer and Supay became the same entity in the minds of the Indians, for their contemporary representations of El Diablo through masks reveal a most mythically significant imagination. The famous danza de los diablos or diablada of the Bolivian altiplano and the Peruvian Puno department exhibits some of the most spectacular masked dances of the South-American continent. The Asiatic ("chinesco" is the Spanish term used) influence of the devil's masks in that dance is given a pre-Hispanic origin by some and a more recent source by others. (See illustration no. 1). The terrifying-looking monster maintains the distinctive marks of the European devil (horns, tail, snakes), adding heavily ornamented and grotesquely amplified features, such as enor- mous, glaucous eyes (made of light bulbs), sharp disproportioned teeth, huge, reddish pointed ears (often made of rubber) and wicked-looking reptiles between the horns. The "chinesco" aspect of the masks results from the glimmering effects of the silver dots and the pieces of mirror covering the masks. The devils also wear painstakingly-embroidered costumes, some of which recall those of the Spanish bullfighters, with golden and silver designs of dragons and other crea- tures, or geometrical patterns made with beads (see illustration no. 2). The masks and costumes constitute a unit. As dancers, the devils appear as the main characters of the Carnival festivities. In the city of Oruro (Bolivia), the "diablada" has a distinctive religious character since it is performed for the Virgin of the Socavón, the Virgin of the mine workers of the region. The close identification of the workers with Supay (whom they call affectionately "Tio", i.e. "Uncle") is not a contradiction, since Supay is the "owner" of the mines on whom rests the security and prosperity of the workers. A significant syncretism can be observed in some of the dances associated with Christmas festivities, and especially the Nativity scene in the central cordillera of Peru. The various dances and mimes in front of the Nativity derive their origins from several mythologies. Besides the traditional shepherds, angels and peasants, animals of the forest, pumas and llamas also pay their tribute in the celebration of the birth of Jesus. The wild animals (illustration no. 4) dance a pantomime with domestic animals (also represented through masks) in front of the crèche, standing usually in the main church square of the village. To a certain extent these strange zoomorphic masked figures not only epitomize the local fauna, but also symbolize the retention of mythical beliefs involving rural deities and nature worship. The folk celebration of the feasts of Saint John (San Juan or São João), Saint Peter (San Pedro), Corpus Christi, Carnival or Mardi Gras, and Holy Week gives rise to a multitude of masked dances in the whole of South America. Moreover, the equinoctial observations give the opportunity to remember some of the ancestral beliefs related to Inti or Pachamama (the earth deity in the Andean countries) and other agricultural deities. In Cotacachi, Imbabura (Ecuador), for example, the summer solstice celebration by Indians and mestizos correspond- ing to the Inca festival of Inti Raymi is observed on Corpus Christi. Several ritual dances are performed in front of the church, including the dance of the abagos or abilus, masked characters who, according to Segundo Luis Moreno, might represent the spirit of Evil. Four performers participate in the dance: two danzantes wearing angel costumes (with wings and feather headdresses rather than the traditional halo, and carrying a sword and jingles attached to their ankles), and two abagos. The latter are a sort of goblin, or children's bogey man, with papier-mâché masks of deliberately grotesque and frightening expressions (illustration no. 5), enhanced by long wigs made of horsehair or agave fibers. They carry a wooden stick, curved at the tip, which they strike against each other, as the angels strike their swords. Their dance is, for the most part, circular in the opposite direction to that of the yumbos. Be- hind the devils often appear the sacharunas (fanciful masked representa- tions of jungle, Indian people). Among the numerous Ecuadorian danzantes , the most spectacular from the point of view of costume and dance are those performing during the Corpus Christi and patron saints' celebrations in the various Indian villages from Imbabura to Tungurahua and Chimborazo. The luster of the costume and the luxurious display contribute to the popularity of these dancers. They wear a colorful headdress heavily ornamented with precious stone imitations, golden and silver spangles and tinsel, and a multi- colored plume of feathers, from the back of which hang several fringes of bright silk. With gloved hands they flourish a kind of sword (or some other object such as a bottle of alcoholic beverage) during certain choreographic figures (see illustration no. 6). The most valuable item of which individual danzantes boast quite readily is a sort of colorful apron hanging from the waist to the feet cover- ed with old silver coins. In addition to a regular simple mask, the most osten- tatious danzantes add a veil made of a net of coins. This display of riches and the solemnity of the dance itself point to a probable ritual origin, in which both ancestral Indian and colonial Spanish elements intervene. In the Peruvian mountains, several masked dances, believed to derive from the war or martial Incan dances, are preserved. One example is the K'achampa of Cuzco origin. Considered a male war dance to celebrate the victory of Incan armies, the choreography includes several figures which are reminiscent of army manceuvers, actual flighting with slingshots in an imaginary battlefield, and a victory celebration. Despite its pre-Columbian derivation, the dancers' cos- tumes and masks reflect traditional European colonial clothing and Caucasian facial features. Likewise, the jija huaca is a warrior dance of the department of Huánuco. One of the masks used in the dance includes zoomorphic ornamen- tation, such as a pair of snakes around the eyes, lizards on the cheeks, and a small frog on the chin. Protruding tusks and teeth reinforce the fiercest ex- pression of the disguise. Most of the individual charac- ters of the dance groups wear interesting costumes and masks. The Pedro and Pablo figures, for example, use huge wings of real feathers on their shoulders, multicolored pointed caps and white masks made of old hats with large eye- brows and beards. The group del negro includes a character called dieciocho who wears a terrifying mask with enormous dilated eyes and huge moustaches to the sides. Another major group of this Northern Chilean festivity, the catim- banos, presents costumes and masks of remarkable uniformity, deliberately reducing the expressiveness of the masks. Some of the most conspicuous masked dances of Venezuela are performed by the diablos danzantes on Corpus Christi day, particularly in towns of the states of Cojedes, Distrito Federal and Miranda. Descriptions of the devil dancers in San Francisco de Yare (Miranda) indicate that they are all members of the local Society of the Holy Sacrament, a fact signified by their wearing a rosary and a cross attached to their chest. In contrast, their masks represent enormous figures of monsters or animals with horns. In that community, these "Christian" devils perform a dance known as la bamba and participate in the musical ensemble by shaking maracas. At the end of the performance of the devils' dance, the humbling defeat of the devils is signified by letting the masks fall from their faces, a clear indication that the mask is the primary identifying element of the characters. The Society to which they belong provides mutual assistance through the collecting of money and pays for the funeral expenses of its members. The leader of this association is recognized among the devils by the four horns adorning his mask. Among the numerous Columbian folk dances, a few associated with the San Juan, Corpus Christi, and Carnival festivities in both mountain and coastal areas involve various characters in disguise. The Danza de los diablos y cucam- bas of the Pacific coast, for example, is performed during Corpus Christi celebration between an equal number of devils and female characters. The devils wear red costumes with yellow stripes and snake designs on the sides of the trousers. Their masks in the shape of an elongated cap are covered with small mirrors and various kinds of trinkets. Several Carnival dances in the Atlantic coastal area enact imitations of birds and other animals, such as the Danza de los Collongos, Danza de los Gallinazos, and Danza de los P ajaros. All participants of such dances wear zoomorphic masks characterizing the various birds of the regions, or, in the case of the Gallinazos ("vultures"), the central dramatic parts of the donkey and the dog. The San Juan and San Pedro festiv- ities in the Department of Huila also rely on the participation of several devils, 33</page><page sequence="12">of quite different mask expressions, and animals (such as wolves). This occa- sion constitutes a true Carnival whose symbolic leader or "king" is known as Pericles Carnaval . Brazil displays a rich tradition of dramatic dances (called bailados) of distinct- ly mestizo character. They constitute some of the most traditional choreographic structures and form an important part of Brazilian folk theatre. Since the main themes of Brazilian bailados are religious (conversion of pagans, resurrection of Christ) or historical (Portuguese maritime exploits, battles of Christians and Moors), often based on the Iberian medieval popular theatre (reenactment of the Chanson de Roland), numerous characters representing the various facets of the dramas wear different forms of disguise. In addition, totemic figures are often combined with human figures, such as the conspicuous bull of the dances Bumba-meu-boi and Boi-bumbà. In the version of the latter from the state of Pará, the bull is often substituted by stylized, bizarre-looking birds, interpreted by some as Amerindian totems. The Bumba-meu-boi dance symbolizes death and resurrection, in an acted satire of the white oppression of the Black people, as in other dramatic dances such as Congos , Caboclinhos and Cheganças. Several popular revelries in Brazilian folklore also include masked characters who act through dance certain parts of the embaixade (lit. "embassy" or cor- tege of the various personages of the folk play). For example, the dance of Christians and Moors in São Luís do Paraitinga (São Paulo) includes a fight between the "spy" of the Moors and the "spy" of the Christians, both dancers wearing papier-mâché masks of simple expression. Since the subject matter of such dances draws on Iberian catechetical theatre, the chronicles of Char- lemagne's conquests as known in Brazilian, Peruvian, and other South American folk renditions have penetrated into the local folk theatre. The famous romance (or ballad) Carlos Magno e os Doze Pares de França has been as popular in Brazil and in Spanish America, and has given rise to a series of story telling and dances in which the "doze pares de França" (or the 24 soldiers at the vanguard of the battle front) wear masks of typically Caucasian features. Carnival is undoubtedly one of the major occasions in South America for the performance of a multitude of secular dances in which the wearing of masks is almost de rigueur, to reinforce the ritual and magic character of the festivity. Although imported to the New World by Europeans, the expressive components of Carnival in most South American cities owe a great deal to Amerindian or Black African folk traditions. In Pujili (Ecuador), for example, the same danzantes described for the Corpus Christi festivity reappear at Carnival time. In Recife (Brazil), the dancers and characters of the famous cortege known as Maracatú 34</page><page sequence="13">reveal their unmistakably African character. Since the late 1920's in Rio de Janeiro, the yearly presentation of the escolas de samba (or "samba schools") combine European, African and Amerindian elements in their costumes, dances and music. Traditionally masks associated with Carnival celebration, especially in the urban areas, tend to draw on European sources. The presence of King or God Momus (the supreme entity of Carnival), the costumes representing animals (horses, wolves, foxes, bulls and others), and even the Indian disguises with bows and arrows are all customs with European antecedents. Devil masks during Carnival are also traditional in Europe. Clown masks of all sorts reveal the folk imagination of the local makers. Zoomorphic masks appear in special contexts, such as the Urso de Carnavá in Recife, a bear figure whose costume weighs up to 30 kilos, with an elaborate multicolored mask made of papier mâché. Some bear masks are made of silver paper. Strangely, Afro-American traditional dances in South America rely only slightly on masks. The well-developed West African tradition of maskers does not seem to have been retained as other cultural elements. It is significant, however, that ritual dances of the Afro-Brazilian religions call for a facial ex- pression at the moment of spirit possession, i.e., the appearance of the gods, which in itself functions as a mask, namely, the visual exteriorization of the presence of a new personality. This lack of masks may be explained by the fact that gods (or orixás) are abstractions with concrete attributes but not concrete visual representations. (This also explains the easy acceptance of Catholic saints' lithographs in the shrines to represent African gods.) The only orixá costume which actually covers the whole head and face of the initiate is that of Omolú or Obaluaiê, the god of smallpox and contagious diseases. Through a large hood made of African straw, Omolú hides his face to avoid public exposure of the disease. Tropical forest Indian cultures of South America have ceremonial and ritual dances during which spirits, gods and a complex of mythical beliefs are invoked. Quite often, masks are used in the context of such dances, especially among tribal groups such as the Uanana, Uitoto, and Tariana of Columbia, the Tukuna of the Upper Amazon area, the Chuncho of Peru, the Chiriguano of Bolivia, and the various groups of the Upper Xingu in Brazil (Xanvante, Carajá, and others). Etant donné que la foi aux totems se rapportait très souvent aux animaux, les costumes, les masques et les danses devenaient les symboles des acti- vités de ces animaux. Puisque les religions Inca et pré-lnca avaient souvent des dieux zoomorphiques (comme le jaguar, le puma et le condor), il semble raison- nable d'attribuer une origine précolom- bienne à plusieurs danses masquées zoo- morphiques parmi les populations con- temporaines indiennes des hauts plateaux de Bolivie, du Pérou et de l'Equateur. Parmi les nombreux personnages ou figures fantastiques des danses actuelles dans les Andes, le diable apparaît très souvent. C'est le Supay des Indiens de la montagne, qui participe dans la danza de los diablos ou diablada. Cette danse utilise l'un des masques les plus spectacu- laires du continent. Les masques des diables ont une certaine influence asiati- que («chinesco» en espagnol) qui s'entre- voit par les effets brillants des petits miroirs et des pustules argentées qui re- couvrent les masques. Un syncrétisme remarquable s'observe dans plusieurs danses exécutées lors de la célébration de Noël, particulièrement celles devant la crèche où des animaux sauvages (représentés par des masques en cuir avec des moustaches, barbes et sourcils en fourrure d'animaux) dansent une pantomime avec des animaux domesti- ques. Ces masques symbolisent non seule- ment la faune locale mais également la rétention de croyances mythiques ayant trait aux divinités rurales et à l'adoration de la nature. La célébration folklorique des fêtes de la Saint-Jean, Saint-Pierre, Corpus Christi (Fête-Dieu), Carnaval, et de la Semaine Sainte met en jeu une multitude de danses masquées dans toute l'Amérique du sud. Parmi tant d'autres, nous trouvons les exemples suivants: la danse des abagos ou abi lus en Equateur, les diablitos, yum- bos et danzantes équatoriens, les danses rituelles de San Pedro de Atacama au Chili, les diablos danzantes du Venezuela, la danza de los diablos y cucambas en Colombie. Parmi les danses de carnaval, les exemples suivants sont mentionnés: danza de los gallinazos de Colombie, le Maracatú et les escolas de samba du Brésil, les danzantes de l'Equateur. Les danses guerrières ou dramatiques indi- quées sont la K'achampa du Pérou, et le Bumba-meu-boi, Congos , Caboclinhos et Cheganças, du Brésil.
