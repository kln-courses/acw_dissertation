COONEY THE nineteenth century was the great period of private collections of Egyptian antiquities. Some pioneer collecting had been undertaken in the pre- ceding centuries, but it was not until the opening of Egypt by Napoleon's dramatic expedition in 1798 that interest in Egypt swept western Europe. Among European collectors, mostly French and English, this interest, maintained for over a cen- tury, resulted in the accumulation of a re- markably large body of material. Much of this has now entered public collections, but a considerable number of objects re- mains in private hands to serve as a nu- cleus for the formation of new collections, public or private. In America of the early nineteenth cen- tury, while there was great literary and theological interest in ancient Egypt, there was nothing comparable to the pri- vate collections of western Europe. The very few Americans who did acquire Egyptian objects later in the century- collectors like Wilbour, Smith, Gorringe, and Anderson-were almost without ex- ception persons who had lived in Egypt and had some connection with Egypto- logical studies. American interest in Egyp- tian art lagged so far behind the European that it was not until the present century that any considerable collections were formed, a movement that started only shortly before the private collections of Europe began to be dispersed. It would be pleasant to record that American collectors were continuing the earlier European tradition, but in fact it is difficult to state with any certainty how much Egyptian material is in private hands in this country and, more to the point, how much of it is worth while. Sev- eral private collections, each containing some important and unpublished items, are known, but apparently there exists an extensive number of unrecorded antiqui- ties scattered over the country. Certainly the dealers have sold many single objects to collectors; and at auction sales the pre- viously unknown collectors who bid make one suspect that there are many groups of a few objects each in unknown private hands. One of the important collections, the property of Mr. Albert Gallatin of New York City, is published in the following pages; owing to the generosity of its owner the major pieces the collection contains are here for the first time made available to scholars. The Gallatin collection has been assembled almost entirely on the basis of artistic quality. As in most muse- 1</page><page sequence="2">2 JOURNAL OF NEAR EASTERN STUDIES ums, the products of the New Kingdom predominate, but, for a private collection, there is a remarkable range of material., no single period of Egyptian art being un- represented. With the exception of a very few objects purchased at an earlier date, the collection has been assembled during the past twenty-five years in London, Paris, Cairo, and New York, and it now includes well over one hundred specimens, of which only a selection can be included here. The inclusion of several objects from famous earlier collections gives the Gal- latin collection a physical continuity with its predecessors of the nineteenth century. One piece, the alabaster head of Amenho- tep III, goes back to the Napoleonic Ex- pedition, passing for the past century and a half from collection to collection. For many pieces, the splendid archaic lion, for example, it has been possible to recover long bibliographies; for others, thanks to the traditional reluctance of dealers to re- veal the source of their pieces, the earlier history is still unknown. All known facts pertaining to each object are included in the catalogue entries. The entries are introduced by the num- ber of the object in Mr. Gallatin's cata- logue of his collection. For the attributions I am responsible. The dating of this un- documented material is frequently a mat- ter of considerable difficulty, especially when uninscribed; but, of the sculptures catalogued here, I am still uncertain of the correctness of only two attributions, in both cases royal heads, Nos. 7 and 56. I doubt that either attribution is far from the mark; but one of the advantages of publication is obtaining the opinions of others. No. 1. Pls. I-III. A lion recumbent with head frontal resting on legs, carved in quartz of deep cream color resembling alabaster. One of the most appealing sculptures yet known from the archaic pe- riod of Egyptian art. The sculptor has captured the great force of a lion by giving his sculpture an enormous head, a lion's most typical characteristic. The ears are large (right ear broken) and plain, pro- jecting at right angles to the body. The hardness of the medium forded the elimi- nation of many details, the eyes not being indicated; the mouth is formed by a groove turning up slightly at each end, outlined with a convex ridge. The tail, in typical archaic fashion, runs up the center of the back with its end looped to left and terminating in an oval. The haunches are modeled in high relief. On the base the form of the legs is only roughly indicated, and here the surface is not polished but clearly shows the blows from a hammer of some even harder stone. Copper tools, presumably the only metal tools available when this sculpture was made, would be blunted by the hardness of the quartz, and the entire statue had to be made with stone tools, probably of diorite or dolerite. All the exposed surfaces of the sculpture were then worked over with an abrasive until a fine dull polish was achieved. Prob- ably a temple sculpture, a votive offer- ing(?). From Gebelein. Length 22.7 cm., height 12 cm. End of Predynastic Period to early First Dynasty. BIBLIOGRAPHY. Despite its importance as a great and rare work of art of the formative pe- riod of Egyptian history, this sculpture seems never to have been reproduced; the present il- lustrations are the first ever published. This lion was purchased by the English collector, the Reverend Randolph Berens, in Luxor about 1894 and is first mentioned, so far as I know, by Petrie, Naqada and Ballas (London, 1896), p. 46 (67), where he states that it was found with other pieces some years earlier at Gebelein. Berens placed the lion along with portions of his collection on loan in the South</page><page sequence="3">EGYPTIAN ART IN THE COLLECTION OF ALBERT GALLATIN 3 Kensington Museum, where it apparently was seen by Jean Capart, who mentions it and a larger lion in Primitive Art in Egypt (Phila- delphia, 1905), p. 182, identifying the material as alabaster and asserting that these lions were "found at Abydos." He gives no authority for this provenance, and, as he was apparently un- aware of Petrie's reference, his statement must have been based on a misunderstanding. The sculpture next appears in print in Catalogue of an Exhibition of Ancient Egyptian Art (Bur- lington Fine Arts Club, London, 1922), p. 109, C, where for the first time the object is well described although not illustrated. The editors of this catalogue, likewise unaware of earlier references to the sculpture, suggested Hieraconpolis as the provenance. The sculp- ture was then still in the Berens collection, from which it soon afterward passed into other hands, later reappearing in the Gallatin collec- tion without documentation. Mentioned in W. S. Smith, A History of Egyptian Sculpture and Painting in the Old Kingdom (Oxford, 1946), p. 10, without comment, as unrepro- duced piece. The other sculpture of a lion mentioned by Petrie as coming from Gebelein also entered the Berens collection and was briefly described by Capart as of black and white granite. Along with the Gallatin lion it was exhibited at the Burlington Fine Arts Club Exhibition in 1922 (p. 109, D), where it is described but not illus- trated. This is the last recorded location of the object since when its history has been un- known. In 1925 the Berlin Museum purchased in the European art market a now famous archaic lion in black and white stone which is almost certainly this missing specimen from the Berens collection. It was first published by Schifer but is more accessible in A. Scharff, Die Altertiumer der Vor- und Friihzeit A*gypteqs, Pt. II (Berlin, 1929), p. 62 (94). The Berlin lion answers the description given in the Bur- lington Catalogue of 1922 with a discrepancy of only 1.5 cm. in length, which, in the Eng- lish system, is only slightly over half an inch. The Berens collection, a vast multitude of objects of many periods, was dispersed at Sotheby's in London from late in 1922 into 1923. The numerous catalogues apparently do not record this lion or any of the finest archaic objects. They were doubtless sold privately to a dealer who, with the strange psychology of the art trade, concealed the source when he sold the sculpture to Berlin. If this identifica- tion is correct, the Berlin lion has a modern history and a provenance. While the Berlin and Gallatin sculptures have the same provenance, they are probably not exactly contemporary in date. The devel- oped style of the Berlin lion suggests that it is of slightly later date than the Gallatin lion. However, the obdurate medium of the latter clearly accounts for its simplicity of style and for the seemingly unique composition of the head resting on the legs, which is not repeated in later renderings of this animal. In our pres- ent state of knowledge of this period it is im- possible to estimate how widely in time the two pieces are separated. No. '. Pl. IV. Fragment of yellowish limestone relief. The scene represented in raised relief is one common in tombs of the Old Kingdom, the butchering of a bound bull to assure a supply of meat for the tomb owner. One servant cuts the bull's throat and has an extra knife inserted in his- skirt. The painted representation of blood spurting from the throat of the slaughtered animal is unusual. Only the hands of the other butcher cutting a leg of the bull are preserved. In the upper left in raised relief the traditional inscription, "the sacrificial animal, the bull." The elongated hands are typical of the best work of the later Old Kingdom. Colors: red, bodies of men, blood of bull and knives; green, traces on the reed-leaf of inscription. The unusually large scale of this relief makes it possible that it is royal work. Height 48.7 cm., width 50.5 cm. Sixth Dynasty. No. 7. Pls. VI-VII. Head of a king in hard yellow limestone wearing nemes headdress with scattered remains of bright blue pigment in its folds. Uraeus head</page><page sequence="4">4 JOURNAL OF NEAR EASTERN STUDIES missing, the body represented in wide S- folds in high relief running beyond crown of head. The back of the headdress ends in a braid of which only the root remains; this in turn probably connected with a rear pillar. The face is short and wide, of marked individuality and strength despite the obvious idealization. The mouth is formed with full lips, deeply depressed at each end and outlined with a thin roll of flesh (particularly noticeable on the upper lip). The spoonlike treatment of the ears without interior detail is unusual but, strangely, is duplicated in Cairo 71586, an unpublished, archaic, limestone statue of a woman. Height 17.8 cm. This head is without close parallel in royal sculpture in the round and conse- quently is not easily identified. It suggests the work of the Twelfth Dynasty, while clearly not identifiable with any known head of that Dynasty, and thus must have been produced shortly before or after that time. Both the Eleventh and the Thir- teenth dynasties are poorly represented in surviving royal sculptures, but the broad structure of the face, seemingly a continu- ation of an Old Kingdom type found again in some portraits of the first two kings of the Twelfth Dynasty, favors a lo- cation late in the Eleventh Dynasty, with Mentuhotep III (Se-cankh-karec) as the most probable subject. The technique of outlining the entire mouth with a roll of flesh is characteristic of reliefs of the Eleventh Dynasty and is found occasion- ally' on those of the early Twelfth Dynas- ty. The narrow eyes of the Gallatin head are apparently characteristic of portraits of Mentuhotep III, for they occur on the Brooklyn relief referred to in footnote 1. The material, hard yellow limestone, is a typical Middle Kingdom medium; the form of the headdress and the uraeus are consistent with an Eleventh Dynasty at- tribution. Other contemporary reliefs from Ar- mant, Dendera, and T6d furnish almost the sole additional comparative material. They show (in profile) the same concept of royal portraiture as this head, particularly in the convention of the depression at the corner of the mouth. The great quality of these reliefs infers the existence of com- parable royal sculptures in the round, but, with the exception of the Gallatin head, only rough statues in sandstone have been identified from the late Eleventh Dynas- ty. The sole detail not certainly typical of the later Eleventh Dynasty is the even spacing of the folds of the nemes head- dress. I know of no examples in the round of this period for comparison, but all three reliefs quoted in footnote 1 show, in pro- file, a somewhat similar treatment. (The illustrations published here are misleading in suggesting that the face is soft; actually it is almost harsh. In his Tell el Amarna, the excavation report of his brief season at that site, Petrie inex- plicably published only a small minority of his rich finds. His field records, if ever they existed, have disappeared, and it is probably forever impossible to establish a complete register of the objects he discovered at Amar- na. For the exhibition in London held at Ox- ford Mansions September 19-October 15, 1892, Petrie issued a small and now rare pam- phlet, Catalogue of Antiquities from Tel el Amarna, which in a very few instances men- tions objects not listed in his excavation re- port. Possibly the Gallatin relief is the one de- scribed on page 15 of this pamphlet as "sculp- tor's trial piece of Khuenaten's head"-the lo- cation implies a relatively large relief. Of the various contemporary accounts of the exhibi- tion which I have been able to trace, the only one of any value, and that a very limited one, is The Illustrated London News, Vol. CI (Sept. 24, 1892), p. 387, drawings p. 392. The very considerable number of objects entering the Amherst collection from this exhibition, al- though of great importance, failed to attract the interest of scholars, almost none of them being published during the next three decades. Nor was the collection represented in the first important English exhibition of Egyptian art, assembled by the Burlington Fine Arts Club in 1895. The first and only complete record of this material ever to appear was in the sale catalogue issued by Sotheby. In 1944 the late Professor Percy E. Newberry told me that Howard Carter had compiled this catalogue. Carter was one of Petrie's staff at the excava- tion of Amarna, and so had a firsthand knowl- edge of the material from the excavation. Head of a crown prince or a high priest of Ptah of Memphis in fine-grained black granite. The close-fitting wig, of exceptionally de- tailed workmanship, is formed of conven- tionalized representations of curls spring- ing from a plain oval area on the crown of the head, graduated in size as they curve downward. The pronounced roundness of the wig and head recalls the heads of Taharka; the detailed precision of the wig and in particular the rounded form of the small flap hanging in front of the ear, ap- parently unknown before this period, point to the same date. This type of wig with rounded flaps near the ears is fre- quent in neo-Memphite work of the Thir- tieth Dynasty, small sculptures and sculp- tors' models of late but uncertain date, but the use of this rounded flap is par- ticularly characteristic of portraits of Taharka. The vigor of the Gallatin por- trait, with its strong suggestion of an in- fluence of the late Eighteenth Dynasty to the early Nineteenth Dynasty, and its similarity in form to the round heads of the Twenty-fifth Dynasty suggest that it is a product of the great Theban school of archaizing portrait sculptors late in the Twenty-fifth Dynasty.
