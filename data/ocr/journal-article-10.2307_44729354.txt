ABSTRACT Locations of key body segments of Hybrid III dummies used in FMVSS 208 compliance tests and NCAP tests were measured and subjected to statistical analysis. Mean clearance dimensions and their standard deviations for selected body segments of driver and passenger occupants with respect to selected vehicle surfaces were determined for several classes of vehicles. These occupant locations were then investigated for correlation with impact responses measured in crash tests and by using a three dimensional human-dummy mathematical model in comparable settings. Based on these data, the importance of some of the clearance dimensions between the dummy and the vehicle surfaces was determined. The study also compares observed Hybrid III dummy positions within selected vehicles with real world occupant positions reported in published literature. INTRODUCTION Considerable attention has been given in vehicle safety standards and in vehicle design to the location of the dummy or occup? it within the vehicle, because the location is considered to be an í ¡ portant factor in whether the vehicle will pass or fail occupant protection requirements in compliance crash tests. The test procedure calls for seating the 50th percentile male dummy in a seat set at mid-track adjustment. Several studies in the past have attempted to determine how actual drivers sit in cars. In this process driver anthropometries were compared for selected seat positions [1][2][3][4]*. Most of these studies, although adding to a better understanding, did not readily provide more insight, because the experiments were done in the laboratory and the selection of seat locations were not necessarily representative of those that the real world occupant population would choose under real-world driving conditions. Other researchers have attempted to obtain some occupant-to-vehicle measurements for persons seated only in the driver's position. Such studies, however, did not lead to firm conclusions because in most instances either the sample size was too small or the assessment techniques could not yield sufficient and definitive information about either the occupant or the position of the seat within the vehicle. In the past, safety belts were the primary means to restrain the occupant during a crash. To increase their effectiveness, belt retractors and various automatic preload mechanisms were employed, which made the occupant's location in the vehicle less critical. In contrast, introduction of the air bag appears to have brought the occupant location sensitivity issue again into the foreground. For example, the seat's fore-and-aft location can significantly affect the injury outcome of an unrestrained occupant, depending upon whether the occupant interacts with a deployed or deploying air bag and/or impacts other vehicle surfaces. While there are many other factors that could affect the occupant injury outcome, most investigators agree, that seat location is one of the more important factors to consider. This study aims to examine 1) the extent of variability of occupant clearance dimensions in currently manufactured vehicles when the Hybrid III dummy is placed in driver and passenger seating positions in accordance with NCAP and FMVSS No. 208 test procedures and 2) to explore if occupant clearance dimensions differ among several classes of vehicle sizes. The findings are compared with real world occupant clearance data reported by other researchers. The study also aims to explore if a relationship exists between dummy responses and clearance dimensions. This is done through statistical analysis of the data used in this study and through a computer model analysis created by the Ford Motor Company. TREATMENT OF ERRORS IN THE RECORDED TEST DATA In the initial review of the data dealing with orientations of vehicle components and the dummy to vehicle clearance dimensions, some inconsistencies, misplaced entries and omissions were found. Errors that could be explained and corrected from companion occupant data were adjusted. Others, that were found to be beyond reasonable means of resurrection, such as measurements that could not be matched or were clearly in error, were removed from the tables. DATA SEGMENTATION Because LTVs and PCs provide considerably different occupant to vehicle interior spatial relationships (see Figure 1), the combined FMVSS 208 and NCAP occupant clearance dimension and occupant compartment geometry files were partitioned into passenger cars (PCs) and LTVs sets and driver and passenger subsets (Tables 1.1 thru 2.2). The overall driver-passenger dimensional clearance data provide a glimpse of how the occupant relates to vehicle interior surfaces for this fleet of PCs and LTVs. However, within each of these vehicle groups there is a large variation in vehicle sizes. The expectation would be that occupants are provided more space in larger size than in smaller size vehicles. Therefore, the PC group was subdivided by wheelbase into small (up to 102 inches), mid-size (102 inches to 108 inches) and large (greater than 108 inches), and the LTVs were subdivided into mid-size( up to 107 inches) and large (greater than 107 inches) groups. The subdivisions of LTVs had to be limited to two wheelbase groups because of the relatively small number of vehicles in the data bank. These groupings were based on visual inspection of how vehicle sizes appear to cluster for the formation of three fairly uniform sets of vehicles in terms of numbers and weight dispersions. Similar logic was used to select the two wheelbase groups for LTVs. Of further interest was the question on how domestic and import vehicle manufacturers accommodate occupants within the vehicle's interior. Inasmuch as the LTV data set was too small to subdivide by import or domestic manufacturer, this subdivision was limited to passenger cars. The mean values of the clearance dimensions for these two groups of data are displayed in Table 4. METHODOLOGY The aims of this study were to determine how the placement of the dummy in mid -track seating position: (1) affects the dispersions of the dummy's head, chest and knee clearance dimensions within the vehicle, (2) relates to real world occupant self-positioning within the vehicle, and (3) may affect impact responses of the dummy occupant within the measured placement variables. To facilitate this study, the variables were classified as being of primary, secondary or tertiary importance. The raw data from the FMVSS 208 compliance test and NCAP test reports were compiled, audited and reviewed for consistency. Subsequently, the data were subjected to statistical analysis, which resulted in the calculation of the mean and standard deviations for each set and subset the data. Initially, it was not clear whether the measurements should be subdivided into wheelbase or vehicle weight sets. Upon review of dummy impact response correlations, it was concluded that vehicle segmentation by wheelbase was a more consistent and relevant parameter. RESULTS Figure 1 was constructed to show that PCs and LTVs have different component and occupant segment orientations. For ex an j a , the mean windshield rake angle (WA) for PCS is 28 degrees and for LTVs - 36 degrees. The mean steering wheel angle (SWA) is 67 degrees for PCS and 61 degrees for LTVs; The mean seat back angles (SA) is 23 degrees for PCS, and 20 degrees for LTVs. The mean femur inclination (PA) is 23 degrees in PCS and 2 1 degrees in LTVs 2 1 , and the respective mean tibia angles (TA) are 35 degrees and 42 degrees. Vehicle component orientations to the dummy's femur and tibia in each wheelbase group are shown in Figures 2 through 4. Figure 2 compares, in bar chart form, the mean values and variances of the five parameters (WA, SWA, SA, PA and TA) by wheelbase groups for PCs. The data indicate very little dispersion in the orientation of vehicle components within the three wheelbase groups. Figure 3 shows that the interior layout for domestic PCs differs somewhat from that of imported vehicles, but differences between the means within each population are insignificant. However, in the overall, import vehicles have slightly steeper windshield and seat-back angles and somewhat less vertical steering wheel settings. Comparison of LTVs by wheelbase groups, in Figure 4, shows very little difference within each assessed parameter, indicating surprising uniformity between large and small LTVs. Figures 5 through 7 compare the head, chest and knee eleara, ;.0, dimensions with selected vehicle interior surfaces for drivers and passengers of PCs and LTVs. In all instances, clearance in PCs between the occupant and the vehicle's internal surfaces increase as the vehicle wheelbase increases from the small to the mid-size wheelbase groups. For example, the HW on the driver increases from 447 mm to 520 mm, the CS from 257 mm to 276 mm, the KDL from 142 mm to 158 mm, and the KDR from 141mm to 147 mm. Except for HW, which increases further by 35 mm, all other dimensions remain either the same or show reduced values as the wheelbase increases from the mid-size to the large wheelbase group. Similarly, all dimensions in LTVs increase as a function of wheelbase length except for the steering wheel to driver chest dimension which remains nearly the same. Figure 8 indicates that there is virtually no difference between domestic and imported vehicles in driver and passenger HH dimensions for the small wheelbase PCs. On the other hand, both driver and passenger HH dimensions are consistently larger for mid-size and large imported PCs than for domestic PCs. As shown in Figure 9, except for the driver in the mid-size PC group, all other imported PCs have lower HW values. Surprisingly, the largest differences in HW are in mid-sized PCs. The mean HW for the PC driver in domestic vehicles is 497 mm compared to 549 mm in imported vehicles. Comparable values for the passenger are 490 mm and 547 mm respectively. Figure 10 indicates that chest to steering wheel and chest to dash dimensions are larger in imported vehicles with the largest difference (305 mm vs. 254 mm) occurring in the mid-sized wheelbase group. Similarly, imported vehicles exhibit larger chest to dash dimensions than domestic vehicles (552 mm vs. 479 mm for mid- size, and 533 mm vs. 441 mm for large PCs). Figure 1 1 shows the nose to top of the steering wheel (NR) and abdomen to bottom of the steering wheel rim (RA) dimensions being consistently larger in imported vehicles. Figures 12 and 13 indicate that corresponding right and left knee to knee cushion distances are similar for respective driver and passenger positions within each of the wheelbase groups, but the imports show consistently higher values: a minimum difference of 3 mm on small (145 mm vs. 142 mm) and a max. of 55 mm on large PCs (185mm vs. 130mm). DISCUSSION Parkin et al. [1] and Husher et al. [2] provided extensive reviews of research done to date on relationships of clearance dimensions between seated real world occupants and dummies in comparable vehicle settings. They have shown that more work needs to be done to determine the location of the seat that real world occupants select when driving or riding their vehicles. Both authors suggest that the current seating procedure of the dummies in FMVSS 208 neither replicate the seating position of the real world occupant nor yield consistent body segment to vehicle interior surfaces' dimensional relationships. However, it should be taken into account that these studies had to deal with a large number of measurements, which were not only difficult to make but nearly impossible to control for consistency. Therefore, the conclusions in the papers should be viewed with reservation. Parkin et al. concluded that the positions specified for dummies appear to be markedly different than those selected by humans. For example, he observes that the mid-size dummy's head is some 4.7 cm. behind where the 50th percentile real world occupant's would be. In contrast, tibia inclination appears to be highly variable among occupants in the same seated position, but reasonably good uniformity is noted between drivers and passengers within a wheelbase group. COMPARISON BETWEEN SELF-SEATED REAL WORLD OCCUPANTS AND SEATED HYBRID III DUMMIES IN THE FORD TAURUS The data from FMVSS 208 compliance tests and NCAP tests provide some insight on where the dummy occupant is in relationship to critical vehicle interior surfaces. However, the data cannot tell us where real world occupants sit in vehicles either as drivers or as passengers. To compare the dummy's seated position with that of a real- world occupant, it was necessary to find a common vehicle for which suitable dummy and volunteer data were available. NHTSA's test files contained data on a 1994 Ford Taurus that was tested with the Hybrid III dummy that could be related to Husher's volunteer driver data in a 1988 Ford Taurus. Inasmuch as Husher's driver occupants had highly diverse anthropometry, it was necessary to complement his findings with additional volunteer tests. Such tests were done with 12 adult male volunteers, all licensed drivers, who were asked to seat themselves in the driver and passenger positions of a 1991 Ford Taurus (it is noted that the 1988 and 1991 Ford Taurus models had the same interior layout; the 1 994 Taurus had minor interior modifications, but without substantially affecting the HW and the CS and CD dimensions). The test subjects were asked several times to adjust the seat and the seat back to a position that they would select while driving or riding as front seat occupants. Seat location with respect to its mid-track point and other dimensions relating the test subject to vehicle components were recorded as shown in Table A3. The test population was a randomly selected sample of a group of males who deviated no more than +/-7.6 cm from the desired 175 cm standing mean height. Table 5 lists the average HW, CS, and CD clearance dimensions (see Figure 1 for reference to the dimensions) from the Husher study, the 1 2 volunteers in this study, the dummy clearance data from the 1994 Ford Taurus, and comparable dimensions from the mid-size PC group. These data sets permit a comparison between clearance dimensions of seated real world mid-size occupants and the mid-size Hybrid III dummy that is seated at the midtrack seat location. Based on the seat track position selection by the 12 volunteers seated in the Ford Taurus, it appears that adjustment of the seat location 32 mm to 36 mm rearward from the mid-track setting for vehicle impact tests would bring the dummy's head and thorax considerably closer to the position recommended by Husher. These observations do not agree with the findings of Parkin et al who claim that the real world occupant's head is forward of the dummy's head. Though Parkin's head position of real world occupants is different than noted in this study, all researchers agree that the mid-track seat position is not necessarily the location that real world front seat occupant selects. During the testing of the volunteers it was noted that people tend to adjust and orient themselves in the seat using several criteria. It appears that head to windshield clearance is a far less important initial consideration than, for example, visibility, comfortable relationship to steering controls and the reach of vehicle control pedals in the case of drivers or comfortably resting feet on the floor or toeboard in the case of passengers. While this study will not resolve immediately the dummy position question within the vehicle, it may encourage others to study occupant to vehicle spatial relationships in different vehicle models and types of vehicles. This study showed that setting the dummy in mid-track seat position results in some clearance variations between the dummy and interior surfaces not only between vehicles in different wheelbase groups, but also within the same wheelbase group. It appears that no matter what care is employed to set the dummy within the vehicle, fairly large differences in dummy to vehicle clearance dimensions will exist because of particular orientation and layout of interior surfaces within different vehicle makes. Ergonomicists should develop studies on how people select seat adjustnents as they prepare for driving or riding in the vehicle. Such iiif irmation then could be used to establish more realistic seat adjustment and seating procedures that would better relate the location of the dummy occupant to that selected by the real world occupant. IMPORTANCE OF OCCUPANT POSITION IN VEHICLE CRASH TESTING FMVSS 208 and NCAP test procedures require great care and precision when setting the dummies in the vehicle for crash tests. In view of relatively large dispersions of some of the clearance dimensions observed in these tests, it needs to be determined if set up precision is really needed and what the relative importance of each of the clearance parameters is. Past experience suggests that for those test and occupant restraint conditions in which the dummy's body segments do not contact any vehicle surface, the clearance dimensions may be of little, if any, importance. In contrast, for those occupants whose body segments barely graze or significantly hit a vehicle surface, clearance dimensions may be of great importance. For unrestrained dummies, clearance dimensions are usually of significant importance as they may determine whether the occupant will impact either a fully deployed air bag or one that is still deploying. To determine the importance of occupant spacing, a statistical regression analysis was done using the NCAP and the FMVSS 208 compliance test dummy response values and clearance dimensions invehióles equipped with both driver and passenger airbags and 3 -point belt restraint systems (numbers of vehicles with other types of restraint combinations were too few for analysis). In the analysis, we were looking for the correlation, if any, between dummy responses and certain key dummy to vehicle clearance dimensions and the vehicle wheelbase and mass parameters. The analysis was performed employing the Statistical Analysis System (SAS) regression procedure using the backwards elimination technique. The process begins by calculating statistics for a model including all of the independent variables. Then the variables are deleted from the model one by one until all the variables remaining in the model produce F statistics lower than a specified SLSTAY level of significance (0.1 - the program default for the first model and 0.2 for the second). Furthermore* to ensure a noted correlation in models with questionable sample sizes the SLSTAY level was established at 0.1. At each step of the process, variables showing the smallest contribution to the model were deleted and the remaining variables were incorporated into the model. Even more surprising was lack of correlation between the chest g response and the CS and CD dimensions, regardless of the restraints types used to contain the occupant. It appears that belts and/or air bag restraints may play a more significant role in determining the chest response of the dummy than subject clearance dimensions. As would be expected, there is little, if any, correlation between the femur loads and knee clearance dimensions for belted occupants. This may be because belt restraints limit the engagement between the knees and the knee cushions. Similarly, there was a poor correlation between the femur loads and knee clearance dimensions for unrestrained air bag protected drivers. No correlation was demonstrated for the unrestrained air bag protected passenger, because the data file lacked a sufficient number of cases for such an assessment. Overall, the data tends to show that vehicle characteristics play a larger role than occupant clearance dimensions. For airbag alone or in conjunction with belt restraints, the driver and passenger dummies seated at mid-track position were observed to have impact response values less dependent upon clearance dimensions and more on other vehicle parameters (e.g. weight, wheelbase, crash pulse, and airbag/belt conditions). Furthermore, it is possible that the importance of clearance dimensions are intertwined with other vehicle characteristic that were not considered in this analysis. It is hoped that this paper will stimulate further research into the importance of clearance dimensions, particularly in the air bag impact environment. COMPUTER-BASED SENSITIVITY STUDIES OF DUMMY RESPONSES AS FUNCTION OF CLEARANCE DIMENSIONS BETWEEN THE DUMMY AND THE VEHICLE'S INTERIOR SURFACES To further study the extent of position variations on impact responses of occupants restrained by belts and air bags or air bags alone, comparable response data were generated by Ford Motor Company using a MADYM03D (version 5.1) math model of a generic large car. The model also included a rigid link approximation of the Hybrid III 50th percentile anthropomorphic dummy in the driver position impacting a finite element representation of an airbag. In the previous section it was concluded that the effects of clearance dimensions were less influential than the vehicle crush characteristics (i.e. crash pulse). Therefore, the model excercises in this portion of the study considered the effects of the crash pulse in conjunction with variations in the dummy's seating position. The cabin environment was modeled with contact planes and ellipsoids. It was in this portion of the model development that initial position of the dummy became pertinent. A means to automatically place and orient the header, windshield, steering wheel, and knee blocker with respect to the dummy model was developed using several key occupant-environment dimensions (HH, HW, CS, SCA, WA and KD), from the FMVSS No. 208 compliance and NCAP test data. The dummy was located at a baseline set up at the mean and plus and minus three standard deviation for the HW, CS and KD clearance dimensions, while holding the SCA, WA, HZ (cabin geometry) constant at their mean values. For the study of variations in seating positions, three distinct crash pulses were used to simulate and produce impact response values. Differences in crash pulse characteristics were produced by varying the magnitude of the peak acceleration by +/-s 10% and adjusting the duration of the pulse to ensure an equal area under the deacceleration curve (shown in Table 7). The modeling exercise included two different restraint types: 30 mph unbelted with airbags and 35 mph belted with airbags representative of the respective FMVSS 208 compliance and NCAP test conditions. Comparisons of vehicle interior surface and seated occupant leg orientations show considerable differences between passenger cars and LTVs. The differences are largest in windshield rake angle, steering wheel inclination and tibia inclinations. The differences were smallest in seat back angle and femur inclination. 2. Clearance dimensions between the occupant and the vehicle interior surfaces are largest in the mid-size passenger car group, except for the head to windshield dimension, which is larger in large size vehicles. Increments as the vehicle wheelbase increases are largest in the head to windshield dimension, considerably lower ir the chest to steering wheel dimension and lowest in knee to kntń, o ishion dimension. 3. Clearance dimensions in LTVs increase as the vehicle base increases, except for the chest to steering wheel dimension, which remains nearly the same. 4. Except for the H W dimension in small and large size vehicle groups, all other dimensions are larger in imported vehicles than in domestic vehicles. 5. Driver and passenger clearance dimensions such as the H W and right and left knee to knee cushion are substantially similar in any given vehicle. 6. This limited scope study indicates that real world occupants select a seat location somewhat rearward from the mid-track position setting which is currently used to locate the dummy in FMVSS 208 and NCAP crash tests. 7. Regression analysis of belt-airbag restrained driver dummy HIC against vehicle weight, wheelbase length, and dummy to vehicle clearance dimensions indicates, for frontal impacts, reasonably strong correlation with vehicle wheelbase, vehicle weight i' 1 the head to header dimension. In contrast, the HICs of the passenger dummy shows only weak correlation with the wheelbase and the head to windshield dimension. There is virtually no correlation between the chest g and femur responses with any of the clearance dimensions examined. 8. Regression analysis of airbag protected driver dummy and passenger dummy occupant responses against the vehicle's wheel base and mass parameters and the dummy to vehicle clearance dimensions, indicate little correlation, except for the driver femurs, which show a reasonably good correlation with the knee clearance dimensions. 9. Analysis of the mathematical vehicle-dummy model crash data shows that the vehicle crash pulse is the predominant factor controlling the dummy's impact responses. RECOMMENDATIONS 1 . More extensive research studies should be done to determine real world occupant seat location selection in various size vehicles. 2. To optimize occupant protection in the crash environment, placement of test dummies should be considered in seating positions that are based on selections by real world occupants. REFERENCES 1 . S.
