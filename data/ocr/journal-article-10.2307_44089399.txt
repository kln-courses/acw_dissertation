Arnaud Serandour, Avant-propos, pp. 1-19 : « Les pages qui suivent présentent les actes de la cinquième table ronde tenue par TURA 1062 du CNRS, 4 Etudes sémitiques ', consacrée à l'histoire des représentations de l'espace proche-oriental dans l'Antiquité. Des listes de toponymes qui émaillent les documents rédigés en écriture cunéiforme à l'aube des temps historiques à la naissance de la géographie et de la cartographie en Asie Mineure et aux développements ultérieurs des conceptions de l'Orient dans l'Occident comme dans l'Orient chrétiens des premiers siècles de notre ère, les sources étudiées par les contributions recueillies dans ce volume illustrent la richesse et la variété des apports de l'Orient antique à notre connaissance des rapports de l'homme à son environnement spatial. L'ensemble des communications couvre un champ d'étude aussi vaste que fertile, relatif à la toponymie, à la topographie, à la géographie physique, économique, administrative et humaine, relevant de divers registres de représentations tant poétiques, mythologiques ou plus généralement traditionnelles que physiques et visuelles, à vocation déjà scientifique. Le dossier ainsi constitué est de nature à apporter une contribution non seulement à la géographie historique ainsi qu'à l'histoire des représentations géographiques, mais aussi à celle des mentalités et des cultures anciennes» (p. 11). Archaeology. - When, as inquisitive tourist or professional archaeologist, one is visiting a site for the first time one needs a succinct yet informative description of what to see and what is significant. The problem with many guide books is that the essential information is too often obscured by unnecessary verbiage. In Ross Burns' guide to Syria 1 we have an example of the genre which can be wholeheartedly recommended. The book's dimensions are suited more to the bookshelf than the pocket but the accessibility of the information contained therein more than compensates for the inconvenience. A short foreword explaining Burns' criteria for dating and the transliter- ation of proper nouns from their original languages is followed by a list of abbreviations used in the text and a splendidly graphic key to the maps. The first chapter gives a thorough account of the history of Syria pertinent to the archaeological sites and Chapter 2 takes the form of a discussion of the architectural forms to be found there. The main body of the work consists of a gazetteer of the sites and it is here where the guide scores over its few competitors. The sites are presented in alphabetical order, regardless of location or period. After the site name there is a window giving in note form the variant names of the site, its archaeological period, a star rating of its importance, in which suggested itinerary it is to be found, and a map reference. The star rating is given on the basis of spectacular remains rather than archaeological importance - Ebla (Tell Mardikh) gets one star, for example - but this aid to the tourist shouldn't affect its use by the more serious archaeologist. Another window follows with a description of the location and practical details on how to get to the site. Then there is often a quotation pertinent to the site from some writer in antiquity, a whimsical but interesting device which gives a certain immediacy to the visit. The body of the text begins with a section on the history followed by instructions on how to explore the site and what to look out for. The reviewer has used this guide extensively in Syria and never found it to be lacking in any important respect. It is far superior to any of its rivals for the traveller's attentions. The volume on Judaean Pillar Figurines 2 is an excellent example of the series. Throughout the Twentieth Century many studies of these figurines have been undertaken but a work was badly needed which co-ordinated the findings and produced some new ideas of its own. Raz Kletter has taken on this prodigious task - there are almost 1 000 figurines plus comparisons from Transjordan (139 figurines) and countless examples from other areas in Israel. Kletter begins his study with an examination of the historical and archaeological background to the figurines. He limits his study to figurines from the date of Sennacherib's 701 B.C. campains in Israel to the fall of Jerusalem in 586 B.C. and looks at the possibility of dividing this period into two phases but decides that at present the evidence is insufficient to suggest a reliable partitioning. The received wisdom concerning these artefacts is examined, what their function was, whether they are toys, magical artefacts, cultic objects related to goddesses or statuettes of mortal human beings. Their relationship to the Asherah of the Bible is discussed and Kletter points out that there is, in fact, no direct Biblical textual link with the figurines despite decades of identification by other archaeologists and exegetes. Nevertheless he concludes that they were a popular representation of the Asherah which was situated in the temple and as such were therefore part of the Yahwistic religion of ancient Israel. Another generally accepted idea, that they were deliberately broken in some kind of sympathetic magic ritual, is also challenged. Kletter has found, in an extremely simple yet effective study, that the damage done to figurines can be accounted for by accidental breakage and a theory of deliberate mutilation is not necessary to explain the phenomenon. There are chapters on the figurines' typology, chronology, distribution patterns and aspects of manufacture. Much of this is the collation of old material into manageable data but the most original part of the work is the study of archaeological context. Of the thousands of figurines examined, only 255 have contextual data and some of this is partial or obscure. Mostly the objects were discovered in graves, pools, caves, pits and cisterns so the contexts indicate disposal patterns rather than use patterns. Only about twelve figurines were found in a domestic context and they were never found in groups in this context which casts great doubt on the idea that these objects were the biblical tefillin or that they were used in some kind of popular domestic religious cult scenario. As one would expect in this kind of work, more than two thirds of the book are taken up with tables, data bases and detailed catalogues of the vast corpus of figurines. In an important new study 3, also published by Tempus Reparatum, we have the results of an underwater survey which gives a much more comprehensive picture of maritime activity at the port of Dor than is possible with any single sunken vessel. The study of the natural harbour there is linked with three land-based archaeological projects 4, the subjects of which benefitted greatly in antiquity from the maritime activity represented by the harbour. The authors to their credit have used the results of these land-based projects to interpret what was found under the sea. This is a survey and as such carries with it all the benefits and drawbacks of the methodology, that is to say a comprehensive overall picture is obtained but there is little in-depth excavation work or chronolog- ical context. Owing to the peculiar topography of the site, the areas of the sea bed under investigation were self-excavating or rather were uncovered by storms and currents moving the sand around. What was discovered was therefore rather hit and miss - the divers would inspect known wreck sites after storms to see what had been revealed so there was no systematic excavation as such. Most of the site lies in very shallow water resulting in the further complication for the archaeologist of treasure-hunting by amateur divers, a popular pursuit in Israel. Given the unorthodox excavation methods, the results of the survey are suprisingly informative and comprehensive. Dor was a natural anchorage and as such needed little maintenance and not much in the way of built structures on land. This meant that it remained in constant use throughout the long period of maritime activity in the region. The harbour itself was a safe haven but sailing into it was often a hazardous experience. Hence the above average number of wrecks found in the vicinity. As one would expect, stone anchors are one of the most common artefacts found and a chapter is devoted to their study, where they have been catalogued and classified. This method of presentation has been used for all the different groups and types of artefact, with separate chapters on millstones and stone objects, metal artefacts, amphorae and coarse pottery and the wrecked ships themselves. The final chapter draws some conclusions concerning the type of trade carried out at the Dor harbour and its relationship to other ports in the area, notably the more striking yet less successful harbour of Caesarea.
