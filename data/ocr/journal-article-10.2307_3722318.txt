The massive contributions of these scholars (and others) to the burgeoning series of hymnals during the first few decades of the Reformation not only had their roots in the relatively esoteric world of Renaissance scholarship (especially the study of Greek and Hebrew poetic texts) but, in part, stemmed from the popular literary tradition of the German peasant, a tradition fostered by the Germanophilic aspect of Reformation politics. Johannes Agricola of Eisleben, one of the authors whose works clearly combine the popular and classical aspects of the Reformation, has never been the subject of a literary analysis of his contributions to the Protestant hymnal. Agricola's biography reveals some of the forces which served to mould his literary gifts. He was born in the same small town, Eisleben, as Luther. After essentially the same late medieval education as his mentor, Agricola spent some time in Braun- schweig as a tutor before accompanying his charges to Wittenberg. There he enrolled in the philosophical faculty but soon switched to the theological faculty under Luther's influence. When Melanchthon accepted the chair of Greek and Hebrew, Agricola and Luther studied Hebrew together. Luther's great esteem for Agricola can be seen in the fact that he not only sponsored his candidacy for the master of arts degree but that he chose him as his academic assistant. It was in fact Agricola who was commissioned by Luther physically to throw the papal Bull on to the pyre. Agricola's academic career reached its pinnacle when he was made Dean of the faculty of theology. Soon thereafter he left Wittenberg to follow Luther's policy of popular christian education and found a model Protestant school in Eisleben. For this purpose he composed religious texts as well as a detailed educa- tional plan for the school. In Eisleben he began a folkloric project which placed him in the forefront of the authors of the Reformation, his collection of proverbs.l Published in three collections between 1529 and 1548, these popular literary works established Agricola as one of the most widely known writers of the Reformation. Agricola's literary accomplishments also included one of the most effective political dramas of the Reformation, the Tragedia Johannis Huss, as well as one of the earliest and most interesting Terence adaptations, a translation of the Andria.2 Following his self-imposed exile from university life in Eisleben Agricola returned to Wittenberg where he began, as a temporary substitute for Luther, to preach the doctrine later known as antinomianism. Basically Agricola altered the Lutheran view of the Law. Basing his thesis on Luke xxIv.47 ('. .. et praedicari in nomine ejus paenitentiana et remissionem peccatorum in omnes gentes, incipientibus ab Jerosolyma') he viewed the Law as having been abnegated by the coming of Christ and thus saw the Christian duty to repentance as a result of the inner acceptance of the teachings of Christ. Repentance is, therefore, the result of the revelation of Christ rather than the preaching of the Law. After a series of ever worsening clashes with Luther, concerning this alteration of Protestant dogma, Agricola fled to Berlin where he was appointed General Superintendent of the Mark. A congregational song has therefore been created from a command. The joyful singing of praises to the Lord is underlined by the emphasis on the 'Alleluia', now found at the beginning of the hymn. The congregational motif is carried through the first stanza so that the praise of God springs from the innermost heart of all, cleansing the sins of man through the grace of God. Grace is felt to be the rightful due of mankind in light of Agricola's developing antinomianism, as reflected in his introduction to Simon Hafernitz aus Kloster- mansfeld's Job commentary: '[Gott] ... selt sich als wolt er vns ewig in die hell vnd von seim angesicht verstossen'.3 God only acts as if he would cast man from Him, deny him His grace; in reality, however, His grace is assured by His all encompassing goodness. Thus Agricola departs on his own special understanding of misericordia, a key term not only in Luther's translation of the Bible but also in all the pre-Reformation translations of the psalms. Sch6ndorf records, under the terms misereri and misericordia, that the greatest number of psalm translations have rendered this word as Barmherzigkeit rather than Gnade. He further notes that 'Luther, der sowohl gnade wie barmhertzickeyt kennt, bevorzug gzite'.4 Agricola has purposefully chosen the term Gnade, a term which will become one of the central concepts in his hymns. The rationale for the praise of God found in the psalm, God's mercy and truth, is adapted in the initial verse of the hymn. God's mercy evidences itself in his absolution of our sins. Thus the general claim for God's grace is rooted in the specific act of the cleansing of man's sins. The third verse centres upon the key term Gnade while adding the concept of veritas from the psalm. Just as Agricola ignored the reading of Guete in Luther's translation of the psalm, so he adopts the original Lutheran translation of veritas as trewe in his use of the terms vertrawen and trewlich. Veritas is not warheit as Luther later translates this term in the I53 and 1545 Psalter. Trewe carries with it the con- cept of dependence, parallel to Gnade. Both signify an active interrelationship between God and mankind. As in the second verse, it is this dynamic attribute which generates the forces for redemption. Redemption is assumed as a matter of course because of the existence of a merciful God, demanding only trewe as the price of his mercy. The final verse returns to the motif of praise introduced in the initial stanza. But here the entire Trinity appears rather than the altered image of the God of the Old Testament. God, the Son, and the Holy Ghost are added to the image of God, the Father, in order to reinforce the change in perspective won in the first three stanzas. Agricola has employed two separate levels of speech. The first is the direct exchange between the supplicant and God. Included in the supplicant's appeal, however, is a further level of speech, the recapitulation of the words of those striving against God as well as those promises made by God to man. The adaption of the anthropomorphic God of the Old Testament whose answers counter the appeal of the supplicant is not as puzzling as the role which this variant on the medieval form of the debate poem2 forces on the congregation. The identity of the supplicant is masked at the onset of the hymn. The voice of the congregation and the supplicant are one, raised to appeal to the mercy of God for intercession in the strife of this world. Only in the sixth verse does the identity of the supplicant become evident: 'Du bist meyn son... Gborn hab ich dich dardurch den geyst / dein herlickeit ist anbrochen / Nach dem tod allermeist / heiit ist durch dich gerochen.' This description is that of Christ who has been attempting to intercede with his heavenly father in the earlier stanzas. Thus the congregation and Christ become one, interceding directly with God. This alteration of the debate motif is an extremely effective manner of presenting the new role of the congregation as the centre of religious life. The alteration of the original structure of the hymn into a more folkloric form does not, however, alter the basic intent of the original. The revolt of the non- believers against the power of God remains the basic theme. Agricola has amplified this by the addition of all the heretics and heathens, 'juden vnd heyden', which begins the introduction of the New Testament motif. The original begins with the phrase 'Quare turbabuntur gentes et tribus meditabuntur inania', a phrase which Agricola has the narrator illustrate through his use of the direct speech of the heathens, 'Mord / wiirg tod / wiirg tod / schreyen sie . . .'. The heathens are now those who oppose Christ. The supplicant quotes God's promise of redemption in basically the same manner as the psalm: 'Dominus dixit ad me filius meus es tu ego hodie genui te . . .'. It was this promise 'super Sion montem sanctum suum' which is echoed in God's reply in the hymn. God appoints Christ as the judge of those who sacrificed him. If the hymn was written in the period of the late I52os as may be implied by the use of the earliest proverbs as well as the veiled topical references, then Agricola could well have been the author. Agricola's attack on the Emperor may stem from his visit to the Reichstag at Speier in 1529. The passionate nature of the hymn seems parallel to the earliest proverbs which he was also writing at this time. His return to the Protestant-controlled areas of Germany may have tempered his views and he may have suppressed this text or it may have appeared in a form not preserved in any collection. The publication (or republication) of this text in 1546 is much more understandable. After Agricola's break with Luther a series of relatively mild public attacks on Agricola appeared in Wittenberg, the harshest being Luther's Wider die Antinomer (I539). This broad attack on antinomianism condemned Agricola's frivolity but did not damn his person. Luther did compose such a personal attack, Wider den Eisleben, in 1540, but it was published only after Luther's death in February, I546. Its appearance was not so much an attack on Agricola's antinomianism as a reaction to his conciliatory politics during the Interim struggle of I548. Thus the publication of a pamphlet supporting the Habsburg position stemming from Agricola's pen would have been an effective attack on Agricola in Protestant Germany. Published in the year of Luther's death, this type of attack would have also been in keeping with the milder attacks on Agricola prior to Luther's death. The organic nature of hymn and preface discounts a later compilation, and the possibility that the hymn may have been written in the period when Agricola was concerned with the form of the hymn makes the attribution to him more plausible. The form of the 'Kriegslied' precludes any complicated theological excursus. Purely propagandistic in nature, the 'Kriegslied' was a popular rallying cry for the soldier in the camp or the peasant on the field. The narrative voice is clear and direct, the subtleness present in the congregational hymn are rejected and the evolutionary, dynamic structure of the earlier hymns is abandoned for a strictly thematic, static Weltanschauung. Agricola incorporates a semantic restructuring to place the singer in a dominant position as opposed to the position of the supplicant in the hymns. 'Karle', Charles V, is viewed as a misdirected leader ('Du tewrer weiser Man'), something he could never have been considered by a Protestant poet during the Schmalkaldic Wars, and the narrator speaks to him as to a disobedient child. By placing the singer in a position of supremacy, Agricola heightens his sense of power and importance. Johannes Agricola's literary creations in the realm ofhymnology have, in the past, assumed a much too limited role in modern critics' evaluation of this central figure of the Reformation. The hymns, viewed as a closed group of texts written approxi- mately during the same period, offer the literary critic as well as the hymnologist a rich field for investigation. This study has determined the hymns actually attribu- table to Agricola and has investigated their literary and theological structure. It is, therefore, the first step in a total re-evaluation of Agricola as a literary figure, a re-evaluation earlier hampered by denominational differences. ITHACA, NEW YORK SANDER L.
