METHODOLOGY AHD TECHHIQUE INERTIAL CONSIDERATIONS-The technique of inertial measurements for a three-dimensional rigid body is complex. The discussion which follows is based on the description presented by Synge and Griffith(l2) . The moment of inertia of a rigid body is defined relative to an axis through the center of gravity. In a three-dimensional body, there are an infinite number of axes which can be passed through the center of gravity, resulting in an infinite number of moments of inertia. Fortunately, these measurements are related in a regular manner in an ellipsoid, so that by specifying only six parameters, the entire inertial system of a rigid body can be described about its center of gravity. Thus, inertial properties of a rigid body are described by an "ellipsoid of inertia" or "momental ellipsoid." The properties of an ellipsoid can be treated in a mathematical array called a "tensor," or analyzed using more traditional geometrical procedures, as in the present case. Every ellipsoid possesses three orthogonal vectors that pass through its locus. The directional axes for the ellipsoid of inertia are called the principal axes of inertia and the magnitudes of those principal axes are called the principal moments of inertia. The locus of the ellipsoid of inertia is coincident with the center of gravity of the rigid body. The ellipsoid of inertia can be completely specified for any body segment in either of two manners; i.e., the moments and products of inertia for a given frame of reference or the principal moments of inertia and the orientation of the principal axis system relative to the segment axis system. With the above background, a procedure can be established for measuring the ellipsoid of inertia of a rigid specimen. Winstandley, et al . ( 13 ) , Becker (il), Schaeffer, et al . ( ik ) present different interpretations of a similar methodology. Basically, it is required to determine the moment of inertia of the specimen about six axes passing through a given point relative to the specimen. Because of its relative simplicity, an approach from Winstandley, et al. has been followed in the present study . The moment of inertia of body segments has been measured with a simple pendulum swinging in the vertical plane where torque is the independent variable and acceleration is the dependent variable. The period of oscillation (t), length of pendulum (l) and weight (w) are the three basic measurements in the equation I = wglT2 THF (1) for the moment of inertia about the pendulum's axis of rotation. ANATOMICAL AND ANTHROPOMETRIC CONSIDERATIONS- The living human body is not composed of rigid segments, but of tissues which distort as the body changes position or is subject to varying acceleration. The torso in particular has a flexible spine and contains deformable organs which affect mass distribution of the whole body. The measuring techniques available for determining the moment al ellipsoid of inertia for the whole body and its segments dictate the use of frozen cadavers so that the specimens can be treated as rigid bodies. The anatomical and anthropometric methods used in this investigation were similar in many respects to those used in a previous study of weight, volume and center of mass of segments of the human body(l6). Six male cadavers were obtained from the Health Sciences Center of the University of Oklahoma in Oklahoma City. No specimens were considered that exhibited congenital anomalies, major surgical alterations, general or localized structural atrophy, excessive wasting, or obesity. "Cause of Death" of one subject was listed as "pulmonary embolism" ; death of all others was attributed to cardiovascular embarrassment. All cadavers had been embalmed with standard solution. Each cadaver was x-rayed to detect gross joint anomalies and none was revealed. Next, a series of lié anthropometric measurements was taken on each cadaver in the supine position. After the anthropometry had been completed, lines indicating planes of segmentation were marked. Bony landmarks identified by fluoroscopy and x-ray were used to establish these planes which passed through estimated joint centers of rotation(lO, l6). The positioning hoards were then moved to an environmental chamber maintained at -29°C where the specimens were frozen solid and the following three tasks performed. First, all anatomical, anthropometric and tick marks on each cadaver were located in three- dimensional space using a special whole body 3-D anthropometer depicted in Figure 3. This device measures the height of a point and transfers the location of the point in the horizontal plane to velum on the floor. Second, the specimens were mounted in styrofoam specimen holders for measurement of their inert ial properties on the simple pendulum. Third, after measuring the inertial properties of the whole body, the specimens were returned to the chamber where they were segmented. The inertial properties of all specimens - whole body and segments - were measured alike. An external reference frame was established by the specimen holder box. The measurement axis system used is illustrated in Figure h. Each segment was measured relative to the axis system depicted in Figure 1. The specimen holders were made in the form of rectangular boxes of one-inch thick, tongue-and-groove , rigid foam. All segments were mounted securely to the base of the box and except for the whole body, torso and thighs, the pendulum flexures were attached directly to the box. For the larger specimens, the flexure strings were attached directly to the specimen. In all cases, the specimen holder was suspended from a rigid stand (see Figures 5 and 6) and each flexure passed through a clamp which formed the pendulum axis point . Thus , as the moment of inertia of the box was measured about six axes, the specimen box remained within the defined 3-dimensional orthogonal axis system. Fig. 3 - Whole Body 3-D Anthropometer As mentioned previously, weight, length of pendulum and period of oscillation were the only measurements made during each test. Weight for the total body and torso was measured on a platform scale graduated in ±50 gm divisions and the segments on Mettler scales in hundredths or thousandths of a gram, and the weight was corrected for the acceleration due to gravity (measured locally). The length of the pendulum was calculated as a constant flexure length plus a measured distance from the box surface to the center of mass by a photographic suspension method (IT)« The period of oscillation was an average of fifty cycles measured by a Hewlett-Packard (Model 5T325B) counter triggered manually when the observed total swing displacement was less than 10°. The period was measured twice for each swing axis by two observers and the measurement was repeated until time was reproduced between or within observers by 6 X 10 3 seconds. These measures were used to calculate the six moments and three products of inertia. Input data were there- fore generated for a 3 x 3 matrix solved by the Jacobi method as modified by Folk (lT) to obtain the three principal moments of inertia about the three principal axes. These data define the momental ellipsoid of inertia relative to each specimen holder. A second set of three-dimensional measurements were made before removing the specimen from the specimen holder to establish the orientation of the specimen to the inertial axis measurement system. This measuring device is illustrated in Figure 7* Several points were located on each specimen relative to the box axis system so that an anatomical axis system could be developed. Upon completion of the inertial measurements and segment 3-D point location, each segment1 s volume was measured which concluded the data collection phase of the study. M. REYNOLDS, ET AL. CENTER OF GRAVITY- The differences in cut planes are reflected again in the measured location of the center of gravity. Table 5 reports the location of the center of gravity for the segments of the body. The center of gravity of the head is located relative to an axis system which is described by Eving and Thomas (2l). Basically the coordinates are located in a right-handed axis system whose origin lies on the mid-point of a line(y-axis) between the right and left Tragions. The x-axis passes anteriorly through a point midway between the Infraorbital landmarks (the X- and y-axes lie in the Frankfort Plane, or with a minor modification as to the anatomical location of the landmark at the auditory meatus, Reade's Plane). The z-axis is formed as a normal passing superiorly through the top of the head. The data presented by Clauser, et al.(l6) and Becker (ll) reflect primarily differences in the orientation and location of the head-neck separation. Dempster (lO) and Braune and Fischer (7&gt; 8) did not give coordinates but described the anatomical location of the center of gravity. Quantitative differences are not observable, but their description indicates that the center of gravity lies anterior and superior to the Tragion-Tragion axis with the head in the Frankfort Plane. Thus, it appears that only the Clauser, et al.,(l6) data deviate from the extant data and this difference is due primarily to the cut plane. Variation in the location of the center of gravity in the remaining segments is due to differences in the cut plane. All of the segments are presented in comparable fashion except for the hand. An examination of the hand data with respect to the anatomical location reveals the center of gravity to be on or near the palmar surface of the hand approximately at the level of the transverse crease(s). This location is for a hand that is slightly flexed in a "relaxed" position. In general, the center of gravity appears to be relatively stable for the male cadaver data reported in the literature. For the upper arm, forearm, thigh, and calf, both Dempster (lO) and Braune and Fischer (8) conclude that the center of gravity lies on or near an axis passing through the proximal and distal joint centers of rotation. Parenthetically, Dempster demonstrated that the joint center of rotation moves on a path described as a function of the spatial location of two adjacent links. However, for practical purposes, a mean joint center of rotation must be assumed. In the present study, these joint centers were estimated on the surface of the cut and are referred to as "centroids" in the technical report. In order to compare the previous invest- igators1 observations with quantitative data, the deviation of the center of gravity from the segment z-axis which passes through the proximal and distal joint centroids was calculated. The center of gravity and segmental centroids of each segment were measured in the same three-dimensional axis system. Thus, with three-dimensional location of the center of gravity, the deviation of the center of gravi- ty is the hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by the x- and y-axis coordinates (d = /x2 + yz ) . Table 6 presents the calculated data which are summary statistics for six subjects. The sample size is too small to test the null hypothesis H 0; but upon observation of the individual values , there are no apparent differences between the sitting and standing specimens. The only observable differences appear to be in greater distances in the proximal links (upper arm and thigh); but these differences, small as they are, may reflect our ability to make the more difficult cuts at the shoulder and hip, and to determine the approximate location of the mean center of rotation. The assumption that the center of gravity lies on the axis that passes through the proximal center of rotation and distal center of rotation of a segment appears to be reasonable. In addition, values from regression equations developed by Dempster (22) estimating the center of gravity as a percent of the links length have been compared to the three dimen- sional location of the center of gravity. Table 7 reports the average of the values predicted by Dempster's equations contrasted with the average of our measured data. All link lengths and center of gravity to proximal joint centers calculated from Dempster's regression equations are larger than those measured in this study. This suggests that the regression equations should be re-evaluated to provide more accurate estimates of the link lengths and location of the center of gravity from anthropometric di- mensions. Additional regresstion equations for estimating the location of the center of gravity have been developed and are available in Clauser, et al • (l6). Unfor- tunately, the seated position for our specimen is not the same as that used by Santschi and his co-workers. A comparison of the segmental moments of inertia with the mean values from Becker ( 11 ), Dempster (lO) and the individual specimen values of Braune and Fischer (7) is presented in Table 10. The differences between the results of the present study and those of Becker ( 11) in the principal moments of inertia about the principal axes for the head are possibly due to one of several causes. First, the shape of the head is spherical and for a sphere, all moments of inertia about all axes passing through the center of gravity are equal. Thus, as the difference between the principal moments diminishes, so does the ability to accurately define the principal moments of inertia for the head. In the measured moments of inertia all axes were approximately equal thereby indicating the spherical shape of the head. Second, with embalmed subjects, pooling of the fluid in the head would obviously affect the orientation and magnitude of the principal moments of inertia. There are other possibilities to explain the differences - technique, measurement error, etc. - but these are always present when data are compared from two different experiments . The differences are relatively minor given the above circumstances. For the remainder of the data, the most obvious differences are found in Braune and Fischer1 s (7) data for which the authors can offer no logical explanation. Weight, volume and the principal moments of inertia appear to be closely related. The principal moments of inertia were correlated more highly with volume than weight; however, due to the small sample size, regression equations based on this relationship have a large standard error thereby making their use limited. The principal axes of the limb segments were found to approximate the anatomical axis system. Thus, for all practical purposes, we can assume that the principal moments of inertia lie about the anatomical axes. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS A study of the inert i al properties of the human body and body segments of six adult male cadavers was conducted and the results reported. The experimental design did not attempt to provide a statistically valid sam- pling to establish population estimates for the parameters , and no attempt should be made to use the results reported as population estimates. Differences in the principal moments- of-inertia between cadavers used in this study and the living are unknown, but comparisons for three measured moments of inertia about the three axes formed by the intersection of the cardinal anatomical planes were made. The differences were small for the three standing subjects in all axes and for the X- and y-axes in the seated subjects. A slightly greater difference was found about the z-axis f,or the seated subjects which is probably attributable to the position differences. Furthermore, any attempt to extrapolate the results reported here to adult women and children is probably invalid owing to the significant differences in their tissue distribution from adult men. The results of this investigation have permitted us to draw a number of general conclusions : (1) The relationships of the segment principal moments of inertia to body weight and segment volumes are high with the latter providing, in general, the best predictors of inertia. (2) The principal moments Ixx and Iyy are approximately of the same magnitude for the major limb segments with the principal moment I being approximately 20% or less of the I values. xx (3) The directional angles of the principal moments tend to approximate but are not identical to our segment -refer enee axis system. (*+) For most segments, the difference in the principal moments-of-inertia between the seated and standing subjects are small and fall within sample variability. While shifts in muscle tissue associated with joint movement could not be duplicated in our specimens, the results of this tissue displacement on the moments of inertia are believed to be slight and the estimates for segment moments of inertia in one orientation are usable in any other segment orientation for purposes of modeling.
