      <p>
         <bold>Peter Anstey</bold> is Lecturer in the Department of Philosophy at the University of Sydney. His research interests lie in the field of early modern philosophy and science, with a special focus on the thought of Robert Boyle and John Locke.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Wilbur Applebaum</bold> is Professor Emeritus at Illinois Institute of Technology. He is the editor of the <italic>Encyclopedia of the Scientific Revolution: From Copernicus to Newton</italic> and the author of <italic>The Scientific Revolution and the Foundations of Modern Science</italic>. He is now completing his annotated translation of Jeremiah Horrocks’s <italic>Venus in Sole visa</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>William Ashworth</bold> is Senior Lecturer in History at the University of Liverpool. He is the author of <italic>Customs and Excise: Trade, Production, and Consumption in England, 1640–1845</italic> (Oxford University Press, 2003).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jonathan Barnes</bold> is Professor of Ancient Philosophy at the University of Paris IV–Sorbonne. He has published articles and books on most aspects of ancient philosophy. His most recent book is <italic>Porphyry: Introduction</italic> (Oxford, 2003).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Richard Bellon</bold> teaches history of science in the Lyman Briggs School of Science at Michigan State University. His research focuses on the social and cultural role of natural history in Victorian Britain. He is now exploring the influence of Darwin’s botany in the debates over evolution in the 1860s.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John Blackmore</bold> has taught at UC Santa Cruz, Harvey Mudd College, the University of Vienna, and Tsukuba University in Japan. He has published several books on Ernst Mach and Ludwig Boltzmann, and he is now writing a book titled <italic>Famous Realists.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jimena Canales</bold> is Assistant Professor of the History of Science at Harvard University. Her recent publications include “Criminal Skins: Tattoos and Modern Architecture in the Work of Adolf Loos,” <italic>Architectural History</italic>, Fall 2005; “Photogenic Venus: The ‘Cinematographic Turn’ in Science and Its Alternatives,” <italic>Isis</italic>, 2003, <italic>93</italic>:585–613; “Exit the Frog, Enter the Human: Astronomy, Physiology, and Experimental Psychology in the Nineteenth Century,” <italic>British Journal for the History of Science</italic>, 2001, <italic>34</italic>:173–197; and “The Single Eye: Reevaluating <italic>Ancien Régime</italic> Science,” <italic>History of Science</italic>, 2001, <italic>39</italic>:71–94.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John Clark</bold> is Director of the Institute for Environmental History at the University of St Andrews, Scotland. His past research has focused on the history of entomology. At present, he is entering the fourth year of a major AHRC‐funded project on the history of waste in Britain.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>J. T. H. Connor</bold> is John Clinch Professor of Medical Humanities and History of Medicine (Faculty of Medicine) and Professor of History (Faculty of Arts) at Memorial University of Newfoundland, St. John’s, Canada. His most recent book, <italic>Doing Good: The Life of Toronto’s General Hospital</italic>, has won numerous awards.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Hamilton Cravens</bold> teaches history of science, history of technology, and American history at Iowa State University. His most recent book is <italic>The Social Sciences Go to Washington: The Politics of Knowledge in the Postmodern Era</italic> (Rutgers University Press, 2003). He is working on a synthetic history of the social sciences in America.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Lindley Darden</bold> is Professor of Philosophy and in the Committee for Philosophy and the Sciences at the University of Maryland, College Park, where she teaches history and philosophy of biology courses. She discussed the discovery of the theory of the gene in <italic>Theory Change in Science</italic> (Oxford University Press, 1991). She and Carl F. Craver guest edited the June 2005 special issue, “Mechanisms in Biology,” of <italic>Studies in History and Philosophy of Biological and Biomedical Sciences.</italic> Her current research is on reasoning strategies for the discovery of molecular biological mechanisms.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>William Eamon</bold> is Regents Professor of History and Dean of the Honors College at New Mexico State University, where he also holds the S. P. and Margaret Manasse Chair. He is the author of <italic>Science and the Secrets of Nature</italic> (1994) and has recently completed a book manuscript entitled “The Charlatan’s Tale: A Renaissance Surgeon’s World.”</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Nathan Ensmenger</bold> is an assistant professor in the Department of the History and Sociology of Science at the University of Pennsylvania. His current book project explores the emergence of computing professions in the 1950s and 1960s and the organizational politics of early corporate computerization efforts.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>James Evans</bold> is a professor in the Department of Physics and in the Program in Science, Technology, and Society at the University of Puget Sound. His latest project, coauthored with J. Lennart Berggren, is <italic>Geminos’s Introduction to the Phenomena: A Translation and Study of a Hellenistic Manual of Astronomy</italic> (Princeton University Press, in press).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Silvia Figueirôa</bold> is an associate professor at the State University of Campinas, Brazil. She belongs to the International Commission on the History of Geological Sciences (INHIGEO), acted as president of the History of Earth Sciences Society (HESS) (2001–2002), and is now president of the “Sciences and Empires” International Commission (IUPHS/DHS). She is the author of <italic>Geological Sciences in Brazil: A Social and Institutional History (1875–1934)</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Steven French</bold> is Professor of the Philosophy of Science in the Division of History and Philosophy of Science at the University of Leeds. He has published papers on the history and philosophy of modern physics and also on models, heuristics, partial truth, and a lot more besides.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Matthew R. Goodrum</bold> is an assistant professor of history of science in the Department of Science and Technology in Society at Virginia Tech. His research explores the history of paleoanthropology and prehistoric archaeology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Loren Graham </bold>is Professor of the History of Science at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and a member of the Executive Committee of the Davis Center for Russian and Eurasian Studies at Harvard University. He has published many books and articles; his most recent book is <italic>Moscow Stories</italic> (2006). One of his books, <italic>Science and Philosophy in the Soviet Union</italic> (1972, 1987), was a finalist for a National Book Award. In 1996 he received the Sarton Medal of the History of Science Society.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Chris Hables Gray</bold> is the author of <italic>Peace, War, and Computers</italic> (Routledge 2005), <italic>Cyborg Citizen</italic> (Routledge, 2001), and <italic>Postmodern War</italic> (Guilford/Routledge, 1997), among other works. He currently teaches for Goddard College, the Union Institute and University, and the University of California at Santa Cruz. He is a member of Computer Professionals for Social Responsibility.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John S. Haller, Jr.,</bold> Professor of History and Medical Humanities at Southern Illinois University in Carbondale, has written books on race, sexuality, and medicine. He is a former editor of <italic>Caduceus</italic> and, for the past fifteen years, has served as Vice President for Academic Affairs for the Southern Illinois University system.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ernst Hamm</bold> is Assistant Professor in the Science and Technology Studies Program at York University. His primary research interests are in Enlightenment and Romantic science and the history of geology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Rebecca Herzig</bold> teaches at Bates College in Maine. She is the author of <italic>Suffering for Science: Reason and Sacrifice in Modern America</italic> (Rutgers University Press, 2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David L. Hull</bold> is Professor Emeritus in the Department of Philosophy at Northwestern University. His most recent book is an anthology entitled <italic>Science as a Process</italic> (2001). He is now editing <italic>The Cambridge Companion to the Philosophy of Biology</italic> with Michael Ruse.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jean‐Michel Kantor</bold> has been a researcher and lecturer at the Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS) since 1966. He is currently working on a book project with Loren Graham entitled <italic>Naming God, Naming the Infinite: Mathematics and Religious Heresy.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Hartmut Krech</bold> is an independent scholar living in Bremen, Germany. He has taught the history of anthropology and the cultural sciences at the universities of Bremen and Marburg, and he is about to publish a dictionary of classical Greek philosophical terms.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Kenton Kroker</bold> teaches in the Science and Technology Studies Program at York University in Toronto. His attempt to figure out how sleep became an investigative object is forthcoming as <italic>The Sleep of Others</italic>, and he is now trying to make a case for epidemic encephalitis as a crucial disease for twentieth‐century biomedicine.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Hannah Landecker</bold> is Assistant Professor of Anthropology at Rice University. Her work on the history of microcinematography has appeared in <italic>Critical Inquiry</italic> and <italic>Grey Room</italic>. She is currently completing a manuscript about the manipulation of life in vitro entitled <italic>Culturing Life: How Cells Became Technologies</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Manfred D. Laubichler</bold> is a theoretical biologist and a historian of biology. He is Assistant Professor of Biology and Affiliated Assistant Professor of Philosophy in the School of Life Sciences and the Center for Biology and Society at Arizona State University.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>A. J. Lustig</bold> is Assistant Professor of History at the University of Texas at Austin.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Eileen L. G. Lyon</bold> is an associate professor of history at the State University of New York at Fredonia. She is the author of <italic>Politicians in the Pulpit</italic> (1999) and several articles on modern British religious and political history.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Anne Millbrooke </bold>has taught at the University of Alaska, Montana State University, Wesleyan University, and the University of Hartford. Her book <italic>Aviation History</italic> won several awards, and a second edition is in press. She is now living and writing in Bozeman, Montana.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Iwan Rhys Morus </bold>lectures in the Department of History and Welsh History at the University of Wales, Aberystwyth. He is the author of <italic>When Physics Became King</italic> (Chicago, 2005), <italic>Michael Faraday and the Electrical Century</italic> (Icon Books, 2004), and <italic>Frankenstein’s Children</italic> (Princeton, 1998). He is also the co‐author, with Peter Bowler, of <italic>Making Modern Science</italic> (Chicago, 2005) and the editor of <italic>Bodies/Machines</italic> (Berg, 2002). He is currently researching nineteenth‐century scientific performances.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Lynn K. Nyhart</bold> is an associate professor of the history of science at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. She is completing a book on German natural history in the late nineteenth century.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Barbara Orland</bold> is managing director of the newly founded Center for History of Knowledge of the Federal Institute of Technology of Zurich and the University of Zurich. Her research interests lie in the fields of the history of biomedicine and biotechnology. She has edited <italic>Artifizielle Körper—Lebendige Technik: Technische Modellierungen des Körpers in historischer Perspektive</italic> (Zurich: Chronos, 2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Alex Owen</bold> is Professor of History and Board of Lady Managers Professor of Gender Studies at Northwestern University. She is the author of <italic>The Darkened Room: Women, Power, and Spiritualism in Late‐Victorian England</italic> (1989, 1990, 2004) and <italic>The Place of Enchantment: British Occultism and the Culture of the Modern</italic> (2004).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Dan Plafcan</bold> is a postdoctoral fellow in science and technology studies at the University of Tokyo. His research focuses on the politics of knowledge making and technology building in U.S.–Japan collaborations that concern space‐based remote sensing.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Terry S. Reynolds</bold> is Professor of History at Michigan Tech. He has authored several books and articles on the history of water power and is now working on a paper describing attempts by an Upper Michigan iron mining company to promote agriculture in that region’s harsh climate.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Alan Richardson</bold> is Professor of Philosophy and Distinguished University Scholar at the University of British Columbia. He is the author of <italic>Carnap’s Construction of the World</italic> (Cambridge, 1998) and coeditor, with Gary Hardcastle, of <italic>Logical Empiricism in North America</italic> (Minnesota, 2003).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Shirley A. Roe</bold> is Professor and Chair of the Department of History at the University of Connecticut. She is the author of <italic>Matter, Life, and Generation: Eighteenth‐Century Embryology and the Haller–Wolff </italic>
         <italic>Debate</italic> (1981) and coeditor of <italic>Science against the Unbelievers: The Correspondence of Bonnet and Needham, 1760–1780</italic> (1986).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Kristina Rolin</bold> is Academy of Finland Senior Research Fellow at the Helsinki School of Economics. Her current research project concerns the intersections of social epistemology and feminist epistemology. Her articles have appeared in <italic>Perspectives on Science</italic>, <italic>Hypatia</italic>, <italic>Social Epistemology</italic>, and <italic>Philosophy of Science</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Niall Shanks</bold> holds the Curtis D. Gridley Distinguished Professorship in History and Philosophy of Science at Wichita State University. He is the author of <italic>God, the Devil, and Darwin: A Critique of Intelligent Design Theory</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ann B. Shteir,</bold> Professor of Humanities and Women’s Studies at York University, is the author of <italic>Cultivating Women, Cultivating Science: Flora’s Daughters and Botany in England, 1760 to 1860</italic> (Johns Hopkins, 1996). Now working on gender and iconography, she is writing a cultural history of Flora as goddess of flowers. With Bernard Lightman, she has edited a forthcoming volume on science, gender, and visual culture.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Pamela H. Smith</bold> teaches early modern European history as Professor of History at Columbia University. She is the author of <italic>The Body of the Artisan: Art and Experience in the Scientific Revolution</italic> (Chicago, 2004) and <italic>The Business of Alchemy</italic> (Princeton, 1994) and coeditor of <italic>Merchants and Marvels: Commerce, Science, and Art in Early Modern Europe</italic> (Routledge, 2002).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Robert W. Smith</bold> is Professor of History at the University of Alberta. He has published extensively on the history of the physical sciences in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, particularly the history of astronomy, cosmology, and space science. As McCalla Professor at the University of Alberta in 2005/2006, he is working on a history of scientific discovery.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Marianne Sommer</bold> wrote her doctoral dissertation on anthropomorphism in primatology, using <italic>National Geographic</italic> articles among other sources. As a postdoc at the Max Planck Institute for the History of Science, Berlin, and at Penn State University, she worked on a project in the (pre‐)history of paleoanthropology, which she continues as a junior faculty member in science studies at the Eidgenössische Technische Hochschule in Zurich.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Martin S. Staum,</bold> Professor of History at the University of Calgary, is the author of <italic>Labeling People: French Scholars on Society, Race, and Empire, 1815–1848</italic> (Montreal: McGill‐Queen’s, 2003), and is pursuing research on the nature/nurture controversy in nineteenth‐century French anthropology, psychology, and sociology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>William C. Summers</bold> is a professor in five departments or programs—ranging from Molecular Biophysics and Biochemistry to Women’s, Gender, and Sexuality Studies—at Yale. His research has focused on virology and microbiology, history of medicine and science, and the relations between science and the humanities. He has taught and published on topics ranging from thermodynamics to antiviral drugs to East Asian studies and ancient Greek philosophy.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Philip M. Teigen</bold> is Deputy Chief of the History of Medicine Division at the National Library of Medicine in Bethesda, Maryland. He is writing a history of rabies in Gilded Age Massachusetts. His most recent essay on medical historiography, “William Osler Again,” appeared in the<italic> Bulletin of the History of Medicine</italic> (2001, <italic>75</italic>:745–755).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jennifer Tucker </bold>is Associate Professor of History at Wesleyan University. A cultural historian specializing in the history of science, especially of the nineteenth century, she is the author of <italic>Nature Exposed: Photography as Eyewitness in Victorian Science</italic> (2005) and of articles on gender and visual culture in the history of science. She has just completed a fellowship at the Clark Art Institute, where she began research for her next book, <italic>Powers of Observation: Science, Art, and the Politics of Seeing,</italic> a study of popular visual representations of scientists and scientific audiences in the British Empire.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Simone Turchetti</bold> is a researcher at the CHSTM, University of Manchester. He is interested in the history of applied nuclear physics, and his current project deals with the contribution of Italian émigré scientists to its development. He is the author of “Atomic Secrets and Governmental Lies: Nuclear Science, Politics, and Security in the Pontecorvo Case,” <italic>British Journal for the History of Science,</italic> 2003, <italic>36</italic>(4): 389–415.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Steven A. Walton</bold> teaches in the Science, Technology, and Society Program at Penn State University and works on the intersections of the history of engineering, military history, and users’ “scientific” understanding of the material culture around them. His recent edited volume on scientific instruments in military contexts, <italic>Instrumental in War</italic>, appeared from Brill in early 2005.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jessica Wang</bold> is Associate Professor of History at the University of California, Los Angeles, and the author of <italic>American Science in an Age of Anxiety: Scientists, Anticommunism, and the Cold War</italic> (1999). Her current project examines the history of technocratic politics under the New Deal. Wang will be joining the faculty of the University of British Columbia in July 2006.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Charles Webster</bold> has worked in Sheffield, Leeds, and Oxford. He writes on the social history of science and medicine in the early modern period and on health care since 1900. He is the author of <italic>The Great Instauration</italic> (1975) and the two‐volume official history of the National Health Service (1988, 1996).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Catherine Westfall</bold> is the author (with Lillian Hoddeson and Adrienne Kolb) of the forthcoming <italic>The Americanization of Megascience: Frontier Rings at Fermilab</italic>, in addition to other studies of U.S. national laboratory history. She heads Argonne National Laboratory’s history group and is an adjunct professor in the Department of History at Michigan State University.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>M. Norton Wise </bold>is a professor of the history of science at UCLA. He is also the editor of <italic>Growing Explanations: Historical Perspectives on Recent Science</italic> and coauthor of <italic>Energy and Empire: A Biographical Study of Lord Kelvin,</italic> with Crosbie Smith. He is currently completing a book on bourgeois Berlin and laboratory science.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Charles Withers</bold> is Professor of Historical Geography at the University of Edinburgh. With David Livingstone, he is the coeditor of <italic>Geography and Revolution</italic> (Chicago, 2005). He has research and teaching interests in the historical geographies of science, particularly in the Enlightenment.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Yi‐Li Wu</bold> received her Ph.D. from Yale University and is now Associate Professor of History at Albion College. She has published on popular medicine, gender, and the body in late imperial China. Her current book projects include a history of Qing gynecology and a comparative study of nineteenth‐century British and Chinese medicine.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John van Wyhe</bold> is Director of <italic>The Complete Work of Charles Darwin Online</italic> at the University of Cambridge (<ext-link ext-link-type="uri"
                   xlink:href="http://darwin-online.org.uk"
                   xlink:type="simple">http://darwin‐online.org.uk</ext-link>). He is researching the origins and development of the belief that Darwin discovered evolution while on the Galapagos Islands. He is the author of <italic>Phrenology and the Origins of Victorian Scientific Naturalism</italic> (Ashgate, 2004).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John H. Zammito</bold> is John Antony Weir Professor of History at Rice University. His research centers on the German Enlightenment, especially the relationship between Kant and Herder. His publications include <italic>The Genesis of Kant’s</italic> Critique of Judgment (Chicago, 1992) and <italic>Kant, Herder, and the Birth of Anthropology</italic> (Chicago, 2002).</p>
