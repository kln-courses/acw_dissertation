COHEN THE KING AND THE GODDESS: THE NOLAMBA PERIOD LAKSMANESVARA TEMPLE AT AVANI T he No!amba-Pallava dynasty, though lesser-known in comparison to other contemporary dynasties such as the Rastrakutas, Pallavas and C6olas, was a critical power in the fluctuating politics of South India during the eighth through eleventh centuries. Although the family referred to itself as Nolamba-Pallava, scholars have not found evidence which establishes consanguinity between the No!ambas and the Pallavas. Some time in the eighth century, the Nolambas settled in No!amba!ige (portions of Chitradurg and Tumkur districts in present-day Karnataka and Ananta- pur district in Andhra Pradesh). While serving as a feudatory to the Gafigas, or, as the flux of power demanded, to the Rastrak-tas, the No!ambas were able to consolidate local control. By 878 C.E. the powerful No!amba ruler Mahendradhiraja conquered neighboring families - the Telugu Codas, the Banas, and the Vaidumbas - severed his ties with the Gafigas, and declared himself independent. From that date to the beginning of the eleventh century, the Nolambas remained either fully independent or minimally subservient to other dynasties. They ruled from their capital, Hemavati, over the territory known as No!ambavadi which at its height encompassed Tumkur, Chitradurg, and sections of Kolar, Bangalore, and Mysore districts in Karnataka; a substantial portion of Anantapur district in Andhra Pradesh; and the northern part of Dharmapuri district in Tamil Nadu. The No!ambavadi artistic "style" lacks homogeneity, hence generalizations applicable to the whole region are deceptive. I divide No!ambavadi into three smaller regions - central, eastern and southern - which conform roughly to traditional territorial groupings and coincide with stylistic divisions., This paper, however, is not concerned with stylistical problems. Rather I shall focus on a particular monument, the Laksmanesvara temple at Avani (Kolar district, Karnataka), which has carved on its walls an innovative, abstruse, iconographic scheme. Eastern No!ambavadi is often called Ganigarusasira (or Gafiga 6ooo) in inscriptions. Although this area was traditionally affiliated with the Gafiga dynasty, it was entrusted to No!amba administration by the Gafigas prior to the mid-ninth century, and by the late ninth century was ceded to greater Nolambavadi. Southern No!ambavadi corresponds to the north Korigu region. In this area, No!amba presence was more intrusive: seized ca. 878, north Kofigu, especially around Dharmapuri, remained within the Nolamba realm of administration until about mid-tenth century. Reflecting the political fluidity of eastern and southern No!ambavadi, these subregions were subject to a flux of artistic interactions-presumably due to migrating artisans - resulting in "hybrid styles" which exhibit traits shared with neighboring regions. Hence one cannot define the eastern and southern Nolambavadi monuments as cohesive "idioms." Southern Nolambavadi monuments display an affinity with lower DravidadeSa works, especially in Pandinddu, with a blending of eastern Nolambavadi and Gafigavadi traits. One can only speculate on the intended meaning of this iconographic scheme. The repeated re- presentations of victorious four-armed Devis over fallen asuras and additional, usually fierce, female attendants, suggest an emphasis on the supremacy of the Goddess. The Goddess is manifest in myriad forms: her "dark" destructive nature predominates at the Laksmaneivara temple. Always her powers are superior to the haughty asuras who, in their best moments, may taunt the harried Devas (gods) and render disharmonious the cosmic order. She conquers enemies and hence is revered (or feared) for her martial prowess. Yet, the Goddess is an inherent, integral part of Siva in Paiupata (and other) Saiva schools. The Laksmaneivara temple is dedicated to Siva and his presence is not neglected on the temple walls. Twice he is in raudra form and twice more he performs his dance of destruction/creation (Nateia). It is the terrific forms of Siva and Devi which dominate the temple walls. Conclusion The LaksmaneSvara temple is a No!amba period monument derived from the eastern Nolam- bav.di regional tradition which also is exemplified at Nandi by the Bh6ganandisvara and Arund- cals^vara temples. Though the exterior wall projections are more pronounced and the adhisthdna is slightly more ornate, the Laksmangvara temple morphology is based on the ArunacaleSvara prototype. The LaksmanEsvara iconographic scheme is unique: a regional antecedent for its format remains unknown. The religious leader Tribhuvanakarata-bhatdra's image is incorporated on the temple, at the important placement above the prandala (north vimana bhadra), suggesting his sacerdotal power, patronage, and direct supervision of the iconography. Control would have been shared by, and beneficial to, the Nolamba king Anniga. The name Vira-No!amba (Anniga) is in- scribed on the temple4 and the royal figure bearing a wreath on the vimana south (west karna) wall is probably intended as his portrait. The preponderance of Devi imagery, within a Saiva context, probably reflects Tribhuvanakarata's teachings, which were presumably based on a sanctioned, contemporaneous school of thought, most likely PRsupata or Ka-lamukha. Because of her relentless ferocity, the Goddess, in her "dark" manifestations, was important to kings seeking strength, gui- dance and protection in warfare.5 This aspect of Devi, combined with the destructive, yet com- passionate, potency of Siva, appears to be the cause of the enigmatic Laksmanesvara wall scheme.
