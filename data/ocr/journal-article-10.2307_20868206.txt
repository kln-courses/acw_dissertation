Walter Stephens, Giants in Those Days, Lincoln, Nebraska, and London, Nebraska U.P., xii + 456pp., surveys the history of attitudes to giants from the Old Testament to R.'s time, and shows convincingly that, until the 15th c, they were normally regarded as embodiments of evil; but in the Antiquities of Annius of Viterbo, according to whom Noah and his family were giants, they embodied good, and were the founders of a universal empire, which had legitimately devolved to the papacy. Stephens argues that Pantagruel and Gargantua parody both Annius and Lemaire, but that the rejection of imperialism is consistent with the patriotic overtones of Gargantua.
