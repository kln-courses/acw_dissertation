The general task of the ecocritic is to analyze the interplay of the nonhuman ecosphere (plants, animals, and landscapes) and the narration of human activity (especially techne) as evidenced by language encoded in literary texts. Ecocritics attempt to understand "an immensely com- plex global system, in which energy, matter, and ideas interact," while simultaneously seeking to discover what roles literature has played in the ecology of the human species (Glotfelty xix). A specific concern of ecocriticism is how anthropocentrism and systematic subjectivism have shaped the Western view of the natural world. The perception of Homo sapiens as being the center of (or even the sole reason for) creation finds its origins in Judeo-Christian teleology. The Judeo-Christian concept of history as a linear series of unique, non- repeatable events, detaches human destiny from the cycles of nature. The biblical account of creation sets the stage for the West's anthro- pocentric trajectory: "Man named all the animals, thus establishing dominance over them. God planned all this explicitly for man's benefit and rule: no item in the physical creation had any purpose save to serve man's purposes. And, although man's body is made of clay, he is not simply part of nature: he is made in God's image" (White 9). 3 During the Medieval period the Latin West continued to express human centrality through texts such as "The Book of Nature." In the twelfth century German thinker, Hug of St. Victor expressed the notion that the forms and processes of the natural world were devoid of value except as tools of interpretation. Medieval exegesis reduced the eco- sphere to a system of signs and symbols. Nature became an allegory whose primary purpose was to provide an interpretive text for the divine destiny of humankind (Manes 19). This scala naturae categorized creation into a hierarchical scale of lower, intermediate and higher forms. Although God and the angels were above man in heaven, in the realm of the biosphere, Homo sapiens reigned supreme. Renaissance Humanism fully embraced the scala naturae. Humans, especially by virtue of their ability to use language, were deemed sovereign and central to all life on Earth. Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (1463-1494), taking humanism to its logical conclusion, maintained that the perfection of man was the primary purpose of the world's existence. Mirandola envisioned the transformation of the earthbound human indi- vidual into something akin to the daimon of Greek mythology; a spirit classified between gods and men. The Italian thinker saw the path to full human self-realization was to focus our energies entirely on the intellect. By equating being with the human thought process (Cogito, ergo sum), Descartes, like Mirandola, fundamentally separates the mind from the body. Descartes' system not only devalues human emotion and physical feeling, it also denies the natural world any intrin- sic value. This ecocritical chain of being is not a rigid hierarchy like the scala naturae , but rather a vicious circle. Instead of achieving the Cartesian utopia of mastering nature, Homo sapiens is held hostage to the natural forces the species has unleashed through science and technology. Daimón offers a dissident response to the conqueror's worldview by giving a voice to the natural world. Posse's narrative shares ecocriti- cism's revalorization of Ruskin's pathetic fallacy as "not merely a Romantic indulgence, but an inevitable component of human percep- tion; it is something to acknowledge and celebrate, not to con- demn" (McDowell 372-73). 10 Posse's novel personifies the ecosphere of the Americas as the narrator assumes the role of spokesperson for the "thoughts" and "feelings" of species that lack verbal language. In Posse's topsy turvy text neither the indigenous peoples nor the flora and fauna of the New World are a passive background for the conquest. Instead they are active, thinking subjects who "discover" the conquista- dors' profound alienation from nature: "El 12 de octubre de 1492 fue descubierta Europa y los europeos por los animales y hombres de los reinos selváticos. Desde entonces fueron de desilusión en pena ante el paso de estos seres blanquinosos, más fuertes por astucia que por don. Se los veía como una angustiada pero peligrosa congregación de expul- sados del paraíso, de la Unidad primordial de la que ningún hombre o animal tiene por qué alejarse" (26-7). The "perceptive species" immediately "understand" the new equilib- rium created by the conquistadors' arrival. The former recognize their endangerment. The latter are more concerned with individual human activity and being makers of history than with living in equilibrium with the "Spirit of the Earth": "No sólo los hombres de los pueblos selváticos sino también las plantas y los animales, pronto quedaron convencidos de que estos invasores estaban profundamente enemistados con el Espíritu de la Tierra" (43-44). 10 Michael McDowell states that ecocritics also simultaneously recognize that all ver- bal descriptions of nature, because they reflect human values and are the product of human language, are unavoidably anthropomorphic (372-73).</page><page sequence="7">THE GREAT CHAIN OF BEING 57 As the novel approaches its conclusion the narrator once again gives voice to the flora of the Americas. Daimón presents a more conventional anthropomorphization of nature in Posse's carnivalized use of the Amazon myth. During their search for El Dorado Aguirre and his band of tattered men encounter the mythical tribe of female warriors. Initially the conquerors naively sense the prospect for sexual conquest. Huamán calls on Aguirre to examine the assumptions of his worldview. Through this psychotropic treatment the shaman temporarily brings the Conqueror to a state of ani- mistic consciousness, free from the constraints of historical temporality and, more importantly, the Cartesian ego. Aguirre "sentía que ya el futuro no lo tironeaba hasta hacerlo saltar del presente. Deshisto- rizábase. Uníase a la materia del día como la raíz a la tierra, como la rama en el aire, como perro que fornica (bebeando con la lengua afuera, con las fauces abiertas de entregado gozo)" (177). Under the shaman's influence Aguirre momentarily ceases to be what ecocritic Pete Gunther calls a "disembodied parasite" (205). The conquistador eschews his isolated, subjectivized sense of self and accepts the first law of ecology: "Everything is connected to every- thing." 12 Nevertheless, Aguirre 's transformation is transitory. In the end of the novel Aguirre rejects the path Huamán has offered him and returns to the "Jornada grande," wandering the earth, "making history" while clinging to his dualistic sense of self (222). Read as an ecocritical text Daimón echoes Octavio Paz's observation in El arco y la lira that our species' embrace of techne has irreversibly alienated us from Nature (221-22). Posse's novel also anticipates Tzve- tan Todorov's lament that the conquest of America was a lost chance to recover a feeling of harmony with the pre-established order of the natu- ral world (97). The open-ended conclusion of Daimón reminds us that the daimon of the isolated cogito is not likely to disappear. We cannot erase Cartesian subjectivism from our culture with wishful thinking, magical herbs, or an impossible return to animistic, mytho-poetical con- sciousness. Moreover, science and technology, legacies of Renaissance humanism and Western dualism, will play a central role as our species faces the future. In the meantime ecocritical texts, such as Abel Posse's Daimón, are likely to acquire greater valence as we reconsider our place in the Great Chain of Being.
