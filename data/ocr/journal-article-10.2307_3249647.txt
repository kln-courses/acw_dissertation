The Vrsa figure is invariably associated with Sivaite places of worship all over India, in every period down to our own times. Early examples from Northern Indian temples, in their various regional styles, are in some cases impressive pieces of craftsmanship; but it is in Southern India that they attained, in many varied schools of sacred sculpture, to a level of higher artistic sensibility. Third, I have carefully compared the style-characteristics of the respective Nandi with those of the temple images and based my conclusions on them. I obviously cannot produce here the vast body of comparative material I used in fixing the dates, including epigraphical data. Wherever the style of a Nandi did not seem to agree with that of the temple to which it belonged. I have examined other possibilities and offered different solutions. This last feature is what the Indian farmers do to their bulls in the field, to hold them back from grazing; but it is rather unusual to see it in association with Nandi, who, in this case seems to accept it as normal and shows no sign of revolt or discomfort. The Nandi has a shapely body, and also a nice face which otherwise is devoid of emotion. The thin furrows on the neck and dewlap are somewhat reminiscent of the Western Cilukyan manner, and so is the positioning of the forelegs. The horn-ornament also is reminiscent of CSilukyan instances but the festooned rump garland is clearly a feature of the sculpture of the Kakatiyas. The garland behind the bull's hump has rattling pendants of two types, a bulbous one alter- nating with a bud-shaped one. The rope-garland next to it has buckles which seem as though made of silver. So do those of the rope-and-bead garland around the neck. This Nandi has been as carefully polished, though not to so dazzling a degree, as the N.d- kalsi and Kundgol examples.
