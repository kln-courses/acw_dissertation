Land of the Turkey and the Deer: Recent Research in Yucatan. RUTH GUBLER, editor. Labyrinthos, Lancaster, CA, 1999. xiv + 144 pp., figures, bibliogra- phies. $40.00 (paper). Reviewed by Travis W. Stanton, State University of New York, Fredonia. This volume is a selection of papers presented at the Fourth Miami Conference in 1995. In the very brief preface, Ruth Gubler describes the conference as a forum for scholars from various disciplines to present their most recent research in the Yucatan peninsula. Most of the participants were invited to the conference because of their research in the Mexican state of Yucatan where Gubler's work is focused. Apart from two chapters on modern oral and written literature and an excellent chapter on the Extended Porfiriato of 1910 to 1915, the volume focuses on Maya archaeology with a heavy emphasis on the northern Maya lowlands. The chapters are arranged in order of the first author's last name, giving the volume a disjointed feel. Furthermore, the volume lacks both an introduction and a synthesis, leaving the reader to decide the theme. Two chapters focus on recent archaeology in the southern Maya lowlands. Ramon Carrasco V.'s chapter on Calakmul is of great interest to Mesoamericanists studying the emergence of kingship. Carrasco V. reports on the excavations at Structure IV, the eastern structure of the only E-Group or "observatory" at Calakmul. Data from the few E-Groups that have been investigated suggest that the Middle Preclassic substructures of these architectural assemblages constitute some of the earliest public architecture in the Maya lowlands. From the Late Preclassic through the Late Classic, some of these groups underwent massive renovations in which elaborate caches and royal tombs were placed. Given the sociopolitical prominence of Calakmul in Maya inscriptions and the importance of the E-Group during the emergence and transformation of Maya kingship, these excavations represent a tremendous source of information on changing power relations in the Maya lowlands. The chapter by James Brady is an analysis of rock art at Gruta de Jobonche in southeastern Peten, Guatemala. Brady draws attention to the ritual impor- tance of caves in Maya ideology and reviews a large number of contexts in which stalactites and stalagmites have been found. The Jobonche art consists of four crudely carved faces. Although Brady suggests that they may represent a folk tradition associated with Earth or rain deities, well-known associations for caves in Mesoamerica, he freely admits that the identification of the faces is difficult. The three nonarchaeological chapters focus exclu- sively on Yucatan, Mexico. Edward Terry's chapter is an analysis of Maya influence in the literature of Joaquin Bestard, a modern Yucatecan novelist. Mary Preuss documents a select number of modern stories in the Yucatec communities of Hoctun and Becal. Finally, Gilbert Joseph documents the complex social and political processes behind Yucatan's Extended Porfiriato. This chapter is especially illuminating for those working in the northern Maya lowlands as com- plex modern social relations can be placed in detailed historical perspective. While this volume lacks thematic consistency, it contains valuable primary data that will interest stu- dents of Mesoamerican archaeology. Some of the authors are not extensively published and are difficult to find in the literature. It is refreshing to see these authors, especially from large-scale Mexican projects, publish such exciting work in an easily accessible con- text. recent primary data. Half of the ten chapters are dedicated to archaeolog- ical research in the northern Maya lowlands. This is a major strength of the volume given the unprecedented number of large-scale excavations that have taken place in this region over the past fifteen years. Much of this research is still unpublished, and this volume provides a venue for Mexican and U.S.-based projects to present a small but important fraction of the new data that are contributing to extensive revisions of the northern low- land sequence. The chapter by Tomas Gallareta Negron et al. on the recent investigations at Labna is informative of recent developments in the Puuc region of Yucatan. The authors present data on settlement, ceramic chronology, and architecture from both the periphery and the site center. These data place the dramatic Classic Period social changes leading up to the cultural transformation colloquially termed the "Collapse" in better perspec- tive. In her chapter on palace termination rituals at Yaxuna, Traci Ardren examines dramatic abandonment processes at three architectural groups. She argues that Maya architecture was infused with a symbolic life- force through ritual activities. As power relations changed and architectural groups abandoned, complex rituals were performed to "kill" this life-force. Ardren's paper expands our knowledge of the material patterning of termination rituals and the social contexts in which they occurred. Alfredo Barrera Rubio and Carlos Peraza Lope pre- sent data on the cave art and surface ceramics of Tixcuytun. This previously unstudied cave contains a wide array of images ranging from handprints to anthropomorphic figures. Ninety-eight percent of the ceramics in the cave were ollas including Timucuy and Chac polychromes. Although similar vessels are thought to be for water collection at other cave loca- tions in Yucatan, water is not available at Tixcuytun. These data suggest a reevaluation of vessels found in other cave contexts across the peninsula including the famous Gruta de Chac. The chapter by the late George Andrews is a summary of his previously published architectural study of the palace at Santa Rosa Xtampak. This palace has been the subject of at least six architectural studies, but has yet to be extensively excavated. Given its size, number of stelae and inscrip- tions, and what is currently known about the ceramic sequence and architecture, Santa Rosa Xtampak is one of the most important sites in the Chenes region. Although Andrews's chapter does not provide any new data, it is a succinct summary of his previously pub- lished work and includes excellent line drawings.
