The striking resurgence of myth and magic in the predominantly historical operatic genre appears to bespeak a growing interest in Lullian tragedie en musique, which in turn can be associated with Frederick IPs French orientation in literary and philosophical matters. But how did deities, sorcerers and Louis quatorzien imports chime with Frederick's notorious resistance to superstition and divinely ordained kingship? The juxtaposition of the relevant operas with theoretical writings from Friderician Prussia yields a multi-faceted, somewhat paradoxical answer, revealing that the marvellous, alongside nourishing a new type of operatic spectacle, served Frederick as a vehicle to impart his philosophical agenda to his state, a former centre of Pietism. Having foretold that Frederick's Berlin would resemble Athens, Voltaire at once saw his bold prediction accomplished.3 Friderician Prussia would of course never fully realise Voltaire's Utopia of the enlightened state, much less remain immune to the despotic excesses of absolutism. Nevertheless, it waved the flag of Reason in ostensible fashion, proclaiming reli gious equality in the spirit of tolerance and raising a Catholic church (the Hedwig skirche) in the heart of Protestant Berlin. As a corollary to its intellectual aspirations, Frederick's reign sparked an opulent artistic programme that endeavoured to make Berlin and Potsdam the peers of Paris and Versailles. One of its hallmarks, next to an impressive literary and architectural legacy, was of course its operatic repertory, which was composed in great part by court Kapellmeister Carl Heinrich Graun (1703/4—59) and staged at the newly built Konigliches Opernhaus in Berlin. In keeping with earlier scholarship on reformist experiments in London,7 Rome,8 Parma,9 Stuttgart-Ludwigsburg,10 Vienna,11 Turin12 and Mannheim-Munich,13 one could ascribe Berlin's shift to myth and magic to a renewed interest in Lullian tragedie en musique, which in turn bespeaks Frederick the Great's cultural horizon. Indeed, Frederick's orientation in literary and philosophical matters was exclusively French, as shall be noted, yet how did deities, sorcerers and Louis quatorzien imports chime with Frederick's worldview, and more particularly with his manifest hostility to superstition and the concept of divine ordination? On what particular grounds could relics of pagan religion and French absolutism enter the operatic sanctuary of an avowed atheist, who would lash out against clergymen throughout his reign? Apollo's return If Berlin became an eighteenth-century Athens, it could not do so overnight. The liberal arts had fared anything but well under Frederick's predecessor, Frederick William I (r. 1713—40). Save for the performance of chorales, Handelian excerpts and instrumental Tafel- und Jagdmusik, little honour had been bestowed by the 'Soldier King' on music, let alone on opera, which remained all but absent from Berlin between 1714 and 1741.15 Apart from economic considerations,16 it was a religious factor that kept opera away: Pietism. Pietism emerged from the sixteenth- and seventeenth-century Reformed Churches of England and the United Provinces, achieving its greatest diffusion in the Lutheran and Calvinist Lander of the German-speaking region. Seeking to promote personal devotion in daily life, the movement professed moral sternness and ascetism in vociferous sermons, a number of which appeared in print. On the one hand, it was cast as an intellectually inane artefact of pre-enlightened civilisations; on the other, it helped validate the deist concept of 'natural religion', that is, a faith unhindered by theological disputes. On pursuing the former strategy, Voltaire paid lip-service to Bernard Le Bovier Fontenelle's De I'origine des fables (1689—99) and Pierre Bayle's Dictionnaire historique et critique (1697), two pioneering tracts of enlightened philosophy that had decried mythology as a lapse of reason and summoned up repulsive visions of priestly impostors indoctrinating the masses with fables.37 Whereas Fontenelle considered mythology as belonging to an irrational stage in the history of mankind, the universal path of which led from ignorance to enlightenment via (Christian) faith, Bayle unequivocally dismissed the pagan gods as anthropomorphic fetishes indulging in parricide, rape, cannibalism, sodomy and incest. According to Bayle, the literal — rather than allegorical — sense of myths had been imposed on superstitious savages through sermons, intimidation and drug-induced hallucinations. The latter institution had enraptured Frederick while still a prince under his father's severe tutelage, in the winter of 1727—8. Vying to outdo the Saxon-Polish Elector August III, Frederick sought to acquire copies of Johann Adolf Hasse's scores for Dresden and had many of them revived, if not with Hasse's original music {La Clemen^a di Tito, 1738 and Arminio, 1745), then at least with their librettos clothed in new tunes by Graun {Cajo Fabri^io, 1734 and Lucio Papirio dittatore, 1742). On 13 December 1748, a privileged group of spectators — those selected by Sveerts — welcomed the premiere of Ifigenia in Aulide at the Konigliches Opernhaus with considerable applause.113 The Italian verses had, as usual, been furnished — or 'lumped together' (dixit Lessing) — by Leopoldo de' Villati, whose prime source of inspiration was, not surprisingly, Jean Racine's Iphigenie (1674).114 Although the Greco-Trojan story may have had few surprises to offer Graun in view of his earlier Iphigenia in Aulis (1728) for Brunswick, the new scenario, which Frederick may have prepared with Algarotti, contained exciting dramaturgical innovations.
