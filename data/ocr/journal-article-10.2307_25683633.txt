As soon as the literate person had begun to sum up his life and his personality in the form of an auto biography" inscribed in his tomb, he included in it statements on his moral personhood. In the course of the centuries these statements grew into rounded selfportraits in which he reported on his doing what he recognized as right actions and his shunning what he judged to be evil doing. He understood his knowledge of right and wrong as an innate capa bility which was articulated by him self as a thinking person, an T'. Altogether, he thought of himself as a person shaped by innate traits which were fostered by growth, education, and experience. An in tense lover of life, he felt sure that rightdoing brought success and happiness, whereas evildoing was bound to bring failure.
