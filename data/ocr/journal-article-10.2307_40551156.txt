<plain_text><page sequence="1">Gretchen van Slyke RIOT AND REVOLUTION IN THE SALON DE 1846 In the penultimate chapter of the Salon de 1846, entitled "Des écoles et des ouvriers," Baudelaire expounds the broad social implications of the esthetic principles which have guided his judgment throughout this work. Yet this chapter, which occupies a crucial place in Baudelaire's theory of Romantic art, has provoked wildly divergent interpretations of his political stance. It suffices to pause on the first paragraphs, in which the sender invites the receiv- er's vicarious participation in a gendarme's assault upon a republican rioter: Avez-vous éprouvé, vous tous que la curiosité du flâneur a souvent fourrés dans une émeute, la même joie que moi à voir un gardien du sommeil public, -sergent de ville ou municipal, la véritable armée, - crosser un républicain? Et comme moi, vous avez dit dans votre cœur: "Crosse, crosse un peu plus fort, crosse encore, municipal de mon cœur; car en ce crossement suprême, je t'adore, et te juge semblable à Jupiter, le grand justicier. L'homme que tu crosses est un ennemi des roses et des parfums, un fanatique des usten- siles; c'est un ennemi de Watteau, un ennemi de Raphaël, un ennemi acharné du luxe, des beaux-arts et des belles-lettres, iconoclaste juré, bourreau de Vénus et d'Apollon! Il ne veut plus travailler, humble et anonyme ouvrier, aux roses et aux parfums publics; il veut être libre, l'ignorant, et il est incapable de fonder un atelier de fleurs et de parfumeries nouvelles. Crosse religieusement les omoplates de l'anarchiste!" It is precisely the socialist critique of individualism and private property which motivates Baudelaire's aversion for the bourgeoisie and for the republicans; for, despite all their obvious political differences, they both stood on the side of individualism and private property. Furthermore, I shall suggest that Baudelaire's irony in this chapter consists in the deliberate juxtaposition of bourgeois and socialist discourses. Because the transition from one type of discourse to another involves a subtle quid pro quo, this play of irony fulfills a strategic function in the complex socio-political arena of the July Monarchy. In order to interpret the opening paragraph of "Des écoles et des ouvriers," we must situate it within the context of the entire chapter. Bearing in mind Baudelaire's antipathy for both the conservative bourgeoisie and the repub- licans, we must suppose that he endorses neither of these options. The remain- der of the chapter, in which Baudelaire abandons the bourgeois discourse that characterizes the first paragraph, elucidates the reasons for this double rejec- tion. Sharing Leroux's preoccupation with the need to promote a social Gestalt, Baudelaire makes passionate arguments for associative schemes of organization. For only the doctrine of association can arrest the insidious advance of individualism which Baudelaire deems responsible for the lamenta- ble state of art and society under the July Monarchy: Cette glorification de l'individu a nécessité la division infinie du territoire de l'art. La liberté absolue et divergente de chacun, la division des efforts et le fractionnement de la volonté humaine ont amené cette faiblesse, ce doute et cette pauvreté d'invention. . . . L'individualité, -cette petite propriété, -a mangé l'originalité collective. (Il, 492) Only the doctrine of association can ensure the eventual regeneration of art and society: Dans les écoles, qui ne sont autre chose que la force d'invention organisée, les indivi- dus vraiment dignes de ce nom absorbent les faibles; et c'est justice, car une large produc- tion n'est qu'une pensée à mille bras. (Il, 492) Thus the doctrine of association which Baudelaire elaborates in the remain- der of the chapter aims to provide a therapeutic solution for the malady of individualism which he sees consuming the social body of the July Monarchy. Furthermore, in his staging of the confrontation between the gendarme and the republican, Baudelaire throws into the face of the bourgeoisie a dilemma of their own making- a dilemma to which they can offer no response save that of violence, which can only exacerbate tensions and risk precipitating civil war.</page><page sequence="3">GRETCHEN VAN SLYKE 297 Such strategies were frequent in the socialist discourse of the period. One need look no further than Leroux in order to find a similar use of bourgeois discourse which is ultimately turned against the bourgeoisie in order to diag- nose and treat systemic social disorder. The leitmotif of Leroux's address "Aux philosophes" consists in the following assertion: "L'anarchie civile et politique est ... la loi de notre temps."5 His analysis may have been initially pleasing to the bourgeoisie, for he appears to blame all the ills of society upon the insubordination of the lower classes. But after throwing the matter into the broader historical perspective, he concludes that it is the secularization of society that lies at the root of anarchy. It is at this point that Leroux makes an important shift in his analysis. Attempting to give voice to proletarian dis- content, he argues that the bourgeoisie, characterized as "des hommes de lucre et d'égoïsme, . . . occupés de leur intérêt privé, . . . livrés à une seule passion, l'avarice" (1,23), must bear the burden of responsibility for the erosion of reli- gious authority which had maintained the rigid structures of class privilege and property distribution under the Ancien Régime: "Donnez-moi donc d'abord des supérieurs que je puisse respecter, ou souffrez que je haïsse les supérieurs que vous me donnerez" (I, 23). Leroux also maintains that justifiable expres- sions of proletarian ire follow from the contradiction between the professed egalitarianism of the July Monarchy and its oligarchical institutions. Dissolv- ing the prestige of the State, such inconsistencies precipitate the growth of cynicism, self-interest and anarchy among the disillusioned lower classes. Then Leroux clearly exposes the dilemma of the bourgeoisie: L'égalité reparaît donc aussitôt que la religion est enlevée au peuple. Le peuple est alors dégagé de toute obéissance; et voilà, ce qu'ont entrevu ceux qui ont érigé cet axiome hypocrite d'une politique infâme: // faut au peuple une religion.** Oui, il faut au peuple une religion ... ou l'égalité; c'est-à-dire que de toute façon il faut à l'esprit humain, l'égalité, qui est sa loi. Il faut à l'homme, à l'esprit humain, l'égalité par l'ordre ou l'égalité par le désordre; l'égalité par le consentement mutuel et l'harmonie, ou l'égalité par la discorde et l'anarchie; l'égalité enfin par la société, ou l'égalité par la dissolution de la société. Il faut au peuple l'égalité la plus grossière, la plus matérielle, la plus fausse par conséquent et la plus décevante, si vous ne pouvez pas constituer reli- gieusement les différences qui existent entre les hommes. (I, 25) On the one hand, Leroux warns that the bourgeoisie's consistent rejec- tion of proletarian demands will merely precipitate the violent dissolution of society. On the other hand, he does not recommend total concession to their demands, for in his view the proletariat has begun to absorb the ideology of individualism to which the bourgeoisie has long adhered. Thus Leroux judges that neither the conservative bourgeoisie with their impossible desire to return to feudal stability nor the republican elements of the proletariat with their mystified demand for strict equality can provide a satisfactory response to the riddle which the Sphinx of the Revolution has been asking since 1789. Under- lining the last term in the revolutionary formula of Liberté, Egalité, Fraternité,</page><page sequence="4">298 FRENCH FORUM only the socialist doctrine of association can point the way out of the futile conflict of the bourgeoisie and the proletariat. Subordinating political revolu- tion to moral transformation, the socialist solution envisaged by Leroux aims to detach both these antagonistic factions from the "système ^individua- lisme politique" ("Aux politiques," I, 194). Putting down the socio-eco- nomic inequalities which persist among classes, Leroux foresees the institution of a highly integrated social hierarchy which would respect "les inégalités natu- relles et les différences de génie et d'inclinations, seuls éléments véritables" ("Aux philosophes," I, 43), and thereby induce harmony between individual interests and collective well-being. Only then will society, reorganized as a Religious Democracy, succeed in realizing "le chef d'oeuvre de la faculté créa- trice ou artistique," which is "un Gouvernement" ("Aux politiques," I, 207). Thus Leroux equates the socialist ideal of association with the Romantic ideal of genius. Having embraced the doctrine of association, Baudelaire, like Leroux, argues that the ultimate source of social disorder lies in the spirit of individu- alism: "Et comme aujourd'hui chacun veut régner, personne ne sait se gouver- ner" (II, 491). Insofar as he diagnoses unbridled individualism at the seat of republican agitation, Baudelaire may be rightly suspected of sympathy with the State's repression of that group, for obvious parallels exist between the belligerent exclamations of the conservative bourgeois by-stander and the sub- sequent declarations of Baudelaire: "II ne veut plus travailler, humble et anonyme ouvrier, aux roses et aux parfums publics; il veut être libre, l'ignorant, et il est incapable de fonder un atelier de fleurs et de parfu- meries nouvelles. Crosse religieusement les omoplates de l'anarchiste!" Ainsi, les philosophes et les critiques doivent-ils impitoyablement crosser les singes artistiques, ouvriers émancipés, qui haïssent la force et la souveraineté du génie. (Il, 490) Les singes sont les républicains de l'art, et l'état actuel de la peinture est le résultat d'une liberté anarchique qui glorifie l'individu, quelque faible qu'il soit, au détriment des associations, c'est-à-dire des écoles. (Il, 492) Yet that ostensible display of sympathy for the "gardien du sommeil public" (II, 490) can obscure only momentarily the deeper considerations which determine this superficial and uneasy alliance. While the bourgeoisie put down republican agitation in order to maintain the political stability conducive to continuing prosperity, Baudelaire condemns instead the sore inadequacy of the political institutions envisaged by the republicans. Owing much to the Amer- ican model of democracy, their conception of society was wholly atomistic- zoocratic, as Baudelaire calls it (II, 298). If Baudelaire's indictment of individ- ualism finds its clearest target in the republicans, his criticism also strikes the bourgeoisie. Just as the republicans translated their faith in individualism into the political doctrine of universal suffrage, the conservative bourgeoisie adhered to the same credo in the form of the liberal economic philosophy of</page><page sequence="5">GRETCHEN VAN SLYKE 299 laissez-faire. Interpreting the principle of "la liberté du travail" in their own best interests, the bourgeoisie viewed all attempts to organize the workplace as a reactionary reversion to the guilds and corporations which had flourished during the Ancien Régime. Consequently, Baudelaire is opposed to the repub- licans as well as to the bourgeoisie, both of whom were staunch enemies of the socialist doctrine of association. Paralleling his indictment of individualism in the political realm, Baude- laire's analysis of the effects of individualism in the artistic realm reveals an attitude which led him to reject imitation in favor of an expressive or super- naturalistic esthetics (II, 432-33). Baudelaire perceives a fundamental analogy between art and politics, which is borne out in his invitation to philosophers and critics to assail "les singes artistiques, ouvriers émancipés, qui haïssent la force et la souveraineté du génie" (II, 490), whom he calls a few pages later "les républicains de l'art" (II, 492). Certainly the category of "singes artisti- ques*1 includes artists who imitate other artists as well as those who imitate nature, but the Salon de 1846 roundly condemns all forms of imitation as characteristic of periods of decadence. Still the question remains: what is the basis of the analogy between mimetic art and republican politics which Bau- delaire appears to take for granted? Reference to Leroux may clarify this matter. In the third part of his address "Aux politiques," Leroux develops this analogy at length when he maintains that mimetic art and republican politics can both be traced back to individualism, which he holds to be characteristic of critical (in the Saint- Simonian sense of the term) periods of history. Then, shallow and uninspired theorists allege that art is nothing but "la copie de la Nature" (I, 206) and gov- ernment nothing but "la reproduction identique, ou ... la représentation des intérêts individuels" (I, 207). But confident that progress will dissolve individ- ualism into association, Leroux dismisses these reductive claims: L'art sort de la nature, mais n'est pas la reproduction de la nature. Et de même le pouvoir social sort de la société, émane des individus, mais ne se confond pas avec les individus, et n'est pas le simple résultat de l'agglomération des hommes qui habitent un pays, ni l'expression adéquate de leurs intérêts particuliers et de leurs vues particulières. (1,207) It is likewise the charge of individualism that prompts the harsh criticism of Eclectic painters which runs through the Salon de 1846. Baudelaire first formulates his condemnation of Eclectics in the opening chapter, which, according to his explicit recommendation, must be read as a necessary pream- ble to "Des écoles et des ouvriers": Ainsi un point de vue plus large sera l'individualisme bien entendu: commander à l'artiste la naïveté de l'expression sincère de son tempérament. . . . Qui n'a pas de tempé- rament n'est pas digne de faire des tableaux, et, comme nous sommes las des imitateurs</page><page sequence="6">300 FRENCH FORUM et surtout des éclectiques, -doit entrer comme ouvrier au service d'un peintre à tempéra- ment. C'est ce que je démontrerai dans un des derniers chapitres. (Il, 419) Linking the Eclectics' misconception of individualism with their practice of imitation, he subsequently intimates that this double failure relates to their austere rationalism, for they disdain the creative resources of passion, which "rapproche les tempéraments analogues et soulève la raison à des hauteurs nouvelles" (II, 419). These accusations receive a lengthier development in the chapter "De l'é- clectisme et du doute." Explicitly reserving for "Des écoles et des ouvriers" his analysis of the "causes majeures" of that philosophy, "dont les ravages sont plus grands que jamais" (II, 473), Baudelaire devotes this entire chapter to a discussion of the symptomatic manifestations of Eclecticism in the arts, a domain in which it has produced "les conséquences les plus visibles et les plus palpables" (II, 473). He levels the charge of doubt and skepticism against Eclectic impartiality. From such impoverished and impoverishing rationalism Baudelaire deduces their inability to conceive an ideal: "Quelque habile que soit un éclectique, c'est un homme faible; car c'est un homme sans amour. Il n'a donc pas d'idéal" (II, 473). The chapter concludes with a resounding denunciation of the mimetic trap into which Eclectic painters inevitably fall: Un éclectique ignore que la première affaire d'un artiste est de substituer l'homme à la nature et de protester contre elle. Cette protestation ne se fait pas de parti pris, froide- ment, comme un code ou une rhétorique; elle est emportée et naïve, comme le vice, comme la passion, comme l'appétit. Un éclectique n'est donc pas un homme. (Il, 473) Baudelaire's condemnation proceeds from the assumption that the method of the Eclectic painters parallels the method of the Eclectic philosophers, whom the socialists unanimously denounced as the intellectual pillars of the July Monarchy. Here, once again, it is helpful to refer to Leroux, whose anal- yses of Eclectic philosophy popularized the analogy between Eclectic thought and art which Baudelaire appears to take for granted. Judging that the funda- mental error of Eclecticism consists in its pervasive skepticism, Leroux charges in his "Réfutation de l'éclectisme" that such systematic doubt precludes the perception of unifying principles and providential ideals in all domains of endeavor: ... si ces philosophes accrédités et investis d'une certaine autorité dans l'Etat n'ont pour toute philosophie . . . qu'un déplorable Pyrrhonisme, voyez quel dommage il en résulte! ... il [Eclecticism] enchaîne les esprits, il ôte à l'intelligence ses forces ... ; il empêche tout sentiment religieux, social, patriotique de germer et de croître; il jette dans la société et dans le gouvernement de la société non pas seulement de la léthargie et une lâche tor- peur, mais le principe de la démoralisation et de la corruption. (Il, 275) Because their rationalist psychology leads them to emulate the illusory objec- tivity of science and industry, the Eclectic philosophers fall into a specular or</page><page sequence="7">GRETCHENVANSLYKE 301 mimetic trap which Leroux explains by means of a suggestive analogy with painting: Le dernier mot de votre philosophie est une impasse. (11,490-91) Such declarations of the indisputable superiority of art in the past do not seal Baudelaire's resignation to the inferiority of art in the contemporary world. He confidently pinpoints in the mode of production the primary cause of the relative richness or poverty of art in various periods: "Là des écoles, et ici des ouvriers émancipés" (II, 49Ί). Encouraged by the belief that his gener- ation is living through an era of transition, Baudelaire casts an expectant eye towards the future. His hope that a great tradition may be not merely resur- rected out of the past, but born anew from the providential ferment of the present, is nourished by the conviction that the potential for regeneration remains untapped in those rare but powerful artists like Delacroix who con- trive to "surnager et paraître dans le chaos d'une liberté épuisante et stérile" (II, 492). Literally "excentriques" (II, 492), insofar as they are for the time being relegated to the periphery of the art world, these artists nonetheless</page><page sequence="8">302 FRENCH FORUM succeed in exerting their "domination sourde et involontaire" (II, 491) well beyond the confines of their solitary studios. Ostensibly rebuffed but furtively obeyed by "les singes artistiques," their authority provides an embryonic model of a hierarchical scheme of association which may renew the practice of collective originality and assist in the birth of another great tradition. At the origin of the subtle network of influence created by such an artist as Delacroix, Baudelaire perceives the magnetic attraction of "naïveté," which he defines as "la domination du tempérament dans la manière" (II, 491). In another chapter of the Salon de 1846, Baudelaire posits that naïveté and tem- pérament translate the metaphysical properties of calorique into the psychol- ogy of genius. Acting as the "travail intérieur" of nature, calorique results in matter's "perpétuelle vibration, laquelle fait trembler les lignes et complète la loi du mouvement éternel et universel" (II, 422). Baudelaire considered Leroux's endeavor "un dictionnaire des croyances humaines" (II, 40), and among his works he must have stumbled upon "De Dieu, ou la vie considérée dans les êtres particuliers et dans l'être universel," where a similar concept of calorique plays a major role. A term which was in current usage at the time, calorique generally functioned as a scientific designa- tion for heat.7 But in Leroux's article, calorique represents an immanent uni- tary principle in nature, the physical manifestations of which do not exhaust its metaphysical cause. The source of the law of universal and general move- ment, its chief characteristic is vibration. In his discussion of calorique, Leroux argues that we have no immediate experience of objects in nature. We instead perceive only the quantity of calorique which invests a particular object at a particular time. When an object's caloric investment changes, its state also changes. Such changes can never affect an isolated object. Calorique, which he calls the "action de la Vie universelle, qui lie entre eux tous les corps"8 and the "contact électrique de tout" (p. 39), designates the flow of energy within a universal field. Thus a change in energy at any point in the field induces change throughout the entire field. Concluding his discussion of calorique within the physical universe, Leroux indicates its metaphysical source: II semble en effet que toute notre science moderne n'ait fait que vérifier et confirmer cette idée des anciens, que tous les êtres étaient plongés dans un fluide unique, l'éther, et que le Dieu suprême gouvernait le monde en imprimant la vie à chacun de ces êtres par le moyen de ce fluide, (pp. 38-39) Thus calorique comes to describe a panentheistic (A. Béguin's term) concep- tion of God- an imponderable electric fluid- through which Leroux clearly marks his distance from Christianity and its anthropomorphic traditions. The cornerstone of Leroux's religious faith, calorique also determines his social philosophy. Although he maintains that calorique is "réparti à chacun</page><page sequence="9">GRETCHEN VAN SLYKE 303 dans une certaine mesure" ("De Dieu," p. 39), he deduces from its differential investment among individuals their various degrees of sovereignty: La Souveraineté n'est à l'état parfait, à l'état complet, qu'en Dieu. Mais de Dieu elle descend continuellement dans ses créatures Elle est la lumière donnée à chacun, à quelques-uns, à tous; et comme elle est la même lumière en essence donnée en propor- tions seulement différentes, à un instant quelconque de la durée, à chacun, à quelques- uns, à tous, elle est la cause qui rend possible la société humaine et légitime un gouverne- ment de cette société, en harmonisant et faisant concourir chacun, quelques-uns et tous. . . . Elle est, à chaque moment, à l'état latent dans chacun, à l'état d'expansion dans quelques-uns, à l'état de manifestation dans tous. ("Aux politiques," I, 163) Then he designates those rare individuals in whom calorique exists in a state of expansion as the "aristocratie de l'avenir, . . . c'est-à-dire les vraiment meil- leurs, en d'autres termes, les frères aînés, les plus savants, les plus aimants" ("Aux politiques," Ι, Ί64). Despite his oft-repeated profession of democratic ideals, Leroux is clearly not an egalitarian in the strict sense of the term: A aucun instant de la durée de l'Humanité, /'identité des conditions ne sera l'égalité véri- table; car nous ne sommes pas identiques. Nous n'avons identiquement ni les mêmes besoins, ni les mêmes aptitudes, ni par conséquent les mêmes droits. Cette prétendue égalité par identité serait la destruction de la liberté de chacun. ("Aux philosophes," 1,25) Thus the differential investment of calorique is central to his conception of Religious Democracy, which he defines against the leveling tendencies of Babouvist political thought: II faut au peuple l'égalité la plus grossière, la plus matérielle, la plus fausse par conséquent et la plus décevante, si vous ne pouvez pas constituer religieusement les différences qui existent entre les hommes. ("Aux philosophes," I, 25) Prompting Leroux to posit various degrees of sovereignty in the political sphere, calorique impels Baudelaire to determine various degrees of aptitude in the artistic sphere. Calorique may naturally imbue creative individuals with naïveté and tempérament, but Baudelaire is convinced that these qualities remain "un privilège divin dont presque tous sont privés" (II, 491). Hence, their unequal distribution among artists gives rise to the distinctions, funda- mental to the Salon de 1846, between the rare créateurs and the multitude of ouvriers. From naïveté, which consists in the domination of tempérament over the model given in nature, Baudelaire infers the necessary domination of the créateur over the ouvriers, who, according to the associative ideal sketched out in these pages, "font, par obéissance et par tradition, ce que le maître fait par la fatalité de son organisation" (II, 491). At the same time that artistic genius reveals the providential laws which guide the development of the nat- ural realm, it also indicates the hierarchical form that artistic schools, which</page><page sequence="10">304 FRENCH FORUM Baudelaire considers a subgroup of more extensive schemes of social organiza- tion, would assume under the dictates of these same laws. The correlation between the esthetic ideal and the social ideal may be illus- trated in another fashion. Just as calorique establishes the profound unity of nature, so naïveté and tempérament prompt genius to fulfill this universal imperative through the subordination of detail to a harmoniously integrated, hierarchically ordered whole: "Tart n'étant qu'une abstraction et un sacrifice du détail à l'ensemble" (II, 424). Likewise, the doctrine of association com- mands the submission of less gifted individuals to genius, whose hierarchical government is endowed with theocratic prestige: Tel qui rentre aujourd'hui dans la classe des singes . . . n'est et ne sera jamais qu'un peintre médiocre; autrefois, il eût fait un excellent ouvrier. Il est donc perdu pour lui et pour tous. C'est pourquoi il eût mieux valu dans l'intérêt de leur salut, et même de leur bonheur, que les tièdes eussent été soumis à la férule d'une foi vigoureuse. (Il, 492) Some 17 years later, despite the bitter disappointments of history which had forced him to relegate the associative ideal to the status of a Utopian mirage,9 Baudelaire again takes up and more fully elucidates the analogy between artistic genius and social organization which structures the argument of "Des écoles et des ouvriers." As he writes in Le Peintre de la vie moderne: Un artiste ayant le sentiment parfait de la forme, mais accoutumé à exercer surtout sa mémoire et son imagination, se trouve . . . comme assailli par une émeute de détails, qui tous demandent justice avec la furie d'une foule amoureuse d'égalité absolue. Toute justice se trouve forcément violée; toute harmonie détruite, sacrifiée; mainte trivialité devient énorme; mainte petitesse, usurpatrice. Plus l'artiste se penche vers le détail, plus l'anarchie augmente. Qu'il soit myope ou presbyte, toute hiérarchie et toute subordina- tion disparaissent. (Il, 698-99) Marking an attempt to bring social practice into conformity with the prov- idential laws deduced from the principle of calorique, the doctrine of associa- tion which Baudelaire exposes in the penultimate chapter of the Salon de 1846 contains many reminiscences of the theocratic ideal conceived by the Saint-Simonian school of socialism on the eve of the Revolution of 1830.10 Among the numerous points of comparison, one should stress the hierarchi- cal structure of association whose graduated ranks intend to recognize the unequal distribution of natural gifts among individuals. At the summit of the hierarchy stands the artist-priest-savant who, through the inspiration of his passions, divines and directs the destinies of mankind. Thus the artist consti- tutes the radiant center of a highly integrated collective order which attempts to resolve, through a model born of an idealized conception of medieval soci- ety, the contemporary problems resulting from the advent of social, political and industrial revolution.</page><page sequence="11">GRETCHEN VAN SLYKE 305 The obvious intermediary between Baudelaire and the Saint-Simonians is Leroux.11 Briefly a disciple of that socialistic religion in the early months of the July Monarchy, Leroux turned apostate in order to found a more demo- cratic form of socialism. Yet he never became a strict egalitarian, for his uncompromising idealism did not allow him to reconcile the moral imperative which underlay his social theories with the reality of popular aspirations. Con- vinced that society had to find a means to distinguish among individuals on the basis of their innate aptitudes, Leroux translated into terms of calorique the Saint-Simonian promotion of capacité ("A chacun suivant sa capacité, à chaque capacité suivant ses œuvres") and designated those rare beings who possessed high levels of calorique as the sole legitimate lawgivers. So despite his desire to relax the severe lines of the Saint-Simonian model, Leroux could not entirely divest his thought of their authoritarian structures. Starkly rede- fining the Saint-Simonian legacy passed on to him, Baudelaire resolves, in favor of a more arrogant expression of authority, the contradictions which remain implicit in Leroux's socio-political thought. So it is that in "Des écoles et des ouvriers" Baudelaire infers from the differential investment of calorique among individuals a dictatorial model of strictly graduated rights and privi- leges, summed up in the following statement: "Peu d'hommes ont le droit de régner, car peu d'hommes ont une grande passion" (II, 491).
