(Reproduced with gracious permission of the Librarian)</page><page sequence="5">The Anglo-Norman Verse Prophecies of Merlin INTRODUCTION History Wace chose not to include Merlins prophecies in the Roman de Brut, the translation of Geoffrey of Monmouth's Historia regum Britanniae which he completed in 1155, because, as he says, he did not understand them: 'Ne vuil sun livre translater/ Quant jo nel sai interpreter' (7539-40) ('I do not want to translate his book,/ For I do not know how to interpret it').1 In keeping with his image of cautious historian, Wace claims it would be a disservice to his public if he were to translate the prophecies and make errors in the process. Thus, he prefers silence to misinformation: 'Nule rien dire ne vuldreie,/ Que si ne fust cum jo dirreie (7541-42) ('I would not want to say anything,/ Lest it were not as I would say it'). Wace had reasons for this omission, probably political in nature, but they cannot be addressed fully here.2 What needs to be emphasized is Wace's self-consciousness as an editor when he decides to alert his audience to the existence of the prophecies. His decision not to include them also reminds us of the differences between the concept of a text in his age and in our own, and of the editorial processes so often used by writers of his period whose idea of a translation often included translating, collating, and adapting material drawn from multiple sources.3 In an attempt to make Wace's Brut more complete,' three redactors inserted complete versions of Merlin's prophecies?that is, translations into Anglo-Norman verse of the Latin text, the Prophetia Merlini {PM)?into the Brut (Anglo-Norman MSS D, Ly andv4). There is also a copy added to a fragment of the end of the Brut (B, also Anglo-Norman), another in an anonymous Anglo-Norman Brut (H), two others not associated with any Brut poem (F and O, also Anglo-Norman), and one fragment of the prophecies (Anglo-Norman MS London, British Library, Additional 48212).4 These Anglo-Norman verse prophecies (henceforth the ANP) can be seen to belong to the earliest of the three written traditions of Merlin's prophecies, as proposed by Caroline Eckhardt in her edition of a fifteenth-century English</page><page sequence="6">2 ANGLO-NORMAN VERSE PROPHECIES OF MERLIN commentary on the Prophetia Merlini? Referring to the early Latin texts of the PM, Eckhardt cites three modes of transmission to characterize these early traditions: 1) as part of Geoffrey of Monmouth's Historia regum Britanniae {=Historia), 2) as a text independent of the Historia, and 3) as individual predictions cited in other works.6 She calls this tripartite early development the 'Historia tradition,' since whatever the setting, it is Geoffrey's Merlin prophecy from the Historia, a limited and generally stable text, that is being transmitted.'7 Eckhardt identifies two other Merlin traditions which are more loosely related to Geoffrey's text. The first, which she calls the post-Historia tradition, involves the production of prophecies attributed to the Merlin figure popularized by Geoffrey; Geoffrey himself participated in this tradition through the Vita Merlini, a poem in which some of the prophecies from the Historia are reproduced with new elements.8 Other participants in this post-Historia tradition include Richard of Ireland, to whom are attributed the French prose Prophesies de Merlin, around 1276, and the anonymous author of the 'Six Last Kings' prophecy found in the English Brut chronicles of the thirteenth century.9 Closely related to, but not exactly a subset of, what Eckhardt calls the post-Historia tradition, is a range of texts which participated in the wider interest in Merlin, texts of large political interest but where that interest was not necessarily generated by the Historia; these include the Welsh poem, 'Gwasgargerdd Myrddin' (c. 1163), a political prophecy supposedly uttered by Myrddin from the grave, and the Prophetia Merlini (c. 1153-54) of John of Cornwall, whose commentary was based on prophecies many of which did not come from Geoffrey.10 Lastly, Eckhardt signals a third tradition of prophetic material associated with Merlin or the non-Historia tradition: this tradition, which belongs to romance and not to historiography or political writing, includes Robert de Boron's Merlin and other texts which emphasize the characterization of Merlin as enchanter, but bear little if any relation to the Merlin of the Historia.11 The ANP belong, then, not to the post-Historia tradition as does the Continental French prose text of Richard of Ireland, but to the Historia tradition, presenting a version of the prophecies almost identical in content to those found in the vulgate and First Variant versions of the Historia. The ANP are also the first complete vernacular versions of the PM; the earliest known complete Middle English version is the one embedded in an unedited section of the fourteenth-century text, Thomas Castleford's Chronicle.12 If patterns of manuscript preservation can be seen as more than accidental, it appears that the Anglo-Norman aristocracy and its descendants were more interested in the bulk of Merlin's political prophecies than were either the</page><page sequence="7">INTRODUCTION 3 English-reading public or the Continental French public. Why would the D redactor want to mention Merlin s moving of the stones to Stonehenge before he has introduced Merlin in the narrative, and thereby place the event prior to the death of Vortigern? To foreshadow events is the most likely possibility, though this event and others in the preamble are related in a very undramatic manner. What is more puzzling is a more serious, perhaps purposeful, confusion of chronology which results from placing a narrative of rhe famine and Cadwallader s exile here in this passage. In the vulgate Historia, the First Variant, and Wace, Cadwallader appears at the end of the narrative, many generations (roughly 150 years) after the death (or disappearance) of Arthur. In the vulgate Historia, Geoffrey implies that roughly 250 years passed between the death of Cadwallader (c. 689) and the exile of the Britons marking the end of British dominion, and assigns the beginning of definitive Anglo-Saxon rule to Athelstan's reign (924-939) in the tenth century, a marked departure from the traditional view that British dominion ended in the seventh century.26 The author of the First Variant</page><page sequence="12">8 ANGLO-NORMAN VERSE PROPHECIES OF MERLIN keeps Cadwallader at the end of the text, but eliminates the allusion to the time between the end of British rule and the beginning of Anglo-Saxon rule by making the tenth-century King Athelstan a contemporary of the seventh century Cadwallader, in order, it would appear, to bring Geoffreys history into accord with traditional Bedan chronology of Anglo-Saxon dominion beginning in the seventh century. Wace also keeps the exile of Cadwallader and the Britons at the end of his text, and makes Athelstan appear to be a contemporary of Cadwallader. However, as if to reconcile Geoffreys vulgate text with the First Variant, Wace provides Athelstan with the correct genealogy of the tenth-century king, by making Athelstan appear to be who he really was?i.e., a very strong Anglo-Saxon leader, but not the first, having followed a long struggle among Anglo-Saxon leaders, rather than a long period of British dominion?albeit many years too soon. This reorganizing of chronology?or perhaps jumbled flashbacks and reprises?creates another loop-like situation such as is found in the A interpolation, where the narrative ends up like a dragon chasing its tail, with post-Arthurian content (Cadwallader) becoming pre-Arthurian only to become post-Arthurian again as Wace's text is picked up. This new chronology seems to add confusion to the narrative and appears to be unpolemical. In addition, the D redactor eliminates the final section on Merlin's predictions of Vortigern's death; following Helias's epilogue, Merlin just gets up and leaves! However, if taken as less purposeful?that is, if the D redactor found the version of the prophecies with an existing preamble and just inserted it where he saw fit, regardless of the chronological confusion that might result in the mind of the audience?the opening lines of the preamble could be read simply as an overview of early British history, from the Britons' perspective. However, despite the differences between the two families of ANP, similarities outweigh the differences, largely owing to shared attitudes toward the 'sacred' nature of the prophetic text evident in the Latin PM tradition as well. Geoffrey's Historia is extant in over two hundred manuscripts; of these, in addition to the nearly two hundred witnesses of the vulgate Historia alone, eight MSS represent the First Variant version, including one in which the First Variant is conflated with the Second, and another representing a hybrid conflation of the First Variant and the vulgate, plus the vulgate itself; eighteen represent the Second Variant version.35 Although no edition of the Second Variant has yet been published, Neil Wright's edition of the First Variant demonstrates that in the eight manuscripts (five 'pure,' three conflated), the only chapters that underwent virtually no changes when compared with the vulgate were those containing the PMJ6 As prophetic material, Merlin's text was treated as sacred: the original words were as important as the concepts they conveyed. Although the alexandrine version is wordier than the decasyllabic, the great majority of the main characters and descriptions remain the same, denoting an attitude toward prophetic material similar to that of the Latin redactors. This brings us back to the question, why did Wace omit the prophecies? He had translated saints' lives, and thus had worked with texts on the periphery of the sacred. He was also and foremost an historian, and as Sir Richard Southern has pointed out, 'Prophecy filled the world picture, past, present and future; and it was the chief inspiration of all historical thinking.'37 For reasons which remain obscure but which were most likely political in origin, Wace was afraid, however, to handle the prophecies, even to translate</page><page sequence="15">INTRODUCTION 11 them, although he was writing during a period which produced at least six Latin commentaries on the PM?S In a 1938 article in the British Museum Quarterlyy R. Flower provides evidence which may shed some light on Wace s reticence.39 Flower reports that the MS now called Add. 45103 was copied at Christ Church, Canterbury, in or around 1275, in a script more familiar in psalters than in vernacular manuscripts.'40 The provenance of the manuscript suggests that this revised version of Wace's Brut was prepared for the more scholarly clerical community, rather than for the lay community, which in turn suggests the possibility that the social composition of Wace's audience may have been a factor in his avoidance of the cryptic prophecies, perhaps more suited to a reading audience with time to contemplate the text and its often arcane symbolism, rather than a largely lay listening audience which might become restive during the recital of a baffling, barely accessible 600 line passage of prophecies. The PM as Literature Michael Curley has distinguished three parts in Geoffrey's PM: Part I, prophecies 1-6 (112:1-6, pp. 74-75; A 1-80, L, 1-75) or what is told in the main body of the Historia; Part II, prophecies 7- 11 (113:7-11, pp. 75-76; A 81-126, Z, 76-117) or from the end of the Historia through the reign of Henry I, the 'lion of justice'; and Part III, prophecy 12 on the fate of the lion's cubs to the end of the prophecies (prophecy 74) (113:12-117:74; A 127 670, Z, 118-590).41 It is also worth noting that by far the longest of the three sections of the PM is devoted to events projected beyond Geoffrey's own era. As pointed out by Curley and others, Geoffrey's text is characterized by his use of animal symbolism?an aspect which prompted Rupert Taylor ro go as far as to name this style of prophecy 'Galfridian'?and an ever-increasing vagueness from the beginning of Part III onward; Faral commented that at that point in the text, Geoffrey had apparently succumbed au d?lire le plus ?chevel?.'42 It is undeniable that, as Southern comments in reference to the wide dissemination of Merlin's prophecies, we have the strange phenomenon of apparently unrelieved gibberish claiming the anxious attention of men of high intelligence and sophistication, and here again we can only explain the phenomenon by the need which men felt to believe in a wide dissemination of divinely inspired truth about historical events throughout all ages and peoples.'43 It is ironic perhaps that this gibberish' fascinated some of the most prominent intellectuals, including John of Salisbury who attempted to explain events in Brittany during the Becket controversy in the context of</page><page sequence="16">12 ANGLO-NORMAN VERSE PROPHECIES OF MERLIN Merlin's prophecy of the Eagle of the Broken Covenant (114:14, p. 76; D, 149-52, Z, 139-40); John of Cornwall, who at the behest of the bishop of Exeter translated and commented on some newly discovered prophecies attributed to Merlin; and Gerald of Wales, who sought to bring to light unknown prophecies to create a fusion between contemporary events and ancient Celtic prophecy.44 On closer inspection, along with the sibylline and biblical echoes, and animal imagery from ancient Celtic sources, the 'gibberish' in the PM is much more patterned than has previously been noted, and it also becomes purposefully more vague through the less frequent use of geographical references after prophecy 11 (113:12, p. 76 ff; Z), 134?end, Z, 122-end). In Part I, references to place names including the forests of Africa, Gaul, London, Menevia, York, Cornwall, the City of Legions, Armorica (i.e. Brittany), and Durobernia (i.e. Canterbury) are interwoven with animal labels representing people including the Boar of Cornwall, the German Worm, the Red Dragon, and the White Dragon. This use of recognized place names and animals associated with peoples or places identified in the text lends an illusion of comprehensibility, building confidence in the reader. With somewhat fewer specific geographic and animal figure references, Part II was probably read as an integral text with somewhat less confidence than Part I, though isolated prophecies were interpreted from this section: some more frequently cited examples include the prophecy of the people dressed in wood and in iron corselets' (113:9, p. 75; D, 95-96, Z, 88-89) frequently read as the coming of William Is conquering army of Normans;45 the prophecy of the Eagle of the Broken Covenant (114:14, p. 76; D, 149-52, Z, 139-40) read as Eleanor of Aquitaine, who 'will rejoice in her third nesting';46 the 'Vae tibi, Neustria prophecy (found in some MSS in section 114 between prophecies 13 and 14, but not in Wright or Z&gt;; Z, 124-26), read as the scattering of Henry Is body parts upon burial;47 the prophecy of the 'She-lynx' (115:18, p. 77; D, 177-82, Z, 163-67) on account of whom 'Normandy will lose both its isles and be deprived of its former dignity,' read as King Henry III, although three prophecies later there is a passage which places this section in time by referring to Cadwallader's summons of Conan.48 The increasing vagueness of Part III did not preclude, however, isolated prophecies from being read with relative surety in later centuries. The best known of these is prophecy 32 which refers to a girl from Canutes forest who is sent to cure her people (116:32-33, pp. 78-79; D&gt; 295-308, Z, 261 75), often read by the French as a prediction of the coming of Joan of Arc, even though the reference is embedded in a context of struggles among peoples in Britain and contains no allusions to conflicts generated by outsiders.49</page><page sequence="17">INTRODUCTION 13 Although it affords the possibility of specific readings of isolated prophecies, the last two-thirds of the PM, roughly from prophecy 21 to the end (115:21-117:74, pp. 77-84; A 204-670, Z, 184-591), is increasingly opaque, though this did not deter commentators; in fact, aside from actual commentaries and isolated references made by historians and other scholars, Julia Crick has found that the prophecies sections of more than twenty Historia manuscripts contain glosses relating their contents to events in the Anglo-Saxon and Anglo-Norman periods and later, not including manuscripts in which the PM is glossed with events found in the Historia itself.50 Part III contains fewer references to cities; references to regions, however, such as the forest of Calidon Wood and the Daneian Forest, remain frequent until the beginning of the astrological prophecies at prophecy 73 (117:73, p. 84; A 631, L, 559). Not surprisingly, the River Severn, which separated Wales from English Mercia and Wessex, and Cornwall are the last place names before the two long, final astrological prophecy passages. In fact, in the last two-thirds of the text, rivers appear to replace cities as geographical markers, forming a patchwork with references to other water images. In prophecy 30, the Severn Sea flows through seven mouths (116:30, p. 78; A 267, Ly 234), the River Usk boils for seven months (A 268, Z, 235), the baths at Bath grow cold and poisonous (A 271-72, L, 238-39), and the Thames turns into blood (A 274, Z, 242); other water images include the three springs of Winchester (one good, two evil) (116:31, p. 78; A 277-84, Z, 245-52), the Thames surrounding London (116:37, P- 79; A 344, Z, 307), the English Channel narrowing (116:38, p. 79; D, 351-54, Z, 312-14); the Thames overflowing (116:49, P- 81; A 445, Z, 394); the Spring of Galabes (116:50, p. 81: A 451, Ly 399), and turbulent seas (116:67, P- 83; A 593, Z, 527). Virtually all of these water symbols are sinister and contrary-to-nature, thus contributing to the apocalyptic tone of the latter portion of the text. Other nature images which are interspersed among the dark water images are primarily of trees, most notably the positive image of the Cornish oaks that will flourish when 'the island shall be called by the name of Brutus and the title given it by the foreigners shall be done away with' (115:20, p. 77; A 197-202, Ly 179-83).51 Less benevolent is the mystical tree on the Tower of London (116:27, P- 78; A 247-60, Z, 217-29) which has only three branches, the first of which is torn away by the North Wind; the second branch destroys the third branch through an overabundance of leaves, normally a good thing, but here with a noxious result, and becomes healthy to foreign birds but injurious to native ones. This unusual tree is a symbol with at least a double function: on the surface, it is an image of nature turned against itself, of a</page><page sequence="18">14 ANGLO-NORMAN VERSE PROPHECIES OF MERLIN world upside down, but the tree is ironically a symbol of a relatively common situation in history and politics, struggles among ruling factions who mistreat their own but are welcoming to outsiders. In this unnatural world, the Daneian Forest speaks, exhorting Wales to join with Cornwall to defeat Winchester (116:35, p. 79; D, 317-30, Z, 283-95). As this unnatural natural world grows more menacing, the animals grow more numerous and peculiar: from the beginning of the text where the Red and White Dragons, the Boar of Cornwall (Arthur), and the German Worm (Gormund) carry out fairly transparent feats, one moves to the Lion of Justice (the epithet of Henry I), the most benevolent of the characters other than the Boar of Cornwall, to the lions cubs who are transformed into salt-water fishes (113:12, p. 76; Z&gt;, 127?28, Z, 116-17). It is probably these same cubs who wreak havoc (114:15, p. 76; Dy 153-60, Z, 141-47) and lead to the appearance of the She-lynx (115:18, p. 77; Z), 177, Z, 163) during whose time Normandy loses both its isles (probably England and Ireland, not Jersey and Guernsey). Other fierce boars arrive, followed alternately by flood, famine, fornication, a hedgehog, a heron, more boars, a fox, a wolf, a bear, a snake, an ox sporting a wolf's head, an ass, a goat, swans, a raven, kites, an owl, the Snake of Malvern, a worm, and seven lions with goats' heads, and still greater wantonness. The circle seems to close temporarily with the appearance of another dragon (the Dragon of Worcester, 116:54, p. 81; D, 485, Z, 433), echoing those found toward the beginning of the text, but the narrative does not end here. This dragon is defeated by the Giant of Wickedness (116:54, p. 81; Dy 481, Ly 430), one of several anthropomorphic figures of Part III, followed by another series made up primarily of animals: the Boar of Totnes, a lion of Gloucester, a bull, an adder, a fox, a snow-white Giant (a Christ figure to parallel the Giant of Wickedness as Antichrist?), a man with a sickle, the Charioteer of York (116:63, P- 82; D, 551, Z, 490), three more bulls, the Farmer from Albany (116:65, p. 83; Z&gt;, 573, Z, 509), a plague, and a cure brought by the foster daughter (or ward) of the Scourger (116:66, p. 83; Dy 583, Ly 518), followed by two final pairs: two men, then a horned dragon and a lion. As relentless and arbitrary as this list may seem, it is marked by a cyclical quality and by a theme of perpetual change. Cycles of beasts, trees, and water alternate with cycles of trees, beasts, and famine, followed by wantonness, beasts, rivers, etc. The cyclical nature of the narrative is also underscored by the motif of metamorphosis. While none of the human figures in the prophecies change form, many of the animals metamorphose into other animals, as when the ashes of the Mountain Ox are transformed into</page><page sequence="19">INTRODUCTION 15 swans which swim away on dry land as if it were water; the swans then eat fish inside fish and swallow up men inside men,' finally in old age being transformed yet again, this time into sea-wolves in order to continue the cycle of treachery beneath the sea (116:48, p. 81; Dy 435-42, Z, 386-91). Read metaphorically, the ghastly food chain in this passage portrays a world without apparent sense, yet one which follows a set path of destruction from which there is no escape in any medium?air, land, or sea?one stage following upon another. Although the swans which rise from the ashes of the Mountain Ox are reminiscent of the phoenix, this transformation is not symbolic of a renaissance or a passage to a higher plane of existence, but rather one stage in a nightmarish progression where the emphasis is on one figure being absorbed by another, subsumed and thus destroyed, not given new life in order to flourish in a new form. It is difficult to read the motif of metamorphosis here as symbolic of various rites of passage, or as means of narrating the resolution of conflicts; rather it appears to be a means of projecting fears of catastrophe, with the emphasis on the inevitability and the arbitrary, chaotic nature of change.52 Just before the narrative appears to disintegrate into an astrologers nightmare?or a G?tterd?mmerung, as Tatlock called it?there is one final reference to the victory of Cornwall which has been turned into a Boar by a soldier to protect it from the wolf whose tail is coiled around it.53 But even this victory is short-lived as the Boar devastates the provinces and then hides its head in the Severn (116:70, p. 83; D&gt; 616, Z, 545), in a symbolic act of ignorance, drowning, and defeat. Men become drunk, harvests dry up (more famine), and, in yet another image of the world upside down, roots and branches change places (116:70-117:72, pp. 83-84; Z&gt;, 617-28, Z, 549-58). Nothing is what it seems. What remains is to read the stars. While a series of astrological predictions may seem to us as opaque as the narrative which precedes them, and equally far-fetched, by ending this text with astrological predictions, Geoffrey was participating in the growing interest in astrology toward the middle of the twelfth century, fueled by the progress of Islamic science, the invention of the astrolabe, and the possibility of more exact observations and charts toward the middle of the twelfth century. Together with biblical, sibylline, and other pagan sources of prophetic insight, astrology was becoming 'one more weapon in the prophetic armoury.'54 In an introductory summary to his OpusMaius (1266-67), Roger Bacon addressed the following ideas on prophecy to the Pope:</page><page sequence="20">l6 ANGLO-NORMAN VERSE PROPHECIES OF MERLIN If only the Church would examine the prophecies of the Bible, the sayings of the saints, the sentences of sibyl and Merlin and other pagan prophets, and would add thereto astrological considerations and experimental knowledge, it would without doubt be able to provide usefully against the coming of Antichrist... For not all prophecies are irrevocable and many things are said in the prophets about the coming of Antichrist which will come to pass only through the negligence of Christians.55 While Geoffrey could not have foreseen with accuracy future developments in astrological prediction, the mid-thirteenth-century redactors of the ANP were likely aware of views such as Roger Bacons and may possibly have shared them. However, Wace did not. In addition to the historians trepidation in the face of political prophecy, it is quite possible that he was also deterred from translating the prophecies?or from including the ANP prophecies as represented by D and L if the exemplars were in existence by the mid-twelfth century?by the astrological predictions at the end. A poet who claims to have been angry with himself for having believed the tale-tellers' stories of the forest of Broc?liande may very well have been too careful to include astrological predictions in his BrutJ6 A third possible explanation for Wace's reluctance to translate the prophecies is that they served little purpose for him and his audience and may perhaps have been counter-productive. That is to say, the earliest intent of the prophecies may have been to envisage a more glorious future for a minority population?in Geoffrey's text, the indigenous Britons or Welsh. As a Norman poet writing for the Anglo-Norman, Anglo-Angevin aristocracy of the mid- to late-twelfth century, Wace was a member of a minority population, but one which was currently in power and in need of narratives which sought to enhance and perpetuate its prestige rather than in searching out strategies to regain what was lost long ago. Granted, some members of Wace's mid-twelfth-century audience may have been interested in the prophecies because of the general interest in vaticination in the period. However, a text containing exclusively a group of political prophecies reclaiming territory, cultural superiority, and political power on behalf of those whose land they held, would not have had the appeal for Wace's audience that these kinds of prophecies would have had for the Anglo Norman audience of the thirteenth century, at a time when the French speaking English public was becoming more English in its self-identity and interests.57 Whatever his reasons, it remains clear that while Wace was fascinated by the past and the lessons it held for contemporary audiences, he was content</page><page sequence="21">INTRODUCTION 17 to leave the deciphering of visionary predictions?particularly if they were cast in thickly-veiled symbolic language?to others.
