All these factors mean that the premisses from which we judge of the real sense of the work are very complicated : Sponde is writing to please Henry of Navarre, and to further his own ambitions ; at the same time he is making a serious contribution to Henry's theological instruction, helping him to interpret current affairs correctly in the theological perspective ; he is also bearing in mind the wider audience which the published work will reach ; and on a different level, he is writing from his own personal standpoint. 2 To say nothing of the literary ambitions which show through in the style. 1 In the same way D' Aubigné writes his meditation on Psalm LXXXIV primarily for an individual, but in addition for " ceux que pareille amertume de cœur afflige journellement." 2 This standpoint itself is complex, being a mixture of classical and Calvinist elements that sometimes remain in a state of tension.</page><page sequence="9">572 FRANCIS HIGMAN It may be added that Sponde's Méditations attempt a very difficult extension of the scope of the meditation genre. The medita- tions of Bèze, Duplessis-Mornay and D'Aubigné rarely go beyond the personal level of the relationship between the individual soul and God. They are private prayers, and concerned solely with the regulation of the author's, or recipient's, private life. Sponde ventures further, and attempts to deal in theological terms with a political situation - we have for example noted the references to Coutras and the army of mercenaries. This presents a new range of problems : for as long as one deals only with the individual, one can adopt the " eternal perspective " - losses, hardship, defeat in this world will receive their recompense in the world to come. But the eternal perspective is infinitely more difficult to maintain on political matters, especially in 1588 : for surely the political will of God must be active in this life. One can understand the death of an individual in battle ; but surely the will of God is that the cause should triumph - and in these terms a military defeat is well-nigh incomprehensible. What is the meaning of " Thy Kingdom come? " These are the problems which Sponde sets himself to tackle in the Méditations. How far does he succeed ? The early, " human " movement which sees the hand of God in victory but which questions His wisdom in defeat is replaced by a " theological " trust in God in all circumstances ; the early formalist assumption that the preachers of the true faith are by definition the Chosen People is replaced by a spiritual view of the People of God, defined by their attitude of soul rather than by their words. What raises the book above the level of a theological tract, and gives it the moving quality of a personal confession, is the fact that this exposition of faith is not achieved without a struggle. Far from being an expression of absolute faith in God, the whole work reflects a soul sincerely struggling, fighting to understand, to overcome the questionings of human weakness, to accept the incomprehensible will of God - to believe. This is illustrated by a linguistic point : a comparison with Bèze's Chrestiennes meditations or with D'Aubigné's similar works shows that interrogatives are far more numerous in Sponde's meditations than in those of the other authors. The others make statements, profess their faith - as Sponde himself does in the Stances de la Cène which follow the Méditations and which in a sense form a conclusion to the train of thought ; they are the calm after the storm. Good theology they may not be ; but as an essentially human document, illustrating the marvellous complexity of motives, the conflicting hopes and fears in the mind of one man, they are far from devoid of interest.
