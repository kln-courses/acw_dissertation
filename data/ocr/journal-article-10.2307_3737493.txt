Many originate with non-lyric texts, and their gradual acceptance within the lyric genre indicates changes in the subject matter perceived as suitable to it, and in the amorous philosophy which underpins it. Among such new themes, that ofjealousy is especially significant, because of both the frequency with which it appears in Renaissance verse, and the implications which this has for the concept of love expressed within the lyric. Jealousy appears in the lyric mainstream with Sannazaro's 1530 Sonetti e canzoni, subsequently becoming established as an integral part of the genre2 (a bibliography of 'jealousy' texts can be found in the appendix to this article). Although the theme is popular throughout Italy, its place remains most assured in Southern petrarchism, a regional bias which can be explained in various ways: the canonical status of Sannazaro's verse, the comparative freedom shown with respect to the Petrarchan model, and the appeal exercised by the potential ofjealousy poems for rhetorical extravagance. Radical changes occur in the philosophy of love governing the lyric during the Quattro and Cinquecento, with the gradual acceptance of novelties such as conjugal love, infidelity, and a succession of different ladies within a single canzoniere.6 A process of secularization beginning with the courtly poetry of the Quattrocento heralded the declining importance of narratives based on religious and spiritual development in favour of mundane accounts unfolding around the minutiae of courtly life. Despite this, jealousy remains absent from the collections of most 'hard-line' Petrarchists during the Cinquecento with the exception of Della Casa, and is rarely accorded a significant role within amorous narratives. Most early poems on the theme are found in anthologies, where their striking language and style stand out among the bland compositions forming the bulk of Petrarchan poetry. Only with the verse of Torquato Tasso and Giambattista Marino does jealousy come to represent an unquestioned part of the lyric repertoire. Renaissance love philosophy is ambivalent in its treatment ofjealousy, a feature probably originating in the medieval distinction between different manifestations of the emotion, generally dependent on the social and marital status of the lovers concerned. Typically, Andrea Cappellano's De amore, the forerunner of the Renaissance love treatise, fluctuates in its interpretation, defining jealousy variously as 'turpis [. . .] de muliere suspicio', as a force capable of attenuating love, and more frequently as a prerequisite for its existence.7 The intimate connections between love and jealousy are evident in the postulation of two types of jealousy, corresponding to two types of love: bad jealousy associated with husbands, and good jealousy typical of the courtly lover.8 Cinquecento discussions tend to perpetuate this confusion, although generally expounding only one of these opposing views. Latin has no word for jealousy, relying where necessary on the Greek term 'zelotypia', on its translation 'obtrectatio','5 or more commonly on vague synonyms such as 'timor' which reappear in vernacular poetry.16 As jealousy came to play a significant role in Italian literature, writers were at pains to create for it an iconography analogous to that of other personified emotions. The allegorical figure most commonly drawn upon is Envy, traditionally linked with jealousy, and given a precise physiognomy by Ovid in his Metamorphoses.7 Early depictions rely heavily on the personification of Envy as an old woman; later, the emotion comes to be associated with a variety of mythological monsters. Additionally, poets employ a series of metaphors, usually negative images for love, belonging to the Petrarchan tradition, (cold, bitterness, war, snakes), or to the Latin elegy (disease, poison). The first detailed representation of jealousy in vernacular Italian literature appears in Boccaccio's Filocolo (III. 24), where we find an episode describing the goddess Diana's visit to the house of Gelosia.'8 Closely modelled on a scene in Ovid's Metamorphoses (In. 760-86) involving Envy, the description plays on the negative qualities attributed to Jealousy, personified as an old woman. Boccaccio's imitation of Ovid is extremely close, reiterating not only the general context, but also many individual details: Jealousy and Envy are pale, thin, and sleepless, their houses are hidden among high mountains, permanently cold and dark, and surrounded by an array of unpleasant natural features.19 However, Boccaccio omits some of Ovid's more grotesque elements, replacing them with details indicating Jealousy's opposition to love: the emphasis on dark and cold, joylessness, and the tearfulness of Jealousy and her house. The choice of Envy as a model is determined by the close association of the two emotions in classical and medieval thought; however, it is significant also because it confirms that at this point jealousy as yet possessed no autonomous figurative associations, relying instead on characteristics borrowed from other personified emotions. Most notable is the rhetorical technique by which jealousy comes to represent a diametric opposite to love, systematically presented in a series of antithetical statements. The imagery with which this contrast is expressed derives partly from the pictorial representation of jealousy already discussed, and partly from the love lyric, whence the antithetical pairs ice/fire, peace/war, light/dark. One consequence of using this type of imagery is that the lyric presentation of love in the presence of jealousy automatically becomes positive, in order to preserve the dual structures essential to Petrarchism. Contrasting qualities which usually indicate the ambiguous nature of love itself come to be associated with two distinct emotions, allowing poets to develop paradoxes whereby jealousy, seen as a direct consequence of love, is yet the cause of its destruction.20 Also retained in the lyric is Boccaccio's use of a combination of metaphor and personification to describe jealousy; individual elements destined for success are the single origin of love and jealousy, and the plant metaphors which open Boccaccio's address. Significant on a theoretical level is the departure both from medieval trattatistica d'amore which sees jealousy as a pre-requisite for love, and from the novella tradition: jealousy's inclusion within this serious tale of courtly love goes far towards explaining its eventual acceptance in the lyric. A further contribution to the fortunes of the theme is made by Lorenzo de' Medici, who exploits Boccaccio's two digressions and combines them with a series of new elements drawn from Latin literature. In his first Selva we find a brief portrait of jealousy, loosely based on the descriptions given in the Filocolo, and used to elaborate a 'mythological' tale explaining her origins and attributes. According to Lorenzo, the evil Gelosia is the child of Chaos and twin of Love. During the Golden Age, she was banished to Hades, where she was suckled by the Furies, but was then restored to Earth by Jupiter in an attempt to curb the powers of Love. From Boccaccio's predominantly anthropomorphic representation, owing as much to medieval misogynist rhetoric as to its Ovidian source, Lorenzo produces a figure characterized by monstrous attributes. Although still an old woman and a goddess, jealousy now has the hundred eyes of Ovid's Argus (Metamorphoses, I. 625-29) and the numerous ears of Virgil's personified Rumour (Aeneid, Iv. I73-90). A series of pictorial metaphors represent the psychological effects ofjealousy, with the goddess shown feeding on 'ombre vane e pensier tristi', and chewing on the heart of the unfortunate lover, which, like the liver of Prometheus, regrows as it is consumed (Aeneid, vI. 597-600). In Lorenzo's depiction, jealousy develops from a personified natural force within the context of a third-person narrative, to the metaphorical representation of an emotion attributed to the narrating 'I'. Despite this shift, however, distance is maintained between the poet and jealousy by means of personification; although affecting the poet, jealousy is a largely external force. The poem opens with an apostrophe of jealousy mirroring that which ends Lorenzo's ottave, repeating the vocative 'O' and the key images 'mostro' and 'veneno'. Both this sonnet and its later imitations favour a structure marked by a sequence of separate one or two line segments, containing elements of a list of metaphors for, or qualities of,jealousy. Each part of the list is generally characterized by the presence of binary or ternary structures;26 these in turn are linked through the repetition of words or syntactical structures.27 The sonnet's exclamatory tone prevents any notable structural manipulation; most lines are self-contained, with only one example of enjambement between lines five and six. Among Cinquecento imitators of Sannazaro's sonnet, perhaps the most important is Luigi Tansillo,28 who composed a series of five sonnets and two satirical Capitoli on the theme of jealousy.29 Tansillo draws on his Neapolitan predecessor, on Ariosto's depiction of jealousy in the Orlando furioso, and on two plays by Marc'Antonio Epicuro, the Cecaria and the Mirzia, to produce a series of portraits which form a vital reference point for later writers, with their extensive metaphorical repertoire, and complex rhetorical patterns.30 Tansillo's influence is visible in numerous imitations, most notably by Torquato Tasso and Giambattista Marino; the sonnet 'O d'Invidia e d'Amor figlia si ria' is used to exemplify the causes and effects ofjealousy in both Tasso's Della gelosia and Giordano Bruno's Degli eroicifurori. Tansillo's 'O d'Invidia e d'Amor figlia si ria', 'O di buon genitore e di rea madre', and 'Chi vuol veder la piena d'occhi ed orba' follow the pattern established by Sannazaro, using a series of metaphors arranged within a repetitive rhetorical structure. Many individual images originate with Sannazaro: the 'crudel mostro' andjealousy's ability to turn day into night are examples. The familial image 'sorella di morte' is transformed into the more significant 'di Invidia e d'Amor figlia si ria', and subsequently the allusive 'di buon genitore e di rea madre I figlia', identifying jealousy not on the basis of likeness, but by its causes. The following section identifies some of the most important metaphors and rhetorical devices employed in the representation of jealousy from Sannazaro to the Marinisti of the Seicento; the treatment is for obvious reasons not exhaustive, and seeks to explore those elements which represent constants, rather than exceptions. Snakes and Poison Jealousy's association with snakes and poison originates with the classical figures on which early personifications are based. In the lyric tradition, the snake becomes a metaphor for jealousy in its own right ('serpente', 'serpe', 'angue', 'vipera', 'aspide'). It is often portrayed as hidden among grass or flowers, suggesting treachery. A variation on this image is the gnawing worm ('verme', 'tarlo'), drawing on its use by Petrarch as a metaphor for love (RVF, 304). Also frequent are references to unspecified poisons ('veleno', 'tosco') and to bitter substances ('assenzio', 'fiele') opposed to the sweetness of love. Verbs of poisoning or contamination are common ('infettare', 'ammorbare', 'awelenare', 'attossicare', 'contaminare'), as are adject- ives suggesting dirtiness ('sozzo', 'fetido', 'putrido'). The image of poisoning frequently occurs in conjunction with that of infection or illness, giving rise to a further group of metaphors ('peste', 'pestifero'). Classical monsters The monstrous nature of jealousy is a constant. At times these monsters are simply generic ('mostro', 'fiera', 'belva'). Alternatively, individual monsters, invariably belonging to classical mythology, may be named (Furia, Circe, Argo, Tesiphone, Arpia, Megera, Aletto, Scilla, Sfinge, Medusa, Medea). Jealousy is generally represented as possessing an active capacity for evil ('rio', 'crudo', 'maligno', 'empio', 'ingordo', 'fiero', 'iniquo', 'crudele', 'rapace', 'orrendo', 'scelerato', 'infame', 'malvagio'). Family relationships Jealousy is frequently endowed with a 'family' of related emotions, a practice beginning with Lorenzo's narrative account. In the later lyric, relationships vary, with most poets following the example either of Sannazaro, who defines jealousy as the sister of death, or of Tansillo who establishes a more complex relationship with jealousy being the child of Love and Envy. The relationship of jealousy to other emotions can be schematized as follows: CAUSES (parents): Amor, Invidia, Timore, Tema, Odio, Sospetto EFFECTS (children): Sdegno, Vendetta, Ira, Odio, Sospetto, Timore LIKENESS (siblings): Morte, Invidia Infernal origins Another frequent element in the imagery used to describe Jealousy is her origin in Hell ('inferno', 'Cocito', 'abisso'; 'infernale'), in accordance with the rhetorical 587</page><page sequence="12">Jealousy and the Italian Lyric tendency to ascribe infernal origins to anything unpleasant.41 In the lyric, jealousy's infernal origin gives rise to two possibilities; the first, merely descriptive, allows the poet to emphasize the 'badness' of Jealousy in a hyperbolic way. A second possibility, frequently used to close the sonnet, is an exhortation to jealousy to return to Hades whence she came. Images of Death In connection with the image of hell, poems on jealousy often contain images of death and killing ('morte'; 'mortifero', 'micidiale'; 'morire', 'ammazzare', 'uccidere', 'troncare'). Again, the use of this metaphor is twofold: most frequently, jealousy is portrayed as the death of love or hope, often themselves represented metaphorically; alternatively, the lover himself may be portrayed as in danger of death. Petrarchan Images Alongside the metaphors listed above, a substantial group of images is drawn from the Petrarchan tradition; the most common include 'piaga', 'nemica', 'fiera', 'giogo', 'guerra', 'guerriera', 'affanno', 'dolore', and 'pena'. Alongside these are epithets frequent in the lyric: 'aspro', 'rio', 'crudele', 'infelice', 'crudo', 'freddo'. Individual metaphors are accompanied by more complex images drawing on the tendency of the Petrarchist lyric to express itself by means of antithetical statements. In poems on jealousy, the antithetical and ambiguous nature of love itself, of the poet's experience, and his perception of the lady, is replaced by an oppositional relationship between love and jealousy, and between the poet's condition when suffering from jealousy, and when free from it. These images are identical to those used elsewhere for love itself, expressing the contrast in terms of sweet/bitter, fire/ ice, light/dark, and so forth. The formal arrangement of these metaphors follows a similar pattern in almost all jealousy poems. Two elements in particular stand out: the vocative used to open the majority of poems, and the employment of enumeratio in their construction. Other important features are the use of repetitive devices such as anaphora, and the intensification of antithetical images by means of chiasmus or other bipartite structures. An examination of the opening lines of poems on jealousy confirms the extent to which the sonnets by Sannazaro and Tansillo were regarded as canonical. Sannazaro's opening 'O gelosia . . .' is repeated verbatim by Minturno, and with a slight variation by Epicuro, 'Tu, gelosia .. .', and Parabosco, 'Ahi cruda gelosia .. .'. Though these stylistic and metaphorical elements are present in all Petrarchist poetry to a greater or lesser extent, the intensity attained in jealousy poems is exceptional, both in terms of the strength of language and imagery employed, and in the concentration of rhetorical effects. Despite its non-Petrarchan origins, the theme of jealousy quickly achieves the fixity typical of more canonical subjects. Following Sannazaro's early sonnet, little attempt is made to depart radically from his formulation until the Baroque; even in Marino and his followers developments tend to consist in intensification of existing features, rather the introduction of new material. In this sense, the theme ofjealousy serves better than more well-established topoi as an exemplification of the need felt within the Petrarchist lyric for canonical models.
