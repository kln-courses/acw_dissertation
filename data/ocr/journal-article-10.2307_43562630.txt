<plain_text><page sequence="1">Gertrude Prokosch Kurath Masked Dances of Native North America Native tribes of North America have used masks as ritual disguises, as aids in the identification with supernaturais. Surviving impersonations are distinctive, spectacular, and meaningful, be they individual or collective. Milieu, Meaning, Materials The purposes, forms, and materials depend largely on the natural envi- ronment. Theriomorphic masks reflect the wildlife of earth, sky, and sea. Anthropomorphic masks visualize deities in charge of weather control and of human well being. Masks fusing animal and human features combine the powers of several supernaturais. Yaqui Chapayeka hide helmet mask. Black and red designs on white. Pascua, Arizona 58</page><page sequence="2">Masks and associated musical instruments are constructed mostly of natural, regional materials, especially wood and hide, also natural pig- ments, gourds, corn husks, evergreens, yucca, flowers, turtle and tortoise shells, seashells, deer hoofs, butterfly cocoons, horns, tusks, fur, hair, feathers, quills. Recent additions are cloth, buckram, paper, mirrors, ribbons, spangles, beads. Metal is favoured for tools as well as ornaments. The disguises usually cover the face and head, rarely the whole body, which may be dressed in a uniform, a blanket, or ordinary clothes. The ma- terials and constructions affect the wearers, their mobility and expressive- ness, the degree of realism and stylization. Surviving Examples Masked dances are not ubiquitous. Over vast expanses of Canada's inte- rior, boreal tribes evidently used no masks, not even for animal rites. On the Great Plains, the former warriors cling to a few ritual vestiges on the Deer Kachina helmet mask. Black and white horns and gourd nose; evergreen collar, yucca visor. They represent ecologically diverse areas, exemplified in the follow- ing descriptions. Eastern Woodlands In Iroquois villages of New York and Ontario unearthly creatures march from home to home. Wry wooden faces gleam red and black against the Kwakiutl composite wooden mask. Thunderbird beaks open to reveal humanoid, symbolic design. British Columbia 60</page><page sequence="4">white snow. Brass eyes stare. Long strands of white horsehair blow in the wind. In burlap, levis, and boots, the "False-faces" trudge behind a leader. They beg tobacco and in return exorcise disease. The maskers enter the longhouse sanctuary crawling, stumbling, moan- ing, thumping staves and turtle shell rattles. They scramble to their feet, hop and gyrate with arms akimbo, while two chanters thunder turtle shells against a wooden bench and growl hypnotic songs to the great good mask with a smiling face. Not all of the masks are smiling. Some are twisted, with dislocated mouth and protruding tongue. The maskers scoop hot ashes from a stove and scatter them over the floor and over patients with face ail- ments. They cure them and initiate them into the False-face Society with its venerated masks. They are aided by a company of cornhusk maskers. Two tall maskers are leaning on staves by the door, one with thick lips and spiny wrinkles, the other with spoon lips. Facing two matrons, they spar with thumbs erect. They lead the congregation in the Doorkeepers' Round Dance. Then they burst outdoors, to rejoin the spirits of wind and disease, now placated by the ceremony. Sometimes the maskers regale the community with less traditional an- tics. During midwinter festivals near Syracuse, N.Y., two companies clatter back and forth between two longhouses. After a traditional ceremony, a group in buckram masks parodies white people's foibles and also the sa- cred Iroquois rituals. In North Carolina the linguistic Iroquois relatives, the Cherokee, cele- brate jovial vestiges of therapeutic rituals. At midwinter male soloists cari- cature white or tribal aliens, in amusing wooden masks. One man, masked as a bear, growls and crawls on all fours. Finally the community circles in unmasked animal dances, to song and a small waterdrum - dances similar to those of the northern Iroquois. Southwestern Semi-desert in New Mexico and Arizona other agriculturalists adjoin pastoral tribes in a land of bristly sand hills, verdant streambeds, rocky mesas, and coni- ferous mountains. They invoke animal spirits as well as raingods of the four directions. 61</page><page sequence="5">During the cold season Pueblo villagers fill their adobe plazas with game animal impersonators. Deer spirits lean on foresticks as they lope, kick up their heels, and jerk their heads. They wear traditional kilts and fringed rain sashes; they limit their disguise to face blackening, antlered head- dresses, and yucca visors. Lumbering buffalo bulls wear real, horned heads, and kilts with serpentine lightning designs. Buffalo maidens wear sun bustles. To regulated songs and rhythms of double-headed cotton- wood drums, they manoeuvre in precise formations, while antelope and mountain sheep gallop among them. They control the weather as well as the hunt. Summer rains and winter snows depend on the numerous Kachina, the raingods, in the Kiva sanctuaries. Their fanciful hide helmets, with animal or human features, are as stylized as the movements. Benevolent Kachina stamp and shake rattles in a stationary line, while singing old and new songs. On separate occasions Hopi ogre Kachinas, with gruesome jaws, dash about and frighten children. In winter and summer, in plaza and Kiva, sacred clowns weave among animal and corn dancers. The Koshare symbolize the ancestors and their potency for the fertility of descendants. They have considerable mobility in the disguise of black and white stripes on face and body and cornhusk headpokes. Independently, they improvise gestures for crop growth, coded long ago in dream time. Sometimes, again, they enact lewd mime or parody Catholic rites. At Easter strange hordes surge across the plazas of Yaqui villages in Ari- zona and Sonora. Pilatos are disguised by black cloth visors and cloaks, Chapayekam by hide helmets with horns, big ears, and long noses. The Chapayekam caper, wriggle their hips, rattling deerhoof belts. To flute wails and drum thuds they stampshuffle toward the shrouded altar and the bier with an image of Christ. On the third advance the horde tears through a mesquite screen. Finally these forces of evil are vanquished by forces of good, the altar Fiesteros and the unmasked Matachini. They rush into the atrium and the Chapayekam burn their masks. On the outskirts of this hybrid Passion Play, other clowns perpetuate the religion of the woods. Three Pascólas - old men of the fiesta - invoke ani- mals and birds. Then they dance zapateados to string instruments. Then they don black masks expertly carved of wood, ornamented with white paint, bits of mirror, and crosses. Marine North Tribes of the Pacific Northwest seashore and luxuriant forests have used the cedar for fantastic carved and painted masks. The curved designs are surrealist, splitting up natural forms and recombining the symbolic motifs. The caste system and fearsome winter ceremonies are as intricate as the designs. A few maskers have their arms free for the gesture code. Canni- bals terrify the congregation, even biting people. Other mimes are inhibi- ted by the mechanisms of their cumbersome masks; they work hinges to move wings or beaks. The most complicated masks are composite. An ani- mal mask opens up to show a man's face, as symbol of animal-human identity. By contrast, the Eskimos of the bleak Arctic coasts carve driftwood masks of expressive simplicity. During convivial winter feasts, comedians mime animal or human characters, walrus, fox, old man. Women sway their hands, ornamented with miniature finger masks. The Eskimos share one feature with the sophisticated forest dwellers - the rhapsodic, irregular rhythms of the singing and of the single-headed shaman's drums. Interactions Participants In true rituals, the dancers and musicians cooperate with each other and with the supernaturais. Maskers believe in their identification with the be- neficent powers, though in varying degree. Mask makers follow the tradi- tions, with creative leeway. Musicians and composers likewise adhere to the ancient frame. Dancers should not deviate from the tribal style. 64</page><page sequence="8">The spectators of the masked dances are important. Generally the native spectators respond to the intentions of the drama - by reverence, fear, or merriment. In public plaza dances some white visitors are responsive. Some are casual, even rude. Diffusion Environmental similarity and contacts account for recurrences within the Southwest area, despite linguistic diversity. There are differences be- tween the dances of the Rio Grande Pueblos and the Western Zuni and Hopl. There are variations among Pueblos. The giant Shalako are peculiar to Zuni, the Rivermen to Cochiti. Yet a basic repertoire pervades their ceremonialism and distinguishes them from non-Pueblo tribes. Considerable interborrowing has taken place in the whole area, even in song texts which are not mutually intelligible. The Pueblos share customs with tribes of different provenience. For instance, the 14 Ye'i of Navaho medicine rites bètray Kachina influences in the helmet masks and in the line dancing, though the Ye'i are unique in the mythical designs and in their falsetto singing. Southwestern influences have spread further. In shows, tribes from coast to coast imitate the mimetic Pueblo eagle dancers, with halfmasks and wings; and the geometric Apache mountain spirit dancers, with black hoods and fanciful crowns. So do white imitators, with modifications. In turn, Southwesterners have received customs from Plains tribes, by their own admission. The buffalo in the game animal dances are related to the Plains buffalo cult with associated sun symbolism. A few tribes have accepted masked pageants from Colonial Spanish in- vaders and missionaries. Several Pueblos feature a Santiago on a hobby horse and bearded "viejo" clowns. The Pueblos, Yaqui, and adjacent Mexicans have acclimatized a Matachina dance of mitred men, the Yaqui without masks, the Pueblos with ornate visors and costumes, and an at- tendant farce with a Malincha, a Monarca, and two Tsaviyo clowns who kill and revive a bull actor. The Iberian origin is evident in the longways forma- tions, the bull, and the music of string instruments; the native flavour in the earthen style, gourd rattles and helmet masks of the clowns. 65</page><page sequence="9">The Yaqui incorporated Spanish-Catholic features in their fiestas, while the Pueblos added the Catholic pantheon and customs to their animistic traditions. On the other hand, Protestantism did not hybridize. Throughout the continent, converts lost their native ceremonialism. Modern imports have their pros and cons. Mask makers benefit from new tools and materials, in greater virtuosity and in sales promotion. Elec- tricity illuminates homes and sanctuaries. Motor vehicles aid corn grow- ers, seal hunters, and fiesta visitors. But machine madness and deafening disco conflict with nature worship. Tiermasken spiegeln das Leben der Wildnis auf dem Land, in der Luft und im Wasser wider. Anthropomorphe Masken ver- anschaulichen Götter, die sich um das Wet- ter und das menschliche Wohlergehen küm- mern. Masken mit tierischen und menschli- chen Eigenschaften vereinen in sich die Kräfte verschiedener überirdischer Wesen. Masken und die zugehörigen Musikinstru- mente werden in der Regel aus den natürli- chen Stoffen der jeweiligen Gegend herge- stellt, vor allem aus Holz und Haut, aber auch aus Farbstoffen, Kürbissen, Getreidehülsen, Immergrün- und Manioka-Stauden, Blumen, Schildpatt von See- und Landschildkröten, Rotwildhufen, Schmetterlingskokons, Hör- nern, Hauern, Pelz, Haar, Gefieder und Schwungfedern. Heute nimmt man zusätz- lich Tuch, Streifleinen, Papier, Spiegel, Bän- der, Pailletten und Perlen. Metall wird bevor- zugt für Werkzeuge und Verzierungen ver- wendet. Die Verkleidungen bedecken gewöhnlich Gesicht und Kopf, selten den ganzen Körper, der in einer Uniform, einer Decke oder den normalen Kleidern stecken kann. Die Stoffe und die Art ihrer Gestaltung haben Einfluß auf die Träger, deren Beweglichkeit und Ausdruckskraft und den Grad an Realismus und Stilisierung in ihrer Darstellung. Qu'elles soient in- dividuelles ou collectives, les formes person- nifiées par les masques qui subsistent de nos jours sont caractéristiques, spectacu- laires et pleines de sens. Les fonctions, les formes et les matériaux dépendent largement de l'environnement naturel. Les masques d'animaux représen- tent la faune de la terre, du ciel et de la mer. Les masques anthropomorphes figurent les divinités chargées de commander le beau ou le mauvais temps et de veiller sur le bien-être des humains. Les pouvoirs de plusieurs êtres surnaturels sont réunis dans les masques dont les traits sont à la fois ceux d'êtres hu- mains et d'animaux. Les masques, ainsi que les instruments de musique qui y sont associés, sont confec- tionnés généralement dans des matériaux naturels, trouvés sur place, notamment en bois et en peau, mais aussi en pigments na- turels, en courge, en maïs, en plantes vertes, en yucca, en fleurs, en écaille de tortue, en écaille, en coquillages, en sabot de cerf, en cocons de papillon, en corne, en broches, en fourrure, en poils, en plumes, en tuyau de plume, auxquels sont venus s'ajouter récem- ment le tissu, le bougran, le papier, les mi- roirs, le ruban, les paillettes et les perles. Pour les outils comme pour les ornements, on préfère le métal. Les déguisements couvrent généralement le visage et la tête, et rarement le corps tout entier, qui peut être revêtu d'un uniforme, d'une couverture ou de vêtements normaux. Le matériau utilisé et l'architecture du mas- que exercent une influence sur celui qui le porte, sur sa mobilité et son expressivité, ainsi que sur le degré de réalisme et de styli- sation. Les danses masquées ne se trouvent pas partout. De toute évidence, dans de vastes territoires de l'intérieur du Canada, les tribus boréales n'avaient pas de masques, pas même pour les rites consacrés aux animaux. Dans les grandes plaines, les anciens guer- riers restent attachés aux quelques vestiges rituels subsistant dans les réserves. Les tri- bus où les cultes faisant usage de masques sont encore florissants, sont très dispersées dans des régions naturelles différentes.
