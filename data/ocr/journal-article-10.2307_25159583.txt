<plain_text><page sequence="1">The 1978 May Show: May 3 through June 4, 1978 Fifty-ninth Annual Exhibition by Artists and Craftsmen of the Western Reserve After two years, the May Show is back to its spring time slot. The procedural changes introduced last year affect ing the selection process and management have helped in assembling an exhibition of high quality. The use of slides for the initial screening of entries proved valuable. Jurors were able to select carefully all the objects they wanted to see, and the artists did not have to bring works-often large or requiring special installation-to the Museum without an indication of jurors' interest. Since the usual large number of entries did not have to be handled and stored, it was possible to permit the art ists to assist in assembling works with special installation requirements. In short, the exhibition was more easily and more efficiently organized and managed. However, some artists felt their work did not photograph well, thereby reducing the possibility of being accepted for the final jury. The simultaneous projection of slides of various views and the optional detail slide of a two-di mensional work was most helpful in fully comprehend ing an entry. In the end, however, the success of this ex hibition depends on the many fine artists residing in northeastern Ohio. The quality of the slides was improved over last year, although a few were still unintelligible. Again, The New Organization for the Visual Arts (NOVA) generously provided information to artists concerning the process of making slides as well as sources where the artist might hire professional assistance. In addition, Martin Linsey, of the Museum's Department of Art History and Educa tion, gave a demonstration for artists at the Museum on photographing works of art. This year there were two outside members participat ing in both the slide screening and the final juries. William S. Lieberman, Director of Drawings at the Mu seum of Modern Art in New York City, served on the jury for paintings, sculpture, graphics, photography, and Cover Gary Schlappal, Military Maneuvers: The Aftermath, $1000 Award for Crafts electrical entries; Toshiko Takaezu, potter, of Quaker town, New Jersey, was on the crafts jury. There has been much discussion about the makeup of the May Show juries. The current combination of outside jurors and Museum personnel seems to be working well. The outside members contribute a fresh eye and the in-house members insure that each work is looked at carefully and understood. In contrast to recent years, the jurors quickly agreed on the $1000 painting award which went to Moe A. Brooker's work, entitled Afternoon Delight II. This large canvas is impressive with its vibrant colors. The artist has investigated the expressive and spatial effects that can be achieved through the organization of colors, various shapes, and vibrant lines. Eric Lintala received the $1000 sculpture award for his two entries. The jurors were impressed by the imagina tion as well as the craftsmanship that distinguished his sculptures. In trinity Lintala establishes interesting spa tial relationships and shadow patterns by suspending three cylinders over rectilinear panels on the floor. For the second time the graphics prize was split be tween a print and a photograph. Phyllis Sloane received $500 for her screenprint Redhead in Rose Room. The motif allowed the artist to demonstrate her interest in flat, abstract patterns which severely reduce the spatial qualities of the composition. Douglas Dalton received an equal amount for his dramatically composed, clearly de lineated, and beautifully printed photograph of a church interior. For the first time in several years, the overall quality of the crafts was good-with the areas of jewelry and ce ramics especially strong. Gary Schlappal was awarded $1000 for his large, cylindrical stoneware piece embel lished by drawings. The Horace E. Potter Memorial Award for Excellence in Craftsmanship went to Barbara Udelson Samson for her creatively designed and beauti fully crafted box to hold and store Chanukah candles. This year there were fewer Special Mentions awarded in all categories; the jurors wanted to make these awards more meaningful. For the 1978 May Show 1,995 objects by 1,130 art ists were entered, which is an increase over last year by 216 entries and 144 artists. From the slides, 593 objects 152</page><page sequence="2">Juries of Selection were chosen to be brought to the Museum; from these, the jury selected a show of 207 objects created by 177 artists. Again this year, the overall size of the exhibition is small-but that results in a selective show of high qual ity. In spite of an active exhibition schedule and continued gallery renovation, the May Show operates smoothly due to the cooperation and assistance of most of the Mu seum departments. This year an extra effort on the part of everyone was necessitated by having the normal work ing time cut almost in half. In this abbreviated period the exhibition itself had to be designed and installed, the catalog prepared, labels printed, sales slips filled in, re jected objects returned, the three previews organized, and many other tasks carried out to insure that the 1978 May Show opened on time. Those departments and in dividuals deserving special recognition include: the Reg istrar's, Comptroller's, Public Relations, Publications, Photography, Printing, Building and Grounds, William E. Ward and Joseph L. Finizia (the Museum's Designer and Assistant Designer), and the staff jurors. The Depart ment of Art History and Education gives numerous talks and prepares an audio-visual tape on the May Show. This year's show was successfully organized with the able assistance of Andrea Redhead, with additional aid from Barbara Maher, Anne Chadbourne, and a number of other part-time workers and volunteers. Throughout the year, I rely on the help and encouragement of Edward B. Henning, Curator of Modern Art, and Margaret Wilson, the departmental secretary who pre pared the catalog copy. Tom E.
