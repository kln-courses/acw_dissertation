Repeated analyses have, however, shed but little light on the essential problem of why Isolde is spared the public humiliation of being burned during the ordeal of the glowing iron. Not, of course, that the episode as such has been neglected ; in fact, there is virtually no work of Tristan secondary literature which does not derive overpowering conclusions from this scene or from the authorial commentary which follows it.1 The primary considerations of the critical analyses of this episode are centered around two tantalizing quotations, one from the narrative section which precedes the ordeal: 15550 in disen dingen haete îsôt einen list ir herzen vür geleit vil verre ûf gotes höfscheit ...2 and one from the concluding narrative commentary describing the signi ficance of the successful outcome of the ordeal: dâ wart wol goffenbasret und al der werlt bewaeret, 15 7j 5 daz der vil tugenthafte Crist wintschaffen alse ein ermel ist. In this reexamination of the ordeal, I will demonstrate that in reality Isolde is more concerned with arousing the sympathy of the court than with appeasing and reconciling herself with a «hövescher» God; in ad dition, I propose an expanded definition of the word «wintschaffen» which robs the social criticism attributed to Gottfried of blasphemous overtones. Gottfried tells us from the beginning that Isolde's success in plotting depends upon a certain conception of God: «in disen dingen haste îsôt / einen list ir herzen vür geleit / vil verre ûf gotes höfscheit» (ij 55off.). Most critics find these lines to be a direct attack on God and Christianity: «Desillusioniert wird schließlich auch der oberste Wert der höfisch-rit terlichen Welt, desillusioniert wird auch Gott.»13 Keferstein finds Gott fried's heretical views to stem from his position in the «bürgerlich städtischen Sphäre», as contrasted with the «ritterlich-höfischen Welt» of Wolfram: «Er betrachtet die ritterliche Welt von außen, sieht ihre schöne, allzu schöne und glänzende Oberfläche, sieht sie als illusionären Schein, den es zu entlarven, den es zu desillusionieren gilt» (p. 425). Gottfried Weber, in many respects a follower of Keferstein in this matter, maintains that Isolde's conception of God appears «gänzlich entgottet», because the Father seems to be satisfied by the mere com pletion of formulae: «die Geste des Almosens und die Geste der Buß freudigkeit genügen.»14 Building his case for Gottfried's «inhaltlich zweifelsohne antichristliches Werk» (I, 301), Weber maintains that Gott fried's excessive use of oaths indicates a lack of true belief in God; with this, he commits the all too common hermeneutical crime of attributing to Gottfried himself the words and opinions of his characters. Stockum, however, who agrees that Gottfried employs more formula oaths than the other medieval epic poets, finds they are significant «mehr für die Konvention des Sprachgebrauchs als für die Gesinnung des Dichters»." But, it is, in my opinion, not merely coincidental that the characters in this epic mouth «fromme Formeln» (Stockum); through this, Gottfried demonstrates just how empty, how common the name of God, and thus the conception of God, has become in a Christian society. But in making this distinction between Gottfried's words and his mean ing, is it necessary for the concept of God to be devalued ? It is clearly the verbal conception of God that is attacked here; but, that is no reason to assume that the God beyond grammar and semantics is also attacked, that God has anything to do with this manmade «hövescher got» of epic poetry and courtly justice. For it is not Christ, «[der] füeget unde suochet an», but society itself that manipulates Christ and through Him morality for its own convenience. True, Gottfried attacks the ordeal, but not as an evil of itself; in fact, we are actually relieved and delighted to discover it is ineffectual. For Gottfried it is only a symptom of society's weaknesses. Gottfried directs his criticisms towards a theory of morality which in cludes a conception of God; by attacking the conception created by society, he does not necessarily attack the essence behind that conception. To make such an interpretation from his attack on the «hövescher» God and the «wintschaffen» Christ is to fall victim to the same blindness as that demonstrated by the courtly response, «von liute and von lande», to Isolde's successful ordeal.
