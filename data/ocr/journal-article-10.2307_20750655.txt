Giovannino Guareschi While many scholarly studies have addressed the history and mem ory of the partisan Armed Resistance to Nazif ascists during World War II, fewer have focused on the unarmed or "White" Resistance that took place at the same time by Italian soldiers interned in Germany and Poland. In the Italian collective imagination as well, popular memory of the Resistance remains largely congealed around partisan sacrifices and guerilla warfare. But fortunately, a more complete picture of prison life in the Lagers continues to emerge that validates this experi ence as a heroic form of anti-fascism.2 This present study focuses upon a form of prisoner entertainment as Resistance, the Favola di Natale, a Christmas show written and produced in the Sandbostel Lager in 1944 by Giovannino Guareschi. After the war, through his editorship of Candido, his anthologies and films of the Don Camillo saga, and the famous libel suit that Alcide De Gasperi brought against him, Guareschi achieved interna tional fame. But to this day, the dwindling numbers of surviving Italian ex-prisoners of war simply think of Guareschi as the "cantore collettivo" of their travails (Nello, "Guareschi" 44). The Favola provides an excellent example of Resistance literature that attests to the Italian POW experience in the greater Third Reich primarily for two reasons. First, since Guareschi actually wrote and performed the fable as a musical production to satirize his German guards and assail the morally impoverished Nazi regime, it allows us to see how significant Resistance efforts took place behind barbed wire. Second, the publication of the Favola and its performances on stage after th? war sheds tremendous light upon Guareschi's attempts to make sense of his sacrifices as a POW; the work, in other words, gives us a snapshot of Guareschi's bitterness that eventually dissipated to the point where he could forgive his captors. Telling tales to Resist: Guareschi as "Fabulist" Shortly after Italy signed an Armistice with the Allies in September 1943 and opted to get out of the war, the Germans seized Guareschi, then an artillery lieutenant stationed in Alessandria, and pressured him to swear allegiance to Hitler or Mussolini. When Guareschi re fused, they shipped him to an interment camp in Poland. After an ar duous train trip, he debarked to find himself among fleas and bed bugs, immersed in mud and surrounded by graveyards found along the perimeter of the camp (Diario Clandestino, xiv). Since Italy had not declared war on its former ally, the Germans classified Guareschi and the other Italians they captured as Internati Militari Italiani (IMI) in stead of Prisoners of War, thus nullifying their 1929 Geneva Conven tion rights. As internees, therefore, the International Red Cross could not provide Italian soldiers adequate assistance, and the status of im prisonment and rules governing treatment remained both murky and in limbo (Nello, "La Resistenza clandestina" 147).3 To help himself cope with the deprivations, Guareschi began to maintain both a chronicle, wherein he noted the particular events within the prison, the status of the weather, and the changes in his health and morale, and a journal in which he elaborated his attempts to come to terms with various experiences of prison life. After the war, he returned to Italy with over twelve notebooks that are today housed in his extensive archives located in Roncole Verdi. In 1949, he pub lished several passages from them in the Diario Clandestino, and forty years later his children compiled others in Ritorno alla base. Having had extensive experience in journalism, and some in radio broadcasting, Guareschi thought to raise morale in the Lager by shar ing short reflections from his notebooks in the form of public lectures. As he went from barrack to barrack, he spoke about everything from hunger and longing to see his children to his conception of humor. In all, from October 1943 to July 1945, he held eighty-three of these en counters, an average of one a week (Bertellini 8). Together with other enterprising prisoners, he helped to form a culture of Resistance that aimed to protect human dignity and stiffen the resolve to persevere. Quite often, these talks played upon hidden meanings and double en tendras that even Italian-speaking German guards in attendance could not understand. One former inmate, for example, recalled listening to a lecture: In fondo, allo stanzone, c'era un "Sonderf?her", un ufficiale ammini strativo della Wehrmacht. The genre seemed especially appropriate for the Lager for several reasons. First, he read the tales aloud ? fairy tales come from a long and rich oral tradition, best appreciated when recounted by a master teller and heard with others in a collective audience (Zipes, Fairy Tale as Myth 10). Second, fables invite listeners to suspend belief, to give themselves over to extraordinary and improbable phenomena, and thus enter into a world where time and space have a different reality. As the folklorist D. L. Ashliman notes: The fantasy world of traditional magic tales has an appeal that transcends time and space. These tales take place in an otherworldly realm where wishes come true, where right prevails, and where special rules supercede the expected laws of nature. This realm is akin both to the fairyland of countless legends as well as to the spirit world of various religions. It is an existence that inexplicably runs parallel to ordinary life. On rare occasions the two worlds merge, allowing ordinary mortals to experience, even to cross over into, the other world (159). Thus, inmates could find solace in focusing on life away from their harsh circumstances that surrounded them. For however brief a time they spent listening to Guareschi's tale, they could escape?both psy chologically and emotionally?the concertina wire, armed guards, filthy sanitary conditions, and threat of death. In some respects, they could return in time to a more innocent part of their youths, far re moved from the frozen fields and gray skies of Germany, when they first began to listen spellbound to fairy tales.6 Third, even as fables transport listeners to a magical realm lost in ilio tempore, they can powerfully reflect aspects of real life. As Zipes has discussed, "the fairy tale records the breakdown of old world struc tures, chaos, confusion, and the striving to attain a new world which might allow for more humane and conduct. [...] Often it is difficult to distinguish between the unreal and real in the tale" (Zipes, Breaking the Magic Spell 35). Seen in this light, fables thus gave Guareschi and his fellow prisoners a means to interpret the harsh life they faced. As L?thi relates: "The fairy tale is a counterpart of reality only insofar as it pre sents a contrast to its outer appearances, not its true nature. The true na</page><page sequence="5">Guareschi's Resistance in the Favola di Natale 627 ture of reality is exactly what the fairy tale attempts to perceive and de pict. The fairy tale conceives of itself and presents itself as a means of perceiving the essence of the real world" (91)7 Fables thus helped the internees to cope with their lives that had been swept up by the war. Considerations of the Show in Light of its Genre: In constructing his Christmas story, Guareschi borrowed heavily upon motifs common to many fairy-tales. We have already discussed how Guareschi would interrupt the narrative to ask his listeners a question, a frequent motif in the telling of tales as a means of direct communication, one that Collodi employs often in Pinocchio. Additionally, in light of that classic fable, Guareschi also begins in a way that purposely aims to confuse the reader. As children's literature specialist Tiziana Visentin explains, Guareschi "adotta lo stratagemma dell'errore e della correzione, quasi a voler far intendere che la favola che si sta per raccontare ? in continua costruzione, non ? una favola che si conosce a memoria, ma un racconto che si realizzer? in fieri: mano a mano che la narrazione prender? piede" (217).17 In addition, the last fi lastrocca or rhyming scheme, "Stretta la foglia, larga la via," is a tradi tional Italian refrain used to conclude fables in formulaic fashion. The use of repetitions as well, such as those found in certain locutions?"E cammina, cammina, cammina" ; "E via, via, via"?and in this case, the number three?three Funghi Buoni e Cattivi; three Magi and three Sprites; three Passerotti and three Cornacchie?also belong to the tra dition of fairy tales. They provide a formula to recognize the genre and activate the adequate mental schemes toward a proper interpretation (Pisanty 33). The theme of faithfulness?represented by all members of the family who undertake arduous journeys to come together?as well as the story's binary structure of Good versus Evil found in the Bosco degli Incontri also mirror classic fairy tale patterns (Thompson 108). Of course, several of the characters, such as La Fontaine's Ant, come directly from the land of Faerie. Guareschi also uses Collodi's Grillo Parlante, who tells the household that Albertino has decided to go find his father ("Favola" 18). Furthermore, many organic elements in nature?from birds and trees?to countless inanimate objects such as clocks, trains, the Wind and Moon, all acquire the anthropomorphic qualities of speech and reason, an essential characteristic of Faerie. Finally, the structure of the narrative itself encompasses several of the classic fairy tale functions that Vladimir Propp identifies. These in clude a hero, in this case Albertino, who leaves home to confront a dif ficult task, is tested along his way, acquires the use of magical help, and overcomes any number of false heroes or villains (25-65). Examining the fable-like traits in the Favola supports what English folklorist Andrew Lang opined: "Nobody can write a new fairy tale; you can only mix up and dress up the old, old stories and put the char acters into new dresses [...]" (viii).
