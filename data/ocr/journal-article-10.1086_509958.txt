      <p>
         <bold>Jon Agar</bold> lectures in the Department for History and Philosophy of Science, University of Cambridge. His last monograph was <italic>The Government Machine</italic> (MIT Press, 2003), and he is now working on a history of twentieth‐century science.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Duncan Agnew</bold> is a geophysicist at the Scripps Institution of Oceanography in La Jolla, California.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Mitchell G. Ash</bold> is Professor of Modern History and Head of the Working Group in History of Science at the University of Vienna and President of the Gesellschaft für Wissenschaftsgeschichte (Society for History of the Sciences and Humanities). He has published extensively on the history of the human sciences and is now working on a book about scientific and political changes in Germany and Austria during the twentieth century.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Keith R. Benson</bold> is a historian of biology, with a special interest in the history of biology in North America, the history of marine sciences, the history of developmental biology, and biology and society. He is Principal of Green College and Professor of History at the University of British Columbia. He is a coeditor of <italic>The Development of American Biology</italic> and <italic>The American Expansion of Biology</italic>, edited the recent translation of Jacques Roger’s classic book <italic>The Life Sciences in Eighteenth‐Century France</italic>, and is coeditor (with Fritz Rehbock) of <italic>The Pacific and Beyond</italic>, a multiauthored history of oceanography. He is an active member of the History of Science Society and is now Treasurer of the International Society of the History, Philosophy, and Social Studies of Biology (ISHPSSB).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Richard H. Beyler</bold> teaches the history of science and modern European history at Portland State University. His current research interests include the history of biophysics in the twentieth century and the reconstruction of German science after World War II.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John Blackmore</bold> has taught at UC Santa Cruz, Harvey Mudd College, the University of Vienna, and Tsukuba University in Japan. He has published several books on Ernst Mach and Ludwig Boltzmann and is now writing a book titled “Famous Realists.”</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Kirstin Borgerson</bold> is a doctoral candidate in philosophy at the University of Toronto. She is currently a CIHR Strategic Research and Training Doctoral Fellow in Health Care, Technology, and Place, a Research Associate with the Comparative Program on Health and Society, and an Ontario Graduate Scholar.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Luciano Boschiero</bold> is a history lecturer at Campion College in Sydney, Australia. His interests include early modern experimentalism and natural philosophy in seventeenth‐century Italian scientific institutions.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John C. Burnham</bold> is Research Professor of History and Scholar in Residence, Medical Heritage Center, Ohio State University. His books include <italic>Bad Habits: Drinking, Smoking, Taking Drugs, Gambling, Sexual Misbehavior, and Swearing in American History</italic> (1993) and <italic>What Is Medical History?</italic> (2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Harold L. Burstyn</bold> (M.S., Oceanography, UCLA; Ph.D., History of Science, Harvard; J.D., Rutgers) is Adjunct Professor, L. C. Smith College of Engineering and Computer Science, Syracuse University, and a patent attorney in private practice.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Joan Cadden</bold> is Professor of History at the University of California, Davis. Her current research concerns medieval natural philosophers’ explanations of male homosexual desire. She is the author of <italic>Meanings of Sex Difference in the Middle Ages: Medicine, Science, and Culture</italic> and articles on the medical and scientific ideas of medieval women.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Tian Yu Cao,</bold> who studied philosophy at Peking University in the early 1960s, is a conceptual historian and philosopher of modern physics in the Department of Philosophy at Boston University. He is finalizing a manuscript, “The Making of QCD,” and beginning to work on the early history of string theory.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>P. J. Capelotti</bold> is Senior Lecturer in Anthropology and American Studies at Penn State University, Abington College. His publications include <italic>Life and Death on the Greenland Patrol, 1942</italic> (Florida, 2005), <italic>Sea Drift: Rafting Adventures in the Wake of Kon‐Tiki</italic> (Rutgers, 2001), and <italic>By Airship to the North Pole: An Archaeology of Human Exploration</italic> (Rutgers, 1999). His poetry has appeared in <italic>Subjective Substance</italic> and the <italic>Bathyspheric Review</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Alex Checkovich</bold> is a postdoctoral fellow on the Committee for the History of Environment and Technology (CHET) at the University of Virginia. He is working on an environmental history of American cartography and its relation to development after the disappearance of the frontier.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Deborah R. Coen</bold> is Assistant Professor of History at Barnard College, Columbia University. She is the author of <italic>A Scientific Dynasty: The Exners in Imperial Vienna</italic>, to be published by the University of Chicago Press in 2007.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Shana Cohen</bold> completed her Ph.D. in Environmental Science, Policy, and Management at the University of California, Berkeley, in December 2005. She is at work on an environmental history of the garden club movement in the United States.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Hunter Crowther‐Heyck</bold> is Assistant Professor of History of Science at the University of Oklahoma and the author of <italic>Herbert A. Simon: The Bounds of Reason in Modern America</italic> (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2005). His current projects include a study of the relationship between the organizational revolution and the emergence of “systems thinking,” tentatively titled “The Branching Tree.”</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Edward B. Davis,</bold> Distinguished Professor of the History of Science at Messiah College (Grantham, Pennsylvania), is writing a book about Protestant modernism and science in America. His essay “Science and Religious Fundamentalism in the 1920s” was published by <italic>American Scientist</italic> in May–June 2005.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Virginia P. Dawson</bold> is the author of several books for NASA. In 2005, with Mark D. Bowles, she edited a collection of biographical essays in honor of the Wright brothers’ first flight. She is president of History Enterprises, Inc., and an adjunct professor at Case Western Reserve University.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Suzanne Débarbat</bold> is the <italic>astronome titulaire</italic> at l’Observatoire de Paris. From 1985 to 1993, she directed the research unit associated with the CNRS <italic>Systèmes de référence spatio‐temporels,</italic> and from 1987 to 1992 she directed the Department of Fundamental Astrophysics at l'Observatoire de Paris. Since her retirement, she has managed the archives at l’Observatoire de Paris.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>François Duchesneau</bold> is Professor of Philosophy and History of Science at the University of Montreal. His publications include <italic>Leibniz et la méthode de la science</italic> (Paris, 1993), <italic>La dynamique de Leibniz</italic> (Paris, 1994), <italic>Philosophie de la biologie</italic> (Paris, 1997), and <italic>Les modèles du vivant de Descartes à Leibniz</italic> (Paris, 1998).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Fa‐ti Fan</bold> is an associate professor of history at the State University of New York at Binghamton and the author of <italic>British Naturalists in Qing China: Science, Empire, and Cultural Encounter</italic> (Harvard, 2004). He is now writing a book on science and nationalism in twentieth‐century China.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Patricia Fara</bold> is a Fellow of Clare College, Cambridge. Her recent books include <italic>Newton: The Making of Genius</italic> (2002), <italic>Pandora’s Breeches: Women, Science, and Power</italic> (2004), and <italic>Fatal Attraction: Magnetic Mysteries of the Enlightenment</italic> (2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Tarleton Gillespie</bold> is an assistant professor in the Department of Communication at Cornell University and affiliated with the Department of Science and Technology Studies and the Program in Information Science. His book, <italic>Technology Rules: Copyright and the Re‐Alignment of Digital Culture</italic>, is due in 2007 from MIT Press.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Matthew R. Goodrum</bold> is an assistant professor of history of science in the Department of Science and Technology in Society at Virginia Tech. His publications focus on the history of prehistoric archaeology, the history of paleoanthropology, and theories of human origins from the seventeenth century to the present.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Elizabeth Green Musselman</bold> is Associate Professor of History and Chair of the Feminist Studies Program at Southwestern University. She recently completed <italic>Nervous Conditions: Science and the Body Politic in Early Industrial Britain</italic> (SUNY, 2006) and is now writing a book on African and European knowledge about nature in colonial South Africa.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Michael Hunter</bold> is Professor of History, Birkbeck, University of London. He is the author of <italic>Robert Boyle (1627–91): Scrupulosity and Science</italic> (2000) and editor (with Edward B. Davis) of <italic>The Works of Robert Boyle</italic> (1999–2000) and (with Antonio Clericuzio and Lawrence M. Principe) of <italic>The Correspondence of Robert Boyle</italic> (2001).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Rob Iliffe</bold> is based in the Centre for History of Science, Technology, and Medicine at Imperial College London. He is Editorial Director of the Newton Project and Editor of <italic>History of Science.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Matthew L. Jones,</bold> an assistant professor of history at Columbia University, has just completed <italic>The Good Life in the Scientific Revolution</italic>, forthcoming from Chicago.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ludmilla Jordanova</bold> is Professor of Modern History, King’s College London. Her publications include <italic>History in Practice</italic> (2nd ed., 2006), <italic>Defining Features: Scientific and Medical Portraits, 1660–2000</italic> (2000), and <italic>Nature Displayed: Gender, Science, and Medicine, 1760–1820</italic> (1999).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Peter Keating</bold> teaches the history of science and medicine at the University of Quebec in Montreal, where he is also a member of the Interuniversity Research Center for the Study of Science and Technology (CIRST). His most recent book (with Alberto Cambrosio) is <italic>Biomedical Platforms: Realigning the Normal and the Pathological in Late Twentieth‐Century Biomedicine</italic> (MIT Press, 2003).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Christine Keiner</bold> is completing a book on conflicts between Maryland oystermen and scientists since 1880, as well as research on rats, urban ecology, and rodenticides during the post–World War II era. She teaches in the Science, Technology, and Society/Public Policy Department at the Rochester Institute of Technology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Robert E. Kohler</bold> is Professor of the History and Sociology of Science at the University of Pennsylvania. He writes on the institutions and practices of modern science, especially the field sciences. His books include <italic>Lords of the Fly:</italic> Drosophila <italic>Genetics and the Experimental Life</italic> (1994), <italic>Landscapes and Labscapes: Exploring the Lab–Field Border in Biology</italic> (2002), and <italic>All Creatures: Naturalists, Collectors, and Biodiversity, 1850–1950</italic> (2006).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Susan C. Lawrence</bold> is Associate Professor in the Department of History, College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, and Department of Anatomy and Cell Biology, Carver College of Medicine, at the University of Iowa.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Susan Lindee</bold> is a professor in the Department of the History and Sociology of Science at the University of Pennsylvania. Her research focuses on twentieth‐century biological and biomedical sciences, particularly radiation biology, human genetics, and genomics.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Morris Low</bold> teaches at Johns Hopkins University, where he is Professor of East Asian Sciences and Technology. His recent books include <italic>Science and the Building of a New Japan</italic> (2005), <italic>Building a Modern Japan</italic> (2005), and <italic>Japan on Display: Photography and the Emperor</italic> (Routledge, 2006).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Marjorie Malley</bold> is an independent scholar currently living in Cary, North Carolina. She received the Ph.D. in history from the University of California at Berkeley. She has published on the history of radioactivity, the history of luminescence, and over one hundred biographical and topical articles in the history of science for specialists, general readers, and students.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Keely Maxwell</bold> is an assistant professor of environmental studies at Franklin and Marshall College. Her research focuses on how tourism and conservation politics have produced shifting spaces of nature and culture in Machu Picchu. She holds a Ph.D. from the Yale School of Forestry and Environmental Studies.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>W. Patrick McCray</bold> is an associate professor in the Department of History at the University of California, Santa Barbara, and codirector of the Center for Nanotechnology in Society, a national research and education center funded by the National Science Foundation.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Maureen McNeil</bold> is Professor of Women’s Studies and Cultural Studies in the Institute for Women’s Studies, Chair of the Centre for Science Studies, and a researcher in the Centre for Economic and Social Aspects of Genomics at Lancaster University, United Kingdom. She is the author of <italic>Under the Banner of Science: Erasmus Darwin and His Age</italic> (Manchester University Press, 1987).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Gordon McOuat</bold> teaches the history and philosophy of science in the History of Science and Technology Programme and the Contemporary Studies Programme at the University of King’s College/Dalhousie University in Halifax.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Carolyn Merchant</bold> is Chair of the Division of Society and Environment at the University of California, Berkeley. She has written and edited numerous books on environmental history, philosophy, and ethics. Her most recent book is <italic>Reinventing Eden: The Fate of Nature in Western Culture.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Janet Miron</bold> teaches at Trent University. Her work explores prison and asylum visiting by the public in nineteenth‐century Canada and the United States.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Gregg Mitman</bold> is a professor of history in the Department of Medical History and Bioethics at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. He is the coeditor, with Lorraine Daston, of <italic>Thinking with Animals: New Perspectives on Anthropomorphism</italic> and has written numerous articles on evolutionary theory and public health. He is currently working on a book titled <italic>Breathing Space: An Ecological History of Allergy in America.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Michelle Murphy</bold> is an assistant professor in the Department of History and the Women and Gender Studies Institute at the University of Toronto. She is the author of <italic>Sick Building Syndrome and the Problem of Uncertainty: Environmental Politics, Technoscience, and Women Workers</italic> (Duke University Press, 2006) and the editor of the RaceSci Website (<ext-link ext-link-type="uri"
                   xlink:href="http://www.racesci.org"
                   xlink:type="simple">www.racesci.org</ext-link>).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David J. Murray</bold> is Emeritus Professor of Psychology at Queen’s University, Kingston, Ontario. He has written <italic>Gestalt Psychology and the Cognitive Revolution</italic> (1995), <italic>A History of Western Psychology</italic> (1981, 1988), and, with Gerd Gigerenzer, <italic>Cognition as Intuitive Statistics</italic> (1987).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David E. Nye,</bold> who teaches at Warwick University, received the 2005 Leonardo da Vinci Medal from the Society for the History of Technology. His <italic>Technology Matters: Questions to Live With</italic> (MIT Press) will appear in 2006. He is now researching a social history of U.S. electrification since 1940.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Paolo Palladino</bold> is Senior Lecturer in the Department of History at Lancaster University. He is the author of <italic>Ecology, Entomology, and Agriculture</italic> (Amsterdam, 1996) and <italic>Plants, Patients, and the Historian</italic> (Manchester, 2002).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Katharine Park</bold> teaches the history of science and women's studies at Harvard University and works on science and medicine in the later Middle Ages and Renaissance. Her most recent book is <italic>Wonders and the Order of Nature, 1150–1750</italic>, coauthored with Lorraine Daston. She is now finishing a new project, <italic>Gender, Generation, and the Origins of Human Dissection</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Gregory Radick</bold> is Lecturer in History and Philosophy of Science at the University of Leeds. He is a coeditor of and contributor to <italic>The Cambridge Companion to Darwin</italic> (2003). His book on animal language and the sciences since Darwin will appear with the University of Chicago Press next year.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Peter J. Ramberg</bold> teaches history and philosophy of science at Truman State University in Kirksville, Missouri. The author of <italic>Chemical Structure, Spatial Arrangement: The Early History of Stereochemistry, 1874–1914</italic> (Ashgate, 2003), he is currently working on a biography of the German chemist Johannes Wislicenus (1835–1902) and preparing a sourcebook of readings on the extraterrestrial life debate in the twentieth century.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Marsha Richmond</bold> is Associate Professor of Science and Technology at Wayne State University in Detroit. She is completing a book on the German geneticist Richard Goldschmidt, while also investigating the British and American women who worked in early genetics.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Helen M. Rozwadowski</bold> directs an interdisciplinary maritime studies program and teaches history at the University of Connecticut, Avery Point. Her recent book, <italic>Fathoming the Ocean: The Discovery and Exploration of the Deep Sea</italic> (Harvard University Press, 2005), explores the mid‐nineteenth‐century growth of American and British scientific and cultural interest in the deep ocean.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Satpal Sangwan</bold> is a historian of science with the National Institute of Science, Technology, and Development Studies, New Delhi. He is the author of <italic>Science, Technology, and Colonisation: An Indian Experience, 1757–1857</italic> (Delhi, 1991), and a coeditor of <italic>Uncharted Terrains: Essays on Science Popularisation in Pre‐Independence India</italic> (Delhi, 2000). At present he is working on imperialism and environment.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Warren Schmaus</bold> is Professor of Philosophy at the Illinois Institute of Technology. His research focuses on the history and philosophy of the social sciences, and he is the author of <italic>Rethinking Durkheim and His Tradition</italic> (Cambridge, 2004) and <italic>Durkheim’s Philosophy of Science and the Sociology of Knowledge</italic> (Chicago, 1994).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Robert W. Seidel</bold> is Professor in the History of Science and Technology Program and the Department of Chemical Engineering and Materials Science at the University of Minnesota. He has written two books and numerous articles on nuclear laboratories and is working on a history of technology transfer.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David Sepkoski</bold> is Visiting Assistant Professor of History at Oberlin College. His current research focuses on the intellectual and institutional history of recent evolutionary theory, particularly in the field of paleobiology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Amy Slaton</bold> is Associate Professor of History and Director of the Master’s Program in Science, Technology, and Society at Drexel University. She has written on the history of materials standards and specifications and is now completing a book on the underrepresentation of African Americans in engineering.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ida Stamhuis</bold> teaches history of science at the Free University in Amsterdam and publishes on the history of statistics and quantification, on genetics, and on women in science. She is now working on the Dutch statistical mind, 1850–1940, and on women in the early history of genetics.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Isabelle Stengers</bold> teaches philosophy at the Université Libre de Bruxelles. She has written numerous books, including <italic>Order out of Chaos</italic> (with I. Prigogine), <italic>A History of Chemistry</italic> (with B. Bensaude‐Vincent), <italic>Power and Invention: Situating Science</italic>, and <italic>The Invention of Modern Science</italic>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jeffrey K. Stine</bold> chairs the Division of Medicine and Science at the Smithsonian Institution’s National Museum of American History. He is a past president of the American Society for Environmental History and the Public Works Historical Society.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Charis Thompson</bold> is Associate Professor in the Departments of Gender and Women's Studies and Rhetoric, and Co‐Director of the Science, Technology, and Society Center at the University of California, Berkeley. She has published widely on the subject of reproductive technology and is the author of <italic>Making Parents: The Ontological Choreography of Reproductive Technology</italic> (MIT, 2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Floyd E. Thurston</bold> is a former Adjunct Assistant Professor of Environmental Health at Indiana University and recently retired occupational medicine specialist whose particular interest is in studying the effects of advances in technology and medicine on society in England and colonial North America during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Byron E. Wall</bold> is Master of Norman Bethune College, York University, Toronto. He teaches the history of mathematics and the history of science in the Department of Mathematics and Statistics and the Division of Natural Science, respectively. Recent books are <italic>Glimpses of Reality: Episodes in the History of Science</italic> (2003) and <italic>The Price of Prosperity: Civilization and the Natural World</italic> (2004). His current research is on John Venn and the frequency interpretation of probability.</p>
