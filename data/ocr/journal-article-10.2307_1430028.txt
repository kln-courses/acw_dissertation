Kafka's "Fensterblicke" Michael Braun Technische Hochschule Aachen I Kafka's protagonists often find themselves in claustrophobic spaces which could nicely fit out the ambiente of a gothic novel: they meander through gloomy lofts, dark bedrooms, endlessly long corridors, tiny chambers, cramped ateliers, airless attics, dingy quarters, and, last not least, those intricate and tortuous official buildings in Das SchloJ3 and Der Prozej3. The outer world is even more uncomfortable for Kafka's central figures. Particularly in Das Schlof3, hazy, snowy weather reflects their blocked view upon a world which is no longer perceivable in its entirety. Up to this unfinished novel, narrated space is more and more narrowed and concentrated. In Kafka research, space has mostly been described as functional, curved, discontinuous, and flat, but boundless. Kafka's concept of spatiality is principally based on literary labyrinths: [he] creates labyrinths without depth.... The view is constantly cut off by doors, walls, narrow stairways, and courtyards.... In Kafka one gropes - quite literally - from one thing to the next, incapable of seeing further.' But how do Kafka's heros try to see through and penetrate the borders between inner and outer settings? What do they regard as their remedy "Rettungsloch"2 - leading out of the secluded rooms? One may speculate on the endings of Kafka's longest prose fragments. Will Karl RoBmann's nature fit in the "Naturtheater von Oklahoma?" And what is K., the alleged land surveyor, going to find in the castle? The author did not feel comfortable with conciliatory endings, e.g., in Die Verwandlung, he preferred integrating redemptive moments within the text.</page><page sequence="2">12 GERMAN STUDIES REVIEW Taken literally, these qualities support the protagonists' attempts to set themselves free - sich erlosen - from the claustrophobic areas they have to act in. However, neither they nor the readers are able to establish a borderline between inner and outer world because both share confusingly alike characteristics, above all the opaqueness, for example in the first Schlofi chapter. One conventional literary device to fix at least an architectural frame of separation and conjunction between the inside and the outside is the window. It is a threshold to the objective world and to the human mind. The German-Jewish writer from Prague was certainly well aware of the approximately homonym Hebrew words for window (chalon) and dream (chalom).3 As far as I can see, very few interpretations have paid much attention to the window in Kafka's prose.4 Walter Benjamin, who devoted studies to Kafka from 1931 until 1940, pointed at the codex of gestures which the author always redirects and marks without surrendering the symbolic content to a particular reference.5 Among those gestures, the "Fensterblick" ranks as one of the most important motifs, "Bildsymbole."6 The window is known not only as a significant motif in the Bible, in art and literature,7 but it is also a visual, ornamental, and textual metaphor as well as a thematic aspect which isotopically runs through Kafka's work and occurs at central places. I propose a view on Kafka's texts through the metaphoric use of the windows and the gesture of the "Fensterblick." This reading may contribute to the question of perspective in Kafka in so far as the windows disperse, fix, but also redeem his protagonists' perspective on the narrated world. A tourd'horizon through selected literary works of the last two centuries shows that the window as an aesthetic symbol structures fictional perception and perspectivism. The first author to be mentioned here wrote the verse "Gedichte sind gemalte Fensterscheiben" and let his Gotz von Berlichingen shout the notorious Gotz-Zitat out of the window.8 Another key scene is to be found in Werther's extensive letter from June 16, where he reports his first encounter with Lotte: Wir traten ans Fenster. Es donnerte abseitswarts, und der herrliche Regen sauselte auf das Land, und der erquickendste Wohlgeruch stieg in aller Fuille seiner warmen Luft zu uns auf. Sie [Lotte] stand auf ihren Ellenbogen gestuitzt, ihr Blick durchdrang die Gegend; sie sah den Himmel und auf mich, ich sah ihre Augen tranenvoll, sie legte ihre Hand auf die meinige und sagte: "Klopstock!"9</page><page sequence="3">Michael Braun 13 Werther and Lotte enjoy a multisensual sight. While watching the rainy scene, hearing the thunder, and smelling the fragrance of the wet earth, they evoke Klopstock's ode Die Friihlingsfeier, a eulogy of God's theophany in nature. Obviously they look through the window into another text, the book of nature. The password "Klopstock" releases a stream of feeling which is no longer connected with the objects of their sight. Actually, the would-be couple visualizes and textualizes its inner nature. Werther and Lotte unanimously call on an author of verses they do not have to articulate. The window reveals Werther's derivative feeling and his flight into other references. He perceives nature not only enthusiastically, he also receives it skillfully, which is typical of a writing stay-at-home.'0 Eichendorff's heros do not appear as such pensive writers, and if they stay indoors, they mostly watch out of the window. Richard Alewyn has coined the phrase "Fenstermanie" for this predilection: Das Fenster ist nicht nur ein pleonastisches Requisit, sondern ubt eine entscheidende Funktion aus. Man muB einmal , abgesehen von den Ausblicken, die es gewahrt, darauf achten, ein wie erstaunlich starker optischer und akustischer Verkehr sich des Fensters bedient. Es ist nicht zu sagen, wie viele Klange und Lichter iber diese Schwelle in den Innenraum hereinkommen, die alle als Signale verstanden werden, als Vehikel jener "Magie," . . . als GruBe und Rufe und Botschaften aus dem freien in den geschlossenen Raum, aus jener Weite, die die Freiheit und das Leben, in die Enge, die Kerker, Krankheit und Tod bedeuten. I I For Alewyn, the view through the window serves to establish a dichotomy and hierarchy of open and closed space. In Eichendorff's novel Ahnung und Gegenwart( 1810-1812; published 1815) or in Ausdem Leben eines Taugenichts (1826) for instance, the protagonists signify the objects of the outer world through the window, which lets through the sparkling outer scenery. Things may be disorganized for the Taugenichts, but he is still able to cope with the confusion by looking wistfully out of the window. The melancholic prospect out of the painter's untidy studio window in Rome onto a nicer home scenery offers escape from a setting where he feels alienated. And of even more importance, the guileless good-for-nothing flees from the mysterious Italian castle through a window.'2 While the window does not make it difficult for the Taugenichts to get his bearings, in Kafka's Amerika dusty, stifling, overcrowded, lightless rooms frustrate Karl RoBmann's sense for orientation. Hoffmann's narrative Des Vetters Eckfenster (1822), a teichoscopic discourse on various perspectives from a corner window, two cousins, a gouty, resigned writer, and his visitor observe the bustle in the market place below. Not too long before the partially lame writer had met a beautiful flower girl, who was deeply absorbed in one of his books, and embarrassed her with the confession of his authorship. She seemed to be petrified, struck by the thought that books are not only technically (re)produced, distributed, and consumed, but also written by human beings. Facing the independence of the commodity "book" and hence the superfluous role of the author, he fell into a literary crisis. Being under the incessant spell of the window23 and sometimes so desperate that he wanted to hurl himself out of the window,24 he gave literary form to the motif since his early narrative trials. Until 1907 the young jurist Kafka was living in the Zeltnergasse 3, a busy shopping street in Prague. There, in a small room with a view out of the lane window, he wrote his first texts, among them some of the prose sketches later published under the title Betrachtung(l 913). Two of these short prose texts explicitly circumfere the "Fensterblick" and, thereby, anticipate significant motifs of his subsequent narratives. The spectator's befooled eye takes this light materially although it is merely the outcome of a metaphorical allusion to something outside the empiric world. On the other hand, this seems to be the only way to perceive the light, for only the comparison between a physical and a metaphysical world makes the visual impression possible. Yet what is visualized and textualized remains unyielding, untrackable, and indeterminable because the olympic perspective of Karl's hometown has disappeared and his senses are forced to adapt to a foreign and distant world, to a shifting personal perspective as well as to a different language. Karl's logocentric pursuit of the true and just word (esp. in the "Heizer" chapter) maneuvers him into a new labyrinth again and again34 where he mostly fails in reorganizing the material world. Kafka, despite the commentators' zeal for metaphysical interpretations, distrusts the language of metaphysics, for it pretends to be able to appropriate being (Sein) by determining it more metaphorico. The main character, Georg Bendemann, a confident young urban</page><page sequence="8">18 GERMAN STUDIES REVIEW businessman, enjoys a sunny prospect out of his window, sitting at his writing desk39 in the absent mood of a contemplative writer. Just having closed a letter to his "Jugendfreund,"40 he looks through the window onto the river, the bridge, and the faint green hills at the other bank. Time and place are explicitly named: "an einem Sonntagvormittag im schonsten Friihjahr."4' Yet Georg's subsequent subliminal reflections undermine the "realistic" fiction. The expository setting is by no means a tableau vivant, as in Fontane's novels. Georg's emptied "Fensterblick" lacks an object of perception; the style indirect libre accompanies the objectivated way into his mind. Kob's notion of "assoziatives Denken," a key term in his analysis of Amnerika, very well characterizes the techniques Georg's reflections and thoughts follow.42 Like Rofmann, he is neither willing nor able to adapt his acts to others' intentions, to think object-related. Georg starts to narrate a text which indeed hides and mirrors another text - the letter to his friend. Quite convincingly thus, Corngold calls Georg a deluded writer who dissimulates a text.43 The letter stays as closed as the friend's literal flight - "formlich gefluchtet"44 _ to Russia remains an enigma: "Hast du wirklich diesen Freund in Petersburg?"4s Every single word of the father's question - a semantical swing - has entailed different and opposed interpretations. What is questioned: that Georg still has this friend, that Georg, and not his father, can call on the friend, that the friend is (cf. the deixis dieseni) as Georg perceives him, that he is a friend, if at all, or that he is in Petersburg, and not merely in Georg's mind? At least a concise resolving is thwarted by the changing perspective in the father's dark bedroom with its closed window across from a high wall and by the histrionic manner and language of father and son. But the Russian friend's presence that Georg suppresses in front of his light window seizes him physically and is ubiquitous as soon as the father pretends to see through him.46 The windows frame the father's exposed, exhibitionistic behavior and his son's no less exposed attempts to forget and repress what he has observed and intended to do. When the son enters the father's gloomy and separated room he intrudes in another subconscious area which is subject to interpretation, trial - and error.47 The death sentence, an execution of the family conversation, remains an isolated tip of the iceberg. Kafka interpreted the perspective in the story as "vielleicht ein Rundgang um Vater und Sohn," a discourse around, not on, father and son.48 The "Ur-teil" then is the dissemination of the primordial unity or harmony of creator and creature into a (vicious?) circle of perspectives and interpretations. Within this concept of circularity, Kafka frustrates expectations of coherence and commensurability, conjured up by a counterconcept of linearity. Thus I shall briefly refer to what I regard as a profitable, sound approach to Kafka's works. Undermining the conventional mimetic forms and principles of writing, Kafka undoes the hermeneutic structure of his texts. They produce images of reflections. One of them is the window, an aesthetic and poetological figure, which mirrors the characters' view of the world ( Weltanschauung) and the objects of this world. The "Fensterblicke" expose what the protagonists' reflection has created: a reflexive, broken prospect upon the world: "Bei Kafka sind die zersplitterten Bilder und gebrochenen Sinnbezuige Produkt von Reflexion und zielen durch</page><page sequence="10">20 GERMAN STUDIES REVIEW Dekomposition, Entstellung und Verweigerung von Stimmigkeit auf Reflexion beim Rezipienten."60 Kafka himself was very distant towards theory, though he enjoyed interpreting his own stories.6' The need for interpretation is inscribed in the texts. They invite preunderstandings, and a coherent analysis may succeed in clearing up some provocative cracks and fissures within the text and in granting "Lichtblicke in eine unendliche Verwirrung."62 But there is no archimedical spot in Kafka from which an interpretation could start. It can be argued with good reason that apparently Kafka's writing does not know an inwardness, just bodies, gestures, surfaces, "die den Leser in eine Art interpretatives Delirium versetzen, das verzweifelt nach 'tieferen' Bedeutungen hinter und unter der stummen Oberflache des geschriebenen Textes sucht."63 Kafka built himself and his texts up as rooms and like rooms64 which do not leave us without a view. But the graphical, realistic prospect of objects on the one hand and narrated insights in dreams, minds, and thoughts on the other hand are mutually connected; the act of referring (to meanings, etc.) itself remains undecided. Corngold has stated that Kafka's "works themselves construct, dramatize, and reject principles generating types of interpretation."65 That is why Kafka is still compelling for both interpretation and the reflection and reconsideration of its basis - which is the text.
