         <title>Introduction.</title>
         <p>Alan Musgrave (1985, 208) launched an objection against the <italic>Distinction</italic> between observable and unobservable concrete objects when drawn within the confines of Bas C. van Fraassen's (1980) epoch‚Äêmaking and passionately debated view of science called <italic>Constructive Empiricism</italic> (CE). Musgrave's objection is an argument to the effect that the Distinction relies on what by the lights of CE is impossible. The Distinction, which is anthropomorphic, vague, and meaningful, is essential for CE, because CE tells us to believe as true only those propositions of an accepted scientific theory that are about actual observables only (proposition ‚â° class of logically equivalent statements). Van Fraassen (1985, 256) provided a succinct response to Musgrave's criticism, which Musgrave (2002) confessed not to understand ‚Äúand nobody I asked could explain it to me either.‚Äù I argue that van Fraassen's response is not enough; moreover, it does not put the finger on exactly where Musgrave's objection goes wrong; I shall attempt to remedy the situation (Section 4). I show that Musgrave's criticism can be strengthened, in that it shows that CE cannot solve a problem I shall call ‚ÄòMusgrave's Problem,‚Äô which CE however must solve; I end this paper by pointing to a route of how CE can solve it all the same (Section 5). Musgrave's argument is the pi√®ce de r√©sistance, which is why, by way of a thorough warm‚Äêup exercise, I first deal with three other objections. First I argue that Michael Friedman's (1982) objection and Jeffrey Foss' (1984) closely related objection are both wrong (Section 2). Then I provide a way out of the tension in CE, observed by Foss (1984) and by Richard Creath (1985), between the <italic>vagueness</italic> of the Distinction and the <italic>sharpness</italic> of the distinction of CE between pragmatic acceptance and epistemic belief in the truth (Section 3).1</p>
         <p>As we shall see, all objections against the Distinction are not objections against it simpliciter, but against <italic>drawing it within the confines of</italic> CE. All critics argue that drawing the Distinction somehow clashes with certain principles that constitute CE. To emphasize, whether or not the critics <italic>also</italic> want to criticise the <italic>epistemic significance</italic> of the Distinction (which van Fraassen attributes to it) is not relevant for the present purposes. The present purpose is to show that arguments against the very possibility of drawing the Distinction within CE are unconvincing‚Äîthe possibility of drawing it is logically prior to discussing its epistemic significance, any such discussion presupposes the Distinction is in place. Another issue not relevant for the present purposes is how to understand the concept of observability itself‚Äîone could argue that without a clear conception of what observability is we have no clear conception of what CE is either. I address this topic at length in another paper (Muller 2004); in this paper I take the meaning of observability for granted.</p>
         <p>One might wonder whether all these objections are not trivially or obviously fallacious so that they do not merit a response: they will be washed away in the fullness of time, it is a shame they were published in the first place. I do not believe that all of these objections <italic>are</italic> fallacious‚Äîcertainly they are neither <italic>trivially</italic> nor <italic>obviously</italic> so. That is why these objections merit a response. Further, there is even some evidence to the contrary. For in what arguably is the most comprehensive discussion of the Distinction to date, Andre Kukla (1998, 142) writes: ‚ÄúFriedman's objection devastates Van Fraassen's distinction,‚Äù and he refers approvingly to the other objections treated in the current paper: ‚ÄúWe've found trouble enough for Van Fraassen's Distinction; one troublesome point more or less won't significantly alter its status.‚Äù (139). So the Distinction is judged to be in trouble. Whence this defence.</p>
         <fn-group>
            <fn id="fn2">

      <sec id="sc2">
         <label>2.<x xml:space="preserve"> </x>
         </label>
         <title>Compositions of Unobservable.</title>
         <p>In his review of van Fraassen 1980, Friedman (1982) fired an argument at the Distinction which we shall quote in full. As I reported in the Introduction, Kukla (1998, 142) regards Friedman's argument as literally ‚Äúdevastating.‚Äù <disp-quote>
               <p>The observable objects are themselves characterised from within the world picture of modern physics: as those complicated systems of elementary particles of the right size and configuration for reflecting light in the visible spectrum, for example. Hence, <italic>if I assert that observable objects exist, I have also asserted that certain complicated systems of elementary particles exist.</italic> But I have thereby asserted that (individual) elementary particles exist as well! <italic>I have not,</italic> in accordance with Van Fraassen's constructive empiricism, <italic>remained agnostic about the unobservable part of the world.</italic> (Friedman 1982, 278, my italics)</p>
            </disp-quote>
         </p>
         <p>Notice that Friedman objects against drawing the Distinction coherently within the confines of CE: drawing it somehow clashes with an epistemic principle of CE which advices one to remain neutral about what an accepted scientific theory asserts about the unobservable part of the world.2 The epistemic significance of the Distinction is not an issue in <italic>this</italic> criticism.</p>
         <p>CE surely holds that both acceptance and belief in the truth are, or ought to be, closed under implication. So <italic>if</italic> CE accepts the Standard Model of Elementary Particles and their Interactions (<bold>SM</bold>) from physics and accepts therefore that all actual concrete objects consist of the elementary particles of <bold>SM</bold>, then CE must accept (the proposition) that these individual particles exist too. The leap from pragmatic acceptance to epistemic belief is only licensed by CE's epistemic policy when it comes to <italic>empirical</italic> propositions (which we define as being about actual observables only). Since the statement ‚Äòconcrete objects consist of elementary particles‚Äô (Œæ) is not empirical, because it is (also) about unobservable elementary particles, it does not follow, given CE, that we must believe Œæ. On the contrary, whilst CE gladly <italic>accepts</italic> the existence of unobservable elementary particles and <italic>accepts</italic> Œæ, because these follow from the acceptance of <bold>SM</bold>, CE does not believe that it is true that they exist. It seems that Friedman has overlooked the crucial distinction between pragmatic acceptance and epistemic belief, as well as the fact that Œæ is for CE <italic>not</italic> an object of belief or disbelief.</p>
         <p>Kukla has understood Friedman's argument as establishing the incompatibility between (a) the epistemic policy of CE and (b) the <italic>theory‚Äêladenness</italic> of scientific theories, which Van Fraassen subscribes to (cf. Kukla 1998, 139‚Äì141). How can (a) and (b) clash? Kukla says they clash because ‚Äòa composite object of more than 10<sup>23</sup> atoms of Carbon exists‚Äô is laden by an acceptable theory, and hence acceptable, but is also about something observable, hence susceptible to belief. So it is believed that individual atoms exist because it is believed that 10<sup>23</sup> Carbon‚Äêatoms exist, in contradiction to the neutral epistemic attitude about unobservables commanded by CE.</p>
         <p>But, first of all, to acknowledge that our language is ‚Äòtheory‚Äêladen‚Äô is to acknowledge the rather banal fact that the use of particular words and expressions in our language is governed in certain ways by the theories we accept; in fact, theories are the main providers of the ‚Äòsemantic grammar‚Äô (Wittgenstein) of scientific concepts, they provide the most rules how to use these concepts, and this is constitutive for their meaning. To acknowledge <italic>this</italic> fact does not commit one <italic>to believe</italic> any proposition about the world of the theory, whether the proposition is empirical or not. Therefore, the incompatibility between (a) and (b) which Kukla discerns <italic>must</italic> be a chimera. Specifically, CE does not believe that about 10<sup>23</sup> Carbon‚Äêatoms exist. When we veridically see a diamond, say, we are prepared to believe that this diamond is observable and that it exists; but we are not prepared to believe that 10<sup>23</sup> Carbon‚Äêatoms exist, because saying that a diamond consists of 10<sup>23</sup> Carbon‚Äêatoms is an interpretation of what we see partly in terms of unobservables, which CE may <italic>accept</italic> but <italic>does not believe</italic> in. ‚ÄòTheory‚Äêladen‚Äô and ‚Äòladen‚Äêwith‚Äêunobservables‚Äô are distinct predicates of propositions; it seems that Kukla has confused them. Theory‚Äêladen sentences can be empirical (‚Äòladen‚Äêwith‚Äêonly‚Äêactual‚Äêobservables‚Äô) and non‚Äêempirical; only the aforementioned are the type of theory‚Äêladen statements susceptible to belief and disbelief. Empirical propositions can be theory‚Äêladen or not: ‚ÄòToday at such‚Äêand‚Äêsuch place‚Äêtime on the face of planet Earth the sun is visible‚Äô is empirical and not theory‚Äêladen, whereas ‚ÄòToday at such‚Äêand‚Äêsuch place‚Äêtime on the face of planet Earth a gigantic, continuously exploding Hydrogen‚Äêbomb is visible‚Äô (which is what the sun is according to modern physics) is empirical and theory‚Äêladen. As soon as unobservables enter statements about observables, these statements do not involve <italic>only</italic> actual unobservables and therefore fail to qualify as empirical; these statements can at best be accepted on the basis of CE, never believed as true or as false.3</p>
         <p>Mutatis mutandis for Foss' objection against CE. He points out that photons are <italic>unobservable</italic> so taking lots of them cannot not change <italic>that;</italic> but lots of them constitute a visible beam of light when it passes a cloud of cigar smoke, say; from this he concludes that the Distinction is unacceptably <italic>ambiguous.</italic>4 In Foss' objection photons have taken the place of Friedman's atoms and I therefore refer to the previous paragraph.</p>
         <p>Another way to respond to this criticism is as follows: being observable may be closed under composition (building a house of observable bricks results in an equally observable house), but being unobservable is not, anymore than being light (a house is heavy but the molecules composing it are very light). There is nothing ambiguous going on here.</p>
         <p>At the danger of being patronising, let me finally address the question how to understand that according to science ‚Äòa bunch of light‚Äô and ‚Äò10<sup>10</sup> photons‚Äô are <italic>identical.</italic> I would say that both expressions have a distinct meaning but an <italic>identical observable referent.</italic> In ‚Äò10<sup>10</sup> photons‚Äô an expression occurs that has an <italic>unobservable</italic> referent (‚Äòphoton‚Äô), whereas this is not the case in ‚Äòa bunch of light‚Äô; whence their difference in meaning.</p>
         <fn-group>
            <fn id="fn3">
               <label>2. </label>
               <p>The term ‚Äòneutral‚Äô with respect to a proposition stands for ‚Äòneither believing nor disbelieving in the truth of the proposition whilst acknowledging both as genuine possibilities.‚Äô Although the term ‚Äòagnosticism‚Äô threatens to become standard terminology, I see no advantages of flooding philosophy of science with centuries‚Äêold terminology from theological discourse; hence the neutral term ‚Äòneutral.‚Äô</p>
            </fn>
            <fn id="fn4">
               <label>3. </label>
               <p>For an elaboration on the issue of theory‚Äêladenness, of observation, of ‚Äòobservation‚Äêreports‚Äô and their relation to the Distinction, refer to van Fraassen 1993.</p>
            </fn>
            <fn id="fn5">
               <label>4. </label>

      <sec id="sc3">
         <label>3.<x xml:space="preserve"> </x>
         </label>
         <title>Sharpness Out of Vagueness.</title>
         <p>Creath (1985, 335‚Äê336) does not object to the Distinction, but submits it is incoherent to admit that the Distinction is <italic>vague</italic> and then to use it to draw the supposedly <italic>sharp</italic> distinction between pragmatic acceptance and epistemic belief in the truth. Foss (1984, 84‚Äì85) agrees and charges Van Fraassen with having paid hardly any attention in general to the consequences of the vagueness of the Distinction. (Notice again that the epistemic relevance of the Distinction is not the issue here.) Let us recall that a predicate is <italic>vague</italic> iff there are clear examples for which it holds, clear counter‚Äêexamples and ambiguous examples. Being bald, fat, tall, portable, rich, and observable are (unambiguous) examples. But then, should we only <italic>accept</italic> or <italic>believe</italic> a proposition of an accepted theory about a concrete object for which it is ambiguous whether it is observable or not? What do we do with ambiguous cases?5 There is a straightforward answer to this question.</p>
         <p>The answer is that we should accept ambiguous cases but not believe them. Imperative: when in doubt about observabililty, do not make the mental jump to the level of true belief but remain at the level of acceptance. Simply revise the epistemic policy of CE to asserting that only unambiguously empirical propositions of accepted theories are to be believed as true, where an <italic>unambiguously empirical</italic> proposition by definition is a proposition about objects that are actual and unambiguously observable; a neutral attitude is reserved for unempirical and for ambiguously empirical propositions of an accepted theory. In this fashion we draw the line on the safe side without denying that observability is vague. The distinction between pragmatic acceptance and epistemic belief remains sufficiently sharp.</p>
         <p>Consider the ethical principle that it is worse to convict an innocent man than to acquit a guilty man. Following this principle should guarantee that prisons contain criminals only and do not contain a single innocent man (beyond reasonable doubt anyway). Sometimes a criminal will not go to prison, for instance due to some silly error of procedure committed by the prosecutor. If the procedures are complicated, so that errors are bound to happen given human fallibility, but this complexity is necessary to prevent innocent persons to be send to prison (the ethical principle mentioned above), then we are prepared to pay the price of an occasional guilty man not being convicted. Similarly, when we amend CE slightly, in the manner explained in the previous paragraph, we have the guarantee that what we demarcate as scientific knowledge is about actual observables only and never about the wretched unobservables or about borderline cases. The price we pay is that sometimes we remain neutral where we could have believed or disbelieved; but we never believe or disbelieve what we should have remained neutral about. <italic>Scientific knowledge,</italic> then, is by constructive‚Äêempiricist definition the whole of all unambiguously empirical propositional content of all accepted scientific theories‚Äîit grows steadily. This is what I mean by drawing the line on the safe side.</p>
         <fn-group>
            <fn id="fn6">
               <label>5. </label>
               <p>In all interesting cases of unobservables that occur in accepted scientific theories, the objects are unambiguously unobservable (electrons, forces, gluon‚Äêfields, black holes, tau‚Äêneutrino's, superstrings, and so forth), which makes the case of ambiguous unobservables largely ‚Äòacademic‚Äô (in its pejorative sense). <italic>Gratia</italic> Dieks.</p>

Thus even if CE were to walk this road, there is no guarantee whatsoever‚Äîto say the very least‚Äîthere is a road at all that would bring CE to the desired destination.</p>
            <p>Moreover, when we recall that (19) is logically equivalent to:

               </disp-formula>

            <p>I conclude that the strategy of trying to establish the categorical belief in the observability of electrons so as to solve Musgrave's Problem via an antecedent that is a theorem of logic (Rescue Attempt 2) fails. I am therefore back where I started in this Section: neither van Fraassen nor Kukla has shown how to solve Musgrave's Problem, because what they have shown is (16), and that is too weak to carry, ultimately, the objective and non‚Äêpragmatic distinction between knowledge and pragmatics in science (Section 4.3).</p>

      <sec id="sc5">
         <label>5.<x xml:space="preserve"> </x>
         </label>
         <title>Musgrave's Problem.</title>
         <sec id="sc5.1">
            <label>5.1.<x xml:space="preserve"> </x>
            </label>
            <title>Strengthening of Musgrave's Criticism.</title>
            <p>The present Section will end in strengthening Musgrave's criticism (Section 5.1); in the next and final Section I point to a road that leads to a solution (Section 5.2).</p>
            <p>Van Fraassen (1980, 57‚Äì59) regards ‚Äúwhat is observable as a <italic>theory‚Äêindependent</italic> question. It is a function of facts about us <italic>qua</italic> organisms in the world.‚Äù The observability of concrete objects is ‚Äúa subject for empirical science and not for philosophical analysis‚Äù (1980, 57). We should <italic>not</italic> make our judgments about the unobservability of electrons rely on some particular scientific theory in the first place, such as <bold>L</bold> or QED, notwithstanding the fact that physical theories such as QED supply us with the very concept of an electron and its properties (one of which is its unobservability). Musgrave's final conclusion that CE ‚Äúcannot draw the distinction it requires‚Äù follows from conclusion (9), keeping in mind that Musgrave's argument can be repeated for every theory and every (actual or non‚Äêactual) unobservable object, iff the following premise is taken aboard:
<disp-formula id="df22">
                  <graphic xlink:href="df22"
                           alt-version="no"
                           mimetype="image"
                           position="float"
                           xlink:type="simple"/>
               </disp-formula>
Premise (22) then also enforces premise (7) of Musgrave's critical argument.</p>
            <p>Since CE rejects tacit premise (22) of Musgrave's argument, this rejection blocks the deduction of his unwelcome general conclusion that CE cannot draw the distinction it requires. Is CE now safe?</p>
            <p>It seems so. Van Fraassen could take Musgrave's argument as a reductio ad absurdum of statement (22) and could draw the following moral from Musgrave's reasoning: if you rely on theories to tell you what unobservables are, you get into trouble, so don't do it. Perhaps, then, this Chapter in the debate about the concept of observability is yet another illustration of the eternal truth that one philosopher's modus ponens is another's modus tollens. One philosopher (van Fraassen) says that we should not and admittedly even cannot base our belief in the unobservability of electrons on some theory, whereas another (Musgrave) says that we must do so and therefore reject any philosophical view of science that forbids us to do this, such as CE.</p>
            <p>So it seems that CE is safe indeed. Stalemate is not defeat. Furthermore, we now have a solution of Musgrave's problem on our hands: to acquire the belief that (it is true that) some given concrete object <italic>Y</italic> is observable or unobservable, we perform scientific research. Will this work?</p>
            <p>To begin with, van Fraassen has recently declared that the question ‚ÄòWhat is observable?,‚Äô although <italic>in principle</italic> it is a theory‚Äêindependent question, ‚Äúin practice <italic>we must rely on our current best theories</italic> to answer that question‚Äù (Monton &amp; van Fraassen 2003, 414). In practice observability is theory‚Äêdependent but in principle it is theory‚Äêindependent. Hence in principle CE is safe from, but in practice it falls prey to Musgrave's criticism, because in practice CE apparently adumbrates Musgrave's tacit premise (22). So will performing scientific research to decide whether object <italic>Y</italic> is observable or unobservable work <italic>in principle</italic>?</p>
            <p>Let us consider again Musgrave's electrons. For electrons, Musgrave's Problem states: how exactly to acquire the belief that electrons are unobservable when we discard any reliance on theories but investigate the matter experimentally instead? Observability is an objective property of concrete objects in relation to the light‚Äêdetectors above our eyes. The relevant empirical research investigates what types of object the members of Œµ can see, under what conditions and in what circumstances they can see objects. These investigations will provide CE with a sound, objective, empirical basis for its beliefs in the observability or unobservability of any type of object. Now, the results of empirical research fall under our experience. Van Fraassen (1985, 253): ‚ÄúExperience can give us information only about what is both observable and actual.‚Äù But then how will empirical research ever provide a sound and objective basis for believing that some object is <italic>unobservable</italic>?</p>
            <p>Suppose that under a variety of conditions and in a variety of circumstances members of Œµ do <italic>not</italic> observe some putative object <italic>Y.</italic> What, then, must <italic>ce</italic> believe? That (a) Œ≥ is unobservable and exists, or that (b) Œ≥ does not exist? Both possibilities seem alive in the face of the supposed null‚Äêoutcomes of experiments. If <italic>ce</italic> wants to conclude that (a) Œ≥ is unobservable, <italic>ce</italic> must prior to this conclusion believe that Œ≥ exists in order to rule out (b). But how can <italic>ce</italic> acquire the belief that Œ≥ does not exist? If <italic>ce</italic> wants to conclude that (b) Œ≥ does not exist, <italic>ce</italic> must first believe that Œ≥ is unobservable to rule out (a). And so forth ad infinitum.</p>
