Thomas Naval Biodynamics Lab ABSTRACT The objectives of this study are to quantify the biomechanical properties of the human neck which govern head and neck dynamic response and to establish the mechanisms responsible for primary aspects of response. Computer simulations with the MVMA 2-D and VOM 3-D occupant dynamics models were performed using head and neck sled input response data from human subjects at the Naval Biodynamics Laboratory for input and comparison. Predicted dynamic response data and preliminary values for biomechanical parameters in a three-dimensional head/neck model capable of accurately simulating response for -X, +Y, and -X+Y sled acceleration vectors are presented. The established analytical model should accurately predict head and neck responses in simulations of real-world automobile crashes where direct head impact is not involved. Additionally, the model can be used to assist in development of a design plan for the neck of advanced anthropomorphic test dummies. The results presented in this paper are preliminary in that ongoing project effort can be expected to generate improved and more complete model data. HUMAN SUBJECT DATA generated at NBDL were utilized with the goal of understanding and explaining the mechanisms involved in head and neck dynamic response. Most of the work presented here involves use of computer models for simulation of subject response. Thus, in contrast to studies (1-4,9)* which attempt to parameterize head-neck response by plotting together the responses from many sled tests, this work uses test responses primarily for comparison with response data predicted by * Numbers in parentheses designate references at end of paper. simulation models. Examination of the differences between experimental and simulation results guides the adjustment of values for neck parameters and helps to identify and quantify response mechanisms. BACKGROUND This study used NBDL human subject test data from sled tests in three different acceleration vectors: -X, +Y, and -X+Y. The NBDL impact test program has been described in several previous papers (5- 1 5) . These tests are designed specifically for study of head and neck dynamics. In each test, the subject's torso is belted as tightly as possible to a full height seat so that head-neck motions will be related as unambiguously as possible to sled motion. In the case of +Gy (lateral) tests, a sideboard is used to provide additional torso restraint. Accel erometer packages are firmly attached to the head and over the tip of the spinous process of the first thoracic vertebra, Tl. Computer processing of instrumentation data and high-speed film data provides displacements, velocities, and accelerations for linear and angular motions. Figure 1 illustrates a subject in a test seat, with restraint system and instrumentation packages. The laboratory coordinate system on the left in Figure 2 can be used to identify the subject's seated configuration in any test. A subject seated with his back in the direction of sled motion, i.e., downtrack, is facing in the +X direction, as in Figure 1. The laboratory Y-axis is positive to his left and positive Z is upward. SIMULATION OF -Gx TESTS WITH THE MVMA 2-D MODEL Initial simulation effort primarily involved use of the MVMA Two-Dimens ional Crash Victim Simulation model (16-19) to study sagittal plane head-neck response to -Gx acceleration inputs. The MVMA 2-D model's two-joint neck and the head coordinate system are illustrated in Figure A. The MVMA 2-D head coordinate system differs from the NBDL definitions in two ways: 1) the head Z-axis is positive downward and 2) the head origin is at the center of gravity (with instrumentation). Test results and simulation results presented in this paper were made compatible by transforming experimental test data for plotting with simulation data rather than vice versa. The values used for A-P and S-l separations between the occipital condyles (see Figure 3) and the CG of the un i nstrumented head were 1.93 and 5-75 respectively. These values are for measurements in and normal to the Frankfort plane and were taken from cadaver data reported in the literature. The CG is anterior to the head anatomical origin by O.83 cm and superior by 3-12 cm (20). The head anatomical origin is anterior to the condyles by 1.10 cm and superior by 2.63 cm (9»21). The head CG location was shifted anteriorly by O.35 cm and interiorly by 0.20 cm to account for instrumentation mass (9). Head and neck mass and moment of inertia data for the simulations were taken from results of Beier, et al. (20). Head mass was A. 305 kg and the moment of inertia was 222 kg cm*cm. These values were adjusted by amounts 0.522 kg and 75 kg cm*cm because of instrumentation mass (9). The neck mass used was 1 .5A7 kg, a value developed from data of Walker (21). A SENSITIVITY STUDY - A sensitivity study showed little dependence of simulation results on the anthropometric measures described above for values intended to span approximately a 50th percentile to 95th percentile range. The greatest sensitivity seen was for the moment of inertia, for which the larger value caused a four percent decrease in the peak head angular acceleration. The larger A-P dimensions caused an increase of less than three percent in the peak head acceleration, but the range of values used was probably small since variations proportional to exterior head dimensions were assumed. There was very little sensitivity to S-l dimensions. Use of different sources of cadaver measurement data would probably produce greater variation in simulation results than described here for the 50th to 95th percentile variations. A more complete sensitivity study will be carried out and will be described in a future paper. SIMULATION OF -Gx TESTS AT 6 g - With all parameters held constant at values determined in the 15 g simulations, a -Gx simulation was made for 6 g tests averaged for the same five subjects (tests LXOi*79, LXOi»93, LX0533. LX053^ LXO629) - Some of the graphical results are shown in Figures 20 through 23- The degree of agreement between simulation and test results is somewhat less good than for 15 g's. Further simulations made for 6 g suggest that the primary cause of degraded results is the linear torque-deflection relationships that were assumed as a first approximation for neck bending. Nonlinear relationships should probably be established for both the upper and lower neck (see Figure 4). Specific parameter values have not yet been determined for representing these non 1 i near i t i es , but it is expected that bilinear forms will be adequate. The established relationship would be valid for essentially the entire range of acceleration levels for human subject exposure since 15 g is considered to be approximately maximum in the -X vector. A comparison of Figures 8 and 20 illustrates the need for the noni i near i ty to be modeled if the simulations are to predict head-neck dynamics over a range of impact severity levels. While the amplitude of head angular motion for 15 g sled tests is well predicted by the linear representation, the motion is too retarded in the less severe 6 g case. This means that the torque-deflection relationships must be less stiff for smaller deflections - as result from 6 g impacts but more stiff for larger deflections. Such a relationship is illustrated in Figure 2b. Values for the parameters will be determined by making iterative 6 g and 15 g simulations. The 6 g simulations will determine the slope of the lower segment of the bilinear curve; the I5 g simulations will determine the upper slope. The established curve can be expected to improve simulation results for both 6 g's and 15 g's. SIMULATION OF TESTS WITH THE VOM 3-D MODEL With midsagittal plane properties determined from the MVMA 2-D model simulations of -Gx tests, a three-dimensional model -- the VOM 3~ū (24,25) was used for simulation of NBDL tests in which significant nonplanar motions occurred. Both +Gy and -Gx+Gy tests have been simulated to date with emphasis on +Gy tests at 5 g» which is considered medium severity in the +Y vector. Before nonplanar motion tests were simulated with VOM 3-D, features were added to the model so that for planar motion tests it could function the same as the MVMA 2-D; The -Gx simulations previously described were then repeated with VOM 3-D and plots of dynamic responses were found to be virtually coincident with MVMA 2-D plots. The VOM 3-D head-neck linkage is illustrated in Figure 28. This model represents the neck with two joints at either end of an extensible link, as does the MVMA 2-D. While the VOM 3~ū joint model reduces in two dimensions to the MVMA 2-D joint model, it is much more complicated in its full generality. Some of the considerations that were made in developing the 3~ū joint model are outlined in Table 2. Primary biomechani cal parameters of the 3~D joint model are indicated in Table 1-B. They include nonlinear bending stiffnesses and damping coefficients as general functions of the direction at which bending occurs (e.g., for flexion, extension, lateral flexion, or any intermediate direction). The energy restitution coefficient is also a function of the bending direction, or "heading." Additionally, similar properties relate to rotation of the head with respect to the torso (relative yaw). As in the two-dimensional analog, axial compression and elongation properties are modeled for the neck 1 i nk . Figure 29 illustrates the manner in which bending stiffnesses have been represented as functions of the heading angle for bending at a joint. Although the model has the capacity to use fully nonlinear torque-deflection relationships that are themselves continuous functions of the heading angle, these properties have been highly parameterized in the +Gy and -Gx+Gy simulations made to date. In particular, the stiffness coefficient k ( i ) in the figure is the linear coefficient (for the condyles or Tl) , and just three values define its dependence on heading angle. The values at headings of 0 degrees and l80 degrees are the flexion and extension stiffnesses determined from the -Gx simulations; the value at a 90 degree heading is one of the parameters determined by iterative adjustment in simulations with the three-dimensional model. Linear interpolation between the specified points is assumed an adequate joint representation for obtaining a reasonable first approximation to test results. Figure 3 and Figure 30 illustrate differences between the NBDL and VOM 3~D definitions of coordinates for describing head motion. In addition to differences mentioned^ previously in regard to -Gx, MVMA 2-D simulations, the following must be accommodated through appropriate coordinate transformations: 1) +Y axes are opposite in direction; 2) the axes of the VOM 3~ū head coordinate system are along the principal axes for the head inertia tensor and are therefore, in general, rotated in the midsagittal plane relative to the NBDL axes; and 3) Euler angles are defined differently, both with respect to order of rotation and direction (sense) of rotation. As for -Gx, the +Gy test data are transformed for comparison with the simulation data. It may be noted from Figures 3 and 30 that the yaw, pitch, and roll Euler angles (plotted for simulations) are for successive angular displacements about the S-l, L-R (lateral), and A-P principal axes, respectively; i.e., they are for motions of "rotation," "nod" (positive in extension), and "lateral tilt." In accordance with the findings of Beier, et al. (20), the angle between the head super i or- i nf er i or principal axis and a normal to the Frankfort plane was taken to be 3*+ degrees, approximately as illustrated in Figure 3« Beier values were used also for the moments of inertia about the head A-P and S-l principal axes: 210 and 146 kg cm*cm, respectively. Our calculations show that instrumentation mass necessitates adjustments to these values of about 77 and 1 kg cm*cm, respectively. The effect of instrumentation mass on the 34 degree orientation of the axes is small and was not included in our simulations. SIMULATION OF +Gy TESTS AT 5 9 - While -Gx simulations were made for comparison with averaged responses of subjects in five sled runs, simulations in the +Y vector were made for a single NBDL test, run LX2313 (Subject 93)« The approach of making separate simulations for different subjects allows study of correlations between subject response and subject physical characteristics. For any particular NBDL run, however, it has the disadvantages of basing findings on a possibly unusual individual and perhaps on extreme test results. Subject 93 was selected for simulation primarily because more test data were available for him in all vectors, acceleration levels, and initial orientations than for other subjects. The +Gy run LX23 1 3 was selected because its 5*1 9 acceleration level is of medium severity and because the initial orientation is standard (NUCU) . Since, as previously discussed, simulations have shown that dynamic response is relatively insensitive to variations of anthropometry data, it would seem that there is little point in further attempts to find correlations between response and anthropometry. It has been found that mechanical properties of the neck are more important in their effect on head-neck dynamics. Continue effort will likely involve simulation of averaged tests rather than single tests . Initial simulation work for test LX2313 utilized accel erometer data which had not been processed to be made consistent with photometric positional data. Little progress was made in determining of f-midsag i ttal plane properties of the neck until improved, combined data (26,27) became available. Use of photometric data was not found to be as necessary for the simpler head-neck motions in -Gx tests. Results from the best VOM 3~ū simulation of test LX2313 are shown in Figures 31 through 56. While there is much simulation work yet to be done in all vectors, it is felt that the agreement between test and simulation results in these figures is sufficiently good that the neck model and established parameter values constitute a reasonably accurate analog of a human neck. Established values may be found in Appendix A. Probably the most significant discrepancy between simulation and test responses is in the downward (+Z) excursion of the head origin shown in Figure 40. For the test it is about 5 cm, compared to only 1 cm in the simulation. This anomaly is related directly to the poor match at 90 ms for head origin Z-accel erat i on (Figure 36) and also to the unexpected axial compression force (240 Newtons) in the neck at 90 ms (Figure 53)- The cause of this has been determined but the problem was not corrected for the simulation results in this paper. Test data for T1 and head origin positions result in a 3 • 75 cm initial lateral offset of the condyles with respect to Tl, in the direction of sled motion. This means that initial sled motion would have to cause the neck to go into compression as the torso passes under the head and neck. Sensor data do not indicate that this occurs and, further, a 3 • 75 cm lateral offset over a 12 cm neck length seems unlikely given that the head is virtually untilted at time zero. Arbitrarily specifying initial position data for VOM 3~Ū which place the upper and lower neck joints in a vertical plane completely eliminates the noted anomalies. This causes other aspects of simulation response to be degraded, however, and indicates the need for compensating adjustment of various biomechanical parameter values. This has not yet been done. SIMULATION OF -Gx+Gy TESTS AT 7 g - It has been anticipated that a neck model which successfully simulates response in the -X and +Y vectors separately will successfully simulate response in the -X+Y vector, without need for further improvement of parameter values. Early results support this. The results shown in Figures 57 to 68 for a simulation of a -Gx+Gy test at 7«2 g (run LX3097» subject 93) match test results nearly as closely as results were matched for the -Gx and +Gy tests. CONCLUSIONS 1. Simulation of subject head-neck dynamics is a valid and practical approach to determination of biomechanical properties of the neck and to examination of mechanisms of response. 2. Biomechanical parameter values determined in this study are good first approximations for design specifications for a human neck analog. 3* Linear biomechanical material models are found to yield a good, although not totally satisfactory, representation of the human neck. Bilinear forms are probably adequate in an improved material model. 4. The characteristics of head-neck dynamic response seem to be more sensitive to variation of biomechanical property values within normal ranges than to variation of measures of head-neck anthropometry. 5. The mechanical properties of the neck can be determined by consideration of impacts in the -X and +Y vectors; consideration of the -X+Y vector is probably not necessary. Data from tests in the Z vector will be useful in determining improved values for axial properties of the neck. 6. Additional simulation work is needed in the -X and +Y vectors to establish refined values for biomechanical parameters, to establish sensitivities of response parameters, and to better understand response mechanisms.</page><page sequence="21">HEAD AND NECK RESPONSE 6.961 REFERENCES 1. hertz, H.J., and Patrick, L.M.
