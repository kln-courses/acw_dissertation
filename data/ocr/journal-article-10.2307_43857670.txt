I would like to share with people some comments about a website which I main- tain. This website was originally started because I wanted to place my wife's books on it, and thus its name http: //alejandrasbooks.org/. After I placed Alejandra's books on the website it occurred to me that, given the amount of space I was allotted, that some of the items I have been working on could be placed there as well. Thus, a rather eclectic col- lection of items have been placed there. However, there are certain items which I think should be of interest to people studying Mayan colonial texts. Firstly, as you scroll down the page you will come to "Facsimiles." Here there are the facsimiles of Chilam Balam of Chan Cah (24 MB, PDF), Chilam Balam of Chumayel (36 MB, PDF), Chilam Balam of lxii (33 MB, PDF), Chilam Balam of Kaua (115 MB, PDF), Chilam Balam of Tuz Ik (21 MB, PDF), Códice Pérez (48 MB, PDF) and Landa's Rel- ación (9 MB, PDF). In each case I have tried to present the facsimile in its original order, giving the recto pages on the recto side of each folio and the verso pages on the verso side of each folio. These facsimiles are scanned in from various facsimiles which I have gathered over the years. Aside from these facsimiles another item which I think should be of interest is the file named "Combined Vocabu- laries" (20 MB, PDF). This is a collection of many of the principal vocabularies combined into a single file so that one can compare the entries for any given Mayan word from the various source texts. The entries are given using the or- thography as shown in each individual source. Those entries which were originally Spanish-Mayan are given as shown in the original work, but the Mayan word is place in front of the entry, followed by a double colon :: to indicate that the entry comes from a word within the Spanish-Mayan gloss. While this work will never be in a finished state, it is fairly far along. At the moment it has about 2,500 pages and consists of material from 21 sources. (See page 1 of the introduction for a list of these sources.) I have been using this compilation of vocabularies to help me work on translating the edited texts coming from my comparative work on colonial Mayan texts in the book Post Conquest Mayan Literature. I have posted both the compara- tive work and just recently the first 2/3 's of the translation of P.C.M.L. with the thought that perhaps this work might be of some use to people. As I finish "chapters" which I call "sec- tions" I will update the translation portion. There are a couple of files of transcripts of published works: Coronel's Discursos Predicables and Doctrina Chris- tiana and The Franciscan Relaciones and histories of Landa, Lizana and Cogolludo and Avendaño. On November 1st, 2012 Mexican President Felipe Calderón Hinojosa inaugurated the Museo Maya de Cancun, a museum operated by the Instituto Na- cional de Antropología e Historia and located in Cancun's Zona Hotelera (Fig. 1). Curator of the museum exhibitions is Adriana Velazquez Morlet (INAH). The new facilities re- place the former archaeological museum housed in the Centro de Convenciones, in Cancun, which had been closed to the public since 2006 after being severely damaged by hurricane Wilma in 2005. The new museum is located on Boulevard Kukulkan, at km marker 16.5 near the Postclassic Maya set- tlement of San Miguelito. The elegant white building was designed by Mexican architect Alberto García Lascurain. At a cost of 5 million US dollars, the edifice was built over the course of six years. On the ground floor are the ticket booth, a bag check area, and a cafeteria. The exhibition halls are on an elevated level ten meters above sea level to protect the artifacts in case of flooding. On the second floor there are three spacious exhibition halls. There is a hall with temporary shows, and two are permanent exhibits, divided into two sections (Cedularios 2012). One hall features collections focusing on the Northern Maya Lowlands, the other is restricted to archaeological items coming from discoveries in the Mexican state of Quintana Roo. Whereas the museum collections encompass a total of 3500 pieces, in the two halls only about 350 examples are displayed. The oldest find in the Quintana Roo hall is the skeleton of La Mujer de Las Palmas, which was discovered in the cenote of Las Palmas, near Tulum, and came allegedly from a burial 10,000 or 12,000 years old. One of the newest objects is a modern replica of the famous Tortuguero Monu- ment 6, from Tabasco. The objects on exhibit hail mostly from the collections of the old Cancun museum, but some are objects previously on display in the Museo Regional de Antropología , Palacio Cantón, Mérida, and some are recently excavated artifacts from Quintana Roo. Bowl with enema scene There are many major stone sculptures on display: statues, frieze fragments, and architectural elements from Chichen Itza, a glyphic panel from Coba, a stela possibly from Co- zumel, six steps from a hieroglyphic stairway at Dzibanche, one glyphic step from El Resbalón, and even an inscribed panel fragment from the Palace of Palenque. A crudely carved miniature stela from El Meco is of interest, representing a standing anthropomorphic figure holding a stone knife in the right hand, obviously in the act of a human sacrifice (Fig. 2). This monument is comparable to four published stelae from Isla Mujeres, now in Mexico City and Brussels. A sin- gle example of a painted mural is displayed: a polychrome fragment, 44 x 43 cm in size, from the interior of Structure A- VI at Xcaret. Several decorated tiles from Comalcalco are also on view. There are three-dimensional stucco heads, one from the site of El Rey, Cancun. Furthermore, a human skull with a series of tiny perforations on the forehead is on display, found at the site of Chac Mool, on the Caribbean coast (Fig. 3). Small-sized artifacts include tools, metal bells, snail orna- ments, jade necklaces and other jadeite objects. Important is the large number of varied ceramics, comprising cylindrical vessels, several plates, and various ornate censers. The pot- tery dates from the Early to Late Postclassic periods. One of the highlights is the splendid vase from El Señor del Peten, with delicately created epigraphic and iconographie images (Fig. 4). One beautiful carved bowl is inscribed with an en- circling band of glyphs (Fig. 5). An exceptionally well-pre- served Postclassic censer of a Chen Mul modeled type bears postfiring-applied paint and represents a human figure placed on a deity head (Fig. 6). Of interest are also a bichrome plate depicting in red on an orange background in the interior a peccary in side view (Fig. 7), a polychrome bowl with glyphs and stylized deer images on its exterior and interior (Fig. 8), and repaired sherd fragments of a polychrome bowl rendering a rare but unequivocal enema scene, representing a seated human figure with a large oval syringe and a voluminous ceramic container (Fig. 9). The museum is open from Tuesday to Sunday from 10 to 19 pm, Wednesday to 22 pm. The entry fee is 57 pesos, including the visit to the adjacent archaeological site of San Miguelito, the most important ruin on the island of Cancun, consisting of 40 structures, 14 of them recently restored. Los elementos arqueológicos que se localizaron consisten en vestigios de construcciones teotihuacanas, al- gunas hechas a flor de tierra y otras con patios y escalinatas para entrar a las habitaciones (Fig. 10). Ninguna edificación es monumental, más bien corresponden a un asentamiento de tipo habitacional con arquitectura que sugiere diferentes estratos sociales, detalló la arqueóloga Alicia Bonfil Olivera, coordinadora de la investigación de salvamento en la planta tratadora. Bonfil Olivera especificó que de las construcciones, el tiempo sólo conservó cimientos de cuartos, pisos y patios; el elemento en mejor estado es una escalinata recubierta con estuco que fue retirada del sitio donde se encontró. Dentro del asentamiento se descubrieron más de 80 en- tierros humanos, unos depositados en fosas mortuorias (Fig. 11) y otros a manera de ofrendas constructivas (Fig. 12); aproximadamente 60 son de adultos, orientados en su may- oría en dirección norte-sur, en tanto que el resto corresponden a infantes que fueron dispuestos de este a oeste. En tres de los casos los personajes inhumados estaban acompañados por canes. La mayor parte de los entierros de adultos fueron acompañados de vasijas miniatura de estilo teotihuacano con particularidades regionales, la mayoría similares a las que se han encontrado en la zona arqueológica de Teotihuacan; algunos también contenían piezas de concha trabajada, y en otros casos se recolectó una especie de canicas pulidas hechas de materiales minerales aún no identificados, de entre cuatro y ocho milímetros de diámetro, depositadas casi siempre a la altura del esternón de los esqueletos. Durante la construc- ción de una cancha de basquetbol al pie de un cerro en el terreno de la Universidad Tecnológica del Poniente (UTP), en el municipio de Maxcanú, a 65 kilómetros de ciudad de Mérida, se descubrieron una cancha de un Juego de Pelota prehispánico en un área que nunca había sido explorada por arqueólogos. Así lo dio a conocer la arqueóloga Eunice Uc, investigadora del Centro INAH Yucatán, quien se encargó Fig. 13. La restaurada cancha del Juego de Pelota de Maxcanú (Foto: Centro INAH Yucatán) de verificar la información que presentó la UTP al Instituto Nacional de Antropología e Historia (INAH), a través de su rectora, Rossana Alpizar Rodríguez. La arqueóloga Eunice Uc detalló que como resultado del peritaje efectuado en la UTP, identificaron una estructura con las características ar- quitectónicas propias de los edificios paralelos que confor- man las canchas prehispánicas del Juego de Pelota, por lo que de inmediato, el INAH comenzó los trabajos de rescate con el fin de preservar ese patrimonio. Durante el proceso de excavación liberaron dos edificios y el espacio que conforma la cancha, entonces pudieron reconocer sus características arquitectónicas del Clásico Temprano (250 a.C. al 600 d.C.): El Juego de Pelota tiene una orientación norte-sur, está conformado por dos edificios paralelos y relativamente estrechos, de 19 m de largo y siete metros de ancho (Fig. 13). El espacio central entre ellos es plano y de una extensión de 6 m. El Juego de Pelota está elaborado con piedras labradas pequeñas estilo Puuc; en el costado interior tiene tres escalones a partir de un piso de es- 74 mexicon • Yol. La cancha estaba cubierta de estuco, de casi 10 cm de grosor, y nivelado. El Juego de Pelota presenta una delimitación de piedras burdas pero regulares de hasta 60 cm, alineadas y ordenadas cerrando la cancha, por lo que se conocen como cerramiento, y mide 6.50 por 20 m. A unos cinco metros se descubrió un altar circular y poco más al sur inicia el cerro. La construcción se encontró justo al pie de una serranía, semienterrada en una "planada" de tierra roja llamada kankab , lo que es de relevancia porque la construcción se ubicó en un lugar estratégico: al pie de la sierra y justo en un punto cubi- erto de una tierra privilegiada para los agricultores, ya que el kankabal es rica en nutrientes, porque éstos son arrastrados por las corrientes de las aguas que bajan de los cerros. En la parte más alta del cerro se registró una caverna con certeza vinculada al Juego de Pelota, ya que dicha cavidad natural representó el lugar de origen y la morada de los dioses. El Juego de Pelota se encuentra en ese sitio donde se creaba un vínculo con la montaña y la cueva y, seguramente, para realizar ceremonias agrícolas y en estos espacios se celebraba el renacimiento del dios de maíz. Durante las excavaciones en el Juego de Pelota también se encontró cerámica del Preclásico (700 a.C. al 250 d.C.) y Clásico (250 a 900 d.C.), pero la mayor cantidad de tiestos corresponde al Clásico Temprano (250 al 600 d. C.), época a la que pertenece la arquitectura. En toda la periferia del Juego de Pelota no se han descubierto construcciones monu- mentales, el sitio arqueológico más cercano con edificios de grandes proporciones es Oxkintok, ubicado a unos 7 km de la Universidad Tecnológica del Poniente. En tanto que en un terreno aledaño a la cancha prehis- pánica, donde ahora se construye un laboratorio de prácticas para los estudiantes de la UTP, el equipo de investigadores encontró plataformas bajas de uso habitacional que no pre- sentan arquitectura elaborada, por lo que serán cubiertas para su protección. El Juego de Pelota aún se encuentra en proceso de investigación y quedará bajo custodia de la UTP, con su- pervisión del INAH. Según la rectora de la UTP Rossana Alpizar Rodríguez, a cambio de más partidos de balonmano, los estudiantes de la carrera de Turismo trabajan en el desarrollo de senderos de visita, así como para proponer un corredor turístico con actividades que despierten el interés por Maxcanú. Professor Brian Stross passed away Thursday, April 17, 2014, after a long illness, although he remained active as a teacher and researcher until the last few weeks. Professor Stross received his Ph.D. in Anthropol- ogy from the University of California at Berkeley in 1969. He began teaching at the University of Texas the same year, and remained at UT his entire career. A linguistic anthropolo- gist, Professor Stross studied language use among the Tzeltal Maya, along with many other groups. His interests extended to the iconography and epigraphy of the Classic Maya, and his knowledge of Mesoamerican languages was extensive. He was also interested in foodways, feasting, and the lan- guage systems that applied to foods. His most recent book, Lightning in the Andes and Mesoamerica: Pre-Columbian, Colonial, and Contemporary Perspectives , was published less than a year ago (with John E. Staller, Oxford University Press). Professor Stross worked with many generations of UT students, supervising 23 dissertations over his 45-year career. He participated in the doctoral defense of the last of these students (Dr. Amber O'Connor) just weeks ago. He su- pervised or served on the Ph.D., Masters, or Honors Thesis committees of literally hundreds of students. Brian Stross was a gentle and generous man with enormous curiosity and a singular dedication to his students. He was a warm and gra- cious colleague to generations of junior professors, and as the senior member of our faculty, a wise and steadying presence. He will be greatly missed.
