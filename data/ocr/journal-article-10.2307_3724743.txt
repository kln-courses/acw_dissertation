Such ups and downs are the inevitable price to be paid for a type of analysis that depends on the critic's subjective reaction to a poetic text. The second chapter also has its weaknesses. In Inf., 1.49, we are told, 'Questa e pronome pleonastico ... segnale di rottura sintattica che corrisponde ad una rottura logica, mimetica di una sincope della sbigottita memoria' (p. 38). Certainly not this reader, who is not the first to hear an echo of the Vulgate's 'Nolite confidere in verbis mendacii dicentes: Templum Domini, templum Domini, templum Domini est' (Jeremiah 7.4) or to be swayed by the Martial anger of the first pope. Nor am I the first to point out that Dante creates in blocks: the primary unit of the Comedy is not the single line of verse, but the terzina. Moreover, in the example chosen by Professor Wlassics the unit is not one but two terzine, indissolubly linked by the enjambement: 'Quelli ch'usurpa in terra il luogo mio ... fatt'ha del cimitero mio cloaca / del sangue e della puzza; onde 1' perverso / che cadde di qua su, la giu si placa'. The 'necessarie pause della lettura' in fact build up to the climax where the enjambement ('cloaca / del sangue... onde '1 perverso / che cadde ... ') has nothing to do with sighs or pensosita. The pages on Dante's anthropomorphism (141-64) tell us that the poet experienced the need 'quasi compulsivo di una presenza umana'. This inoffensive statement is used to 'illuminate' some of Dante's imagery, such as that contained in Inf., xxvI.25-30, where 'La figura narrativamente "inutile" del contadino ^ proprio la spia dell'antropomorfismo dantesco' (p. 144). But does not most of this imagery simply reflect the primary aim of 'Dante narratore', how to portray the other-world in such a way as to make it real and convincing for his readers ? Must this not be based above all on human experience? The main prop to our critic's argument is the use of uom in such lines as Inf., IX.4, Purg., I.118-19, Par., v. 6-I8. Antonino Pagliaro has also studied this element in Dante's poem, claiming 'Si registrano circa un centinaio di siffatte similitudini, mediante le quali e caratteriz- zato al vivo un atteggiamento umano esterno o interiore' (Ulisse (Messina-Firenze, 1967), pp. 669-70). Professor Wlassics, however, asks us to consider Inf., xIII.45 ('.. . e stetti come l'uom che teme') and the question: 'Perche "come l'uom che teme"? Perche non semplicemente "temendo", o spaventato sgomento smarrito o simili? t come se D. non potesse, o non volesse, prendere conoscenza appieno di una complicata emozione, senza prima impiantarla in una figura umana, ipotetica, anonima. . . ' (p. I53). This strikes me as a self-defeating game. First, Pagliaro's 'centinaio' must be halved (om is used nine times; uom sixty-three times in the Comedy: many of the occurrences are not relevant - e.g., Purg., I. 107, 'uom sanza cura'). Second, anyone familiar with the development of Provenqal and French omlon will hardly be surprised to find numerous examples of this construction in early Italian poetry: Pier delle Vigne himself ('com'om ch'6 in mare ed a spene di gire'); Inghilfredi ('e sto com'om ch'e di duol quasi vinto'); Rinaldo d'Aquino ('com'omo c'a disagio / aspetta d'aver agio / poco di bene piglia per talento'). To continue would be wearisome. Let me merely point to a new discovery: 'l'antro- pomorfismo' in Rinaldo d'Aquino, where forty per cent of his canzoni contain an example of this 'impersonal' construction or cliche. The strength of Dante narratore remains that of a highly sensitive reader of the Comedy. The weaknesses are those of a critical approach that ignores many of the recent developments in our understanding of the complexities of literary creation and which shows itself at times incapable of distinguishing between poetic utterance and literary tradition.
