The archaeological Maya site of Chilonche, also written El Chilonché, or Chilonché, is located in the municipality of Santa Ana in the southeastern section of the Department of Petén in Guatemala (Fig. 1). The topo nym Chilonche is derived from the chilonche tree (Eugenia sp.), that frequently grows in this area of the basin of the Rio Salsipuedes. The ancient ruins are sited approximately 10 km north of the village of El Ocote, on an elevation of400 m above sea level. The distance between Flores, the capital of the Petén, and Chilonche is 36.8 km as the parrot flies. Prior to 1995 the ruins were visited by an inspector of the Guatemalan Departamento de Monumentos Prehispánicos y Coloniales. Julio A. Roldán of the Atlas Arqueológico de Guatemala (AAG) project of the Instituto de Antropología e Historia (IDAEH) provided the first report in November of 1995 (Laporte and Mejia 2000:87). The AAG, then headed by Juan Pedro Laporte, conducted several archaeological surveys, inves tigations, mappings, and excavations during several years (Quezada et al. 1996, 1997; Chocón 1997; Mejía et al. 1998: 173; Chocón et al. 1999; Laporte and Mejía 2000: 87; Laporte and Mejía 2001). This work resulted in the registration of 19 mounds in the central area, and 177 mounds at the periphery. An E-Group architectural complex was found 1.7 km NE of the Acropolis. In the Acropolis, located on a seven meters high artificial platform, there are six structures forming a pla za. The northern and southern struc tures are six meters high and exhibit vaulted rooms. Several remains of mural paintings and glyphs were de scribed on interior walls of six vaulted rooms of the North Structure (Chocón 1997; Laporte and Mejía 2001; 2005: 74). Accompanied by Haroldo Tesucún Vitzil, an employee of the IDAEH, I visited for the first time the site in 2000 in order to attempt to inspect the reported murals within the vaulted rooms of the Acropolis, but only red painted patches could be recognized and photographed and no epigraphic or iconographic images were observed. Jorge Chocón (1997), Chocón, Heidy Quezada, and Héc tor Mejia (1999: 304-305), and Laporte and Mejia (2001: 68-69, 70, Fig. 7; 2005:74) reported in the North Structure of the Acropolis six chambers, exposed by looters, showing faint traces of mural paintings on the walls of one room, and around the springline of the vault a painted hieroglyphic text in black, enframed by red and orange colored bands. In Room 2, on the north wall, a fragmentary image portrays at least four standing anthropomorphic figures, as well as texts in red paint (Chocón et al. 1999:321, Fig. 14). On the eastern wall of Room 3, at the springline, another painted glyphic band was discovered, equally in a very bad condition (Fig. 2). These painted glyphs evidence that Chilonche belongs to Maya sites with hieroglyphic inscriptions. Berthold Riese (2004:3) pro posed for Chilonche a site abbreviation of CHT. Ian Graham (1975) applied for painted texts on masonry walls the abbre viation Mrl., therefore the two inscriptions registered by the AAG project may be provisionally designated as CHT:Mrl. 1, and 2. No photographs of the texts have been published. Oscar Quintana Samayoa of the Programa de Rescate del Proyecto de Sitios Arqueológicos en Petén, IDAEH, docu mented in 2005 particularly the depredations and destruction of architecture caused by looters and treasure seekers in the Acropolis group. Moreover, a new sketch map was drawn, documenting the locations of trenches and tunnels dug by looters (Quintana 2006:154-156). In 2006 Cristina Vidal and Gaspar Muñoz, Spanish ar chaeologists and architects from Valencia, who work since 2004 at the nearby site of La Blanca, directed an archaeologi cal reconnaissance at Chilonche and recorded several vestiges of polychrome mural paintings, mostly in a dark red (Vidal and Muñoz 2007: 115-116, Figs. 14, 15). On March 13 of 2009 Muñoz, Vidal and other members of the Proyecto La Blanca (www.uv.es/arsmaya) inspected Chilonche in order to document visible architecture in the Acropolis. The mask measures 3.5 m x 2.5 m and is 1.5 m high; some red pigments adhere to the stucco coat. Field work in November and December of 2009 by the Proyecto La Blanca not only centred on the sub-structure with this early stucco sculpture (3E1 Sub), but included the mapping of the Acropolis and adjacent structures. On December 4, 2009, after documenta tion and conservation work, the looter's tunnel entrance was closed for security reasons with a stone and mortar wall. Dur ing the field season of 2010, one of the objectives of Proyecto La Blanca was to conduct some excavations inside this tunnel as well as architectural drawings of sub-structure 3E1 and other architectural remains (Vidal, Muñoz, Horcajada and May 2011, Vidal and Muñoz 2011). The observations were facilitated by an electric lamp, powered by a portable generator. The remains of the mask are located in a sub-structure (3E1 Sub), underneath the Late Classic, vaulted architecture of the Acropolis complex, on its northern fa?ade. Looters have entered this early phase of the complex by digging vari ous trenches and corridors which are very low and narrow. There are several passageways illegally dug by the unknown grave diggers, exposing the three-dimensional monumental stone-and-stucco sculpture with its two lateral surfaces. Cor ridors surround the presently visible sections. In 2009 a total of 16 trenches or tunnels were documented. The top and bot tom portions of the sculpture are still buried, and may consist not only of a face but of the complete body of a supernatural being. Recognizable are the eyes, the nose and an earring of the head. The stucco surface is damaged, showing marks of the diggings at several parts. The stucco is smooth and white, with traces of red pigment. The design is created in high relief and there are several lines executed in low relief. The style is elegant and shows curvilinear double lines, scrolls, and circlets (Figs. 3-5). The very narrow and low trenches offer no sufficient space to allow a satisfactory photographic documentation. Chronologically, the stucco sculpture may date to the Late Preclassic-Early Classic period. The first printed report of this architectural sculpture ap peared in an article published in summer of 2011, consisting of nine pages and illustrating details of the sculpture in three photos (Muñoz, Vidal and Quintana 2011). The geographical coordinates of the North Structure of the Acropolis complex were established during my visit in 2011 with a hand-held GPS navigator, which provided the following readings: Latitude 16° 48.726' North, Longitude 89° 33.749' West.
