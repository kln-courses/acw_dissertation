<plain_text><page sequence="1">E ntering the great Clementine Chapel of the Palace of the Popes in Avignon, where Picasso's last paintings are on exhibit, is a shattering experience. The garish primary colors, leaping like a profanity out of 201 paintings circumscribing the enormous hall, decompose the gracefully sculptured ogival vaults that in another time combined with the hieratic frescoes covering the walls to transmute this massive space into a delicate and airy order. Immediate encounter with the paintings themselves exacerbates this uncomfortable sense of dislocation. The Picasso breaking up this sacred space is a demonic Picasso. He is a Picasso divested, who goes insolently naked. Every brush stroke mocks the "sacred" archetypes - the shapes - which we habitually enclose and anesthetize the anguish of our terrible experience - and profanes the Art, including his own, which consoles us in our dearth. As we move from one painting to another, however, it breaks in on us - periplum - that this unrelentingly derisive iconoclasm, this brutal disintegration of "the luminous bounding line" that "arrests" motion, in the last paintings of the century old dying man with the "terrible eye" is a profanation that celebrates human life, a Festival. It is a reduction by violence of our coercive and domesticating anthropomorphism, a de-struction of our ancient frame of reference, that pictures our not-at-homeness in the world and distances us from existence.
