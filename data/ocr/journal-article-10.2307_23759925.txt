<plain_text><page sequence="1">News &amp; Notes Hurricane exposes ancient jade mines in Guatemala NEW YORK (The New York Times/William J. Broad). For half a century, scholars have searched for the source of the jade that early Mesoamerican civilizations prized and fash ioned into precious objects of worship, trade and adornment. The searchers found some clues to the source of jadeite, but no lost mines came to light. Recently, scientists exploring a mountainous region in Guatemala say they have found the mother lode - an area with huge jade boulders, other rocky treasures and signs of ancient mining. The exact location is being held back to protect the site. In part, the discovery is the result of a devastating storm that hit Central America in 1998, killing many people and touching off floods and landslides that exposed old veins and washed jade into river beds, some of which turned up in stores, arousing the curiosity of scientists. In the end the scientists made a series of discoveries culminating in bus-size boulders of Olmec blue jade, some astride creeks. The find includes large outcroppings of blue jade distributed all over the hillsides. It also includes an ancient mile-high road of stone that runs for miles through dense forests. The deposits rival the world's leading source of mined jade today, in Myanmar (Burma), say the experts, among them George Harlow, jade specialist at the American Mu seum of Natural History, Russell Seitz, who decades earlier directed a jade hunt in Guatemala for more than two years for the Peabody Museum at Harvard and Virginia Sisson, a geologist at Rice University, who has recently examined jades in Myanmar as well as Guatemala. The implications for history, archaeology and anthropology are just beginning to emerge. For one thing, the find suggests that the Olmecs exerted a broad influence in the Guatemalan highlands as well. Fash). Gordon Randolph Willey died of heart failure on the morning of April 28, in Cambridge, MA. He was 89. Widely recognized as the premier American archaeologist of the latter half of the 20th century, Willey was renowned for his innovative and su perbly documented research at numerous Maya archaeologi cal sites in Belize, Guatemala, and Honduras. Willey also made both substantive and theoretical contributions to the archaeology of North and South America, and comparative studies with the Old World. He is recognized as the creator of the field of .settlement pattern studies' in archaeology, a tremendous methodological advance that he pioneered in the Viru Valley on the northern coast of Peru in the late 1940s. Prior to his Viru Valley work, Willey made important contributions to the archaeology of the Southeastern U.S. through his creative new methods of pottery analysis and reconstruction of ancient culture history. It was during his years in the Southeast that he met Katharine W. Whaley, whom he married in 1938, and with whom he had a devoted union for 63 years. Born in Chariton, Iowa, on March 7, 1913, Willey re ceived his bachelor's degree from the University of Arizona and earned his Ph.D. from Columbia University in 1942. He went on to work with the Smithsonian Institution until assum ing the Bowditch Professorship at Harvard in 1950. He taught in the Anthropology Department for 36 years. Willey's 1949 book, .Archaeology of the Florida Gulf Coast' (re-issued in 1998), became an instant classic, and is still carried in the back pocket of virtually all field archaeolo gists working in that part of the U.S. His masterful syntheses of New World culture history (An Introduction to American Archaeology: Volume I, North and Middle America; Volume II, South America), and The History of American Archaeol ogy (co-authored with his former student, Jeremy A. Sabloff, and now in its third edition), cemented his status as „the doyen of North, Central, and South American archaeology." His contributions to the field and his personal example are memo rialized by the many awards and symposia that have been institutionalized in his honour in the American Anthropologi cal Association, the Society for American Archaeology, and the Peabody Museum of Harvard University. Willey produced dozens of Ph.D.s during his career at Harvard, two full generations of the finest archaeologists in the U.S. He served as President of the American Anthropo logical Association (1960-1962) and the Society for Ameri can Archaeology (1968-1978). His numerous honours in clude: the Alfred Vincent Kidder Award for Eminence in the Field of American Archaeology from the American Anthro pological Association, the Viking Medal from the Viking Fund, the Huxley Medal from the Royal Anthropological Institute (U.K.), the Order of the Quetzal from the Govern ment of Guatemala, and honorary doctorates from the Uni versity of Arizona and the University of Cambridge (U.K.). A great lover of limericks, Willey wrote many a fine one himself for his colleagues and students. Among many other things in his retirement years in Cambridge, he lavished most of his writing time on crafting archaeological mystery novels, the first of which (Selena) was published and continues to be widely read.</page></plain_text>
