The Didrichsen Art Museum will be hosting its second major Maya exhibition „Maya II - The Mystery of a Lost City" from September 9, 2005 to March 12, 2006. The exhibition will présent about a hundred pieces from Cancuen, a major Maya site located on the Pasion river. Archaeological work in recent years under the direction of Arthur Demarest of Vanderbilt University and in collaboration with the Guatemalan Minis try of Culture yielded one of the largest and most magnificent Maya royal palaces ever found, as well as some of the most beautiful pieces of Maya sculpture known. Professor A. Demarest is Coming to Finland for the opening ceremonies and again later in the autumn to give talks on the project. He is carrying out a new approach to archaeo logical préservation that not only will protect the ancient site but will also provide economic support and development for the modern Maya villagers who live in the area. The valuable treasures and the exciting stories from the excavations to be presented in the exhibition will be an adventurous experience, and the humanitarian actions taken for sustainable development in the area will most certainly raise high appréciation among visitors. This type of support is very highly valued in the Nordic countries. „Archaeologists can't just keep excavating ancient Maya ruins while the modern Maya Settlements nearby are starving," Demarest says. The archaeological team has determined that a structure previously identified as a minor palace is not only one of the largest and most elabórate résidences of ancient Maya kings Vol. XXVII • April/Juni 2005</page><page sequence="2">discovered but also one of the best preserved. The SIHIL Workshop which runs from 3-5 July will provide an introduc tion/review of the glyphic writing System and a Workshop on the Terminal Classic texts of Caracol. At the end of the meetings (July 9), the tutors plan to organize a trip to Caracol with Workshop participants and any other interested parties. Due to the format of the Workshop, participants are urged to register in advance. The Conference proper (July 6-8) Starts after the close of Workshops and is in the format of a symposium in which scholarly papers are presented to professional and aficiona dos of Maya culture. It is envisioned that the Conference will provide an interdisciplinary arena that will allow students, staff, faculty and practitioners to discuss and exchange ideas and information on the latest developments in the field of Belizean Archaeology.
