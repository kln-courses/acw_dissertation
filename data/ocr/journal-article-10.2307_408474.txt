As John Reddick energetically argues, one of the crucial symbolic devices which Stifter uses to show this alienation is projection.15 Red- dick's argument is compelling-but, un- fortunately, he focuses only on "Der Hochwald" in this context. Eric Blackall, for instance, views it as "heartless" and "evil;"18 and Roman Struc argues that the demonic in nature can be interpreted as "the counterpoint of the demonic in man."19 But in Stifter's creative vision, whether we see nature as "good" or "evil," it is above all a kind of screen or mirror reflecting "virtue" or "vice" within humans. It is this mutual estrange- ment between man and nature with all its tensions which is symbolically played out in tale after tale, many of them culminating in man's destruction. To put it most simply, the drama is of humans who symbolically sully and violate nature, and who conse- quently reap the inexorable punishment for their foul deed. Why, one might ask, is such behavior deemed so heinous? One possible clue lies in the language which Stifter uses to por- tray both man and his surroundings, for it is the language of human sexuality.20 The snow in both the story "Bergkristall" and the short essay "Weihnacht" is not simply clean: it is "rein und unverlezt" (2,2: 210), "makellos" (GW VI: 564)-the implica- tions in both cases being those of virginity. Similarly, the pitch-burner in "Granit" ventures beyond the normal territory in- habited by humans, penetrating an area of intact purity "wo der Wald noch ist, wie er bei der Sch*pfung gewesen war, wo noch keine Menschen gearbeitet haben" (2,2: 47). Stifter's treatment of sexuality is per- haps the central feature of his prose-not least because it becomes clear in this con- text that, for all his assertions to the con- trary, he regarded the conduct of man and that of nature as manifesting different sets of laws. In this respect, one should distin- guish between the damage which nature does to nature, and that which man does to nature. The hailstorm in "Kazensilber," for instance, destroys whole areas of the forest, kills numerous plants, and floods the fields-but there is no moral dimension to this: destruction and re-creation is simply a part of nature.
