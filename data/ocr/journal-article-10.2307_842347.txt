INTRODUCTION THE Atacama Desert in northern Chile has yielded a wealth of archaeological findings from pre-Columbian times which include a number of musical instruments. This work is intended to show an hypothetical reconstruction of the possible rituals and use of musical instruments in what has been called the Culture of San Pedro de Atacama during its apogee period A.D. 300-900.1* Regarding musical instruments from that region and in that period, I have scanned a wide range of documentation for references to archaeological specimens. Besides musical instruments, per se, further archaeological evidence is provided by relics such as carved wooden figurines holding various instruments in their hands. On the basis of such findings, and in the light of more recent ethnographic data, relationships can be postulated hypothetically between pre-Columbian musical instruments and ritual - which involved shamanism, human sacrifice, and the use of hallucinogenic drugs. My search for evidence of ritual in San Pedro culture has been done mainly through examining the literature concerning existing archaeo- logical remains such as snuff-trays and other equipment relating to hallucinogenic drugs, which were of fundamental importance (Mostny, 1958, pp. 390-1), and also through studying ethnographic references to rituals which are still practiced in that area? In the case of quotations, where the reference cited is in Spanish or French, the English translation is attributable to the present author. When discussing musical instruments here, they will be described in general rather than specific terms. For example, only average dimensions will be given, and specific geographical locations will not be stated. Synoptic tables of instruments, and their geographical and chronological distribution, are provided on pp. 44-5 below. *For endnotes, see p.61 ff. 26</page><page sequence="2">Besides the final list of references cited, an extensive bibliographic appendix on manuscripts and published literature in Latin America has been included which may prove useful for those who are interested in studying pre-Columbian culture history in greater depth. 2. The archaeological site of San Pedro de Atacama corresponds to what is today the village of San Pedro, situated approximately 2,440 m above sea level, besides several archaeological settlements grouped near the San Pedro river, to the north of Salar de Atacama. The Salar (from the Spanish word sal, 'salt') is a salt bed or basin of many square km in extent, produced when an inland salty lake dries up and only the salt remains. The cemeteries and dwellings excavated are referred to by the name of the settlements in which they are located. The most important among these are Coyo Oriente, Quitor, Solor, Sequitor, and Catarpe (Torres, 1987, p.38; Pumarino, 1978, p.34). For the study of pre- Columbian musical instruments, Coyo, Tchecar, Tchilimoya, and Sequitor P. Orl. must be added also. The dryness of the Atacama Desert has made the Atacama zone an exceptionally rewarding place for archaeological research, since many objects from the pre-Columbian period have remained almost unchanged, as if mummified. Even objects made of wood, textiles, and basketry have been preserved until today, whereas in most other places they would have disappeared long ago on account of climatic humidity. Here, these objects have been helpful not only for the diagnosis of Atacamefio culture, but also to understand phenomena in other places (Torres, 1987, p. 32; Berenguer &amp; Dauelsberg, 1989, p.141). This may be one of the reasons why the cultural complex of San Pedro appears to be one of the richest centres for pre-Columbian musical instruments for certain periods of pre-Hispanic Chile (Mena, 1974, p. 81). 28</page><page sequence="4">The beginnings of San Pedro Culture go back to the period of 500 B.C to A.D. 300-400, also known as the Early Intermediate Period (Bittmann et al., 1978, p. 26; Berenguer, 1984, p. 13). At that time, 'The Salar de Atacama was a real ethnic crucible, in which were blended peoples from the area, from the trans-Andean forest [northwest Argentina] and from the Bolivian highland' (Berenguer, op. cit., p. 12), who were attracted by the productive land for growing 'corn, beans, pumpkins, squashes, pepper, quinoa, etc.' that started to be developed in the oasis where the river, that goes to the Salar, empties (Bittmann et al., 1978, pp. 26-7). Around the year 300 B.C., the influence of the Tiwanaku culture starts from the south of Lake Titicaca in Bolivia. This fact will deeply trace San Pedro culture, determining its period of greatest flourishing. This period, known as Medium, coincides with Classic Tiwanaku and is characterized by the influence that this highland ceremonial centre had throughout the north of Chile (op. cit., pp.31 and 34; Berenguer &amp; Dauelsberg, 1989, p.129; Browman, 1980, p. 108). For this flourishing period we will tentatively adopt the opinion of Bittmann, Le Paige and Nuifiez (1978), Browman (1980, p. 108) and Llagostera and Costa (1984, p. 14) of establishing it between the years A.D. 300 and 900, even though there are other opinions. It is assumed that there was a strong ideological dependency during the years A.D. 600-900 on the part of San Pedro towards Tiwanaku, reflected in the artistic representations on objects such as snuff-trays, and in the 'generation of black and red engraved ceramics' (Thomas, Massone &amp; Benavente, 1984, pp. 82-3 and 99). Unlike other places up north, the presence of Tiwanaku 'is manifested ... almost exclusively by elements of worship or magical-religious significance'.? This suggests that the relationship of Tiwanaku with San Pedro was, perhaps, through shamans (Llagostera &amp; Costa, 1984, p. 14) or 'intermediary "leaders" ' who would have participated in the 'sphere of religious interaction' (Berenguer, Castro &amp; Silva, 1980, p. 87). Nevertheless, this ideological penetration could actually have had more economic than religious motivations, where the products which passed through San Pedro to various original destinations were eventually diverted to Tiahuanaco6 not for religious purposes - as it would have been claimed - but rather for economic interests (Berenguer, 1984, pp. 20-1). Among the typical objects of Tiwanaku in San Pedro, there are, as it was said, the snuff-trays and other items of the 'hallucinogen complex',7 where representations of figures of the 'Gateway of the Sun' of Tiahuanaco appear (Thomas, Massone &amp; Benavente, 1984, pp. 82-3). Also found were 'skull trophies and isolated heads', representations of 29</page><page sequence="5">'sacrificers' in petrography, 'big spoons decorated with ritual characters, pyrography bones' and masks (Bittmann et al., 1978, pp.31 and 34). Between A.D. 900 to 1300 the decline of the Tiwanaku Empire took place, probably because of the collapse of the Wari (or Huari) Empire located to the north of Titicaca and in the surrounding Peruvian valleys (Berenguer &amp; Dauelsberg, 1989, p. 139; Browman, 1980, p. 108) and because of the Cochabamba Valley separation (Tiwanaku maintained with them a close commercial exchange). After the years 1200-1300 the Tiwanaku influence disappeared, both in the highlands and in the Atacama zone (Browman, 1980, p. 109). Around A.D. 900 to 1000, the lack of contact with Tiwanaku starts to be noticeable in San Pedro. The manufactures are declining, perhaps decadent, maybe because of the lack of religious motivation (Bittmann et al., 1978, p.46). It seems that there was political and territorial fighting (ibid.; Berenguer &amp; Dauelsberg, 1989, p. 166). The snuff-trays were used to make fire, which could indicate an apparent decadence and loss of meaning for the ritual of hallucinogen inhaling (Berenguer &amp; Dauelsberg, op. cit., p. 178). During the period A.D. 900-1536, what has been called by Bittmann, Le Paige and Niifiez (1978, p.43) the 'Atacamefio people', were being shaped. Then, commercial exchange went on, including now Aymara people from the highlands, and communities from northwest Argentina (op. cit., p. 47). Around the year 1450 the Atacamefio language, Kunza, and other typical Atacamefio cultural characteristics were being shaped, and the area came into the Inca domination, being part of the Collasuyo, or meridional province of the new empire (op. cit., pp. 50-1). Neverthe- less, many of the activities of the Atacameiio people did not suffer great changes (op. cit., p. 54). In 1546 Atacama came into contact with European culture represented by the army of the Spaniard Diego de Almagro. The imposition of a new faith (Christian), plus the impact of this different culture, caused the disintegration of the original Atacamefio culture during the colonisation (op. cit., pp.55 and 58). The Atacamefio people continue to celebrate several religious ceremonies (cf. Pumarino, 1978, p. 37) and still maintain certain customs today, even though they have lost their language, Kunza, and are experiencing an acculturation process that is perhaps faster than ever, the old traditions being shaped and replaced by new cultural patterns. 30</page><page sequence="6">3. RITUAL AND MUSICAL INSTRUMENTS DURING THE APOGEE OF SAN PEDRO CULTURE (A.D. 300-900) 3.1 Ritual The formation of a society based on land work seems to have brought important changes in the cosmology of all inhabitants of that zone. Thus, rituals arose to 'magically control some natural elements, such as rain, water, fertility; elements previously taken for granted, but now seen as the keys to survival' (Llagostera &amp; Costa, 1984, p. 14). The same authors also believe that at the same time, new divinities emerged, the so called 'extraclanics' which, as the name suggests, were above the totemic divinities of individual clans. These became widely recognised and formed the basis of real religions (ibid.). An example of this is Tiwanaku, whose ceremonial centre was located on the shore of Lake Titicaca, in Bolivia (loc. cit.). There, a theocracy was established which, 'around the year A.D. 600, was administered by shamans of San Pedro de Atacama' (loc. cit.). Consequently, the presence of the Tiwanaku cult in San Pedro de Atacama was decisive regarding ritual practices which began to develop at that time. Although the habit of inhaling hallucinogens was deeply rooted in San Pedro before Tiwanaku's influence, this practice increased considerably, probably stimulated by new beliefs and rites introduced in San Pedro (Berenguer, 1984, pp. 20-1), and may have been accompanied by a 'hallucinogen different from the point of view of quality, maybe much more powerful than the ones already in use' at that time in San Pedro (Berenguer and Dauelsberg, 1989, p. 162). 'The basic set of equipment for the "rapd" inhaling complex8 is composed of snuff-trays, inhaling tubes, spatula, pounding mortar' (Torres, 1984, p. 25). San Pedro de Atacama is the place where the most important concentration of snuff-trays and inhaling tubes has been found. Among the basic items of the 'rape' complex' equipment, the snuff- tray stands out at that time; it is a 'device made of stone, wood or bone, made into a container or receptacle' (Thomas &amp; Benavente, 1984, p. 169) whose cavity is usually even and rectangular, though sometimes round or ovoid. In this cavity was deposited all the hallucinogenic powder, to be inhaled through a tube going directly to the nose. This receptacle or container usually has one or more handle-like parts extending perpendicularly from one of the smaller sides of the rectangle. On this handle, diverse motifs appear (Torres, 1984, p.25). Among the motifs appearing on snuff-tray handles, the topics most frequently represented are decapitators or sacrificers, felines (jaguar, puma, etc.), birds and other animals, anthropomorphic representations, and other types of motifs (Torres, 1984, pp.30-4). Torres (1987, p. 42) has also determined a repertoire of sixteen basic topics which would correspond to sixteen specific partialities of San Pedro society, that is, considering that snuff-trays were apparently for personal use, for in graves only one has been found beside each body. There are also snuff-trays with anthropomorphic representations which have an object similar to a panpipe in their hands, as will be discussed later. The representations of animals - feline, cdndor,10 quirquincho (armadillo), lizard - may be samples of a 'different audience attending ... [the shaman's] ritual practices, especially the individual ones, such 32</page><page sequence="8">as healing, divination and, in general, aspects of. . . [the shaman's] trip'." They might also represent the idea of a 'double' or alter-ego of the shaman during a ceremony of drug consumption (Berenguer, 1984, p.21). For Llagostera (1984, p. 16) 'the snuff-trays would correspond to the chalice or to the paten, where everything produced by the trance, the psychic transformation and the attainment of a supernatural state, was saved'. One of the most used topics at that time, linked to the hallucinogen complex, is the one of the sacrificer, represented on snuff-trays.12 It seems that this character was involved in rites of decapitation, for many times he/she has in one hand a head-trophy and in the other an axe. Sometimes the head-trophy is replaced by 'keros [a kind of vase], felines or dogs, panpipes, comets, receptacles or other implements of worship' (Nhifiez, 1964b, p. 5). Further evidence which confirms the association of the 'sacrificer' with rites of decapitation is the fact that head-trophies tend to appear in the same regions where the representations of the sacrificer have been found. Thus, to the north of Loa river, the archaeological findings of these two elements are much rarer (ibid.; Berenguer, Castro &amp; Silva, 1980, p. 83). Other objects linked to the hallucinogen complex and possibly having a direct Tiwanaku influence, are the big spoons decorated with worship characters, pyrography bones, and the ritual masks (Bittmann et al., 1978, pp.31 and 34). 3.2 Archaeological remains of musical instruments and their hypothetical relations to ritual As previously mentioned, the study of music will arise basically from the message the instruments can give. As to the possible functions they have had - and therefore the type of music they played - we can speculate and maybe outline some future hypothesis (cf. Mena, 1974, p.70; Hickmann, 1988, p.121; Olsen, 1990, p.177). Of the four classes of musical instruments: idiophones, membrano- phones, chordophones, and aerophones, distinguished by Hornbostel and Sachs (1961), only two of these appear to have been reported in this zone, namely idiophones and aerophones. Regarding membranophones, no bibliographic references have been found which show that they existed in San Pedro culture.13 There is no reference to pre-Columbian chordophones in this zone either. Idiophones Among the idiophones found in San Pedro de Atacama, we have, in 33</page><page sequence="9">the first place, the pyramidal metal bells, each of them with a hole, through which a string passed, joining them so that *sound can be produced concussively. They are referred to as 'strung rattles' by Alvarez and Grebe (1974, p. 27). In terms of the Hornbostel and Sachs classification (hereafter referred to as 'H/S') they would probably qualify as 'shaken strung suspension rattles' (H/S 112.111). The pyramidal metal bells constitute what some decades ago were known as Chorimori by certain scholars (Mostny, et al.).14 Currently, Grebe and Hidalgo (1988, p.83, P1.4) have reported this instrument being known as Chorromdn. This name is closer to Chorromo, mentioned in 1950 by Carlos Lavin, in his article 'Cultura Atacamefia' (Jose P6rez de Arce's files). Maria Isabel Mena (1974) divides them into three types: bronze pyramidal bells type A, bronze pyramidal bells type B, and conical bells made of bronze and other metals, type C. Fig. 4 shows an example of type A from Quitor 8 (reproduced from P6rez de Arce, 1982, no. 10). FIG. 4. Bronze pyramidal bell, type A. Mentioning type A, Grebe says that they are made of copper (cf. Iribarren, 1969, p.92), with an average height of 1.5 to 2.0 cm and a diameter of 7.5 cm, being called 'pyramidal copper bells big type and low' (Grebe, 1974, p. 24, P1. 2, Fig. 11). Bells of this type would have their origin in the period from 500 B.C. to A.D. 300 (Perez de Arce, 1982, no.10), and would be the one which constituted the 'chorimori or concussive bells' (Mena, 1974, pp. 24 and 71; Pdrez de Arce, ibid.). Bells of type B and C are smaller and because of their reduced size they produce little sound. For this reason, Mena (1974, p.72, P1.8) thinks that it is not certain that they were used as a musical instrument. Due to their arrangement in graves, it is supposed that they were used as ornaments,15 and/or like pendant rattles in some celebrations and ritual dances.16 These have been found sewn onto the clothing of mummified bodies (Le Paige, ibid.; Mena, ibid.; Grebe, ibid.). Another idiophone found in San Pedro is the clapper bell (H/S 111.242.122) of wood.17 'These bells are of trapezoidal shape where 34</page><page sequence="10">the upper part or peak as well as the bottom opening are elliptical, having the biggest axis on the base opening. Each bell has several clap- pers, suspended inside by strings from the apex of the instrument. Sound is produced when the bell is shaken: the clappers strike against each other as well as against the walls of the instrument' (Mena, 1974, p. 29). Before studying the different types of clapper bells, let us take a closer look at one of them. A large wooden clapper bell from Quitor dates from about A.D. 1050 according to Claro (1989, p. 54).18 Although this is after the period A.D. 300-900 established for the purpose of this work, I mention it because it is different from other clapper bells and pertains to the last years of San Pedro culture. It is 14 cm high, with a diameter of more than 20 cm and less than 42 cm. It has a 'Greek-like' cross engraved on the front. Aerophones Regarding wind instruments, Maria Ester Grebe (1974, pp.28-9) states that 'pre-Columbian aerophones located in archaeological excavations in Chile . . . [represent] the most developed and numerous instru- mental type in South American Indian organology'. First, we have the simple vertical flute, of which there is no data proving its ritual use. It is an open single end-blown flute with fingerholes (H/S 421.111.12). Vertical flutes found in San Pedro are made of bone, and their average dimensions have not been determined by the authors studied (Iribarren, 1969, p. 97; Grebe, 1974, pp.30 and 33-4; cf. Latcham, 1938, p. 198). Within the same group we have the kena (or quena) which, unlike the simple vertical flute, is an open notched flute, characterized by a groove or bevel in its upper open end (Grebe, 1974, pp.30 and 34-5). Kenas found in San Pedro de Atacama are made of wood or reed, and are between 12 and 21 cm long by 2 cm diameter approximately. They have in general three to five fingerholes. We will stick to the definition given by Maria Ester Grebe in her work on the pre- Columbian musical instruments in Chile: 'For the purpose of the current work, the term "whistle" will be limited to a type of vertical flute, very small and of various shapes, that in general produces only one sound, it does not have fingerholes and has a closed lower end' (Grebe, 1974, p.36). The whistles 'were used, perhaps, to mimic sounds of the surroundings and especially the sounds made by birds and totemic animals, besides having the well-known function as a signal instrument' (Grebe, 1974, p. 38). They were used probably by shamans, as a whistle was discovered in the bag of a shaman from Topiter (see note 27). PRESENT-DAY MUSIC AND RITUAL IN THE ATACAME9NO ZONE Music is an essential constituent of certain rites which are practised by the Atacameiio people today, and which are generally believed to be survivals from the distant past. Although there is no direct evidence from earlier times that, besides instruments, songs were also used, this seems highly likely in view of the fact that both vocal and instrumental music is found in present-day rituals?. Basically, two rites of fertility are known nowadays: the 'Taldtur' and the 'Convido a la Semilla', 'both associated with the cleanliness of irrigation canals, and with seed-planting' (Mostny, 1969, p. 132; Alvarez &amp; Grebe, 1974, p. 26) as part of agricultural activities carried out since pre-Columbian times. These rituals are closely linked with water, an essential element for soil fertility. The songs are in the Kunza language, and the exact meaning of the words has been forgotten. Melodies of these songs are exclusively based on the notes of the major triad. For this reason, these melodies have been classified as 'triphonic' (Alvarez &amp; Grebe, op. cit., p. 22 - see note 35). The most commonly used instruments during these rituals are the clardn, the putu (similar to the pre-Columbian trumpets), and the chorromdn or chorromo already mentioned above when discussing pre- Columbian idiophones.38 Some recordings of Taldtur songs have been made by Emil de Bruyne in Caspana (to the east of Chiu-Chiu, near the Bolivian border - see Mostny, 1969, p. 132); by Grete Mostny and Carlos Lavin in Peine (near the south-eastern corner of Salar de Atacama), and by UNESCO in Caspana and Peine (Dannemann, 1975, pp. 93-5). Both the Lavin recordings (1956) and the UNESCO ones are kept in the Sound Archive of the Department of Musicology of the Universidad de Chile, Santiago. The Lavin recordings include, besides the Taldtur, the Convido a la Semilla and other songs. The UNESCO recordings include another ritual song, apparently ancient: the Cauzu'lor. 5. SOME FURTHER REMARKS AND CONCLUSIONS Going back to Section two where the flourishing, development, and decadence of San Pedro culture was discussed, it is important to highlight the scenario where all the events took place. The desert climate deeply marked these people whose offspring have made of water a real object of worship by means of their fertility rites. The 46</page><page sequence="22">desert circumscribed them to limited spaces - the oases - inhabited for thousands of years during which they began changing their customs and, eventually, themselves as a result of migrations, wars, and colonizations. In relation to the latter, the Tiwanaku Empire was mentioned in Section 2, which with all its ideological legacy had its apogee during the same epoch in which San Pedro was flourishing. This apogee has been associated with the influence of Tiwanaku. All these different successive stages in San Pedro have been basically determined through the archaeological remains from that zone. Among these remains, evidence obtained from ceramic objects has been of paramount importance: for seeking information about religion and ritual among these people, evidence from snuff-trays has been inconclusive. It has been necessary also to systematize the ceramic material to be able to complete the corresponding chronological studies (Thomas, Massone &amp; Benavente, 1984). Regarding ritual, it appears to be important to study the rites that are being performed in the zone at present, for some concepts related to the magical-mythical world of the inhabitants of the zone would have survived from those years: for example, some ideas on the feline (Serracino, 1980, p. 105). The idea that the feline was one of the main divinities in those rites has arisen, not only from different representations made of it on snuff-trays, but also from masks representing the feline, which have been found in graves of possible shaman-sacrificers (Mostny, 1958, pp.383 and 390-1). The feline might have been chosen because it would represent 'the biggest and most dangerous wild animal of the region' (ibid., p. 391). As a matter of fact, today the feline is considered a symbol of power by the people of the region (Serracino, 1980, p.105). As stated previously, these rituals appear to be closely related to the consumption of hallucinogenic substances, through which the shaman adopted the feline characteristics. The different representations of sacrificers on snuff-trays and the finding of similar representations in the graves of decapitated people, demonstrate this connection (Le Paige, 1964, p. 61). Tables 1, 2, and 3 are based on data given by Mena (1974), Grebe (1974), and P6rez de Arce (1982). In Table 1 there are sixteen different types of instruments, of which eight are idiophones and eight are aerophones. Table 2 shows the distribution of these instruments, at eight archaeological sites, while Table 3 provides a chronological survey of instrumental remains found in the zone. From Table 1 we can observe that most of the instruments seem to 47</page><page sequence="23">have played a possible ritual role, especially the stair-shaped panpipe, the big clapper bell, and the rattle. From Table 2, differences in terms of quality and quantity among instruments coming from the different archaeological sites should be noted. These differences might be due to the following variables (not necessarily given in order of importance): a) different levels of difficulty in terms of accessibility that each instrument studied had for the researchers upon whose works this paper is based; b) differences in the intensity and kind of archaeological research method performed in each place; c) different degrees of moisture of different places that would have allowed the preservation of instruments made only of some specific materials, or of instruments left in the driest places; d) different predispositions towards certain musical instruments by the groups who inhabited the place. The last variable is difficult to verify, because the dates of instruments found do not always correlate with the chronological periods in which those places were inhabited. It is important to remark that there is a 'problem of selectivity of contexts' because, as pointed out by Perez de Arce (1989, p. 2): Most instruments come from funeral burial. That means [that they] have been chosen by the human group to be included in the trousseau of the dead, therefore they do not necessarily represent the variety and frequency of objects that were used for the performance of music in the same Culture. From Table 3, the first thing to be noted is a decrease in instruments within the period A.D. 300-900 if we exclude earlier and later instruments which only coincide with the extreme initial or final dates of this period. This decrease may be due to various different reasons (see two paragraphs above when discussing Table 2), and it must not necessarily be supposed that in this period under the Tiwanaku domain, instrumental music was less cultivated. It should be noted that, although the chorromdn dates from so remote a period as 500 B.C. - A.D. 300, it is nevertheless one of the few instruments that is still used in some rituals of the Atacameiio people. This could indicate a lack of sufficient archaeological evidence to justify more definitive statements in relation to musical instruments. There are some points of view regarding the study of instruments from San Pedro about which there is not enough certainty because of contradictory information given by some researchers. In the first place, there is a disagreement concerning the material of which some instruments are made. From observing the same bells, some authors affirm that they are made of bronze and others say that they are of copper. About the same kena some say that it is of wood and others of reed, and similar conflicting claims occur with the transverse flutes. 48</page><page sequence="24">There is also some disagreement regarding the pitches given by some aerophones. In this regard, it is important to note, as pointed out by Marti (1955, p.241), that: In all wind instruments the sounds of the main scale may increase and change their pitch by increasing or diminishing the breath force, or by closing and opening the tube end, where the sound emerges ... or by cross-fingerings ... it is impossible to specify all the scales played. Here, Hickmann (1988, p. 121), brings up an interesting point when discussing the pre-Columbian vessel flutes of Ecuador: Comparison of the surviving examples only confirms that an absolute pitch standard was unknown. Makers of the period were apparently little concerned with the tonal implications of their instruments when deciding the size. Each flute was conceived individually, its length and tonality not determined by convention. This implies that they were most likely played not in ensembles but rather as solo instruments. Finally, there is also some lack of agreement in the classification of some instruments, such as the vessel flutes. A way to get a better understanding of pre-Columbian music could be through comparative studies among different native groups in the Americas. It seems likely that cultures in the Americas would have been sufficiently homogeneous in some broad characteristics to allow the formulation of some fairly probable analogies. If we add the probability that changes in the pre-Columbian Americas were relatively slow, and the fast process of acculturation has only taken place in recent centuries, we see how the study of different native music features in the Americas would be useful in order to imagine the past possible ritual use of archaeological musical instruments found at present in San Pedro de Atacama.9 It seems, however, that these studies will remain at a highly speculative level. Postscript We should not forget the ethical implications of archaeo-musicological studies when dealing with profanation of tombs at the hands of archaeologists. In most cases these profanations have been executed without the permission of the descendants who still live in the zone. Archaeologists in many cases have virtually ransacked the graves of natives' old relatives. I mention this in trying to overcome my guilt, after realizing that many of the references used, in writing this paper, are based on archaeologists' tomb profanation and their later reports and conclusions. The case is different if native people who live in the area consider that it is not an improper action to follow. By ignoring this ethical issue we are tacitly encouraging the 49</page><page sequence="25">continuity of neo-colonialism, imperialism, and Eurocentrism in musicological studies. Here I would like to mention that a few years ago some Native American students (from certain Californian indigenous groups including the Chumash) felt extremely uncomfortable and finally decided to avoid attending classs at Haines Hall in the University of California at Los Angeles (UCLA), because the building kept the sacred burial objects and remains of their 'grandparents', among others, just stored in boxes, as part of the anthropological research material held by the university.
