Spanos It is due to art's poetic nature that, in the midst of what is, art breaks open an open place, in whose openness everything is other than usual. - Martin Heidegger, "The Origin of the Work of Art" I feel along the edges of life for a way that will lead to open land. - David Ignatow, Rescue the Dead</page><page sequence="2">Ut pictura poesis: "as is painting, so is poetry." When the artists of the late Renaissance, under the influence of Aristotle's Poetics, appro- priated this Horatian formula to read, "as is poetry, so is painting," it came to express the essence of Western humanism: the Word (logos) as ratio. From that time, the idea that painting is a speaking picture informed the art of the West until late in the nineteenth century when the Modernist urge to abstraction challenged its authority in the name of another, immaterial reality. Looked at superficially - dis-interestedly - Don DeMauro's art, in its obsession to articulate real objects, to bring them into presence, appears to constitute a return to this tradition. He has, in fact, been called a "Neo-Realist" by those who have commented on his work. However, looked at from a phenomenological standpoint, i.e., interestedly, or to adapt Heidegger, care-fully, DeMauro's art - precisely his obsession to "return to the things themselves" - is revealed to be not a continuation of the humanistic tradition but a de-struction of its essential iconographic assumptions, a calling into question of its complacent anthropomorphic imaging in a far more powerful, subtle, and fruitful way than most Modernist abstractionism does. By de-struction I do not intend anything negative. On the contrary, to destroy in this sense means, as Heidegger says, to dis-close, to open up "a positive possibility of the most primordial kind of knowing." In breaking the habit of anthropomorphic realism, DeMauro's art does not nullify things-as-they-are; rather, as a breaking open, it makes them new. It dis-covers the ur-ground underlying the received, the derivative, structures of humanistic iconography, retrieves an origin which is prior to the word as name, an uncanny realm of silence. DeMauro's art is thus not reassuring as humanistic art is intended to be. It is dis-locating. Far from fulfilling the anthropomorphic imperatives of the Horatian formula, it violates them. DeMauro's art is, in this scene, profoundly unspeakable. Or, rather, finally, it says the profoundly unspeakable. For in destroying the word that informs the logocentric art of the humanistic tradition, these drawings and paintings release a "voice" that says something - something which the word as ratio cannot say, indeed drowns out and inevitably relegates to oblivion. Like Samuel Beckett or David Ignatow, whose generously er-ratic or ec-centric art strikes me as the verbal equivalent of his, DeMauro's work calls forth a silence at the heart of things that ultimately speaks being, brings it near, not to be grasped but to be experienced - heard - as a happening in all its disquieting and suggestive mystery. The editors are honored to be able to present a sampling of the art of this important young postmodern artist in boundary 2. It is truly work.
