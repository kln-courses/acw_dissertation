DE BORHEGYI ABSTRACT A unique, miniature, anthropomorphic stone hacha from the Gulf Coast of Mexico is described and illustrated. The author also discusses other miniature stone objects from Mesoamerica, such as mushroom stones, yokes, "toad altars," "doughnut-stones," metates, spherical stone balls, and star-shaped maceheads, and suggests that some of these Mesoamerican miniature stone objects may have been used in connection with the various forms of the pre-Columbian ball game. IN A RECENT article I described (Borhegyi 1966) a miniature plain, horseshoe-shaped ceremonial ball-game yoke from the Milwaukee Public Museum collection reported to have come from the Tajin area near Papantla in northern Veracruz (reproduced here as Fig. 2 b'). Since writing the article, the Milwaukee Public Museum was fortunate enough to obtain an even smaller minia- ture stone yoke (Fig. 1 a) and another miniature stone artifact- a miniature anthropomorphic hacha or "thin stone head" (Fig. 1 b) - also related to the pre-Colum- bian ball game of Middle America. This miniature stone yoke is 8 cm. long, 6 cm. wide, and 2.5 cm. in height. The miniature "thin stone head" or hacha was donated to the Museum in 1966 by Mr. Rich- ard P. Herzfeld of Milwaukee. It is from the Gulf Coast of Mexico, State of Veracruz. Made of grey volcanic stone and almost identical in size with the miniature stone yoke illustrated in Fig. 1 a, the specimen is 8.5 cm. high; its upper section is 5.5 cm. The matching profiles of the miniature anthropomorphic hacha represent a wide openmouthed human face with slanted eyes and closed eyelids, wearing a helmetlike head covering. Both sides of the "helmet" are elaborately decorated, and there is an incised line transversing from front to back. (For two very similar but regular-sized stone hachas from Veracruz, see Proskouriakoff 1954, Fig. 11 e, and Kubler 1954, Fig. 137.) Except for the two upper incisors which are present though broken, the mouth of the individual represented on the hacha is toothless, and he has no tongue. It is possble that his two upper incisors were originally so carved as to show tooth filing, but this cannot be positively ascertained because of the damage they have sustained. As on most thin stone heads, the occipital section is flat and slants vertically downward at about an 80? angle. Furthermore, the specimen has the typical form of a Veracruz hacha; it was possibly notched in back and is wedge-shaped in horizontal section (Fig. 2 f and f'). The unique feature of the hacha described above is its size. Generally, thin stone heads known from the Gulf Coast of Mexico, Chiapas, Guatemala, and Salvador range in height from 18 to 30 cm. and from 14 to 18 cm. in width. Also, like most of their larger counterparts, both the miniature stone yokes and the hacha are made in the style of the Middle or Late Classic period (A.D. 400-900). With the excep- tion of one other miniature anthropomorphic stone</page><page sequence="3">FACTS AND COMMENTS 545 hacha which I examined some 15 years ago in the house of an Indian laborer at Santiago Atitlan, Guatemala, I know of no other miniature stone hacha of this type. However, two miniature pottery yokes with pottery hachas attached to them are reported from Tabasco. They are now in the Jose Luis Franco collection in Mex- ico City (see Knauth 1961: 187, and Fig. 2). Undoubtedly the miniature stone hachas, like the miniature stone yokes, were small-sized replicas used in some yet unknown fashion in pre- or post-ball-game cere- monies rather than during the ball game itself (Borhegyi 1966). They may have been part of a cache offering in honor of the tutelary gods of the ball game to bring success to a supplicant. In fact, the inanimate expression of the face and the closed eyes and open mouth of the individual represented on the hacha suggest a "trophy head" or a decapitated human head, human sacrifice by decapitation being a fate which often awaited the players of the pre-Columbian ball game (cf. Covarrubias 1957: 183; Knauth 1961; Proskouriakoff 1954: 69, Figs. 10 k-p; and Borhegyi 1965: 23, footnote 28). The miniature stone hachas and the miniature stone yokes are of Classic period origin, but the custom of making small-sized replicas of larger stone objects must have been widespread in Mesoamerica, stemming from earlier, Preclassic times. In Mexico and Guatemala, the following miniature replicas of larger stone objects have been noted by the author: (a) star-shaped stone maceheads (Fig. 2 e') (b) plain and effigy mushroom stones (Fig. 2 c'; Bor- hegyi 1961) (c) Legless, two-legged, and tripod stone metates and stone manos sometimes employed in connection with miniature mushroom stones (Fig. 2 a1; Bor- hegyi 1961, Fig. 1) (d) miniature stone palmas and crested stone heads (cf. Proskouriakoff 1954, Fig. 10 s) (e) "toad bowls" and "toad altars" made of stone (see Shook and Kidder 1952, Fig. 78 c, d, e; see also a large "toad-stone altar" in the Guatemalan Museum, No. 2372) (f) plain and effigy "doughnut-stones" (Fig. 2 d') (g) spherical stone balls (Stone 1943: 81-3, P1. III b) The exact function of the spherical stone balls and of the "doughnut stones," whether of miniature or of regular size, is still a mystery. The spherical stone balls have a wide distribution, ranging from Patagonia north- ward, including sections of the Antilles, into Tennessee (Stone 1943: 81). In Mesoamerica, at least, some of these stone balls may have been used in connection with some form of the ball game, especially since they fre- quently occur in the same areas from where hachas, yokes, and palmas are reported. "Doughnut stones" occur throughout the Guatemalan Highland and Pacific Coast and are even reported from the Maya Lowlands, from the Late Preclassic through the Late Classic, and possibly into Postclassic periods. They are generally of vesicular lava or limestone. (For this distribution of such "perforated stone objects," or "doughnut stones" see Shook 1949; Kidder, Jennings, and Shook 1946: 141-2.) It has been suggested that the regular-sized "doughnut stones" may have been employed as digging-stick weights (Shook 1949: 20-1; Woodbury 1965: 165), but more likely they were mounted on wooden handles and were probably used as ball-game bats, clubs, ceremonial maces, scepters, or batons (Borhegyi 1965: 25, footnote 35). When so hafted, they closely resemble the strange "torch- shaped" objects held by some individuals on Olmec sculptures (Coe 1965: 765, Figs. 5, 8, 12, 49, 51, 52). One such Late Preclassic stone mace or "torch" (32 cm. long) made of grey volcanic stone is illustrated by Shook (1965, Fig. 1 d) from El Sitio, an archaeological site on the Pacific Coast of Guatemala. This object of unknown use may actually represent a hafted "doughnut stone." The fact that some of the Olmec sculptures and figures holding such "torches" also wear "football helmets" and have in their other hand "knuckle-dusters" (cf. Coe 1965: 764-5, Figs. 8, 12, 50, 52) -objects which may have been used as ball-game handstones or as hand weapons during close infighting (cf. Borhegyi 1967) would strongly suggest that the "doughtnut stones," too, may have been employed in pre-Columbian times throughout Mesoamerica in some connection with the ceremonial ball game. Since it is clear that the various Mesoamerican mini- ature stone objects, many of which were actually found in ritual caches near ballcourt structures (Borhegyi 1961), were never intended as children's toys, they must have played some specific and possibly closely interrelated ritual role in the pre-Columbian Maya and Gulf Coast Mexican cultures. It is therefore suggested here that they be considered as a "complex" related in some way to the pre-Columbian ball game. Quite likely there once existed some form of ceremonial or functional relation- ship between the Preclassic and Classic Maya "doughnut stones," the mushroom stones, the Preclassic Maya "toad altars" and "toad bowls," and the Classic period "Pipil" stone yokes and "hachas." Just what was the exact nature and the extent of this relationship is still to be deter- mined. It may not be coincidental, however, that the subject matter depicted on the Preclassic Maya "dough- nut-stones" and mushroom stones - crouching or seated jaguars, bats, sacrificial victims, trophy heads, dismem- bered limbs, vultures, toads, coatimundis, and deer - are also the most popular motifs employed on such Classic period ball-game paraphernalia as the stone yokes and "thin stone heads." It is clear, therefore, that the complex of Mesoamerican stone miniatures and its pos- sible relationship through time to various forms of the pre-Columbian ball game merits closer attention in the future.
