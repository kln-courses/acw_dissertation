His is not figurative, but abstract art. Moravia considers the development of modem abstraction as a symptom of the disjunction, not to say dis- integration, of the normal relationship between reality and our appre- hension of it; this relationship becomes displaced by intellectual aes- theticism as art becomes both dehumanised and dehumanising. It is the 'nothingness' envisaged by John Fowles in The Ebony Tower, where the move away from figurative art is likewise seen as a rejection of the body and a sterile academic exercise: Fowles writes that "what had really been set up by this jettisoning of the human body and its natural physical perceptions was a vicious spiral, a vor- tex, a drain to nothingness." It is a metaphor for the condition of alien- ation and absurdity which follows the disjunction between "la realta" and our perception of it, disintegration of the relationship between mind and world. The task which Dino seems to have set himself in the course of the novel is to repair the relationship between the perceiving mind and the world, consciousness and its objects, which is expressed through language and art. It is through his relationship with the two main female characters of the novel, his mother and Cecilia, that Dino achieves a new rapport with the world around him. The two women represent alternative and opposite positions with regard to the problematic relationship between surface and depth, self and world, form and meaning, and these differences are articulated in their relationship to language.</page><page sequence="4">WOMEN AND LANGUAGE IN LA NOIA 189 The mother, a representative of the bourgeoisie profiting from the industrial and economic expansion of the 1950s, is obsessed by wealth, possessions, and "la forma" to the point of being totally iden- tified with them. As she has lost touch with reality, so her language remains on the symbolic level of form, indicating nothing but itself; "content" has disappeared and language signifies, not the world, but an absence of relationship to it. Cecilia represents the opposite pos- sibility; she cannot be trapped in "la forma," and evades all attempts to attach her to a linguistic or social definition. Cecilia's position is diametrically opposed to that of the mother; she suggests a reality which cannot be reached by the normal processes of giving meaning or the ways of understanding offered by a capitalist society, and which therefore cannot be controlled by them. If Cecilia acts in some sense as a cure to the social and linguistic sickness embodied in the figure of the mother, she does so unwittingly and unconsciously. Her oppo- sition to the mother is not intellectual or even rational, but predicated on an all too familiar version of feminity that is at once elusive and beyond the norms of language and reason. It is something of a paradox in this novel that the vacuity of a particular ethical and ideological position is revealed by a vacuous notion of gender and gender differ- ence. It is Dino who goes beyond Cecilia to reach a heightened meta- physical and intellectual understanding. Critics have noted in Dino's mother her obsession with money, her snobbery and concern with social appearances; these obsessions are reduced to the level of futility and absurdity by the sight of her aged, withered flesh and empty life, the woman's body marking the corrup- tion and decay of the social one. As the novel progresses, the figure of the mother takes on the quality of a cypher, an extreme example of that dissociation which Dino himself must come to terms with. After the lengthy interroga- tions near the beginning of the book, Dino speaks no more of her until he returns to ask her for money: she is presented to us beside her safe, the source of her money and of his own dilemma. She makes a final appearance at a reception which she gives for her wealthy friends: "il conformismo del denaro" (316) which Dino iden- tifies at the "ricevimento" denotes an abstraction from individuality and difference, an assimilation into collective form which is inevita- bly political. Pullini sug- gests that "di quella noia di cui Dino e l'incarnazione intellettuale e consapevole, Cecilia e l'incarnazione animale, istintiva."is There are strong parallels between Cecilia's relationship to lan- guage and Dino's empty canvas: dissociation leads in both instances to abstraction and tautology. Abstract art (the period portrayed in La noia had seen the work of Abstract Expressionist painters such as Pol- lock, Rothko, and Newman), impersonal and dehumanised, divorced from the reality which it is capable of representing, can be considered a sterile academic exercise, drawing attention only to itself; in Ab- stract Expressionism, for example, the surface of the canvas, its pos- sibilities in terms of geometrical shapes and juxtaposition of colour, texture, and method of application of paint, become an end in them- selves. Dissociation operates the same way in language when it fails to indicate anything beyond itself. Moravia has said that in writing La noia he was much influenced by Wittgenstein's theories of language and truth: "lessi Wittgenstein e molte cose mi si chiarirono... Mi fece capire il valore della tautolo- gia: quanto la tautologia possa essere inquietante da un punto di vista logico. Nacque cosi il personaggio di Cecilia."19 In his Tractus Logico-Philosophicus, Wittgenstein discusses the two poles of tautology and contradiction as not factual but logical truths, which cannot be taken to signify reality: "Tautologie und Kon- tradiktion sind nich Bilder der Wirklichkeit steht."20 A tautological proposition is one which is true only in terms of itself. Its truth does not depend on the state of the world, but neither does it tell us any- thing about the world: "Tautologie und Kontradiktion sind nicht Bilder der Wirklichkeit. Sie stellen keine mogliche Sachlage dar... Hence, her repetitive statements such as "diversa vuol dire diversa" and "niente vuol dire niente" (176). Despise his mother as he will, Dino's real problem lies not so much in the "malattia degli oggetti" (7) but in that he is still an accomplice to his mother's world where ownership and possession constitute the only real value. When his view of Luciani's flat, for example, is blocked by a bottle, Dino casually decides to "togliere di mezzo l'og- getto ingombrante, acquistandolo" (219). This equivocal complicity is expressed in the ambiguity of his relationship with his mother: lei mi premeva il piede come fanno gli innamorati fra di loro; soltanto che eravamo madre e figlio e il legame che ci univa non era l'amore bensi il denaro. D'altra parte io non potevo rifiutare questo legame, perch6 rifiutarlo avrebbe voluto dire rifiutare anche il legame di sangue che esso sottintendeva (48-49). Dino suffers his own form of blindness as he attributes to the object of his desire the desire to be an object, "come un oggetto privo di qual- siasi volonta all'infuori di quella di essere posseduto" (142). As long as Cecilia conforms to his image of her, she remains literally an ob- ject, or less: "in realta, Cecilia nuda o vestita, sdraiata sotto di me du- rante l'amplesso o allungata al mio fianco dopo l'amore, al buio o in piena luce, pareva perdere ogni giorno di phi, ai miei occhi, la sua con- sistenza di persona, anzi di oggetto riconoscibile" (127-28). Yet Cecilia evades this position as object and resists Dino in her dissociation from language. By subverting, however unwittingly, the linguistic relation between them in which Dino proposes and the fe- male is neatly disposed of into a package definition, Cecilia becomes the source of desire: "provavo un desiderio violento di lei che mi men- tiva e dunque mi sfuggiva e perci6 diventava reale e attraente" (202). For Dino it is not enough to possess Cecilia physically; she must at all costs be made to enter the symbolic realm of language, brought out of her "silenzio misterioso" (252) where she is "infantilmente im- penetrabile" (256; my emphasis). Sexual possession, through which Dino attempts to close the gap between possessor and possessed, is revealed and condemned as a blind illusion, the "illusione maschile che vede nel possesso fisico il solo possesso reale" (170-71), the "solita illusione maschile di raggiungere il possesso in un sol colpo e senza parlare" (212). Dino at first equates physical possession of Ce- cilia with, to quote John Berger in G., "the variable mystery of her presence," the constantly shifting and elusive sense of what consti- tutes a woman other than her sexual, physical self. Possession ex- cludes the possibility of love, which at this point is the same as desire: il fatto di poter disporre di Cecilia, ossia del suo corpo, quanto volevo, ogni volta che volevo, e in tutti i modi che volevo, dandomi l'illusione di possederla a fondo, ossia di avere con lei un rapporto tanto completo da renderne ormai inutile la continuazione, mi aveva convinto di non amarla (129). His later frenzied assaults on her are a doomed attempt to close the gulf between his consciousness and hers by physical contact: "mi sen- tii assalire dal solito, cieco impulso maschile, che mi spingeva a pren- derla perch6 non riuscivo a possederla, come se prendendola l'avessi posseduta" (329). Of this male condition, Simone de Beauvoir re- marks appropriately on "l'ambiguite de sa condition charnelle. I1 n'as- sume orgueilleusement sa sexualit6 qu'en tant qu'elle est un mode d'appropriation de l'Autre: et ce rave de possession n'aboutit qu'a un echec."23 Evading Dino in language and escaping his definitions, Ce- cilia is an (unconscious) subversion of that society which would seek to possess through sex, money, and language: "quel che fosse Cecilia io non riuscivo a saperlo" (229). The image of the relationship between language and that which it defines is one of containment and imprisonment. Dino makes in- creasingly manic attempts to 'enclose' Cecilia and in despair at his failure is driven close to the edge of crime. These attempts are worth quoting for their self-conscious imagery of domination and entrap-</page><page sequence="13">198 SHARON WOOD ment. Dino seeks to enclose her in a definition through obliging her to confess ("Mi restava, tuttavia, I'insaziabile bisogno di chiuderla nella sua confessione come in una gabbia di vergogna dalla quale non le fosse pii possibile sfuggire" [247]), showing her to be venal, defined by her relationship to money: Mi dissi, ad un tratto che, dopo il fallimento dei miei precedenti ten- tativi di possesso, il denaro poteva forse essere la trappola in cui avrei potuto rinchiuderla (250); by marrying her: Pensavo di sposarla con tutte le pompe religiose e borghesi e, una volta sposata, farle fare un gran numero di figli che, anche loro, avrebbero contribuito a definirla e chiuderla nell'immagine per niente enigmat- ica della matemita (294); by judging and condemning her: "afferrare Cecilia, chiuderla in un peccato, inchiodarla ad un giudizio" (312); or imprisoning her: "la guidai verso la camera, aprii la porta e la spinsi nel buio, come l'avrei spinta in una prigione per rinchiudervela per sempre" (321; all em- phases mine). He makes a final desperate attempt to reduce her to the status of an object, by killing her: "uccidendola l'avrei strappata a tutto ci6 che la rendeva inafferrabile e l'avrei chiusa nella prigione de- finitiva della morte" (331). In his desperation to possess Cecilia, Dino risks what Barthes calls "l'acte ultime de toutes les objectivations, le meurtre."24 Dino's relationship with Cecilia can only end in par- adox, the recognition that "l'autonomia e il mistero di Cecilia" can- not be made "noto," "comune," or "insignificante," (207) by physical aggression, social or linguistic definitions: "non possedevo Cecilia e lei era altrove" (167). Cecilia, whose function in the novel can be seen as an illustration of the gap between language and reality, is herself a symbol for Dino, as for Balestrieri. The two men present alternative solutions to the dilemma represented by Cecilia; Dino, at least, assumes Balestrieri's situation to have paralleled his own. Both seek to possess Cecilia through increasingly frenetic sexual activity, which can only lead to contradiction and frustration.2s While Dino signs only a blank can- vas, Balestrieri paints enormous erotic nudes: his art is figurative, but pornographic. It is perhaps the pictorial equivalent of Dino's porno- graphic use of language, through which he would penetrate, possess, and control Cecilia. Dino seeks to resolve his situation through attempted suicide. It is a symbolic death with metaphysical overtones, while the subse- quent establishment of the ethic of "contemplazione" is the culmi- nation of the undermining of hierarchies immanent in modes of pos- session, "il crollo di un'ambizione insostenibile" (346). "Contemplazione" is the renunciation of the oppositional and hier- archical structure postulated by bourgeois economic, sexual, and lin- guistic practice. It means separation and differentiation, and Cecilia's physical absence is no longer a source of anxiety. Cecilia is granted, nominally at least, the status of subject; she will no longer be the ob- ject of his sexual aggression: "quest'amore poteva o non poteva ac- compagnarsi con il rapporto fisico" (346).28 Nor is she any longer to be equated with the empty canvas: "non ero affatto sicuro che quel nesso che avevo visto per tanto tempo tra Cecilia e la pittura, ci fosse davvero" (346). Piiu ampi- amente consiste nella coscienza che la ricerca &amp; vana e che non resta se non di adattarsi al presente.29 Dino's is a private, metaphysical solution to a problem which is fundamentally social and political. There is a paradox in the adoption of "contemplazione" as a way out of the normal structures of relating to ourselves, others and the world, imposed upon us by a heavily in- dustrialised and capitalist society. Acceptance of the subjectivity of the other person here implies renunciation not only of the compul- sion to dominate and to possess, but also of the possibility of action,</page><page sequence="15">200 SHARON WOOD or contact between individuals.30 Cecilia, the embodiment of absence or lack of connections, is absent from the end of the novel. While Ce- cilia herself cannot be brought into the realm of discourse where meanings are given and exchanged, "contemplazione" can be seen as an appropriation of irrationality, of feminity, of the unnameable and undefinable which lies beyond conventional forms of reason and logic offered by discourse and modem social life.
