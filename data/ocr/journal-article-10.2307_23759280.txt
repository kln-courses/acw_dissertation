In April 2009, the World Digital Library opened its digital portal (www.wdl.org). It is its aim to make available via the Internet significant materials from all over the world. Among the little more than one thousand manuscripts made accessible so far is a manuscript on the Maya that supposedly dates to 1548 (http: //www.wdl.org/en/item/2961) and which at present belongs to the Centro de Estudios de Historia de México, an institu tion created by the Mexican billionaire Carlos Slim. The man uscript does not have a title and is referred to under the given title El modo de cómo hacían la pintura los indígenas ("How Vol. XXXI • Juni 2009</page><page sequence="2">56 the Indians Did Their Paintings"). It consists of three folios that measure 32.5 by 22.5 centimetres. The text contains drawings of Maya numbers, time periods, and supernatural beings. A closer look makes it clear that this manuscript is a fake that belongs to the Canek group of hoax manuscripts (see article by Hanns J. Prem in Ancient Mesoamerica 1999). A detailed analysis of this forgery is in preparation. The text of the Pintura manuscript is in the handwriting found in other forged documents of the Canek group. Its drawings copy illustrations from the third English or the second Spanish edition of Sylvanus Morley's "The Ancient Maya" (Morley 1956, 1972). Some are also taken from the Madrid Codex. The inclusion of this forged manuscript in the World Digital Library undermines the goal of the library to make "available on the Internet, free of charge and in multilingual format, significant primary materials from countries and cultures around the world" (http://www.wdl.org/en/about/). A forgery nurtures instead cultural misunderstanding and it misleads the general public. It is the wolf in a sheep's skin among many original items in this library. Study of the effects of Prehispanic cinnabar mining at Toluquilla, Queretaro, Mexico Mexico City (iNAH/www.inah.gob.mx). The Instituto Nacion al de Antropología e Historia (INAH) is collaborating on an inter-institutional project developed at the archaeological site of Toluquilla, Queretaro, where physical anthropologi cal studies are being conducted on 30 Prehispanic skeletons, with the aim of ascertaining whether the population worked in the mining industry. The initiative developed by the Que retaro INAH Centre, the National Polytechnical Institute's (IPN) Applied Science Research Centre, the National Auton omous University of Mexico's (UNAH) Geosciences Center, and Hungary's Geological Institute, hopes to demonstrate the relevance of mine exploitation in the southern region of the Sierra Gorda. The archaeological sites of Toluquilla and Ranas have been linked to Precolumbian mining, specifi cally to obtaining cinnabar, a mineral widely used in funer ary rites all over Mesoamerica. Elizabeth Mejia, director of the Toluquilla Archaeological Project, declared that the bone samples belong to male and female individuals of several age groups, found in burials during the excavations. The objec tive of the study is to detect mercury bio accumulation (the presence of toxic substances) whose presence would reveal constant exposure of the inhabitants to the mineral by min ing or manufacturing it. The main question is if Toluquilla was inhabited by miners; another one is if people buried at the site had contact with mining material that caused health effects, which, according to mercury toxicology, may involve abortions, premature teeth loss, or anaemia. Archaeologist Mejia of the Queretaro INAH Centre, mentioned that physi cal anthropological studies will first be conducted on 30 of 50 samples. The remains of 190 individuals have been located at Toluquilla, what allows analysis on a large scale. C -dates for occupation at Toluquilla range between 300 BC and 1450 AD. "Between 200 BC and the beginning of the current era, cinnabar exploitation in Sierra Gorda began, with work or ganization being specialized to devote some time to mining and to training experts. This allowed the exchange of cinnabar with other Mesoamerican regions, as foreign material [at the site] reveals", declared Mejia. According to archaeologist Alberto Herrera, director of the Ranas Archaeological Project, there are 7 Prehispanic mine shafts associated with the Toluquilla level, which bring to 20 the number found here and at Ranas Hill. Discovery of possible Olmeca-Xicalanca burials near Cholula Mexico City (iNAH/www.inah.gob.mx). During excavations conducted by archaeologists of the Instituto Nacional de An tropología e Historia (INAH), in connection with the salvage project "Colector Pluvial 12 Oriente desde la 4 Norte a la Prolongación 6 Oriente, San Pedro y San Andres Cholula, 2009" in San Pedro Cholula near the historical centre of Cholula, 17 Prehispanic skeletons were discovered. The burials accompanied by offerings have been recovered with the collaboration of archaeologists Martha Saenz Serdio and Ananta Gricelides Mazadiego Cruz during the construction of a rainwater collector. The remains were found in foetal position, oriented to northeast, north and east (characteristic of Postclassic burials in the area), accompanied mainly by ceramic offerings such as pots and flat bowls. Furthermore, a jadeite bead and a complete obsidian knife were found. Sex and age of the buri als have yet to be determined, but most of them could have been of adult and juvenile age. Ashuni Emmanuel Romero Butrón, director of the excavations, remarked that the burials date before the arrival of Tolteca-Chichimeca groups around 1150 AD. The skeletal remains may also be c'^-tested to determine their age. The date of the burials may relate them to the Olmeca-Xicalanca, who had their capital city at Cacax tla, Tlaxcala. If this dating is validated, it would confirm the presence of this group at Cholula during the early Postclassic period (900-1150 AD) to the northeast of the Great Pyramid. This information contradicts the Tolteca-Chichimeca histori cal sources, which only make reference to a population settled to the south of the Great Pyramid. The Maya carved stone box from Hun Nal Ye, Guatemala Graz (Karl Herbert Mayer). The archaeological site named Hun Nal Ye, a cave shrine, is located on the grounds of the finca San Vicente Chicatal, in the extreme northwestern sec tion of the municipality of San Pedro Carchá in the Guatema lan Department of Alta Verapaz. Leonidas Javiér Morales, the finca owner, discovered the unlooted cave with its archaeological objects in March 2005 and reported his find to Lie. Salvador López of the Ministerio de Cultura y Deportes, Instituto de Antropología e Historia. In May 2005 the cave was visited by Javiér, López, and Dr. Brent Woodfill (then a graduate student at Vanderbilt Univer sity); subsequent investigations were conducted by Woodfill and Licda Mirza Monterroso (Oficina de Derechos Humanos del Arzobispado de Guatemala). During this survey, the cave was mapped, three test pits were dug, and the artifacts were photographed and drawn. The cave is small, about 25 m long, and of difficult access. It features beautiful stalactites. The artifacts found include several Early Classic ceramic vessels, two ceramic boxes, and a rectangular stone box. This lithic vessel is embellished with figures of deities and scribes, as well as elaborate hieroglyphic texts on all four vertical exterior surfaces and on the horizontal lid. It is carved from an unidentified volcanic stone and consists of two parts: a body with four small supports and matching lid. The two-part box is 38 cm long, 21.7 cm wide, and 10.9 cm high, including the 2.8 cm high cover. The nubbin sup ports are 1.6 cm high. The lid is 2.8 cm thick. The box is in a pristine condition. Several months after the archaeological investigations and before an official registration of the artifacts were undertaken, the cave, closed for protection with a locked gate entrance, was looted in or around August 2005 by unknown persons. Eight of the most beautiful and complete ceramic vessels were stolen, others broken, and the extraordinary 1500-year old stone box also disappeared. Fortunately, the majority of the artifacts had been documented and photographed before in situ, and several had been drawn by Walter Burgos. Besides the inscribed monuments, like stelae, altars, and architectural elements, which comprise the major class of vehicles for Maya writing, there exists a substantial corpus of small-sized objects that were evidently conceived by the ancient Maya artists as portable artifacts. The box from Hun Nal Ye belongs to the inscribed sculptures of the latter class. These particular texts are usually designated as miscellaneous texts, abbreviated as MT (Mayer 1997). The Corpus of Maya Hieroglyphic Inscriptions Project of the Peabody Museum, Harvard University, Cambridge, uses abbreviations, or codes, for archaeological sites with Maya inscriptions. Woodfill uses for artifact designations from Hun Nal Ye the three-let ter code HNY; his system is followed here. According to these conventions the box will be provisionally labeled as "HNY:MT-1". The Early Classic stone box depicts on the lid the image of a deity, interpreted either as the Maize and/or Moon God, accompanied by a rabbit and a moon symbol containing a single glyph block. The scene is surrounded by a textual frame, encompassing 16 glyph blocks. The long vertical Side 1 portrays two personages, either human beings or deities, facing each other, seated on serpent thrones, and accompanied by glyphs. This strange scene has also been interpreted as depicting the Jaguar Gods of the Underworld holding codices in their hands. The inscribed vertical panel between the figures is composed of six glyph blocks. Discovery of royal Mayan tomb in the Oropéndola Temple, Copan, Honduras Copan Ruinas (Asociación Copán/www.asociacioncopan.or g). In a press conference at the Centro Regional de Investi gaciones (CRIA) in Copan Ruinas, information was released on the finding of a tomb within the Oropéndola Temple, one of the most important discoveries at Copan Ruinas, Hondu ras. Ricardo Agurcia Fasquelle, Honduran archaeologist and executive director of the Copan Association, along with his team of archeologists and technicians, began secret excava tions in June 2008. This tomb (Tomb 08-01) is assumed to be that of an unknown early ruler, one of those who were in power between 440 and 550 AD before the construction of Oropéndola, which has been dated between 550 and 700 AD. It is not yet known whether the tomb contains the remains of a male or female. The excavation began in an area below the Oropéndola Temple after a floor was discovered during conservation work in the tunnels. The temple known as Oro péndola was built after the famous Rosalila Temple. On the top of the temple is the face of a jaguar which signifies the Mountain of the Jaguar a mythical place of power for the ancient Maya. The tomb is believed to be of Maya royalty because of the quality and quantity of offerings found inside. Although the collapse of the roof of the tomb destroyed much of the skeletal remains, which correspond to that of an adult over 30 years of age and between 1.54 to 1.67 m in height, the tomb has yielded many offerings including ceramic pots containing food, jade ear cuffs, and shells. In addition the meticulous work over the months has revealed three jade necklaces, alabaster vessels, and hundreds of pieces of mica of which many are of unknown origin as they are thought to have been brought into the Copan Valley from as far away as Teotihuacan in Mexico. Obituaries Felipe Solís Olguín (1944 - 2009) Mexico City (iNAH/www.inah.gob.mx). Archaeologist Felipe Solís Olguin, director of the National Museum of Anthro pology (MNA) since 2000, passed away on 23 April 2009. At the time of his death he was working as a curator in the exhibition "Teotihuacan, City of Gods". Felipe Soli's was born in Mexico City in 1944, and was part of the Instituto Nacional de Antropología e Historia (INAH) from 1972. As a field archaeologist, he contributed to the discovery of the history of Mexico City's ancient cultures. He was present in 1975, when the Chapultepec aqueduct was explored. In 1978, during the salvage of the Coyolxauhqui monolith, it was he who recognized the figure as the moon deity Coyolxauhqui. He was director of the National Museum of Anthropology and also curator of important exhibitions in the museum. He was also the curator of the Mexica collection and from 1990 to 2000 he was archaeology sub-director.
