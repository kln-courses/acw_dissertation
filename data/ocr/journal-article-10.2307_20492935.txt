Domed studs of shiny red and blue glass, intricate panels of red, blue and translucent green on the handles are echoed by the red and blue studs which punctuate the rim of the foot, enriching the flat silver surfaces and complementing the complex gold filigree and glitter of chip-carved panels.' The 'Tara' brooch uses coloured glass in much the same way but on a diminutive scale (Fig. 2), while the great paten from Derrynaflan carries the use of coloured glass to unique heights of virtuosity in terms of complexity and variety.2 All three eighth-century pieces are justly celebrated for their fine metalwork and especially for the technical mastery of their gold fili gree decoration.3 Yet in its own way the glass inlay is also remarkable, 1. Much of the beauty of the Ardagh Chalice is derived from the dramatic use of strong colours in the glass ornament. unique in contemporary medieval Europe and unlike the filigree work, it has a tap-root in the Celtic Iron Age. It is a development of an indigenous enamelling tradition, a tradition onto which new styles and ideas were grafted in the seventh -and eighth centuries.4 In turn, the new glass colours and patterns in metalwork were themselves trans lated into the painted decoration of contemporary gospel books., the closest relationship being with the Book of Durrow where glass studs, millefiori panels and other patterns in enamel appear to have been copied by the illuminator (Fig. 3).5 Specialists in enamelling will cor rectly object that enamel is distin guished from other glass working because the prepared inlay of ground glass is heated to the point where it not only melts but bonds to its metal back plate and frame. The earliest use of enamelwork in Ireland is found mainly in the small scale decoration of dress fasteners and penannular brooches where relatively large beds of enamel are inlaid with millefiori glass. molten glass that has been poured into moulds or frames and given a false look of walled cells, principally by the use of silver or gold grids embedded in the surface of the glass.6 The domed studs produced in this way are copying true cloisonne studs in gold, gamet and glass made by germanic goldsmiths in the sixth and early seventh centuries. But in this borrowing, and unlike the process by which filigree tech niques were adopted, the multicoloured stud looks like its models but was produced using a very dif ferent method. This involved the use of molten glass, and in particular a distinctively bright opaque red which resembles sealing-wax. The glass is made opaque and coloured by den dritic cuprite and the same colourant was used in Celtic enamelling in Britain and Ireland in, respectively, the Roman and pre-Christian Iron Age.' It has, however, been recently established that the glass itself has a different com position from both its Iron Age Celtic and Roman predeces sors although it has the same brilliant appearance.8 In the early medieval period this cuprite red glass was used for champleve enamelling, where the enamel lies in recessed fields prepared by casting or excising the metal base. Red cuprite glass, however, is difficult to use for enamelling because if any oxygen is present when the pre pared glass is heated to melting point it dis colours irreversibly to a blackish green.9 The medieval Celtic red glass, however, may have had a lower working temperature than its Iron Age equivalent and we also know it was some times applied in a fondant state. This use of hot glass inlay has been described as 'pseudo-enam elling' and it is this which links enamelling and the production of multicoloured decorative studs in the seventh and eighth centuries and explains, at least in part, the Irish preference for a hot process in the place of the cold, precision-cut inlays which were their inspiration. Gold or copper were certainly preferred as the base metal by later medieval enamellers.'? Nevertheless there are some fine examples of red enamelled silver from Ireland and Pictish Scotland in the sixth to seventh centuries (Fig. 4)." The first colour to be used and the predominant one in later work, was the opaque sealing-wax red (Fig. 6). In early medieval Celtic work enamel was used in small fields as a background to a pattern reserved in the metal of the base. This decoration was typically a sinuous design of peltas or spirals derived from the Celtic late La Tene tradition (Fig. 4). In the fifth and sixth cen turies relatively small amounts of enamel were used, mainly on dress fasteners such as pins and open-ring (penannular) brooches.12 These brooches and the distinctive 'hand-pins' had originated in fourth to fifth century Britain but were developed and manufactured in many parts of Ireland in the sixth and sev enth centuries (Fig. 5).'3 A distinctive type of dress fastener, the 'latchet', was also popular in Ireland at the same time and this was sometimes richly enamelled (Fig. 6). From the sixth century onwards many highly decorative enamelled mounts were made for hanging bowls (Figs. 7, 8). These are certainly the products of Celtic workshops but there is controversy about where they were made: the only workshop evidence comes from the Pictish heartlands in Inverness, while the majority of the sixth and seventh-century pieces have been recovered from Anglo-Saxon areas of Britain.'4 On the other hand, designs found on the bowls are closely paralleled and developed in the same way in Ireland.'" Ireland was the source of later enamelled bowls found in Scandinavian graves of the ninth and tenth centuries. Early enamelled bowl mounts are fascinating because they com bine traditional Iron Age La Tene derived curvilinear omament with both technical as well as artistic innovations. New colours of enamel were introduced and inlays of brighdy coloured millefiori glass. The finest example is an exotic import in an Anglo-Saxon burial at Sutton Hoo, Suffolk, a context datable to the 630s or earlier. Close examination reveals that Celtic craftsmen tried several techniques to fix these millefiori inlays, the sign of an innovative and an experimental period. The Sutton Hoo bowl also shows an important further develop ment because other colours of enam el were used, demonstrating the use of new opaque glasses before the 630s. Pale green and opaque blue enamels are striking additions to the repertoire (the blue is distinct from the clear, dark blue rods also used on this piece, Fig.7).'7 Gilding first appears on a disc of germanic-style interlace in the base of a hanging bowl deposited not later than the 650s (Fig. 8).18 These innovations in ornament, gilding and the use of coloured glasses herald the development of a new Insular style in metalwork which reached its zenith in Ireland with the Ardagh chalice and associ ated pieces in the eighth century. The change to the new style can be clearly seen in a group of buckles distinctively enamelled in Celtic style, but copying contempo rary germanic forms of the seventh century; two have millefiori set in broad fields of red enamel but on one opaque yellow is also used. Several others, however, use both red and yellow enamels but these are set in distinctive angular cells (Fig. 9).'9 These introduce a new style in Irish metalwork which mimics inter locking angular cells, typically cruciform, 'T' or 'L' shaped, but with a number of variants such as an angular 'S' or 'Z'. They resemble cloisonne work, where the cell walls are applied to a separate base, but they are on a coarser scale and tend to avoid the curved cell shapes of their germanic models (we have already seen how these were imitated in studs by the use of moulded glass). Technically this is still champleve enamel where the walls are part of the base plate. By the end of the seventh century there are examples of brilliant multicoloured Irish versions of foreign models, notably an enam elled buckle from Lough Gara, County Sligo (Fig. 9). By the eighth century angled cells filled with contrasting colours were widely used on metalwork whether grand or relatively humble. Green, seen on the Lough Gara buckle, remains rare, the predominant colours are red and yellow flamboyantly displayed on mounts, studs, brooches and brooch-pins. On quality pieces the main field is framed with a narrow enamelled border (Figs. 11, 14). Enamel was also used on church treasures other than chalices and patens, notably an early group of small portable shrines whose distinc tive house shape is peculiar to the Irish ecclesiastical tradition. The 'Emly' and Monymusk shrines are the most famous examples (Fig. 10). Deep blue enamel is also found on these prestige fittings, with several examples in the Blackwater hoard, discussed below. There is even a remarkable eighth-century shrine hinge with enamels and two cabo chon settings of translucent glass with surface-painted decoration.20 Nowhere else in Europe at this date is anyone producing comparable polychrome glass work. The bright colours proved attrac tive to Viking raiders in the ninth century. A hoard recovered recently from the River Blackwater was in the hands of a Scandinavian trader and included sections of fine enamelled mounts, many demonstrably from shrines in a variety of styles (Fig. 12).21 Several pieces were carefully cut, presumably destined for use as decoration on the lead weights which are relatively common survivors from this period and a vivid reminder of the trading activities associat ed with the Scandinavian raids and settlements of the ninth and tenth centuries.22 Enamelled fragments are less common than pieces of gilded bronze but common enough to suggest that enam elled metalwork was widespread in Ireland by the ninth century. If only these tantalising pieces, great and small, could be dated by context or style with more precision. There are magnificent Irish enamelled mounts found in a rich Viking burial in Norway at Oseberg, a burial now known to have taken place in the year 834, thanks to tree-ring dating (Figs.1 1,14).23 This only gives a terminus ante quem for their manufacture. Several other Irish enamels have been recovered from Viking graves in Norway, many of which may have been old before they left Ireland.24 There are already signs in the eighth century that these bright colours are going out of vogue in the great monastic workshops.
