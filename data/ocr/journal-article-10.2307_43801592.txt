In this single instance Michelangelo sets aside his dictate for originality and avails himself of the pastoral genre. This does not mean that his pastoral poem epitomizes those of his contemporaries, however. On the contrary, his iconoclastic composition not only concludes with four stanzas strongly reminiscent of the eighteenth canto of Ariosto's Orlando furioso (XVIII, 26 ff.). It is also dissimilar</page><page sequence="4">374 ROMANCE NOTES from the pastoral compositions of his contemporaries in that it is not sensual and romantic. A careful examination of the poem reveals that Michelangelo has fully exploited the common pastoral literary motif and Ariosto's personifications to formalize his own personal credos, which he has characteristically captured in nuce in the verses which read: Di fuor dentro si vede quel che hanno: pace sanza oro e sanza sete alcuna. (w. 17 and 18) Michelangelo illustrates that ideal pace sanza oro in stanzas three through eight, beginning with a portrayal of the peasant's impoverished, peaceful life in the third and fourth stanzas. As the peasant hoes in the hills, he can count his riches one by one. He has no locks on the door of his grass hut, nor is he fearful of any violence. He is avid only for a green meadow or for that grass that flowers most beautifully. Consequently, a plow is his greatest treasure, his plowshare is his honored gem, a pair of baskets is his credenza, and his hoes and pitchforks are his golden vases. The strongly contrasted imagery of utilitarian objects and useless symbols of wealth prepares Michelangelo's direct address in the fifth stanza to those to whose materialistic pursuits he has just alluded. He openly denounces these greedy collectors of treasures, gems, credenzas and golden vases as they who disusa[no ] 7 ben della natura / Cercando Vor , le terre e ' ricchi regni (wĒ 34-35). The enter- prises of these bassi ingegni are commanded by pride, while they are taught by sloth and lust, and their envy seeks the misfortune of others. Consumed by the ravages of their insaziabil foco , they are blind to their fragile and undemanding mortality, of which Michel- angelo reminds them in his sententious conclusion to the fifth stanza, 7 tempo è breve e 7 necessario è poco . Michelangelo summarily states his view to them in the final verses of the fourth stanza which read: colui che 'l mondo impera, e ch'è sì grande, ancor desidera, e non ha pace poi; e 'l villanel la gode co' suo buoi. (w. 46-48) Michelangelo then seals his statement on peace vis-à-vis the wealthy and the poor with a direct comparison of Ricchezza and Povertà , which he has personified for this purpose. Ricchezza , lav- ishly adorned by gold and gems, is nonetheless terrified and fraught with worries. She passes her day deep in thought, noting predictions and omens, and is saddened by every change in the weather. Povertà , instead, is utterly carefree in her homespun. She is lieta , flees the world to the sureness of the woods, and, in so doing, acquires ogni tesor. Not by accident this thumbnail sketch of Povertà is strongly reminiscent of the Biblical portrayal of the poor described in the Beatitudes: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." As he underscored the peace associated with the im- poverished peasant's life, Michelangelo also emphasized its godliness. Specifically, the peasant's life is free from sin. Michelangelo lays bare this fact in verses 25-38 by enumerating the sins which find no place in the peasant's humble existence. They are five of the seven mortal sins of orthodox Catholicism: envy, pride, avarice, sloth, and lust. The two remaining mortal sins, gluttony and wrath, are inferred to be absent by Michelangelo's pointed references to the peasant's acorn diet (v. 24) and to his peaceful, joyful existence (vv. 4-8, 13, 15, and 23). Michelangelo drives home his association of godliness with such a peaceful, impoverished existence in his concluding presentation of the peasant (vv. 58-73), wherein these elements are fully integrated. The peasant reduces all financial matters to the simple terms of</page><page sequence="6">376 ROMANCE NOTES grass, water, and milk. Utterly uninterested in monetary and mate- rial pursuits and peacefully bending to fortune, the peasant centers his life around God and is not tormented by doubts and uncertainties. He ceaselessly honors, loves, fears and prays God for every facet of his daily life, which is wholly ordered by the three virtues. They are specifically cited in verse 68: fede , ispeme e . . . desio. Thus Michelangelo has portrayed the peasant's life as at once free from mortal sin and possessing every virtue. Michelangelo's verses about peasants and poverty expose the reason for his strict association of poverty with godliness expressed in every aspect of his personal life and capsulized in his exchange with Pope Julius II regarding the frescoes on the vault of the Sistine Chapel. To the Pope's comment that the work should be enriched with "colori e d'oro, ch'ell'è povera," Michelangelo replied: "Padre santo, in quel tempo gli uomini non portavano addosso oro, e quelli che son dipinti non furon mai troppo ricchi, ma santi uomini, perch'eglino sprezzaron le ricchezze." 1 His shepherd, as we have seen, is one who has pace sanza oro e sanza sete alcuna. Both conceptually and syntactically Michelangelo links this sim- ple, godly life to his second, correlative ideal depicted by his peasant, namely, di fuor dentro si vede quel che ha. The poor in spirit faithfully reflects his inner nature in his humble exterior mein. In like manner, Povertà mirrors her pure, simple spirituality in her panni rozzi e bigi. In each of his anthropomorphic descriptions of Dubbio , Perché , Vero , Falso , and Adulazione Michelangelo reiterates the fuor /dentro motif underlying the preceding portion of his composition, for their inner nature is wholly reflected in every detail of his description of their outer appearance. Having discovered that the dominant thematic nucleus of the capitolo is the integrity of the inner nature and its outward expres- sion, the poem's intrinsic unity becomes clear. Its two, seemingly incompatible halves - the pastoral genre on the one hand, and the personification of inanimate qualities strongly reminiscent of those found in the eighteenth canto of the Orlando Furioso on the other hand - are now both seen to be expressions of the poetic nucleus governing the entire capitolo, i. e fuor dentro si vede quel che hanno.
