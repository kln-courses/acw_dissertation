I ..'... ?X::::%: .??:?:,;.;.,;. - . , , PERSIfl PRIlTIfnG : PROLEGOmIlnfl by ERIC SCHROEDER TAHMINAH VISITING RUSTAM Detail from a Shah Namah manuscript, Persian early Fifteenth Century T IS pleasant to extol what we like, for when we do so we pay a debt and we express ourselves. But the pleasure should put us on our guard. Our private debts are not good measures of the fund they were drawn from, and self-expression is a rather special kind of description. Although praise may properly be either lyrical or critical, the use of praise in the description of works of art should be critical, and the obligation is confessed in the critical dress which it always wears. I have never seen it observed that any painting's like a red red rose that's newly sprung in June. But all the same I have seen many poets, many parsons, and many salesmen masquerading as critics, and I have been such a hypocritic myself. Critical praise, like other exercises in the subordination of self, is extraor- dinarily difficult. After all, we are ourselves as much a party to the enjoyment of art as the work of art is, and it is not easy to peer through the blaze of pleasure and see what the work of art is really like. Lyrical praise is easy; and we have had plenty of it. It is a social evil. It substitutes amateur for professional standards, and if fol- lowed makes fools of the public and rogues of the artists. I have said that lyrical praise is a social evil. Certainly contemporary artists would be in a better predicament if their instructors used museums (by which I mean accumu- lations of the finest exotic art) as free libraries of profes- sional secrets, and if their patrons were content to use them as a bureau of standards, reserving the glow of hu- man warmth for the appreciation of contemporary art. TWENTY-EIGHT</page><page sequence="2">There are many Persian paintings in our museums. The finest of them seem to me as beautiful as anything else those buildings contain. All kinds of people are attracted by them, and some are fascinated. I suspect that color is what first attracts: real gold, real malachite, true ultra- marine, unmixed vermilion are beautiful in themselves. What fascinates us afterwards is something very complex. There is for instance, the irregular loitering interest of narrative in childish form subtly organized in a very ma- ture way, and a perfected daughtsmanship which yet has something in common with children's drawings and re- minds us of something we can no longer see ourselves. A great wealth of detail not boring when explored, a certain tensity which so permeates the whole work as, for example, to make stiff figures look stiff with excitement, all these and other qualities undoubtedly affect us when we look at Persian paintings. Lovers of flowers have special reasons for lingering before them. They have all kinds of spe- cial and general interest and deserve their prestige. Let me quote, however, some of the praise which enthusi- asm has dumped on the surface of Persian painting. I quote and think it worth while to object because it seems to me evident that if we like things for qualities they have not got we are in a fair way to miss the qualities they have. It has been said: "Frequently the mystical strain in the Per- sian mind filters into paintings where its presence is not always apprehended on the surface. But now and then we find a miniature in which the mystic mood finds complete expression. Such paintings as the lovely page in the Bodle- ian Library where a group of mystics converse in a garden, traversed by a winding stream, assure us that the life of the senses, so exquisitely aware, as the painter communi- cates to us, of the beauty of the earth and the springtime, is not as some suppose the sole sustenance and the motive power of Persian art." I cannot assert that the life of the senses is the sole suste- nance of Persian art, or indeed the sole sustenance of any- thing but debauchery and, presumably, the actions of ani- mals. But I object strenuously to the reading of mysticism into this painting. The landscape of this tranquil scene is identical with that of depictions of battle, pomp, and earthly love. Flowers, stream, and blossoming trees are drawn and arranged exactly as if Rustam were carving Zohrab. A certain polite calm which pervades the paint- ing seems to me due entirely to the expertly arranged con- ventional drawing of the quiet gentlemen whose conversa tion is its subject. Eloquence is the decorative quality of speech. It is for such a quality that we should most highly value Persian painting, on this that we might profitably expend our critical, and not our lyrical, powers. As an example of the cold causes of our warm responses, let us remark the most interesting fact about the Persian way of treating natural scenery, which has been justly praised as bewitchingly beautiful. It has only the most indirect connection with nature. The artists learned to draw flowers not from flowers themselves, but from pounces sketched by their masters. Art does not seem to have stolen a curious glance at Nature more often than once in a century. The elements of representation-the ele- phant-bodied champion, the moon-faced one of cypress stature, the iris with broken leaf, the narcissus, the rosy rock, the heaven-touching palace-all existed complete and comprehensible in the corpus of pictorial doctrine, like the notes of an extensive scale. There is a gradual evolution- ary growth and twice or thrice a remarkable personal con- tribution, but by and large this great art is the work of docile pupils. "Scholastic" may suggest dullness; "decora- tive" may suggest emptiness. If this is the case there exists a prejudice against Persian painting, for that is demonstrably a scholastic decorative art. Representation is developed as an exploration of decorative principles, not an exploration of nature. THIRTY</page><page sequence="4">Gifted men, working in the almost monastic quiet of the Timurid and Safavid libraries, practised an art of very great vitality and of great interest to us. Decoration was their hereditary skill, and their steadily viewed end. I can imagine no field so likely to yield us insight into the prin- ciples of decoration as this. Its limitations of formula are an advantage to us; the monotony of its "illustrative values" an equal advantage. If we care to know it well, we must hold fast to it in all its forms, never condemning what is primarily dec- oration as "mere decoration," nor mistaking very complex decorative compositions for vulgar lavishness. Such errors also masquerade as criticism. Indeed, lyrical praise is not so dangerous a substitute, for it is more easily recog- nized than disguised lyrical objection, or invective. Lyrical praise may wear critical dress with a better grace, but in- vective does it more natural. For example: "That delicate susceptibility to sensual im- pressions which underlies the tremulous sensibility of these decorative paintings tended no doubt always to escape from the controlling topic of the main design. The appeal of more lavish exuberance of decorative detail was bound to awaken echoes in the less refined public and to tempt the artist ever further along this path. The love of luxury as such, of the elaborate, ingenious, and highly-finished article, the love of what proclaims aloud the cost of its production, is too widely spread in any complex and sophis- ticated civilization not to clamor for satisfaction. And this claim, though it distorts and falsifies the artist's sensibility, yet since it flatters his vanity as a craftsman is only too readily met." All that sounds very like criticism. But it is really a proclamation of general "truths" startlingly at variance with truth. The luxurious, elaborate, and highly-finished style in Persian painting covers the whole period from the Demotte Shahnama to Tahmasp's great Nizami in the Brit- ish Museum, including practically every surviving classic. The most sensitive drawing occurs only in the most lux- urious and elaborate manuscripts and its decline in the later Safavid period runs at equal speed side by side with the decline in elaboration and expense. Only the most resolute determination that art is always better the older it is could prefer thirteenth to fifteenth century painting. That determination may have moral grandeur or even a wistful charm, but it has no use in criticism. The "tremu- lous sensibility" with which Iranian painting is credited is perhaps the critic's own; I cannot see its use in the cold, fine and deliberate calculation of the finest Persian work- manship. I have used up my space in what is only a flourish before what I may be granted occasion to say later. But if one's mouth is full of dust, one spits it out before speaking, and in approaching a completely exotic art a few vigorous kicks may help to free one from an entangling complacency.
