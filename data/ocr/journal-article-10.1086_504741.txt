      <p>
         <bold>Rima D. Apple,</bold> Professor in the School of Human Ecology and the Women’s Studies Program, University of Wisconsin–Madison, has recently completed <italic>Perfect Motherhood: Science and Childrearing in America</italic> (Rutgers University Press, forthcoming). Her current research examines an innovative rural public health program in Wisconsin during the interwar years.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Barry Barnes</bold> is Professor of Sociology and Codirector of the ESRC Centre for Genomics in Society at Exeter University. He has published extensively both on the sociology of science and technology and on the basic theoretical problems of the social sciences. In 1998 he accepted the J. B. Bernal Prize of the Society for Social Studies of Science for his career achievement in that field.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Richard Bellon</bold> teaches history of science in the Lyman Briggs School of Science at Michigan State University. His research focuses on the social and cultural role of natural history in Victorian Britain. He is now exploring the influence of Darwin’s botany in the debates over evolution in the 1860s.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Joan Lisa Bromberg</bold> is a visiting professor at Johns Hopkins University. Her most recent works include <italic>NASA and the Space Industry</italic> (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2000) and <italic>The Laser in America, 1950–1970</italic> (MIT Press, 1991).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Kenneth Caneva</bold> teaches the history of science at the University of North Carolina at Greensboro. Most of his work has dealt with nineteenth‐century physics.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Michael Chazan</bold> is Associate Professor of Anthropology at the University of Toronto. He is a Paleolithic archaeologist with field projects in Israel, Jordan, and South Africa. He has written on the early history of Paleolithic archaeology, with particular emphasis on the development of chronology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Frederick B. Churchill</bold> is Professor Emeritus of the History and Philosophy of Science at Indiana University. He is coeditor, with Helmut Risler, of <italic>August Weismann: Ausgewaehlte Briefe und Dokumente</italic> (2 vols. [1999]) and the author of many essays on the history of heredity, development, and evolution theories in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Eileen Crist</bold> teaches in the Department of Science and Technology in Society, at Virginia Tech, in the areas of life sciences, technology, and society, as well as environmental science and ethics. Her research is on behavioral science and environmental issues. She is the author of <italic>Images of Animals: Anthropomorphism and Animal Mind</italic> (Temple University Press, 2000) and coeditor of <italic>Scientists Debate Gaia: The Next Century</italic> (MIT Press, 2004).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Michael J. Crowe</bold> is Cavanaugh Professor Emeritus in the Program of Liberal Studies and the Graduate Program in History and Philosophy of Science at the University of Notre Dame. His <italic>Mechanics from Aristotle to Einstein</italic> will be published by Green Lion Press in 2006. He is also preparing for publication <italic>The Extraterrestrial Life Debate—Antiquity to 1915: A Source Book.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Regna Darnell</bold> is Distinguished University Professor of Anthropology and First Nations Studies at the University of Western Ontario. Her publications in the history of Americanist anthropology and linguistics include <italic>Invisible Genealogies</italic> (2001), <italic>And Along Came Boas</italic> (2000), and <italic>Edward Sapir: Linguist, Anthropologist, Humanist</italic> (1990). She edits the Critical Studies in History of Anthropology series and <italic>Histories of Anthropology Annual</italic> (Nebraska).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>James Delbourgo</bold> (History, McGill University) is the author of a forthcoming cultural history of electricity in the American Enlightenment and co‐organizer of a symposium at UCLA entitled “Atlantic Knowledges: The Sciences and the Early Modern Atlantic World.” His research focuses on science, travel, and empire in the British Atlantic.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Michael Dettelbach,</bold> an independent scholar in Boston, works on natural philosophy and natural history in France and Germany in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. He has published extensively on Alexander von Humboldt and is now writing on the explorer as a figure in science, religion, philosophy, and literature.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ted Everson</bold> is a Ph.D. candidate with the University of Toronto Institute for the History and Philosophy of Science and Technology and Program Manager for Biotechnology Studies at the Chemical Heritage Foundation in Philadelphia. His interests include genetic concepts of health and illness and the political economy of biotechnology.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Rivka Feldhay</bold> is Professor of History of Science and Ideas at Tel Aviv University and Lecturer at the Van Leer Institute. She has published widely in the fields of history and philosophy of science, intellectual history, and cultural studies.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Aryeh Finkelberg</bold> is a classicist working in the field of Presocratic philosophy. He holds a doctorate in philosophy from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. He teaches ancient Greek philosophy in the philosophy department at Tel Aviv University.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Maurice A. Finocchiaro</bold> is Distinguished Professor of Philosophy Emeritus at the University of Nevada, Las Vegas. His latest book, <italic>Retrying Galileo, 1633–1992</italic> (Berkeley: University of California Press, 2005), is a survey of the Galileo affair from the time of his condemnation to his partial rehabilitation by Pope John Paul II. He is now working on a synthetic account that would provide both an interpretive understanding and a critical assessment of Galileo’s original trial (1613–1633) and the subsequent Galileo affair (1633–1992).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Thomas F. Glick</bold> is Professor of History at Boston University and the author or editor of books and articles on the comparative history of Darwinism in the Latin world.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Edward Grant</bold> is Distinguished Professor Emeritus in the Department of History and Philosophy of Science, Indiana University. He has written extensively on medieval science, natural philosophy, and science and religion.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David Hahm</bold> is Professor and Chair of Greek and Latin at Ohio State University. He specializes in ancient philosophy and intellectual history, and he is now working on a book on the conceptual foundations of Greek cosmology and physical philosophy.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ellen Herman</bold> is Associate Professor of History at the University of Oregon and the author of <italic>The Romance of American Psychology.</italic> She is interested in the cultural and political history of the modern human sciences and is the creator of the Adoption History Project: uoregon.edu/~adoption.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Nick Hopwood</bold> is Senior Lecturer in the Department of History and Philosophy of Science, University of Cambridge, where he coordinates the history of medicine program and won a 2006 Pilkington teaching prize. He authored <italic>Embryos in Wax</italic> (2002) and co‐edited <italic>Models: The Third Dimension of Science</italic> (2004).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Derek Jensen</bold> is the Curator of the European Book Collections in the L. Tom Perry Special Collections at Brigham Young University, with responsibility for the history of science, early printing, Renaissance, and Reformation collections. His research interests include the study of sixteenth‐ and seventeenth‐century astronomy in cultural contexts.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Stephen Johnston</bold> is Assistant Keeper at the Museum of the History of Science, University of Oxford. His interests center on the histories of practical mathematics and scientific instruments, with a particular focus on Elizabethan England; see <ext-link ext-link-type="uri"
                   xlink:href="http://www.mhs.ox.ac.uk/staff/saj/"
                   xlink:type="simple">http://www.mhs.ox.ac.uk/staff/saj/</ext-link>.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David Kaiser</bold> is an associate professor in MIT’s Program in Science, Technology, and Society and a lecturer in MIT’s Department of Physics. He is the author of <italic>Drawing Theories Apart: The Dispersion of Feynman Diagrams in Postwar Physics</italic> (University of Chicago Press, 2005) and the editor of <italic>Pedagogy and the Practice of Science: Historical and Contemporary Perspectives</italic> (MIT Press, 2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Ari Kelman</bold> is an associate professor of history at the University of California, Davis. He is the author of <italic>A River and Its City: The Nature of Landscape in New Orleans,</italic> which won the Vernacular Architecture Forum’s Abbott Lowell Cummings Prize in 2004.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Frank Klaassen</bold> is Assistant Professor of History at the University of Saskatchewan. His recent publications include “Ritual Magic in the Renaissance” (<italic>Aries,</italic> 2003), “Medieval Manuscripts of Dream Provocation” (<italic>Esoterica,</italic> forthcoming), and “Learning and Masculinity in Manuscripts of Ritual Magic of the Later Middle Ages and Renaissance” (<italic>Sixteenth Century Journal,</italic> forthcoming). He currently holds a three‐year Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council grant for his investigation of late medieval and Renaissance learned magic.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Simon J. Knell</bold> is Professor of Museum Studies at the University of Leicester. His book <italic>The Culture of English Geology, 1815–1851: A Science Revealed through Its Collecting</italic> (Ashgate, 2000) was based on a detailed study of John Phillips’s geological work.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>David Knight</bold> read chemistry at Oxford and has taught history of science at Durham University since 1964. His books include <italic>Ideas in Chemistry: A History of the Science</italic> (1992), <italic>Humphry Davy: Science and Power</italic> (2nd ed., 1998), and <italic>Science and Spirituality: The Volatile Connection</italic> (2004). In 1994–1996 he was President of the British Society for the History of Science. In 2003 he received the Edelstein Award for History of Chemistry from the American Chemical Society.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Nikolai Krementsov</bold> is Associate Professor at the Institute for the History and Philosophy of Science and Technology at the University of Toronto. He is the author of <italic>Stalinist Science, The Cure,</italic> and, most recently, <italic>International Science between the World Wars: The Case of Genetics.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Sheldon Krimsky</bold> is Professor of Urban and Environmental Policy and Planning at Tufts University and Adjunct Professor in the Department of Public Health and Family Medicine at the Tufts Medical School. He has recently published <italic>Science in the Private Interest</italic> and the coedited volume <italic>Rights and Liberties in the Biotech Age.</italic>
      </p>
      <p>
         <bold>Henrik Lagerlund,</bold> who specializes in medieval and early modern philosophy, is in the Department of Philosophy at the University of Western Ontario.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Christopher Lawrence</bold> is Professor of the History of Medicine at the Wellcome Trust Centre for the History of Medicine at University College London. He is the author of <italic>Medicine in the Making of Modern Britain</italic> (Routledge, 1994).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Mary Lindemann</bold> is Professor of History at the University of Miami, Coral Gables. Her publications on early modern medicine include <italic>Health and Healing in Early Modern Germany</italic> (Johns Hopkins University Press, 1996) and <italic>Medicine and Society in Early Modern Europe</italic> (Cambridge University Press, 1999). She is now writing a study of the medical and biological determinants of civic competency in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. In addition, she has published general works on German and European history in the eighteenth century: <italic>Patriots and Paupers: Hamburg, 1712–1830</italic> (Oxford University Press, 1990), and <italic>Liaisons dangereuses: Sex, Law, and Diplomacy in the Age of Frederick the Great</italic> (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2006).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Frank Linhard</bold> teaches history of science at Frankfurt University. He does research on concepts of space in Newton and Leibniz at the university’s Centre for Studies in Early Modern History, Culture, and Science and is the author of <italic>Historische Elemente einer Prinzipienphysik</italic> (Olms, 2000) and several articles on the history of physics and natural philosophy.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jonathan Marks</bold> is Professor of Biological Anthropology at the University of North Carolina at Charlotte and the author of <italic>What It Means to Be 98% Chimpanzee</italic> (University of California Press, 2002).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Robert Mayhew</bold> is Professor of Philosophy at Seton Hall University. He is the author of <italic>Aristotle’s Criticism of Plato’s</italic> Republic and <italic>The Female in Aristotle’s Biology: Reason or Rationalization,</italic> and he is now preparing a translation, with commentary, of Plato <italic>Laws</italic> 10.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Richard McKirahan</bold> is E. C. Norton Professor of Classics and Philosophy at Pomona College, Claremont, California, where he contributes to the Program in Science, Technology, and Society. His scholarly interests include ancient philosophy (especially the Presocratic philosophers and Aristotle), science, and mathematics.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Suzanne Moon</bold> is an assistant professor in the Division of Liberal Arts and International Studies at the Colorado School of Mines, where she teaches courses in science and technology studies and environmental history. She is completing a manuscript on the politics of technological development in colonial Indonesia.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jill G. Morawski</bold> is Professor of Psychology at Wesleyan University, where she is also a member of the Science and Society Program and Women’s Studies. She has written on the history of social psychology and experimental methods; her current projects include a history of the concept of socialization and an analysis of psychologists’ understandings of their experimental presence.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Greg Myers</bold> is Senior Lecturer in Linguistics and English Language at Lancaster University. His most recent book is <italic>Matters of Opinon: Talking about Public Issues</italic> (Cambridge University Press, 2004).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Gerard Naddaf</bold> is Associate Professor and Chair of the Department of Philosophy at York University. He is a specialist in ancient Greek philosophy, particularly the Presocratics and Plato. His current research is on theories of myth, ideas in progress, and the Greek concept of human nature.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Mary Jo Nye</bold> is Horning Professor in the Humanities and Professor of History at Oregon State University. Her research interests include the history of chemistry and physics, the social and cultural history of science, laboratory science, and the relationship between theory and evidence. Her most recent book is <italic>Blackett: Physics, War, and Politics in the Twentieth Century</italic> (Harvard University Press, 2004).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Thomas Oberdan</bold> received his Ph.D. from Indiana University in History and Philosophy of Science after studying mathematics and philosophy. He is Associate Professor of Science and Technology in Society at Clemson University.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Cornelius O’Boyle</bold> is Associate Director of the University of Notre Dame’s London Program. His main area of research is science and medicine in the late medieval period. His recent publications focus on the interactions between medicine, astrology, and magic in fourteenth‐ and fifteenth‐century England.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John W. O’Malley</bold> is a specialist in the history of the Jesuits. His <italic>The First Jesuits</italic> (Harvard, 1993) won the Jacques Barzun Prize for Cultural History. He is also the principal editor of two large collections, <italic>The Je</italic>
         <italic>suits: Cultures, Sciences, and the Arts, 1540–1773</italic> (Toronto, 1999), and the sequel with the same title (Toronto, 2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Nancy J. Parezo</bold> is a professor of American Indian Studies and Anthropology at the University of Arizona. She has penned several works on the history of anthropology, including <italic>Hidden Scholars.</italic> Her most recent book, <italic>Anthropology Goes to the Fair: The 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition,</italic> coauthored with Don D. Fowler, is being published by the University of Nebraska Press.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>John J. Paul</bold> is Professor of History at Fitchburg State College in Massachusetts. He is now researching the development of the modern medical profession in southern India, where both the British colonial government and American medical missionaries had established health care institutions of national and international reputation.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Sharrona Pearl</bold> is a lecturer in the Committee on Degrees in History and Literature at Harvard University. She has recently completed her dissertation, entitled “As Plain as the Nose on Your Face: Physiognomy in Nineteenth‐Century England.” Her interests include the relationship between science and cultural entertainments and the construction and perception of physical beauty in the Victorian era.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Marco Piccolino</bold> is Professor of General Physiology in the Biology Department at the University of Ferrara. His primary historical interests are the scientific revolution in life sciences and the beginnings of electrophysiology, specifically the work of scientists like Marcello Malpighi, Luigi Galvani, Alessandro Volta, Lazzaro Spallanzani, and John Walsh.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Theodore M. Porter</bold> is Professor of History of Science in the UCLA Department of History. His most recent books are <italic>The Cambridge of Science,</italic> Volume 7: <italic>Modern Social Sciences,</italic> coedited with Dorothy Ross (2003), and <italic>Karl Pearson: The Scientific Life in a Statistical Age</italic> (2004). He would like next to write a history of the scientist.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Carsten Reinhardt</bold> is Associate Professor of History of Science at the University of Regensburg. He has written extensively on the history of chemistry, and his current work investigates the history of physical instrumentation in the chemical sciences.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Joan Richards</bold> teaches the history of science as a professor in the Department of History at Brown University. Her first book—<italic>Mathematical Visions: Non‐</italic>
         <italic>Euclidean Geometry in Victorian England</italic>—focused on the reception of a geometrical theory in the wider culture of nineteenth‐century England. Her second book—<italic>Angles of Reflection</italic>—was at once a memoir and an exploration of the logical work and family life of Augustus De Morgan. She is currently writing a two‐generational family history focusing on changing views of rationality in the Frend/De Morgan family. All of these projects—as well as her many mathematical historical articles—are linked by an abiding interest in the ways that mathematics has served as a model of thinking that has developed in interaction with other approaches to the human mind, be they psychological, spiritual, physical, or even phrenological.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Aldemaro Romero</bold> is Chair and Professor of Biology at Arkansas State University, where he teaches history of biological ideas. He has published nearly 450 articles and books, some on them on the history of science in Latin America.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Michael Ruse</bold> is Lucyle T. Werkmeister Professor of Philosophy at the Florida State University. His most recent book is <italic>The Evolution–Creation Struggle,</italic> published by Harvard University Press.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>José M. Sánchez‐Ron</bold> is Professor of History of Science at the Universidad Autónoma de Madrid. He has worked on the reception of Einstein’s relativity in Great Britain and the history of science in Spain. He has just published two books: <italic>Cincuenta años de cultura e investigación en España: La Fundación Juan March (1955–2005)</italic> (2005) and <italic>El canon científico</italic> (2005).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Jan‐Melissa Schramm</bold> is a lawyer and a Fellow of Trinity Hall, in the University of Cambridge, where she teaches primarily nineteenth‐century fiction. Her book <italic>Testimony and Advocacy in Victorian Law, Literature, and Theology</italic> appeared with Cambridge University Press in 2000.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Linda Simon</bold> is Professor of English at Skidmore College. Her most recent books are <italic>Dark Light: Electricity and Anxiety from the Telegraph to the X‐ray</italic> (Harcourt, 2004; Harvest, 2005) and <italic>Genuine Reality: A Life of William James</italic> (Harcourt, 1998; University of Chicago Press, 1999).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>E. C. Spary</bold> is the author of <italic>Utopia’s Garden: French Natural History from Old Regime to Revolution</italic> (University of Chicago Press, 2000) and the joint editor of <italic>Cultures of Natural History</italic> (Cambridge University Press, 1996). She is now working on a history of food and the sciences in eighteenth‐century France.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Mary Terrall</bold> is an associate professor in the history department at UCLA. She has written extensively on history of science and gender and science. Her most recent book was <italic>The Man Who Flattened the Earth: Maupertuis and the Sciences in the Enlightenment</italic> (MIT Press, 2002).</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Nancy Tuana</bold> is DuPont/Class of 1949 Professor of Philosophy and Director of the Rock Ethics Institute at the Pennsylvania State University. She is the author of books and articles in the field of feminist science studies.</p>
      <p>
         <bold>Geoffrey Tweedale</bold> is Reader in Business History at Manchester Metropolitan University Business School. He has published on the history of science and technology (including the pharmaceutical industry) and is now researching the history of occupational health issues. He is the author of <italic>Magic Mineral to Killer Dust</italic> (Oxford University Press, 2001).</p>
