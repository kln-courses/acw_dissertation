<plain_text><page sequence="1">Besprechungen / Reviews 371 Ernst Osterkamp: Lucifer. Stationen eines Motivs. (Komparatistische Studien. Beihefte zur «arcadia», IX.) Berlin/New York: De Gruyter, 1979. 262 pages. The chapter titles tell about the arrangement and the critical stance adopted in this book: Einleitung: Zum motivgeschichtlichen Verfahren; Jacques Auguste de Thou (Thuanus): Parabata vinctus, sive Triumphus Christi, tragoedia: Zur historischen Gestalt des Bösen; Der Wandel der Lucifer-Gestalt im 16. und 17. Jahrhundert: Psychologisierung und neuzeitliche Subjektivitätsproblematik; Joost van der Von del: Lucifer. Treurspel: Der Himmel als Staat; Friedrich Gottlieb Klopstock: Der Messias: Der Teufel in der Aufklärung: Vernunftgebot und Entdämonisierung; Lord Byron: Cain: Die Rebellion des Aristokraten; Lucifergestaltungen der wilhelmini schen Ära: Der Lichtbringer im Prozeß der Zerstörung der Vernunft. Thoroughly oriented toward the cultural - read «social», «political», «religious», «philosophical» - backdrop against which each of the judiciously selected pieces is scrutinized, the argument is stimulating throughout. Though the first two chapters are as informative as those that follow, their thrust is of an introductory nature, and the author truly hits his stride starting with the Vondel chapter. The latter comes with a lengthy discussion of Grotius' oeuvre and hinges on the ably argued view that in Lucifer «gestaltet sich das kollektive Schicksal einer ganzen Klasse: der Sturz der Feudalaristokratie in die welthistorische Bedeutungslosigkeit», and that the bour geoisie welcomes a new order. The Klopstock chapter has a long discourse on Milton's Paradise Lost before it scrutinizes Der Messias, in which the power of reason leads to «Selbsterlösung» in the case of Abbadona, but is inadequate with the figure of Satan - «die (historisch verankerten) Widersprüche warten abseits der Vernunft unerledigt auf ihre Chance zu neuem Aufbruch». With a detour via William Blake, the following chapter deals with Byron's Cain. There is a tendency to read the poet into his poem here; that's an easy thing to do, given the author's focus on the protagonist's penchant for striking an unholy alliance between that which is a-social and that which partakes of anarchy, between Satanism and historical pessimism. There is also mention of Anatole France, a phony rebel whose protests do not aim at bringing about improvements in the society he castigates. Baudelaire emerges as being equally egocentric. The last chapter shows the Lucifer figure, fully anthropomorphic now, at what must be its nadir. Society is no longer to be redeemed (as it is with Vondel, Klopstock, even with Byron), but has now become the protagonist's enemy. Relegated to the sphere of minor literary monuments, this protagonist licks the wounds inflicted on him by his own inadequacy and inferiority in the face of society's standards, begins to alter the algebraic signs - he himself is adequate, more than adequate, but society is wanting, evil, doomed - and eventually will learn the goosestep. This book, I hope, will have many readers; it is rich in (occasionally narrow focused) insights. Ohio State University Hugo Bekker Hugo Bekker</page></plain_text>
