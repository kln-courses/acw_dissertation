Folan have benefitted from the participation and support of the State of Campeche (CORACEC; Programa ción de Desarrollo Rural); the Instituto Nacional de Antro pología e Historia; Estudios Superiores and the Subsecretaría de Cultura of the Secretaría de Educación Publica; as well as the technical assistance of the local délégation of the Secre taría de Programación y Presupuesto. These preliminary acti vities have made it possible for us to install ourselves in Ca lakmul while determining the size and composition of this major urban center as well as making certain advance relating to its architectural styles, ceramics, lithics, epigraphy, physi cal anthropology, paleohydrology, mining and chultunes (Folan and Piña Chan 1982, 1983; Folan 1982, 1985a, b, Fo lan and May Hau 1984). As of this date, Flannery (1972) and Marcus' (1976) model of Calakmul as the capital of a regional State seems as reasonable as ever. In reality, survey work by Abel Morales Lopez (1984) on the trajectory of the Conhuas-Calakmul highway would seem to indicate that Northern Peten architectural style as well as ceramics extend north of the territory thought to form this state. Based on our map of Calakmul (May Hau 1985), we can now state that this major urban center is organized concentrically with the greater part of its architectural units distributed along the edge of a very large, long akalche (Folan and May Hau 1984; Fletcher 1985, letter). Limited excavations indicate that Calakmul underwent several developmental stages perhaps extending from Middle Preclassic (John Clark 1984, personal observation) up to Ter minal Classic times with a considerable amount of evidence pointing to a Postclassic occupation associated mainly with anthropomorphic censers of the type often times linked with those of Mayapan. Although not yet demonstrated, it would appear that at least some Rio Bec architecture postdates that of the Northern Peten (Folan 1985b) in the same manner that Rio Bec seems to antedate Chenes and Puuc forms farther north (Andrews and Gendrop 1985). For example, Structure 3 in Calakmul (Alvarez 1985; Armijo 1985) locally referred to as the Lundell Building, closely resembles Structure IV in Be can (Ruppert and Denison 1943), apparently built after . Structure 3's center room vault collapsed to from a central, ^ raised patio (Folan 1985a) (Fig. 1). In this manner, the différence between Peten and Rio Bec styles may be due to chronological rather than only the linguistic différences suggested by Kaufman (1964). Another thought that has come to mind is that Sylvanus G. Morley's political and hydraulic interprétations of the Maya Lowlands should not be completely abandoned (see also Folan 1980). It would seem that they still reflect more than a bit of past reality (see also Potter 1977:91). Another point of comparison is the zxroyo/akalche/cana\ System encircling the core area of Calakmul and the dépression surrounding Becan. Another similar hydraulic feature is associated with the Late Preclassic site of Cerritos in Belize (Scarborough 1983). Some of the more spectacular finds made in Calakmul in ciude a crypt associated with Structure 7 (Gallegos and Domínguez 1984; Lagunas 1985a, b). This crypt served as the depository of a 25-30 year old maie individual interred with several plates and vases as well as some 2,000 pièces of jade. These include a mask formed of jade mosaics, obsidian pupils and pyrite eyebrows, elabórate ear Ornaments, a large pectoral in the form of a "T" or Ik glyph, a large jade ring and nu merous beads forming various types of arm and neck Or naments. Fronting the crypt were four pedernal eccentrics, more than 50 obsidian needles and a chocolate colored peder nal blade. ( Generally speaking, pedernal is readily availablein the région and was worked into blades, points, bifaces and other forms. John Clark (1984, personal communication) identified the considerable amount of obsidian recorded during excavation as Coming from many of the major Guatemalan and Mexican Highland sources. Needless to say, Calakmul has lived up to ail our expectations while surpassing others. It truly represents a major urban cen ter in every sense of the word with possibilities for testing ma jor models developed toward a better, more complete under standing of Mesoamerica, including climatic change. Zusammenfassung: Calakmul, Campeche, Mexiko. Die vom "Centro de Investigacicnes Históricas y Sociales" der "Universidad Autó noma del Sudeste", Campeche, in jüngster Zeit in der May a-Metropole Calakmul in Süd-Campeche durchgeführten archäologischen Arbeiten konzentrierten sich im wesentlichen auf die Bestimmung der räumlichen Ausdehnung und architek tonischen Zusammensetzung dieser Stadt, wobei eine detaillierte Karte erstellt wurde. Bei Ausgrabungen wurden einige Entwicklungsstadien erkannt, die von der Mittleren Präklassik bis zum Ende der Spätklassik reichen. Keramische Funde weisen auf eine Besiedlungin der Postklassik hin. In einer mit Bauwerk 7 assoziier ten Krypta wurde ein interessanter Grabfund gemacht, der ein Skelett, einige Keramikgefäße, Steingegenstände, über 50 Ob si dian-Nadeln und über 2.000Jade Stücke enthielt. Resumen: Calakmul, Campeche, México. Los trabajos arqueológicos efectuados últimamente por el Centro de Investigaciones Históricas y Sociales de la Universidad Autónoma del Sudeste, Campeche, en el cen tro maya de Calakmul en el sur de Campeche se concentraron esencialmente en la de terminación de la extensión y la composición arquitectónica de este ciudad, siendo elaborado un mapa detallado. Durante las excavacionesfueron reconocidas algunas fases que abarcan del Preclásico Medio hasta el final del Clásico Tardío. Hallazgos de cerámica indican un poblamiento durante el Postclásico. En una tumba asociada con el Edificio 7 se logró hacer un descubrimiento interesante. Contenía un esqueleto, varias vasijas de cerámica, objetos de piedra, más de 50 agujas de obsidianay más de 2000 objetos de jade.</page></plain_text>
