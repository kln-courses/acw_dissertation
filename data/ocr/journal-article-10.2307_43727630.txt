The editor and the coeditors have also contributed essays to this collection. Let a few words about these four contributions suffice, since the others are far too numerous for each to receive comment here. Witte wants to know what the in in Job 31:35 really is. Ezekiel 9:3-6, which has the word twice (in vv. 4 and 6), leads him to see it somewhat generically and tentatively as a word designating some sign of belonging to Yhwh. Other texts, in connection with archae- ological discoveries, lead Witte to conclude specifically that the m of Job 3 1 :35 is an early sort of phylactery containing biblical passages. He continues by showing how this inter- pretation of the in in Job 31:35 fits the close and distant contexts of the entire book. His references to texts and artifacts are somewhat beside the point at times, but his knowledge of Near Eastern texts and archaeology is impressive, as is his critical way of dealing with them. Barton's essay is a study in biblical theology. In Christian theology, God is immutable and so he does not forget, but Barton calls our attention to the fact that in the OT God is often asked not to remember malfeasance. As human beings, we remember our sinful past even when it has been forgiven, but God is asked not to remember it, and in fact God does not remember it. God "forgets" it, thereby changing God's mind. There is some inspired anthropomorphism here, but in the idea of God's change of mind and forgetting, there is also a notion of God's greatness. Kratz has as his point of departure the concept of God as king not only of Israel but of all the world, a concept evident in Josephus Contra Apionem 2 and in Psalm 145 (in the masoretic numbering) but not evident elsewhere in the Psalter. The first part of his study has to do with the text of Psalm 145 itself; the second part is on the function of Psalm 145 in the genesis of the Psalter; and the third part is a comparison of Psalm 145 with other pas- sages in the OT and in Jewish works. At the end of this third part, and of the entire study, we learn that the designation of Psalm 145 as the " Schemac des Buchs der Psalmen" is due ultimately to S. D. Goitein. One senses a certain lack of unity in this essay, but it is good to know what Kratz thinks (and does not think) about Psalm 145, about the development of the Psalter, and about the thought and piety found in the later books of the OT or in other Jewish works. Seow stays close to the difficult text of the Book of Job as he asks once again who the b&amp;j in Job 19:25 is.
