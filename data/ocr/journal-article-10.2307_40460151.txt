The First Wave of Eurasian Steppe Pastoralists into Copper Age Europe (Mari ja GiMBUTAs) . - The first wave of pastoralists from the Eurasian steppe infiltrated Europe at c. 4400-4300 B. C. The ensuing repercussions were the very disintegration of the Kara- novo VI civilization and the coexistence of Kurgan I and Cucuteni A (Tripolye Bj) complexes. By virtue of its widespread settlements on high terraces, the Cucutenian civilization was able to defend itself from incoming steppe elements. Subsequent Cucu- teni AB and Β phases show scant traces of Kurgan influences. This long period of coexis- tence ended ca. 3500-3400 B. C. when the Cucutenians appear to have been overwhelmed</page><page sequence="3">916 Miscellanea Anthropos 74. 1979 by more intensive Kurgan pressures of Wave No. 2. Elsewhere, the martial superiority of the steppe people of Wave No. 1 was disastrous. The Karanovo civilization and its branches in the lower Danube region were subjugated and scattered. The same held true for the Vinca, Butmir, and Petreçti cultures in the central Balkans and Transylvania. It is hypothesized that the dislocated Old European population pushed to the west and north. Vinca traditions reemerged in western Hungary. Lengyel cultural elements vanished in western Hungary and lower Austria; their reappearance in caves and islands of the upper Danube basin and regions as far north as Poland reflects a ceaseless flight throughout the fourth millennium B. C. The grasslands of eastern Hungary were ideally suited to pastoralism carried out by the „Kurganized" Tiszapolgar group. - The culture process in Europe during the fourth millennium B. C. represents drastic changes in ideology and socio-economic patterns. This transformation was initiated by pastoralists of the Eurasian steppe whose success lay in their extraordinary mobility and advantage of superior weaponry. The stratified, patrilinear social structure of these small, agnati- cally-linked herding units were superimposed over the egalitarian substrate of Old Euro- pean agriculturalists. The shift from large stock breeding was only one symptom of the Kurganization process. The steppe people brought a new ideology based on male deities of the sky, war, and hunt. Solar signs, weapon-carrying gods, and ritual roles of the horse, boar and dog replaced Old European symbols connected with the mother creator and lunar symbolism. This is particularly noticeable in the appearance of elaborate, lavishly appointed chieftain tombs that serve as archaeological markers for the path which Wave No. 1 cut across Europe. Repercussions that followed in its wake were also felt in the upper Danube region and on the north European plain where the Funnel Necked Beaker (the Trichterbecher or TRB) complex confronted a Kurganized Rossen-Lengyel group. At the close of the fourth millennium B. C, the Globular Amphora culture of the north European plain epitomized a male-dominated society. Its later development was the Corded Pottery/Battle-axe culture, considered to be an Indo-European culture. (The Journal of Indo-European Studies [Washington] 5. 1977: 277-338.) On the Alleged Diffusion of Hindu Divine Symbols into Pre-Columbian Mesoamerica: A Critique (Balaji Mundkur). - Parallels between certain religious symbols in Hindu and Mesoamerican cultures have been drawn by several modern writers and adduced as evidence suggestive of trans-Pacific diffusion of Hindu influences dating to about the mid-1 st millennium A. D. and later. The symbols concerned are those of the deities of the lunar asterisms and the planets - the naksatra and navagrahãh series, respectively, of the Hindus - as compared with the structural features and symbols of the 20-day period in the 260-day Tzolkin/Tonalpohualli sacred calendar of Mesoamerica; a series of nine lunar hieroglyphs of the Maya; and certain groups of deities of the Aztecs and the Zapo- tecs. These comparisons seem feeble not only because they are superficial and intrinsically contradictory, but also because they are opposed by a vast body of variations in Hindu religious symbolism. Furthermore, they are chronologically incompatible with historical events. The arguments against these comparisons of imagined parallels have broader implications concerning the totally independent development of astronomical-astrological beliefs among pre-Columbian societies isolated from the Old World, and they illustrate the hazards of utilizing a veneer of religious data that would obscure this independence. (Current Anthropology [Chicago] 19. 1978: 541-583.) C'est l'époque de l'entrée en force des tribus et de leur installation. La division fondamentale 'Adnãn-Qahtãn se retrouve dans les groupements qui se sont formés dans les aires de stationnement: à partir de la charnière naturelle constituée par le Nil, on voit, en gros, le domaine Qahtân s'étaler à l'est du fleuve, tandis que les territoires peuplés de 'Adnãns et de Berbères se trouvent à l'ouest du Nil. La défense de pratiquer l'agriculture qui avait été faite aux tribus de la conquête restait en vigueur pendant un siècle environ; ce n'est que plus tard que les Bédouins se mirent à cultiver le sol, en mettant en valeur des espaces qui étaient restés inutilisés. Les Califes Ummayyades leur accordèrent, à cet effet, une aide financière; les Califes Abbassides, au contraire, exigeaient un impôt terrien, dont les Bédouins refusaient le payement, ce qui causa des troubles. Sous les Califes Fatimides, la situation des Bédouins était assez favorisée; pareillement sous les Sultans Ayyubides, après une résistance initiale contre les nouveaux maîtres, causée par les sentiments pro-fatimides des Bédouins, les relations devinrent normales. L'époque Ayyubide, pour les Bédouins, est donc une période de transition, certes moins glorieuse pour eux que le règne des Fatimides, mais une période qui n'est pas fondamentalement différente de la précédente. La coupure se situe exactement au moment où les Mamluks prennent le pouvoir en Egypte. L'éthique bédouine avait permis l'acceptation des Ayyu- bides, étrangers certes, mais hommes libres. Cette même éthique rendait impossible la subordination des tribus aux Mamluks, même si ces esclaves, les Mamluks, étaient les maîtres de l'Egypte. L'affrontement était inévitable. Les Mamluks écrasèrent les Bédouins, physiquement, par des massacres impitoyables, administrativement, par des mesures propres à asservir les Bédouins. Les Mamluks voyaient dans les Bédouins un corps étranger à l'Egypte - étranger autant qu'ils l'étaient eux-mêmes -, mais nantis de ces deux privilèges, d'avoir une filiation généalogique arabe bien établie, et d'être des hommes libres et même nobles. Moralement enfin, les Mamluks sont responsables d'une certaine image du Bédouin, où n'apparaissent que des caractères négatifs ou peu flatteurs. Mais à la lumière de l'histoire, il n'est plus guère possible d'accepter en bloc cette caricature. La politique des Mamluks envers les Bédouins eut une conséquence qu'ils n'avaient certainement pas prévue: les Bédouins se sédentarisèrent, au moins une grande partie d'entre eux. Ils sont devenus, en se sédentarisant, d'authentiques fellahs d'Egypte, ils ont de ce fait arabisé le pays, en lui donnant une autre langue; l'arabe a définitivement remplacé le copte comme langue parlée. Paris 1975. Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique. Prix: 65 F.) Berceuses mongo (G. Hulstaert). - Chez les Mongo, des berceuses sont bien connues et couramment en usage. A côté d'inventions personnelles il existe un certain nombre de berceuses stéréotypées, plus ou moins répandues dans les diverses tribus. Les berceuses présentées ici sont groupées en deux séries: la première donne celles qui servent pour les enfants ordinaires; celles de la seconde série servent uniquement pour les jumeaux. - La division en deux séries est en accord avec les conceptions des MongD sur les jumeaux. En effet, la naissance de deux enfants d'une même couche a un caractère insolite. De là vient que les jumeaux sont considérés comme boyéngwa, c'est-à-dire non sujets aux règles de la magie, aux interdits communs, etc. On ne peut donc pas agir envers eux comme envers le commun des enfants; ils ont droit à un traitement spécial. Pour les calmer on ne leur chante pas les berceuses ordinaires, mais des chansons toutes particulières. Pour les apaiser on ne les menace pas, on ne les réprimande pas. On les supplie comme on prie les génies auxquels ils sont souvent assimilés. C'est pourquoi un terme propre est usité pour ce comportement exceptionnel: -bénga baâsa «calmer les jumeaux», comme on dit -bénga bilima «conjurer les génies, capter leur bienveillance». Les motifs des départs des Burundais et des Rwandais vers l'Uganda (1920-1960) (Jean-Pierre Chrétien). - Les migrations de tra- vailleurs ruraux du Rwanda et du Burundi vers l'Uganda, le Tanganyika et le Congo (Zaïre actuel) qui se sont développées à l'époque du Mandat et de la Tutelle belges con- stituent un phénomène concret de l'histoire contemporaine de ces pays, qui semble trop méconnu des publications spécialisées. Il est très difficile d'estimer le nombre total de ces départs, souvent individuels et clandestins, échelonnés sur toute l'année. Le nombre des Banyarwanda et des Barundi installés au Buganda en 1931 était d'environ 30.000; il était de plus de 200.000 en 1948. Dans l'ensemble du protectorat de l'Uganda, ils étaient plus de 500.000 en 1959 (dont 350.000 Rwandais). A la même époque, plus de 120.000 Barundi et quelques 35.000 Banyarwanda étaient au Tanganyika. La thèse selon laquelle il s'agirait d'un flux séculaire de migration est sujette à caution; il faudrait plutôt insister sur la rupture entre les époques anciennes et le XXe siècle: les déplace- ments de jadis se déroulaient dans des cadres familiaux, sociaux et politiques traditionnels; les migrations modernes revêtent un caractère plus individuel, par la recherche de nouvelles filières d'« enrichissement», extérieures aux échanges traditionnels, enfin par l'expatriation. La densité de population du Rwanda et du Burundi est exceptionnelle. Elle atteignait environ 80 hab./km2 en 1954 avec plus de deux millions d'habitants au Rwanda et un peu moins au Burundi. Mais de nombreux secteurs dépassent les 100 hab./km2. Avec une croissance annuelle de 2,5 %, le doublement doit s'opérer en une trentaine d'années. La pression démographique est donc évidente : elle aggrave indirecte- ment l'érosion des sols trop intensément exploités; elle rend difficile l'épargne vivrière et donc particulièrement sensibles aux disettes les régions aux pluies irrégulières. En outre, tous les sondages montrent le primat de la motivation financière dans le départ des mi- grants. Un autre motif, souvent mentionné, est la fuite devant les «obligations admini- stratives» ou devant «des contraintes insupportables». En lisant de plus près les témoigna- ges, on s'aperçoit que la plupart concernent non «les Batutsi», conçus en bloc comme une classe exploitante ou comme une race de seigneurs, mais très précisément les chefs dits «coutumiers» dont la plupart étaient effectivement, dans les années 50, de sang royal ou tout au moins d'origine tutsi. Les chefs, devenus porteurs de toutes les exigences colo- niales, intervenaient de façon jamais vue dans la vie quotidienne des familles. L'émigra- tion a souvent été décrite comme une rupture avec la famille. La volonté d'échapper au poids des règles lignagères expliquerait entre autres le départ des Alur (un peuple nilo- tique) vers le Buganda, selon le sociologue A. Southall. Dans d'autres cas, il s'agit plus d'une rupture rituelle à la manière d'une initiation, de la nécessité de quitter son village quelque temps pour devenir un homme mûr et pouvoir se marier, pourvu de la dot et du prestige nécessaires. Tantôt c'est tout un groupe familial qui est concerné, tantôt il s'agit d'une décision individuelle, avec évidemment toute une gamme de nuances. Ces mouvements trop souvent expliqués par des «atavismes» ou par des stéréotypes sur le «déracinement» prennent leur signification historique dans leur rapport avec les mutations introduites par le contact colonial. Elles prennent l'allure à la fois d'adaptation et de con- testation, même si l'adaptation est quelque peu illusoire et la contestation plutôt silen- cieuse. (Cultures et développement. America The Patterns of "Amerindian" Identity (Marc-Adélard Tremblay [edit.]). - In this volume are published, partly in French, partly in English, the Proceedings of a Symposium that was held in Maison Montmorency, Canada, in October 1974. It dealt with the Indians and Eskimo of Canada but also with the Eskimo of Greenland. The Sym-</page><page sequence="7">920 Miscellanea Anthropos 74. 1979 posium had as an objective the encouragement of exchanges between university teachers belonging to various disciplines, civil servants of governmental agencies, employees of private corporations and members of the indigenous communities. Thus, the speakers and other participants were men and women trained in various disciplines, and engaged in fields such as education, health, language, labour relations. With an inside view of everyday problems, they brought new perspectives to the building of an overall theo- retical framework on "Amerindian" identity. The Symposium did not lead itself to the formulation of concrete recommendations - this was not its objective - but the various elements which were set forth will undoubtedly serve as background materials in decision- making and in decisive involvements. Early Israelite Warfare and the Conquest of Canaan (Abraham Malamat). - How could the semi-nomadic Israelite tribes, emerging from the desert fringes, surmount an adversary of long military experience and possessing a superior technology ? The Canaan- ites at this stage suffered certain deficiencies which partially compensated for the military inferiority of the Israelites and facilitated a relatively rapid overrunning of the country. The Canaanite population west of the Jordan was not united into an overall, military organization which might have been able to cope with the Israelite invasion. This lack of political cohesion was complemented by a lack of any Canaanite national consciousness.</page><page sequence="9">922 Miscellanea Anthropos 74. 1979 Crossing the Jordan from east to west, the Israelites soon gained a series of successes, especially in the mountainous regions of Western Palestine, though much less in the low- lands. Analysis of the various battles described - particularly in the Books of Joshua and Judges, and to some extent in Samuel - reveals that the early Israelites gained victory over a technologically and numerically superior enemy through efficient application of what can certainly be regarded as the "indirect approach". Among the early wars of the Israelites, we find no actual description of an outright, successful assault upon an enemy city. The adoption of an indirect military approach finds expression in two principal categories of tactics: covert infiltration, thereby neutralizing the city-defences; and enticement, drawing the city-defenders out into the open. Many of the Canaanite strong- holds were not taken by deceptive means, but fell to the Israelites following victories in the field. Two such battles - fought against Canaanite leagues at Gibeon in the south and at the Waters of Merom in northern Galilee - led to the capture of entire blocs of towns. But how were the Israelites able to attain such victories in open clashes - especially at the Waters of Merom where their infantry faced chariotry? The clue to this may be found in the wording of the descriptions of these battles, in the key- word "suddenly", which implies the element of surprise. Finally, night operations were an important factor of early Israelite tactics. Though much more demanding than daytime operations - in training, leadership and courage - night operations benefit from an inherent security of movement, besides allowing for possible psychological bonuses. A small force, entirely out of proportion to the strength of its adversary, can achieve night victories provided that it is the party taking the initiative. Kaeppler). - Ethnographic collections from James Cook's three voyages are important because these voyages made the first extensive contact with Pacific peoples; they are also the first voyages from which we have collections that can be identified and studied. More than 2,000 ethnographic artifacts - "artificial curiosities" - were collected on the three voyages. Objects from these collections, lent by various per- sons and institutions, were exhibited at Bishop Museum during the year 1978. - This well-arranged and excellently illustrated volume had three aims. First, it accompanied and identified the "Artificial Curiosities" of the exhibition, while the photographs gave it a more permanent life and made (and still make) the exhibition available to those unable to visit it. Second, this volume enumerates the artifact types collected on the voyages as far as these can be ascertained today. Artifacts which were considered too breakable, etc., were not sought for the exhibition. The missing types are, however, listed and in most cases a photograph of one example is included. Third, the volume contains an inventory of all the documented Cook- voyage ethnographic objects that could be found. The publication is not meant to be a summary of Pacific ethnography of the time of the voyages of Cook; it acknowledges and honors the achievements of Pacific peoples as they were before the impact of Cook and others of the Western world irrevocably changed their lives [from the Preface and p. 49]. Comparison of Polynesian Genealogies in the Bishop Museum (Kenneth P. Emory). - The Polynesian genealogies housed at the Bishop Museum have been gathered from many parts of Polynesia. They have been studied especially for what they could reveal of history and social organization. Among the most extensive collections at the Museum are those from Hawaii, the Society Islands, and the Tuamotu Archipelago - all parts of eastern Polynesia. Genealogies which reach back any great length of time are those of chiefs, whose claim to power derives from being descended from mythical beings or gods. Recent research indicates that the Polynesians had their origin in the islands of adjacent Melanesia to the west, where it has been discovered that Fijian and the Poly- nesian languages have a common origin. Present radiocarbon dates allow for Fiji being inhabited as far back as 1500 B. C, Tonga 1000 B. C, Samoa 500 B. C, the Marquesas Islands A. D. 100, the Society Islands A. D. 400, Easter Island A. D. 600, New Zealand and Hawaii A. D. 750, with Hawaii receiving new arrivals from the Society Islands about A. D. 1250. What now appears most likely is that people of somewhat diverse origins came together in a western archipelago of the Polynesian area between 1500-1000 B. C. ; and, in comparative isolation, their language and culture took on features which the Polynesians now share in common and which give them their distinctive characteristics. - The comparison between Hawaiian and Maori genealogies illustrates how it is possible to determine a genuinely traditional genealogy as well as the usefulness that can be derived from Polynesian genealogies in the reconstruction of prehistory (protohistory). Whilst the comparison of the genealogies reaches a point beyond which the names cannot be accepted as those of actual ancestors, these names nevertheless indicate a sharing of common tradition. They are names to link the mystical past to the present and transmit its power and glory. (Pacific Studies [Laie, Hawaii] 1. 1977: 1-14.) George Taplin and His Work on Aboriginal Languages (Gordon Grimwade). - George Taplin (1831-1879) migrated to Australia in 1849. In 1859, he was appointed missionary agent for the newly established Aborigines' Friends Association, in 1868 or- dained as a Congregationalist minister. His "Comparative Table of Aboriginal Languages" was published in Egland in 1872. In 1879, a "Grammar of the Language of the Narringeri Tribe" appeared as the appendix in "Folklore, Manners, Customs and Languages of the South Australian Aborigines *. The information which he left has resulted in his being recognized as one of Australia's earliest and most thorough anthropologists.
