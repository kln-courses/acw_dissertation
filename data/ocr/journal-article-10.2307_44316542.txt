Summary Kelsen's early work was driven by his conviction that the facticity thesis , manifest in various forms in later nineteenth- century legal theory, was wrongheaded. In its place he introduced, as early as the Hauptprobleme of 1911, a normativity thesis calling for an explication of the basic concepts of the law entirely apart from any appeal to matters of fact. To be sure, the normativity thesis is programmatic, particularly in Kelsen's early work. Two fundamental doctrines lie behind it: first, constructivism , which Kelsen drew from later nineteenth-century public law theory (the German Staatsrechtsleh- re ), and, second, methodological dualism , a clear reflection of Heidelberg neo-Kantianism. These doctrines provided a basis for a third fundamental doctrine in Kelsen's early work, that of imputation , which represents his alternative to the fictitious legal person familiar from nineteenth-century le- gal science. On first glance, Kelsen's rejection of the facticity thesis, a relic of German and Austrian legal science, is a welcome development. But the radi- cal programme that he offers in order to surmount the problems of a fact- based legal theory gives rise to serious difficulties. One prong of his effort here appears to lead him to the notion that the legal system „presupposes itself", which, sans argument, counts as a petitio principii in his early work. The other prong leads to an antinomy, the insuperable abyss between the normative and the factual. In his classical or neo-Kantian phase, Kelsen re- turns to the first of these difficulties, introducing rudiments of a transcenden- tal or „basic norm" argument in an effort to make sense of the idea of pre- supposition. As for the second difficulty, however, he believes - and, indeed, states candidly in the Allgemeine Staatslehre - that it lies in the nature of things, that it is the price to be paid for a „pure" theory of law.</page></plain_text>
