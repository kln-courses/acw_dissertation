At the same time that Breton alludes to the fundamental re lationship between the real and the imaginary in surrealism, he also opens for exploration a vast field of inquiry regarding the ancestral link to sur realism of such writers as Emile Zola, J.-K. Huysmans, the Goncourts, and even Robert Caze.1 Inasmuch as Zola's hypnotic attraction to concrete reality far exceeded that of his contemporaries, he emerges as the natural ist writer most likely to offer direct points of contact with surrealism.2 This relationship becomes apparent by initially citing parallels in primary texts which a) testify that Zola and the surrealists share a common vision and b) begin to illustrate how Zola's poetic treatment of objects serves as an excellent guide for understanding the image in surrealism. Readers of Les Pas Perdus are familiar with Breton's appreciation of the Lettre du Voyant, where Rimbaud writes: "Le poète se fait voyant par un long, immense et raisonné dérèglement de tous les sens. Toutes les formes d'amour, de souffrance, de folie; il cherche lui-même, il épuise en lui tous les poisons, pour n'en garder que les quintessences." Rimbaud calls for a complete re-orientation of sensation, as he further explains in Alchimie du Verbe: "Je m'habituai à l'hallucination simple: je voyais très franche ment une mosquée à la place d'une usine, une école de tambours faite par des anges, des calèches sur les routes du ciel, un salon au fond d'un lac; les monstres, les mystères: un titre de vaudeville dressait des épouvantes devant moi." Breton has observed the significance of this disordering of the senses by writing: A l'"alchimie du verbe" avait succédé une véritable chimie qui tout d'abord s'était employée à dégager les propriétés de ces mots dont une seule, le sens, était spécifiée par le dictionnaire. Il s'agissait: 1) de considérer de mot en soi; 2) d'étudier d'aussi près que possible les réactions des mots les uns sur les autres. Ce n'est qu'à ce prix qu'on pouvait espérer rendre au langage 321</page><page sequence="2">322 Nineteenth-Century French Studies sa destination pleine, ce qui . . . devait faire faire un grand pas à la connais sance, exalter d'autant la vie.3 Breton looks back to Rimbaud in order to explain his own surrealist man ner of looking beyond things as they are, to explain how the observation of reality helps to discredit the very nature of that reality in favor of l'ima ginaire. Similarly, Max Ernst turns back to Leonardo da Vinci to explain the relationship between the real and the surreal in his chance discovery of frottage : In gazing attentively at the drawings thus obtained ... I was surprised by the sudden intensification of my visionary capacities and by the hallucina tory succession of contradictory images superimposed, one upon the other, with the persistence and rapidity characteristic of amorous memories. . . . There my eyes discovered human heads, animals, a battle that ended with a kiss (the bride of the wind), rocks, the sea and the rain, earthquakes, the sphinx in her stable, the little tables around the earth, the palette of Caesar, false positions, a shawl of frost flowers, the pampas .4 While explaining the process of collage, Ernst also describes the provoca tion of "a sudden intensification of the visionary faculties in me" and the surging forth of "an illusive succession of contradictory images, double, triple and multiple images, piling up on each other with the persistence and rapidity which are peculiar to love memories and visions of half-sleep." His personal analysis of "the intensification of the irritability of the mind's faculties by appropriate technical means" enables him to draw a straight line from da Vinci and himself to the Rimbaud-associated phenomenon of automatic writing in Breton. In fact, he asserts that collage "is some thing like the alchemy of the visual image. The miracle of the total trans figuaration of beings and objects with or without modification of their physical or anatomical aspect." We are observing here the codification of the importance of creative image-making, for which the reconciliation of representation and percep tion serves as a springboard to the surreal. In this regard, Edgar Allan Poe offers another historical guiding thread. Describing a character's mental capacities, Poe writes of "a morbid irritability of those properties of the mind . . . that nervous intensity of interest with which, in my case, the powers of meditation (not to speak technically) busied and buried them selves, in the contemplation of even the most ordinary objects of the uni verse."5 These passages illustrate the existence of a precise historical continuity</page><page sequence="3">Zola 's Object 323 in the conception of a "philosophy of immanence" before it was defined by Breton,6 and they bring together terms and ideas which suggest that Breton had a lapse in artistic awareness and appreciation when he crit icized "ceux qui ne conçoivent rien au-delà de l'immonde reportage, du monument funéraire et du croquis de bagne, qui ne savent qu'agiter sous nos yeux le spectre de Zola, Zola qu'ils fouillent sans parvenir â rien lui soustraire."7 It is, after all, Zola who described the work of art as a corner of nature seen through a temperament approximately ninety years before Ernst published his book entitled Sept microbes vus à travers un tempé rament. It is Zola who wrote to one inquirer, in terms reminiscent of those used by Poe, Ernst, and da Vinci, that his visual recall of objects involved a "matérialisation à outrance."8 It is Zola who preceded Breton's "Les mots, les images ne s'offrent que comme tremplins à l'esprit de celui qui écoute"9 when he explained that things, for him, exist only as springboards from which his creative imagination may take flight: "J'ai l'hypertrophie du détail vrai, le saut dans les étoiles sur le tremplin de l'observation exacte. La vérité monte d'un coup d'aile jusqu'au symbole."10 Zola's hypertrophie awareness of concrete reality is as indispensable to the phenomenon of his image-making as the notion of a communicating vessel is to the surrealist's search for the reconciliation of the container and the contained. Specifically, there appear to be three major areas which offer points of contact between Zola and the surrealists and which serve as a fundamental clue to our understanding of the surrealist image: 1) the alchemy of words, 2) the appreciation of the modern and its correlative association with the animism and metamorphosis of a world forever in the process of becoming, and 3) an awareness of the insolite. Among the Rougon-Macquart novels, Au Bonheur des Dames seems most appropriate for discussion because simultaneously it illustrates the alchemy of words, offers surrealist examples of the unwonted, and repre sents for Zola himself "le poème de l'activité moderne."11 At the begin ning of the novel, Denise Baudu views "the ladies' delight" for the first time and exclaims, "en voilà un magasin" (III, 389).12 A department store. A physical object denoting a place where merchandise is sold. This depart ment store, however, represents much more. With its attractive display of rolls of cloth and pieces of lace, it offers a church-like effect of depth, a "lointain de tabernacle," and becomes "cette chapelle élevée au culte des grâces de la femme" (pp. 389-92). Zola links to the denotation of the department store the connotation of a house of religion. This relationship remains in the back of the reader's mind until Chapter III, where Octave Mouret discloses to Baron Hartmann his desire to transform the store into</page><page sequence="4">324 Nineteenth-Century French Studies a "palais colossal." Developing the metaphor, the Baron asks Mouret where he intends to find the clientele to fill such a "cathédrale" (pp. 456-57). The Baron's correlation of "palace" with "cathedral" parallels the one previously made by Zola and connotes a house of merchandise constructed for the religious honor of a monarch. This is precisely what Mouret has in mind: "la femme était reine, adulée et flattée dans ses faiblesses, entourée de prévenances, elle y régnait en reine amoureuse ... il lui élevait un temple, la faisait encenser par une légion de commis, créait le rite d'un culte nou veau" (p. 461). Throughout the novel, the department store is repeatedly and artistically associated with such words as "culte," "rite," "graces," and "reine," and with such images as "tabernacle," "palais," "cathédrale," and "temple," enabling Zola ultimately to depict the apotheosis of the cult of womanhood and the advent of a new religion : Sa création apportait une religion nouvelle, les églises que désertait peu à peu la foi chancelante étaient remplacées par son bazar [de Mouret] , dans les âmes inoccupées désormais. La femme venait passer chez lui les heures vides, les heures frissonnantes et inquiètes qu'elle vivait jadis au fond des chapelles, dépense nécessaire de passion nerveuse, lutte renais sante d'un dieu contre le mari, culte sans cesse renouvelé du corps, avec l'au-delà divin de la beauté. Starting with the object in itself, Zola pro ceeds to construct a network of associative values between the denotation and the various lexical connotations and images of a given object. The con notations drawn depend entirely on the individual point of view, which Zola alternates between himself and his characters, thereby fulfilling the realistic function of reminding the reader that the object is thought of as such only by the observer. As the images are associated and prolonged, simul taneously and in a spiraling fashion, they take on a life of their own and develop into a symbol. A "veritable chemistry" takes place, in which the substance of Zola's observed or lived reality becomes, through a kind of poetic transubstantiation, an entirely new substance, constituting a sym bolic novel—in the case of Au Bonheur des Dames, the "poem of modern activity." One cannot accurately state that Zola's poetic transformation of matter through language concretely manifests Breton's statement, "le langage a été donné à l'homme pour qu'il en fasse un usage surréaliste."1 s Nor should one suggest that it involves any kind of automatic writing, as the term is understood by Breton. Nevertheless, Zola's alchemy of the word prefigures Breton's contention, in Les Pas Perdus (p. 171), that "words make love" as well as it illustrates the kind of new vitality sought by Breton when he explains, in "Crise de l'Objet," that an object is more than the sum of its parts.16 Moreover, the object in Zola, descriptively transfused by his creative imagination, explains and gives birth to a new reality, a higher reality much like the one promoted by Paul Nougé. Calling for "des objets bouleversants," Nougé writes that "la réalité d'un objet dépendra étroite ment des attributs dont notre imagination l'aura doué, du nombre, de la complexité de ces attributs et de la manière dont le complexe inventé s'in sère dans l'ensemble préexistant en nous et existant dans l'esprit de nos semblables." Nougé continues: "L'on peut remarquer encore que plus la réalité d'un objet est puissante, plus cette réalité a réussi, plus les chances sont grandes également de pouvoir par une invention nouvelle l'étendre, l'enrichir ou la bouleverser pour en tirer une réalité nouvelle."1 7 This is precisely the phenomenon we observe 'm Au Bonheur des Dames. In the words of Zola, the seer, the department store becomes the embryo, indeed the symbol, of vast working societies of the twentieth century, unveiling and even eclipsing new activity, a new city, a new life. The page-long passage describing this higher reality (III, 763) reveals another idea which is perhaps as important in our appreciation of Zola's</page><page sequence="6">326 Nineteenth-Century French Studies representation and perception of things as it is dear to the surrealists', name ly, the celebration of the new and the marvelous of the modern city. Mais s'il sait les deviner, ce sage, alors, que lui les interroge, ce sont encore ses propres abîmes que grâce à ces monstres sans figures il va de nouveau sonder. While Aragon's comments recall Breton's previously observed springboards, they seem also to interpret aptly the scene in Zola's La Curée, where Saccard dines at a restaurant overlooking an ocean of houses; they remind Zola of A Thousand and One Nights, Saccard of 20-franc pieces, and form on their own a living, pullulating sea from which the profound voice of humanity issues forth (I, 387-88). It is a scene which is echoed 17 years later, when Zola writes in L !Argent: Depuis une minute, Saccard était là, à regarder tomber l'averse, lorsque, dominant le roulement de l'eau, une claire sonnerie de pièces d'or lui fit dresser l'oreille. (V, 83) We also note that through his character, Claude, in Le Ventre de Paris, Zola visualizes the manifesto of modern art in the markets of the Halles. The animism and anthropomorphism of objects, evidenced throughout the Rougon-Macquart, and perhaps most easily characterized by the "rum bling" of the department store, the "indigestion" of the Halles, and the "beating heart" of the Stock Exchange, all point to Zola's surrealistically analogical vision of the world, in which man and thing, man and animal, man and vegetable become fused in vivid personifications of "la bête hu maine." While evoking the descriptions which bring to life the landscape and vegetation of life in La Faute de l'Abbé Mouret, one thinks of the an thropomorphic landscapes of André Masson or, specifically, of Max Ernst's painting, The Joy of Living, where the limbs and leaves of plants progres sively turn into insects, animals, and monsters. In another of Ernsts's paint ings, The Elephant Celebes, the animal-monster-machine is strangely remi niscent of Gervaise's fantastic visions of the alembic in L'Assommoir and of Etienne's hallucinatory visions of the Voreux mine emerging as if from a dream. In Nana, the teenage prostitute, an object of desire, undergoes 78 descriptive mutations and incarnates 17 different animals.1 9 The jux taposition of these obsessive, sometimes hallucinatory, passages brings to mind the surrealist game, "le cadavre exquis," the essential distinction be ing that Zola's superimposition of terms appears quite controlled and with determined symbolic import. With the symphony of cheeses in Le Ventre de Paris and the symphony of flowers in La Faute de l'Abbé Mouret, Zola communicates to static elements the fleeting and fluid quality of time by a synesthesic transfer of odors into the realm of music. We not only see how things concert their own existence and can even live and die with an hallucinatory credibility, but how Zola's spontaneous, more-than-lucid, and perhaps even insolent perception of things20 exemplifies Breton's de sire, in Surrealism and Painting (p. 279) to "hunt down the mad beast of convention." Furthermore, I have tried to show elsewhere how Zola's treat ment of the relationship between man and things entails a process of reifi cation, the ultimate result of which produces in turn a loss of alienation and a re-humanization of man's condition.21 In short, the relationship established by Zola between man and things predates and parallels the very goals underpinning E. S. Matta's declaration: "I always defended Breton's definition of Surrealism, which has to do with the total emancipation of man. Surrealism is 'more reality.' There is always the need for man to grasp 'more reality,' for only in this way can we create a truly human condi tion."22 This is a fundamental goal for the surrealists, who seek in the object</page><page sequence="8">328 Nineteenth-Century French Studies image a means for understanding man more profoundly than conventional reality seems to permit. Yet we find in the pages of Zola a surrealist pre dilection for the unusual, the fetish, and the joining of seemingly irreconcil able objects and images. One typically surrealist fetish-object is the glove, found particularly in the paintings of Giorgio De Chirico,23 and found also in Zola's Nana without any apparent function or reason for being: "parmi la débandade peinturlurée des enseignes, un énorme gant de pour pre, au loin, semblait une main saignante, coupée et attachée par une man chette jaune" (II, 1259). Zola's insertion of this enigmatic object suggests Breton's love for the discovery of the strange, as revealed, for example, in Nadja, where he describes a woman's glove made of bronze: "gant que je n'ai jamais pu m'empécher de soulever, surpris toujours de son poids et ne tenant à rien tant, semble-t-il, qu'à mesurer la force exacte avec laquelle il appuie sur ce quoi l'autre n'eût pas appuyé."24 In Au Bonheur des Dames, when Zola describes the display of summer fashions, he writes: "Mais ce qui ameutait la foule, c'était, à la ganterie, un chalet suisse fait uniquement avec des gants. . . . D'abord, des gants noirs établissaient le rez-de-chaussée; puis, venaient des gants paille, réséda, sang de boeuf, disbribués dans la décoration, bordant les fenêtres, indiquant les balcons, remplaçant les tuiles" (III, 621). The surrealists' interest in the latent content of objects appears especially relevant to this passage in light of the fact that Zola's documentary notes reveal that the metaphors and colors were not previously observed in reality. Au Bonheur des Dames contains a summary text which is so potential ly surrealist in its imagery that it is bewildering that no surrealist artist seems to have noticed it. The passage comes at the beginning of the novel, when Denise and her uncle see the department store for the first time: D'abord, ils furent séduits par un arrangement compliqué: en haut, des parapluies, posés obliquement, semblaient mettre un toit de cabane rusti que; dessous, des bas de soie, pendus à des tringles, montraient des pro fils arrondis de mollets, les uns semés de bouquet de roses, les autres de toutes nuances, les noirs à jour, les rouges à coins brodés, les chair dont le grain satiné avait la douceur d'une peau de blonde; enfin, sur le drap de l'étagère, des gants étaient jetés symétriquement, avec leurs doits al longés, leur paume étroite de vierge byzantine, cette grâce raidie et comme adolescente des chiffons de femme qui n'ont pas été portés. (III, 391) Although we would expect to find these objects in a department store, how can we not associate the imagery of the text with Lautréamont's "Beau tiful as the chance encounter on a dissection table of a sewing machine and an umbrella," with Reverdy's "bringing together of two more or less dis tant realities/' with Ernst's "apparently unjoinable realities," with Magritte's roses, with De Chineo's gloves, and with the surrealists' fundamental appre</page><page sequence="11">Zola's Object 331 ation of eroticism and of oriental art. To the extent that Zola emerges as a proto-surrealist writer, we must not overlook the fact that there are differences between the theoretical limita tions of his literary work and the freedom given to imaginative creation by the surrealists. We must distinguish passages in the Rougon-Macquart which have documentary antecedent from those which do not, passages which are contrived from those which are not, passages which bear obviously symbolic content from those which have none. Nevertheless, the object in Zola serves as an excellent illustration and guide for understanding so many of the ramifications of the image in surrealism. By his poetic tran substantiation of reality, by his appreciation of the modern, of the insolite, of a world in metamorphosis, in short, by his utilization of objects endowed with resonances perceivable by most human beings, Zola enables us to com prehend what Aragon meant when he said: "Le vice appelé Surréalisme est l'emploi déréglé et passionel du stupéfiant image, ou plutôt de la provoca tion sans contrôle de l'image pour elle-même et pour ce qu'elle entraîne dans le domaine de la représentation de perturbations imprévisibles et de métamorphoses; car chaque image à chaque coup vous force à réviser tout l'univers."34 It is thanks to Zola that we may embrace Eluard when he writes: "D n'y a pas loin, par l'oiseau, du nuage à l'homme, il n'y a pas loin, par les images, de l'homme à ce qu'il voit, de la nature des choses réel les à là nature des choses imaginées."3 5 It is Zola's object and concomitant apocalyptic vision which explain the recreation of the world promised by the alchemist and so cherished by Breton: Behold, I make all things new.
