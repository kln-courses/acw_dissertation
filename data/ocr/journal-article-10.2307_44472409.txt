NM ABSTRACT Applied Technology Associates, Inc., (ATA) has developed a new angular motion sensor based on the laws of magnetohydrodynamics for the Department of Transportation. The magnetohydrodynamic angular motion sensor (MHD AMS) was originally developed for the Air Force for measurement of ultralow angular motion on pointing and tracking platforms. The transducer developed for the Department of Transportation, MHD AMS model IETL-001 , or simply IETL, is a smaller, lighter version of the original MHD sensor with modifications which allow measurement of the high angular velocities incurred by the Anthropomorphic Test Device (ATD) heads in crash testing. The IETL weighs approximately 35 grams (1 .2 oz.) and occupies a volume of about 5 cubic centimeters (0.3 cubic inch). ATD applica- tion test results verify that the IETL exhibits both low cross-axis angular sensitivity and low linear acceleration sensitivity. These are important fea- tures for angular motion measurement in high shock environments, i.e., crash testing. The principle of operation, the physical and performance characteristics, and the ATD head application test results will be presented. In addi- tion, a brief discussion of other related applications and design improvements will be given. BACKGROUND Typically, rotational motion of the Anthropomorphic Test Device (ATD) or "crash dummy" head has been derived indirectly by post processing the outputs of accelerometer arrays to calculate an- gular acceleration about three orthogonal coordi- nate axes of the ATD head. Nine and fifteen ac- celerometer arrays (1 , 2) * do provide both trans- lational and rotational acceleration information at the expense of having to collect and post process more signals than are required for the six degrees- of-freedom (DOF) motion measurement of the head. Accelerometer arrays were used because good angular motion sensors that operate accu- rately in a high g environment did not exist. An innovative angular motion sensor was sought by the Department of Transportation, specifically the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), to provide a direct, more accurate method of ATD head rotational motion measure- ment. The magnetohydrodynamic angular motion sensor (MHD AMS) was originally developed for the Air Force (3,4,5), but was modified under con- tract to NHTSA for the Hybrid III head application. The IETL-001, or simply IETL (Figure 1), was tested at the Transportation Research Center of Ohio in both the Dummy Calibration Laboratory and in the Impact Simulator Laboratory. A Hybrid III head was instrumented with lETLs along with the 3-2-2-2 nine-accelerometer array, commonly used by NHTSA, for comparison of the angular motion measurements. Bench testing was also conducted at the Naval Biodynamics Laboratory (6) which confirmed performance test results at ATA. A magnetic field is applied perpendicularly through a constrained annulus of conductive fluid by a permanent magnet (Figure 2). As the sensor is rotated about its sensitive axis (sensitive axis is normal to the plane of the annulus, see Figure 2), the fixed permanent magnet and associated mag- netic field move with the case, but the conductive fluid, which functions as the inertial proof mass, remains stationary with respect to its own inertial reference frame. These integral electronics provide 1 ,000 V/V amplification and a single pole lowpass filter with the cutoff frequency set at 2,650 Hz. The IETL is inherently insensitive to cross- axis angular motion and to linear acceleration. The only means of producing an output voltage across the mercury channel is if there exists a relative velocity difference between the mercury and the case, which only occurs for rotation about the sensitive axis of the IETL. Rotational motion about any axis perpendicular to the sensitive axis will not produce any relative velocity difference un- less there is a misalignment between the mercury channel and the mounting plane at the base of the sensor. Similarly, translational motion of the sensor will not produce any relative fluid and case velocity difference, or output voltage, since mercury, mod- eled as an incompressible fluid, cannot move with respect to the case. This assumes that there are no voids in the mercury and that the annulus walls are infinitely rigid. Circumferential fluid velocity relative to the case will not occur because pres- sures equalize when the mercury is accelerated by a translational motion. EXPERIMENTAL TEST RESULTS AND ANALYSIS Performance verification of the IETL in the ATD head application was conducted at the Transportation Research Center of Ohio (TRC) at both the Dummy Calibration and Impact Simulator Laboratories. Precharacterization of the lETLs was conducted at ATA and also at the Naval Biody- namics Laboratory [results contained in reference 6]. The Dummy Calibration Laboratory tests made use of three different test fixtures which were a) the head drop, (b) the head-neck pendulum, and (c) the linear impact test fixtures. The array of tests provided ample data for dynamic performance characterization of the lETLs in an environment typical of crash testing. The Impact Simulator Laboratory tests con- sisted of two Hyge sled tests. Testing was done using an unrestrained Hybrid III dummy in which the head was instrumented with lETLs and the nine-accelerometer array. A more in-depth discussion of the Hyge sled testing will be pre- sented below. HYBRID III HEAD INSTRUMENTATION The Hybrid III ATD head that was Instru- mented for testing at TRC was specially modified with threaded inserts to accept nine linear ac- celerometers (Endevco 7264-2000). The nine-ac- celerometer array in the 3-2-2-2 configuration, commonly used by NHTSA, provided a redundant means of angular motion measurement for com- parison with the IETL results. The inserts in the Hybrid III head allowed attachment of the ac- celerometer cube mounts (two accelerometers per mount) at three skull locations (nose, left ear, and top of the head, see Figure 4). Three accelerome- ters were located near the center of gravity (CG) of the head to complete the nine-accelerometer array. In addition to the nine accelerometers, the Hybrid III head was instrumented with up to eight lETLs located on two different mounts within the head: a pentad mount and the triad mount. The triad unit is comprised of three lETLs and three Endevco 7264-2000 linear accelerometers. The three ac- celerometers on the triad unit function as part of the standard nine-accelerometer array when the triad unit is in place. These CG accelerometers have their respective sensitive axes (seismic masses) aligned with the dummy head CG coor- dinate axes. The six DOF triad unit is designed to be bolted with alignment pin accuracy to the stan- dard six-axis neck transducer currently used in the Hybrid III head, but could also be adapted to the three-axis neck transducer if required. The pentad cube (Figure 7), which is mounted to the backplate, can accommodate up to five lETLs: (one per X axis, two per Y axis, and two per Z axis). Redundant lETLs were used for testing purposes, but only three orthogonally mounted lETLs are needed to measure all three rotational degrees-of-freedom of the dummy head. About half of the tests performed at TRC were without the triad unit. In these cases the three CG accelerometers that were mounted on the triad unit were transferred to a standard accelerometer mounting cube at the same location, again aligned with the Hybrid III head CG coordinate axes. TEST OBJECTIVES The objectives of the ATD head application tests were to verify that the: • IETL is mechanically compatible with the Hybrid III ATD head and electronically compatible with the instrumentation used in ATD testing at TRC; • IETL angular velocity measurement can be differentiated to yield angular accel- eration accurately, and also integrated to yield angular displacement accurately; • IETL performs adequately in Hyge sled tests (A-pillar impact); • IETL exhibits low cross-axis angular and low linear acceleration sensitivity while operating in an acceleration environment representative of a vehicular crash. IETL COMPATIBILITY The IETL meets this objective because it was designed to meet specifications prescribed by NHTSA for the Hybrid III ATD head instrumenta- tion. These include parameters such as size and weight, linear acceleration sensitivity, cross-axis angular sensitivity, operational shock environment, and dynamic measurement range (sensitivity con- straints). The sensor was designed to be inher- ently rugged (shock tested at ATA up to 3000g) and virtually maintenance free. The IETL is compatible with most data acqui- sition systems where signals of less than (+/-) 5V are digitized. The integral electronics enable the sensor to operate with excitation anywhere from (+/-) 5 Vdc to (+/-)18 Vdc instrumentation power supplies. The lETLs were interfaced to TRC's data acquisition system with minimal effort for both Dummy Calibration and Impact Simulator Labora- tory tests. TRC's instrumentation supplied excita- tion to the lETLs and provided signal conditioning (1 ,000 Class filtering and 1 0 V/V amplification) and digitization (8,000 samples/s at 12 bits/sample) of the IETL output signals. The IETL output signals were well behaved and functioned as expected. TRC also provided similar instrumentation for the accelerometer bridge completion and signal conditioning/digitization. Up to six lETLs and all nine accelerometer signals were digitized and col- lected during each test. The head-neck pendulum test fixture, shown in Fig- ure 8, was used for the differentiation and integra- tion comparisons since three different types of sensor techniques were available for measure- ment of angular motion about the Y axis of the head. The same two IETL angular velocity signals (008 and 015) collected during the pendulum test were digitally prefiltered with a fourth order But- terworth lowpass filter (fc=1,000 Hz), and then dif- ferentiated (5-point differentiator based on the Taylor series) to mathematically produce angular acceleration. Another analysis of differentiated IETL angular velocities is given in the Hyge Sled test section. The Hybrid III head and neck assembly, which was affixed to the bottom of the pendulum, was equipped with two rotational potentiometers for measurement of the angular displacement be- tween the pendulum and the neck (beta poten- tiometer), and between the neck and the head (theta potentiometer). Two redundant lETLs (one triad IETL and one pentad IETL) measured the an- gular velocity about the Y axis, and the nine-ac- celerometer array provided a method of deriving the Y-axis angular acceleration. The nine-ac- celerometer array derived angular acceleration was integrated to produce angular velocity. Figure 10 shows the two redundant IETL (serial numbers 008 and 01 5) angular velocity results overlayed with the nine-accelerometer array integrated an- gular acceleration result. The nine-accelerometer array integrated acceleration result is similar to the IETL results, but does not match exactly. This is due to cumulative bias errors which arise from having to process four different accelerometer sig- nals to produce angular acceleration, which is then integrated to produce angular velocity. The IETL result is believable since the angular velocity is approaching zero as expected. Addition of the two rotational potentiometer angular displacement measurements resulted in the total angular displacement of the head about the Y axis during the pendulum impact. A com- parison was made by integrating each of the IETL angular velocities and overlaying the results (Figure 11) with the summed angular potentiome- ter and the nine-accelerometer array double inte- grated angular acceleration results (bias and trend removed before integration produced closest re- sult). The differences between the summed potentiometer result, the IETL results, and the nine-accelerometer array result could be due to scale factor discrepancies. The bias was sub- tracted from each of the IETL velocity signals be- fore integration to set the integration constant to zero at the time of impact. This is necessary be- cause the IETL will have a nonzero level at the time of impact due to the angular velocity of the pendulum, which is not measured via the rotational potentiometers; these only measure the rotation of the head with respect to the pendulum. The IETL results more closely match the summed poten- tiometers than does the nine-accelerometer array result. HYGE SLED TEST RESULTS The culmination of the testing occurred in the Impact Simulator Laboratory at TRC with the Hyge Sled and a generic test buck supplied by NHTSA. Test results are presented for the padded and un- padded 40-degree A-pillar bar with an unre- strained dummy (dummy head at 1 7 inches from A- pillar bar). The acceleration profiles (Figure 1 2) simulated a 26g, 50 ms half sine pulse which ac- celerated the sled to a terminal velocity of 20 mph. Both tests were identical except that for one test the A-pillar bar was unpadded, and the for the other the bar was padded (1 inch of Dytherm 4). The nine-accelerometer array was intact and both the triad block and the pentad block were used in both tests. Nine-accelerometer signals and six IETL signals, three from the triad block (X, Y, and Z lETLs), and three from the pentad mount (two re- dundant Y lETLs and one Z IETL) were collected. LINEAR ACCELERATION/CROSS-AXIS ERROR One of the most important aspects of the IETL sensor is that the sensor exhibits relatively low sensitivity to linear acceleration and low cross-axis angular velocity sensitivity. Typically, the linear acceleration sensitivity of the IETL is better than 0.005 (rad/s)/g, and the cross-axis angular sensi- tivity is better than 2%. A comparison was made between the outputs of the two redundant Z-axis lETLs for both of the padded and unpadded A-pil- lar tests. The difference between the Z-axis lETLs could be due to linear acceleration and cross-axis angular sensitivity error. A cumulative linear ac- celeration and cross-axis angular velocity refer- ence error was calculated for both of the Hyge sled tests by multiplying the CG X-axis linear accelera- tion by 0.005 (rad/s)/g and then adding that result to the Y-axis angular velocity multiplied by 0.02, which is the equivalent worst case cross-axis error. Figure 29 is the cumulative error overlayed with the difference between the Z-axis lETLs in the un- padded A-pillar test. As shown, the reference error is about the same magnitude as the true error. However, most of the difference between the lETLs is due to electronic noise. The same analysis was performed for the unpadded A-pillar case and is shown in Figure 30. IMPROVED CONSIDERATIONS The IETL is the first prototype MHD AMS to incorporate thick film integral electronics. The ad- dition of the electronics greatly improved the per- formance of the sensor. Nonetheless, it is desir- able to design sensors for ATD applications which are smaller, lighter, and more accurate. However, there are no perfect sensors and every type has characteristic fundamental limitations of perfor- mance and tradeoffs must be considered. The ul- timate resolution of any sensor, assuming that all other measurement error sources are zero, is the electronic noise of the output signal. The elec- tronic noise of the IETL is almost entirely due to the amplifier. The voltage output noise of the IETL has a power spectral density (PSD) which is approxi- mately 1 .5e-1 7 v2/Hz, which is equivalent to 4e-7 (rad/s)2/Hz in angular velocity. Decreasing the size of the IETL will reduce the sensitivity, but the output voltage noise will probably remain the same. This will lower the angular velocity resolu- tion of the sensor somewhat, although the accu- racy might still be more than adequate for ATD ap- plications. Further improvements (smaller size and weight) appear to be feasible. RELATED APPLICATIONS The IETL could be used in many other areas of ATD instrumentation, such as thorax and limb instrumentation. Angular motion information of these other areas might be useful for a more fuller understanding of human dynamics. Also, crash testing with cadavers could be another possible application of the sensor (lETLs are hermetically sealed). Finally, instrumentation of the crash vehi- cle is a potential use of the sensor, especially for tests where extreme angular motion is expected, i.e., rollover crashes. In short, the IETL shows promise for ATD related applications where accu- rate measurement of angular motion is important. CONCLUSION The ability to measure angular motion of an ATD head accurately in the highly dynamic envi- ronment of a crash is of utmost importance. The MHD AMS IETL-001 was specifically designed to perform this task. Hybrid III application testing at TRC confirmed that the IETL is extremely well suited for angular motion measurement of the dummy head in crash test environments. Validity of the measurement is confirmed by the compar- isons of the differentiated angular velocity to the nine-accelerometer array angular acceleration re- sult in both hard (Hyge Sled) and soft (pendulum) impacts. Integrated IETL angular velocity to the rotational potentiometer angular displacement re- sults were not exact but it appeared that the error could be explained by a simple scale factor differ- ence (IETL or potentiometer). Six DOF measurement of the dummy head can now be simplified because the lETL/accelerometer triad unit essentially provides the same information as the nine-accelerometer array with one third less channels, has tremen- dously simpler post processing requirements, re- quires no modification of the ATD head, and is much easier to install in the Hybrid III head than the nine-accelerometer array currently used. The three accelerometers on the triad measure the head CG acceleration (accelerometer seismic masses intersect head CG axes) in all three direc- tions, and the three lETLs directly measure the three axes of angular velocity, which can be dif- ferentiated to accurately produce angular accel- eration, analogous to the nine-accelerometer array derived angular acceleration. As suggested earlier, future considerations might be to use the IETL for instrumentation of other parts of the dummy or to instrument the crash vehicle where angular motion information might be useful. Development of improved versions of the IETL might result in smaller, lighter transducers without loss of performance for applications where space is limited, or applications where the added inertia of the sensor must be very small, such as instrumentation of child or infant ATDs.
