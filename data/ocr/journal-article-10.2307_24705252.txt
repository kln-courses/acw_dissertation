SCHNEIDER-HERRMANN In de tijden, dat er nog geen grote opgravingen verricht waren, dus de directe kennis van de Griekse kunst zeer beperkt was, vormden de oude teksten met beschrijvingen van kunstwerken een belangrijke bron. Thans, nu onze kennis onmetelijk verrijkt is, nemen deze een andere plaats in. Wij vormen ons een beeld niet meer naar hun beschrijvingen, maar wegen ge lijkwaardig tegenover elkaar, wat wij uit de teksten en wat wij uit de ar chaeologische vondsten weten. Bij de oude teksten moet men de zakelijke en de dichterlijke beschrijvingen wel onderscheiden. De zakelijke auteurs, uit Romeinse tijd, geven bijzonderheden van een bepaald kunstwerk. Bij de moderne interpretatie van de oude dichterlijke beschrijvingen moet men er in de eerste plaats naar streven van overgeleverde kunstwerken de meest bij de beschrijvingen passende te vinden. Hierbij stuiten wij echter op een grote moeilijkheid. Een dichterlijke kunstbeschrijving gehoorzaamt aan eigen artistieke wetten, die verschillend zijn van en soms tegengesteld aan de wetten der beeidende kunst. Bovendien beschikt de dichter dikwijls over rijker middelen dan de beeidende kunstenaar, vooral in een vroeg tijdperk. Het verschil wordt duidelijk, wanneer men nagaat, hoe elk voor zieh de inspiratie uit de beeidende kunst verwerkt. It is assumed that the poet was not inspired by a real shield but by his general knowledge of art. Besides, influence from Homer's description of the Heracles shield is evident. In his description, the poet lays stress on the demonology. The absence of details—as he assumes his public to know the demons' features—makes recognition difficult for us. Other descriptions and existent works of art, however, can be of assistance. The Aspis poet begins with the centrepiece of the circular shield, the demon Phobos. On the Kypselos chest as de scribed by Pausanias, Phobos—identified by an inscription—was repre sented with a lion's head; a similar representation occurs on a proto Corinthian lekythos (fig. 2). On the Aspis, Phobos must have been figured with his body in profile, but his lion's head turned to the spectator, showing its fear-inspiring eyes and teeth and bearing an Eris on its forehead. To the latter feature the Gorgoneion with two dragons on its forehead shows a close analogy (fig. 3). Eris, however, must have been a winged female figure. Lion's heads with anthropomorphic details repeatedly occur on 7th century masks. Four verses which describe Kèr, are practically identical to Homer's text. Kèr reminds us of a figure standing behind the victorious Pharao in Egyptian and Phoenician art, but has been changed into an evil demon. This now thoroughly Greek demon may have been represented as a woman bending down to grab dead warriors off the battlefield. The Great Phobos on the heads of the Gorgons may be recognised in the snakes usually surrounding the Gorgons' heads and also Athena's shield. Thus Phobos can be represented in different ways. The non-Homeric part of the description of the battle depicts man killing Kèrs. This scene shows a certain affinity to that on an ivory relief from Sparta (fig. 4). Probably the Kèrs were represented as sphinxes, whereas the Kèr mentioned above was a female figure. Thus again different representations of the same demon occur. Achlys is here the personification of the sorrow, resulting from war. 8ι</page><page sequence="30">G. SCHNEIDER-HERRMANN She does not belong to the man-destroying demons but frightens by the crude signs of mourning. A similar mourning woman occurs on a 7th century fibula. Of the works of art which show analogies to the Aspis, most belong to the early archaic period. Characteristic is the figure of Perseus, a favourite subject in that time. Various details also point to an early date: the straight sword of Perseus instead of the curved one of the later period, the poet's conception of the unceasing race of chariots suggesting a frieze-composition, the combination of chariot-race and hare-hunting (cfr. fig. 1). So does the type of Heracles' arms (though Heracles with a shield is entirely unknown to Greek art). The rich choice of pictures as given by the Aspis, is well known in early archaic art (Kypselos chest, Amycles throne, vases, bronze shield straps of Olympia and the metopes of Foce del Sele. The extensive demonology also indicates an early date. We may assume that the Aspis poet was a leader in this field; he even might have inspired plastic artists.
